<GuideQuickAnswer title="Ending Explained">
The ending confirms three rules: the phenomenon spreads through **emotional
connections**, it can move only from the **most recent victim**, and it passes
to the **first connected person who remembers them**.

Rupert remembering Amelia West starts the Galley House branch. In the modern
investigation, the final controlled handoff is **Pippa → Lawrence → Megan →
Ervin**. Reya survives because she is never the first person to remember Pippa,
Lawrence, or Megan, and she has never met Ervin. Ervin then dies as the final
carrier, leaving no known emotional connection for the chain to follow.
</GuideQuickAnswer>

<h2 id="ending-rules">
  The three rules that explain the ending
</h2>

I could follow the deaths long before I fully understood why one particular
survivor became the next victim. The final reconstruction clears that up with
three limits.

### 1. The connection must be emotional

Hearing a forgotten name is not enough.

The next person needs a meaningful connection to the latest victim: family,
friendship, a close working relationship, or another bond strong enough to
restore that person as someone they genuinely knew.

This is why Reya can listen to Ervin through the machine without automatically
taking the chain. To her, he is information and a voice—not a person from her
own life.

### 2. Only the most recent victim can pass it on

Remembering someone from an older part of the Timeline does not restart that
old branch.

The active danger always comes from the latest death. Once the chain has moved
forward, earlier victims remain forgotten history rather than new starting
points.

### 3. The first connected person to remember becomes next

Several people may have known the victim, but the phenomenon does not spread to
all of them at once.

The first emotionally connected person who remembers takes the next position in
the chain. Ervin's entire plan depends on controlling that first memory at each
handoff.

<h2 id="what-happened">
  What really happened at Galley House?
</h2>

Galley House is not the origin of the phenomenon. It is one branch of a chain
that was already moving through other families.

Inside the house, the same sequence repeats:

1. the current carrier dies;
2. a thunderclap marks the death;
3. most people lose every personal memory of that victim;
4. one connected person remembers them;
5. that person becomes the next carrier.

The body remains physically present. What disappears is the victim's place in
everyone else's life.

That is why characters can find someone still warm and immediately describe
them as an unknown stranger. The surviving group unconsciously rebuilds the
evening as though that person had never arrived, spoken, cooked, worked, or
stood beside them.

<h2 id="amelia-west">
  Who is Amelia West?
</h2>

Amelia West is a pianist from an earlier branch of the phenomenon.

The game does not give her a complete biography or state the exact label for
her relationship with Rupert. What it does establish is enough for the ending:

- a piano recital program carries Amelia West's name;
- Rupert recognizes her music;
- he realizes she seems to have disappeared without anyone noticing;
- and he reacts as someone who knew and cared about her.

<h2 id="historical-incident">
  How the original incident proves the memory-chain rule
</h2>

The historical case is not only background for the modern ending. It is the
first complete demonstration of how the phenomenon rewrites a group from the
inside.

### The household becomes smaller without anyone noticing

Rupert enters the local chain before the machine's original 1–11 roster.
After his death, the handoff continues through people already connected to the
household.

The clearest evidence is not a single body. It is the way later conversations
keep revising the number of people who were supposedly present:

- a known guest becomes an unidentified corpse;
- a familiar name on a handwritten list suddenly means nothing;
- survivors insist there have always been fewer people in the house;
- and family relationships disappear even while photographs, objects, and
  bodies still prove those people existed.

By the late historical scenes, the remaining characters are not merely
forgetful. They have rebuilt a coherent but false version of the evening in
which the latest victim was never part of their lives.

### Person 12 explains where the Galley House branch begins

Rupert remembering Amelia is the first local handoff the player can reconstruct.
His death occurs before the numbered household sequence, which is why the
starting roster is incomplete and why Person 12 has to be added manually.

That detail matters to the ending because it proves the phenomenon does not
begin with John arriving at the house. John walks into a chain that is already
active.

### Katherine proves the chain is not confined to the building

Katherine dies at Wintercote, outside the machine's normal local detection
area.

The machine can reach only her death moment rather than a full route through
Galley House. This establishes that the phenomenon follows relationships, not
rooms, antennas, or the physical boundaries of the estate.

### John's fall separates physical cause from supernatural handoff

John's visible fall is easy to misread as a contradiction.

The phenomenon does not need to kill every victim in the same physical way.
A fall, gunshot, or another immediate cause can still occur inside the larger
memory sequence. The supernatural rule determines who is erased and who becomes
the next carrier; it does not require every body to show the same injury.

The important ending takeaway is therefore:

> The chain is tracked through death, forgetting, and the first meaningful
> memory—not through matching every body to one supernatural method.

<h2 id="thunderclaps">
  What does each thunderclap mean?
</h2>

A thunderclap marks a death and the creation of a new Timeline entry.

In the modern Living Room reconstruction, Lawrence counts **four
thunderclaps** and matches them to:

```text
27 → 28 → 29 → 30
```

He then states that there is one new time code for each death.

When I hear one, I check the story in this order:

1. who has disappeared from the latest conversation;
2. who still speaks about that person as someone they knew;
3. whether everyone else treats the name as unfamiliar;
4. which relationship can carry the chain forward.

The person standing nearest the body is not automatically next. The first
meaningful memory is what matters.

<h2 id="modern-chain">
  The final modern memory chain
</h2>

The final controlled sequence is:

```text
Pippa → Lawrence → Megan → Ervin
```

### Pippa to Lawrence

Pippa dies while helping Lawrence search for the film.

Lawrence remembers that she was with him and recognizes what has happened.
Reya does not remember her, so the chain has already selected Lawrence.

### Lawrence to Megan

Megan cannot let Reya remember Lawrence first.

She uses the toy dinosaur from Lawrence's desk as a deliberate memory anchor.
The object restores Lawrence as a real person from her life, and she takes the
next handoff.

### Megan to Ervin

Ervin prepares his own reminder of Megan.

After Megan dies, he allows that memory to return and confirms that he remembers
her. Only then does he become the current carrier.

This is why Ervin cannot simply kill himself at the beginning. His death ends
nothing unless the chain has already reached him.

<h2 id="ervin-plan">
  Ervin's plan and why he must remember Megan
</h2>

Ervin brings the group to Galley House under the impression that they may
discover the origin of the historical deaths.

He has already stopped believing that.

He has traced the phenomenon through the Wests and through the Jacobsons before
them, but the trail keeps going. He cannot identify a first victim, and he does
not have a cure.

His real plan is containment:

1. gather the remaining connected people in one controlled place;
2. let the modern chain move through them in a planned order;
3. make Megan remember Lawrence before Reya does;
4. remember Megan himself;
5. become the last carrier;
6. die with no known connected person left to remember him.

Ervin says Megan and Lawrence are the only living people left who still know
him. Both must be removed from the chain before Ervin takes the final handoff.

If Ervin died before remembering Megan, the active carrier would still be Megan
or whoever remembered her first. His death would be unrelated to the current
handoff and the phenomenon could continue elsewhere.

<h2 id="why-reya-survives">
  Why does Reya survive?
</h2>

Reya survives because she is protected at every final handoff:

- Lawrence remembers Pippa before Reya does;
- Megan remembers Lawrence before Reya does;
- Ervin remembers Megan before Reya does;
- Reya has never met Ervin.

The final point is the real safeguard.

That does not make the plan risk-free. Ervin can only hope that no forgotten
acquaintance still remembers him somewhere else. But among the known people in
this branch, Reya is intentionally left outside the emotional chain.

<h2 id="machine-origin-cure">
  Did the machine cause it, and did Ervin find a cure?
</h2>

No to both.

The phenomenon predates the reconstruction machine and predates the Galley
House incident.

The machine is dangerous because it can:

- restore people erased from memory;
- replay voices and relationships;
- expose a carrier to a forgotten person;
- and intensify remembrance through its noetic equipment.

It is an amplifier and investigative tool, not the original cause.

Ervin also does not cure the phenomenon. He says the trail through the Wests
and Jacobsons never ends, and he is no longer convinced a first cause can be
found.

His plan attempts to stop **this active branch**. It does not prove the
phenomenon has disappeared from the world.

<h2 id="cost-of-erasure">
  What Ervin's family and Eleanor reveal about memory erasure
</h2>

Ervin's wife dies in the conservatory.

When he finds her, he cannot remember who she is. His two daughters die next,
and he cannot even recover the personal grief that should tell him what he has
lost.

By the ending, his own life feels empty:

- he works alone;
- sleeps alone;
- remembers no parents;
- and believes he has no friends.

The horror is not only that his family died. The phenomenon has removed the
relationships that would let him understand himself as a husband, father,
friend, or son.

Eleanor Galley shows a second kind of erasure.

Her memorial names her as the sister of Rupert and Oswald. Ervin concludes she
was not one of the direct carriers because her family could still remember her.
But after the people who knew Eleanor are gone, nobody remembers her either.

<h2 id="final-request">
  Why does Ervin ask Reya to destroy everything?
</h2>

Ervin has already destroyed his personal effects and erased as many records of
his existence as he can.

He then asks Reya to leave nothing behind:

- not the bodies;
- not Galley House;
- not the machine.

Destroying everything serves two purposes.

First, it removes the strongest local tool for restoring erased relationships.
Second, it reduces the chance that another person will find Ervin's body,
identify him, search his records, and reconnect the chain to someone who knew
him.

He is not trying to prove the phenomenon never existed. He is trying to leave
this branch with no usable path forward.

<h2 id="scene-00">
  What does Scene 00 mean?
</h2>

Scene 00 is not the origin of the phenomenon.

It is a restored moment before Time 01, when the Galley family and their guests
gather for a photograph in the Living Room.

The scene provides emotional closure in three ways.

### It restores the people behind the machine profiles

For most of the investigation, I know these people as numbers, portraits,
voices, and missing bodies.

Scene 00 briefly returns them to an ordinary social setting where they joke,
argue, make plans, and gather for a family photograph.

### It shows what the phenomenon actually destroyed

The deaths are only the visible part.

The deeper loss is every ordinary memory proving that these people belonged to
one another. Scene 00 shows the relationships that the surviving Timeline can
no longer preserve.

### It closes Galley House without solving the global origin

Ervin has already explained that the trail reaches beyond the Galley family.

Scene 00 answers:

> What was lost in this house?

It does not answer:

> Where did the phenomenon begin?

<h2 id="unanswered">
  What does the ending deliberately leave open?
</h2>

The game explains how this branch operates, but it does not close the wider
mystery.

It leaves four important questions unanswered:

- who the first victim was;
- whether the phenomenon is supernatural, technological, or something else;
- whether a general cure exists;
- and whether another disconnected branch is still active elsewhere.

It also cannot prove Ervin's containment will last forever. His plan depends on
the belief that nobody emotionally connected to him remains available to
remember him after his death.

That uncertainty does not make the plan meaningless. Ervin has enough
information to close the branch in front of Reya, even though he cannot remove
the phenomenon from the world.

<h2 id="faq">
  The Incident at Galley House Ending FAQ
</h2>

### Who is Amelia West, and what evidence connects her to Rupert?

Amelia West is the pianist named on the recital program found beside the
unidentified Study body. Rupert later recognizes her music and realizes he had
forgotten her. That restored personal memory, followed by the thunderclap, is
the evidence that the active chain passes from Amelia to Rupert.

### Why can the machine show Katherine if she never comes to Galley House?

The machine manual says a person who dies during the Timeline but outside its
local range may leave only the moment of death accessible. Katherine's
Wintercote scene is that exception: the machine cannot follow her through the
house because she was never there.

### Why does John's fall not break the memory-chain rule?

The rule controls the supernatural handoff, not the visible injury on every
body. John can die from a fall while still occupying a place in the same
death-and-forgetting sequence. Physical cause and memory transfer are related
events, but they are not the same thing.

### What exactly does a thunderclap tell the player?

It marks a new death and a new Timeline code. Lawrence makes this explicit when
he counts four thunderclaps and matches them to Times 27, 28, 29, and 30—one
new code for each modern death.

### Why is Reya safe when she hears Ervin through the machine?

Megan remembers Lawrence first by focusing on his toy dinosaur, and Ervin then
remembers Megan first. Reya is left outside those handoffs. She has also never
met Ervin, so his reconstructed voice gives her evidence without restoring a
personal relationship from her own life.

### Why destroy the machine if it did not cause the phenomenon?

The machine can restore erased voices and relationships, making it a powerful
way to reopen a branch. Ervin therefore asks Reya to destroy the machine along
with the house and bodies, after already burning his personal effects and
erasing records of his identity.

### What does Scene 00 add that the ending explanation does not?

Scene 00 restores the household before Time 01 as a family photograph in the
Living Room. It does not explain the global origin; it shows the ordinary
relationships that the later Timeline has erased.

### Why is Eleanor Galley's memorial important?

The memorial identifies Eleanor through her parents and her brothers Rupert and
Oswald. Ervin concludes she was not the direct next carrier because her family
could still remember her, but once everyone who knew her is gone, she is erased
from living memory anyway.

### Is there a second or branching ending?

No separate decision creates another conclusion. Optional scenes and achievement
cleanup add evidence—especially Scene 00—but the narrative ending remains
Ervin's containment plan and Reya surviving to carry out his final request.
