<h2 id="how-to-use">
  How to use this guide without spoiling the mystery
</h2>

I reveal only the smallest clue needed to continue.

My order is:

1. replay the last successful scene;
2. reread its final lines;
3. check whether a new room, person, or time was added;
4. compare the participant list with everyone physically present;
5. open the gentle hint;
6. reveal the exact solution only after I understand why my attempt failed.

This page is intentionally a **stuck guide**, not a second full-code list. It
focuses on the points where a plausible reconstruction fails and explains how
to correct it.

The launch achievement snapshot supports that focus: **72.9%** of players had
earned Persistence, which requires entering the same invalid reconstruction
twice. Repeating a nearly correct code is normal, but a third identical attempt
does not reveal anything new.

### Location-code mistakes I check first

- `LI` is Living Room; it is not a generic label for any interior scene.
- `BI` is Billiard Room; finding an object there does not prove the remembered
  conversation also happened there.
- `WI` is Wintercote, not a room inside Galley House.
- `HE`, `VI`, `TO`, `OS`, `MA`, and `ED` are named bedrooms.
- `CH` is Chapel, not a character code.

I add a location when the game names it. A correct deduction still cannot be
entered while that location is missing from the machine database.

<h2 id="code-not-working">
  Why a correct-looking scene code does not work
</h2>

I do not change every field at once. I use this five-step diagnosis.

### 1. Check for a missing silent participant

A person does not need to speak to count as present.

I look for:

- someone being addressed without replying;
- a person entering or leaving during the scene;
- the machine operator standing beside the speaker;
- someone physically helping, carrying, watching, or searching;
- a body already present in the room.

This is the most common cause of a nearly correct code.

### 2. Check where the remembered conversation happened

The room where a clue or body is found may not be the reconstruction location.

I use background details:

- piano or desk for the Study;
- billiard table for the Billiard Room;
- cooking surfaces and dumbwaiter for the Kitchen;
- beds and personal objects for named bedrooms;
- chapel furniture for the Chapel;
- fireplace and seating arrangement for the Living Room.

### 3. Check whether a thunderclap created a new time

A thunderclap marks a death and a new Timeline entry.

When I hear one, I stop before submitting another scene and ask:

1. who was remembered immediately before it;
2. who disappears from later conversation;
3. who still remembers that missing person;
4. whether the machine has opened a new time.

### 4. Check whether the machine knows the person or location

A logically correct solution can still be unavailable.

I return to:

- **People** when a numeric or lettered profile is missing;
- **Locations** when a named room is not selectable;
- the access card or modern ID evidence when a later character lacks a usable
  number.

### 5. Do not submit the same failed code a third time

The second identical failure earns Persistence. The third teaches nothing.

I go back to the final lines of the previous successful scene instead. The
missing destination or silent participant is often stated immediately before
the reconstruction ends.

<h2 id="death-order">
  How to track deaths, thunderclaps, and memory changes
</h2>

A body alone does not tell me the complete sequence.

For every thunderclap, I check:

1. whether the Timeline gained a new time;
2. who was alive and remembered immediately before it;
3. who becomes an unknown stranger afterward;
4. who still speaks about that person as someone they knew;
5. whether that remembered relationship points to the next carrier.

The room where a body is discovered may not be where the death occurred.
Characters can move, discover, or discuss a body after the relevant
reconstruction.

### Continue forward when

- the newest scene names the next destination;
- it identifies a new person directly;
- it shows who entered or left;
- or it opens a clear new time.

### Go back when

- the newest scene proves only that someone died;
- one character remembers a person nobody else recognizes;
- a relationship suddenly vanishes from later dialogue;
- or the next time has no obvious location.

The missing answer is usually in the final scene before the memory change, not
in the later room where the body is found.

<h2 id="built-in-hints">
  When I use the built-in hint system
</h2>

I use a hint only after all three conditions are true:

1. I replayed the previous successful scene;
2. I can justify the chosen time and location;
3. the unresolved field is still the participant list.

That is better than cycling through every selectable ID.

I do not use a hint when:

- the dialogue directly names the next room;
- a new location has not been added;
- the access card or ID evidence has not been read;
- I have not checked who enters or leaves;
- or a thunderclap has opened a new time I have not inspected.

The built-in hint system is most useful for a participant deduction. It is less
useful when the missing information is already visible in Locations, People,
or the evidence panel.

The `00-HE-L-P` achievement code is a separate joke solution. It is not proof
that the ordinary hint button was used.

<h2 id="next-action">
  Keep going, replay the scene, or return to the machine?
</h2>

### Keep going when

- the scene ends with a clear movement to another room;
- a new person is directly named;
- a new time has appeared;
- or every participant is supported by the scene.

### Replay the scene when

- the time and location feel certain but the code fails;
- someone is visible without speaking;
- two characters use pronouns instead of names;
- or a person enters or leaves midway through.

### Return to People or Locations when

- the needed ID is not selectable;
- a caller or forgotten person has no profile;
- a room is named but missing;
- or a recovered card unlocks a new group of modern IDs.

### Stop guessing when

- the same code has failed twice;
- changing the room creates less evidence rather than more;
- and only one field remains unresolved.

<h2 id="common-mistakes">
  Common mistakes that waste the most time
</h2>

### Including only the speaker

The machine wants everyone physically present, including listeners and silent
characters.

### Using the room where a body was found

The remembered death or conversation may have happened elsewhere.

### Treating thunder as background sound

A thunderclap marks a death and helps establish the new time order.

### Copying the previous participant list

Movement between rooms often changes the group.

### Renaming every profile too early

A wrong name makes later dialogue harder to follow. I rename someone only after
a direct introduction, invitation, letter, ID list, relationship, or other
clear evidence confirms the identity.

### Creating hidden profiles too early

Manual profiles such as Person 12, Person K, L, or P can clutter the roster
before the main deduction is ready.

### Building a full spreadsheet before it is useful

The Timeline and attached evidence handle most of the main route. I keep only a
short external note for unresolved relationships, hidden IDs, and rooms that
have been named but not yet added.

<h2 id="faq">
  The Incident at Galley House Hints FAQ
</h2>

### Is there a penalty for using the built-in hints?

No. The opening notebook explicitly states that there is no penalty for using
hints. The active scene also pauses while the hint panel is open, so I can read
the clue without missing dialogue or movement in the reconstruction.

### What happens if I enter the same wrong code twice?

The second identical failure unlocks **Persistence**. It does not reveal the
correct answer, so I treat it as a signal to stop repeating the attempt and
recheck one field at a time.

### Can evidence from one scene solve a different scene?

Yes. The machine manual specifically notes that scene codes can be constructed
from clues found in other reconstructions. When the latest scene gives no clear
next code, I replay earlier conversations and look for room movements, arrival
times, and relationships that only become meaningful later.

### What does Replay do after a scene is solved?

Replay reopens a successful reconstruction without making me rebuild its code.
I use it to check silent participants, the exact moment someone enters or
leaves, and the final destination named before the scene ends.

### Why can I know a room name but still not select it?

Discovering a location in dialogue or evidence and registering it in the
machine are separate steps. I open **Locations** and add the room before trying
the reconstruction again; another failed code will not add it automatically.

### How far can the machine trace one person?

The manual says the machine follows a person from the beginning of the
available Timeline until that person leaves the antenna detection radius, the
Timeline range ends, or the person dies. This is why some profiles have a full
house route while others stop abruptly.

### Why is Wintercote available if it is outside Galley House?

Katherine dies during the active Timeline but outside the machine's normal
local range. Wintercote is therefore available for the limited remote-death
reconstruction even though Katherine never follows a room-to-room route through
Galley House.

### Is `00-HE-L-P` the normal hint command?

No. It is an optional achievement code using Time 00, Helen's Room, and
temporary profiles L and P. The ordinary hint system is opened through the
machine interface and does not require creating lettered profiles.

### Does renaming a profile solve or change its scene code?

No. Renaming is a note-taking aid after the evidence confirms an identity. The
machine still identifies that profile by its number or letter, so a guessed
name can confuse my investigation without fixing an incorrect time, room, or
participant list.
