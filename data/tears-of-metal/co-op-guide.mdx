Tears of Metal co-op works best when I treat the Campaign as a shared route with
**separate health, Coins, item decisions, and failure risks**.

The Host determines the Campaign’s available owned-item pool in the tested lobby.
Both players can move through the Village, confirm route choices, fight, rescue
Commanders, and receive shared rewards from some paid interactions, but that does
not make every pickup or resource communal.

The most important co-op rule is simple:

> A reward can benefit both players without their Coins, health, or immediate
> survival resources becoming shared.

In the tested session, the Guest accepted the invitation but remained in the menu
until the Host pressed **Start Game**.

My startup order is:

1. choose the player with the stronger progression and owned-item pool as Host;
2. set the lobby visibility and maximum player count;
3. share the Lobby Code when the invitation does not immediately place the Guest;
4. wait until the Guest appears in the lobby;
5. have the Host press Start Game;
6. assign hero and battlefield roles before selecting the first Campaign node.

I do not assume that accepting the invite alone begins the Campaign.

<h2 id="choose-host">
  Which player should Host?
</h2>

In the tested lobby, the Guest received a warning that the multiplayer Campaign
used items owned by the Host.

That makes Host selection a build decision, not just a connection decision.

I choose the Host by checking:

- unlocked weapons;
- owned item and reward pools;
- permanent Village upgrades;
- hero options;
- and the type of build both players want to assemble.

A newer account can still join, but I do not make it Host when the other player has
a meaningfully stronger item pool.

The Host does not need to be the strongest mechanical player. The Host needs to
provide the Campaign pool that gives the team the best available options.

<h2 id="route-voting">
  How route confirmation works
</h2>

Route selection required confirmation rather than silently moving both players as
soon as one person clicked.

The map displayed a flag marker and a waiting state while the other player had not
confirmed.

  <Image

    alt="Tears of Metal co-op Campaign map showing a route flag and confirmation state while waiting for another player"
    width={1280}
    height={720}
    className="h-auto w-full rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    I treat the route flag as a final team check: both players review health,
    Coins, Threat, and the reward before confirming.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Before either player confirms, we check:

- both health bars;
- both Coin balances;
- current Threat;
- whether the next node crosses a Threat marker;
- which player still needs a core Emblem;
- whether the visible reward solves a real build problem;
- and whether the merchant or healing route remains reachable afterward.

One player being healthy does not make the team healthy.

One player having enough Coins does not mean both players can survive a future
revive or healing decision.

I reject a detour when:

- one player is already near critical health;
- the next battle crosses another Threat marker;
- the reward only improves the player whose build is already complete;
- or the detour removes access to healing needed by the weaker player.

<h2 id="shared-loot">
  What loot and resources are shared?
</h2>

Co-op rewards are mixed. I do not use one universal rule for every object.

  caption="Observed Tears of Metal co-op loot, Coin, healing, and revive rules."

### Free battlefield pickups

In one tested case, a free battlefield pickup behaved as a personal collection
rather than automatically duplicating for both players.

Because of that, we call out:

- the item;
- the effect;
- which build benefits;
- and which player is collecting it.

I do not race toward a pickup simply because it appeared beside my hero.

### Paid Chests

A paid Chest costing **75 Coins** was opened by one payment and became available to
both players in the tested Campaign.

  <Image

    alt="Tears of Metal co-op paid Chest costing 75 Coins and opening for both players after one payment"
    width={1280}

    className="h-auto w-full rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    One player paid the 75-Coin price and both players received access to the Chest
    reward. We verify the result before spending from the second balance.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The team decision is still not automatic.

Before paying, I ask:

- which player is losing the Coins;
- whether that player still has a healing reserve;
- whether the reward improves one or both builds;
- and whether a revive is likely before the next economy node.

A shared reward can still create an individual economy problem.

### Coins remain separate

Each player keeps a separate Coin balance.

I do not plan around transferring Coins after a mistake. The player expected to
perform a revive needs enough Coins in their own balance.

That changes how we spend:

- the healthier player can preserve revive money;
- the injured player can preserve healing money;
- the player with the larger balance can fund a shared paid interaction;
- and neither player spends down to zero without checking the next battle.

### Assign healing before collection

Healing collected by one player could not simply be handed back afterward.

I assign it before collection based on:

1. current health;
2. role in the next battle;
3. ability to survive one Captain attack;
4. and which player must remain standing to perform a revive.

The lowest health bar usually gets the healing, but not always.

When the low-health player is playing safely at range and the frontline player is
about to enter a Captain or boss node, the frontline survival threshold can be the
more important team resource.

<h2 id="revives">
  How revives and revive costs work
</h2>

The surviving player performs the revive and pays the displayed Coin cost.

In the tested Campaign, the displayed price was **250 Coins** during one revive and
later appeared as **350 Coins**.

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    The surviving player needed 350 Coins for this revive. I read the displayed
    price each time instead of assuming the earlier cost will remain unchanged.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The downed player’s Coins do not solve the survivor’s payment problem.

After the first down, I immediately change the route:

- both players rebuild a personal reserve;
- optional paid Chests receive more scrutiny;
- merchant detours must offer a clear survival improvement;
- and the team takes fewer battles before healing or the boss.

### Clear the revive area first

I do not begin the revive while:

- a Beacon is active;
- an Archer Tower controls the lane;
- a Captain is standing over the body;
- or several enemies can interrupt the interaction.

My revive order is:

1. move the Captain away from the body;
2. destroy the structure controlling the area;
3. clear the nearest Fodders;
4. confirm the Coin cost;
5. begin the revive;
6. protect the returning player until they can move again.

Attempting the revive immediately can convert one downed player into a failed
Campaign.

When the revive opportunity was missed, the downed player could continue by
spectating. I treat spectating as the fallback, not the plan.



<h2 id="battlefield-roles">
  How I divide battlefield roles
</h2>

I do not have both players attack the same ordinary soldier while a Beacon, Tower,
Captain, or Commander rescue remains unresolved.

My default split is:

### Frontline player

- keeps the Captain or boss facing one direction;
- Blocks or Dodges the readable attacks;
- creates control with Vortex, stun, or army pressure;
- and calls the safe punish window.

### Mobile player

- destroys Beacons and Archer Towers;
- rescues Commanders;
- clears ranged interruptions;
- collects assigned resources;
- and returns during the confirmed elite opening.

Against a dense group, one player controls the Captain while the other clears the
Fodders that would interrupt the main combo.

Against a boss, both players use the same punish window rather than taking turns
triggering overlapping attack strings.

<h2 id="faq">
  Tears of Metal co-op FAQ
</h2>

### How many players does Tears of Metal co-op support?

The lobby displays a maximum-player setting of four. The detailed loot, Coin, route,
and revive observations in this guide come from a two-player Campaign.

### Does accepting an invitation start the Campaign?

Not in the tested session. The Guest accepted the invitation but remained in the
menu until the Host pressed Start Game.

### Does the Host affect the available items?

Yes in the tested lobby. The Guest received a warning that the Campaign used items
owned by the Host, so I let the account with the stronger owned-item pool Host.

### Do all players need to confirm the route?

The tested route screen displayed a flag and a waiting state while another player
had not confirmed. I use that pause to compare both health bars, Coin balances, and
the next reward.

### Is all co-op loot shared?

No single rule covers every interaction. A free battlefield pickup behaved as
personal in one tested case, while one payment opened a 75-Coin Chest for both
players.

### Are Coins shared?

No. Players maintain separate balances. Each player needs their own reserve for
healing, purchases, or a revive they may need to perform.

### Can players transfer Coins?

I did not have a direct Coin-transfer option in the tested Campaign. Team spending
must be planned around the balance held by the player who will make the payment.

### Can healing be given to another player after collection?

The collected healing could not be handed back in the tested interaction. Assign
the healing before anyone picks it up.

### Who pays for a revive?

The surviving player pays the displayed cost. Do not rely on Coins held by the
downed player.

### Does the revive always cost 350 Coins?

The displayed cost changed during the tested Campaign: one revive showed 250 Coins
and a later revive showed 350. Read the current prompt every time and shorten the
route after the first down.

### What happens when a player is not revived?

The downed player could spectate after the revive opportunity ended in the tested
session. The surviving player then had to continue without the second active hero.

### Should both players use the same build?

No. Complementary roles are easier to manage. One player should provide frontal
control and single-target pressure while the other handles structures, rescues,
ranged threats, or fast crowd clearing.
