<h2 id="use-lifepod-zero-point">
  Use the Lifepod as your zero point
</h2>

In the opening hours, the Lifepod is your best anchor because it is safe, familiar, and tied to the first resource loops.

Do not describe routes as “some cave I found.” Describe them from the Lifepod:

- “below the Lifepod” for Copper and Acidic Raion Pouches,
- “toward the coral dome” for Quartz,
- “toward the large Welcome Center structure” for the first unlock hub,
- “toward the green-flora cave” for early Silver.

This is why early routes in the <Link href="/subnautica-2/early-resources">Early Resources guide</Link> are written from the Lifepod. Once you unlock Beacons, you can replace memory with markers.

<h2 id="signals-tab">
  Use the Signals tab before you assume you are lost
</h2>

If an objective marker disappears, open your Signals tab before panicking. Subnautica 2 lets you toggle signals on and off, which means a marker may be hidden rather than gone.

Use Signals for:

- Lifepod and objective recovery,
- Anita and early black box routes,
- returning to important quest locations,
- turning off clutter once you have your own Beacons.

Signals are not a full map. They are route anchors. The mistake is following a signal all the way in, collecting resources, then leaving without learning what the surrounding area looks like.

<h2 id="beacon-unlock">
  How Beacons fit into your route
</h2>

Beacons are the point where Subnautica 2 navigation starts to feel controlled instead of improvised. Once you unlock and craft Beacons, stop trusting memory for places you plan to revisit.

Use a Beacon for any spot that matches one of these:

- you died there once,
- it has a resource you need more than once,
- it is an entrance to a deep or confusing route,
- it is your first base or forward outpost,
- it is a danger zone you want to avoid.

In the current Early Access route, Beacons are unlocked through early blueprint progression by scanning Beacon fragments and useful route objects as you explore. If Beacon crafting has not appeared yet, keep scanning early wreck pieces, habitat objects, and progression fragments while using Signals, Lifepod-based directions, and obvious landmarks.

Once the blueprint is unlocked, a Beacon costs **1x Titanium + 1x Copper**, and you place it with the **Habitat Builder**. After placing it, use **Set Label** to rename it with a functional route name like `BASE-01`, `SILVER-CAVE`, or `DEEP-ENTRY`. If you placed it badly, dismantle it with the Habitat Builder to recover the materials, then move it somewhere cleaner.

<h2 id="shallow-water">
  Shallow water navigation
</h2>

In shallow water, keep your routes short and visual. The surface is close enough that you can reset often, so your main risk is not death; it is losing track of which coral dome, cave, or structure mattered.

Use shallow routes for:

- Titanium,
- Copper,
- Quartz,
- Water Slugs,
- Lucifer Rotsack,
- Fibrous Pulp,
- early scans.

Your shallow-water habit should be:

1. leave the Lifepod with one target,
2. grab only what supports that target,
3. look back at the Lifepod or landmark before entering a cave,
4. return before your inventory is full of random items.

<h2 id="cave-navigation">
  Cave navigation
</h2>

Caves are where most early players get lost because the exit stops looking obvious once you turn twice.

Before entering a cave, check three things:

- **Exit:** What does the entrance look like from the inside?
- **Air:** Do you see bubbles, a ceiling pocket, or a nearby surface route?
- **Reason:** What are you here for: Copper, Acidic Raion Pouch, Quartz, Silver, or a scan?

If the answer is “I am just checking,” stay near the entrance. If the answer is “I need one more deposit,” leave earlier than your panic brain wants to.

<h2 id="deep-water">
  Deep water navigation
</h2>

Deep routes need a different mindset. In shallow water, you can make small mistakes and surface. In deeper routes, a wrong turn can turn into a death loop.

Before entering deep water, set a rule:

**Mark the entry before you descend.**

That marker can be a Signal, a Beacon, a base, a mini outpost, or a very obvious structure. What matters is that you have a return anchor before the environment stops looking familiar.

For deep routes, bring:

- enough oxygen capacity for the route,
- Air Bladder or another emergency return tool,
- a clear exit marker,
- inventory space,
- a reason to be there.

Do not explore deep water because it looks interesting. Explore it because you know what you are trying to unlock.

<h2 id="mini-outpost">
  How mini outposts help navigation
</h2>

A mini outpost is a tiny functional base used as a route anchor. It does not need to be pretty.

A useful early outpost can be as simple as:

```text
Small room or basic base piece
+ hatch
+ power source
+ storage
+ Beacon
```

Use one near routes you repeat often: a deep entry, a resource cave, or a forward exploration point. The goal is not decoration. The goal is to create a safe oxygen, storage, and orientation reset.

If you are still working through the base chain, read the <Link href="/subnautica-2/fully-functional-fabricator">Fully Functional Fabricator guide</Link> first, then use the <Link href="/subnautica-2/first-base-setup">First Base Setup guide</Link> once that page is live before overbuilding outposts.

<h2 id="pathfinder">
  What Pathfinder is and when to use it
</h2>

Pathfinder is an early active Biomod option from the Welcome Center Biolab. When used, it leaves a temporary visible trail behind you, which can help you escape confusing caves.

You do not need Pathfinder for every route. Dash, another early active Biomod option from the same Welcome Center Biolab, is often better for speed and predator escape. But Pathfinder is valuable if you are the type of player who gets turned around inside caves.

Use Pathfinder when:

- you are entering a winding cave,
- you are scanning inside a structure,
- you are teaching a co-op teammate a route,
- you know the exit will not be obvious on the way back.

The tradeoff is simple: Pathfinder helps you remember the route; Dash helps you move faster. Choose based on the problem you actually have.

<h2 id="lost-decision-tree">
  If you are completely lost, do this
</h2>

When you are lost, stop making new decisions. Recover the route first.

<GuideTable
  caption="What to do when you are lost in Subnautica 2."
  columns={lostDecisionColumns}
  rows={lostDecisionRows}
/>

The general recovery rule is:

```text
Stop
→ find oxygen
→ surface or open Signals
→ return to a known marker
→ place a Beacon when safe
→ only then continue exploring
```

<h2 id="coop-navigation">
  Co-op navigation rules
</h2>

Co-op makes navigation easier if the team uses shared names. It becomes worse if everyone describes places differently.

Use these rules:

- pick one person to name Beacons,
- agree on short names before placing them,
- mark danger zones for the whole team,
- do not split four ways before the base and resource network is stable,
- call out routes by function: “Silver cave,” “O2 stop,” “deep entry,” not “over here.”

When a teammate gets lost, do not send everyone searching. Have the lost player surface if safe, re-enable Signals, or return to the nearest named Beacon. One player can guide them while the rest keep the base or resource loop stable.

<h2 id="death-recovery">
  Death recovery without repeating the same mistake
</h2>

If you died while lost, treat the recovery run as a navigation problem, not a revenge trip.

Use the death marker, bring oxygen backup, recover the container, and leave. The full death recovery steps are covered in <Link href="/subnautica-2/what-to-do-first">What to Do First</Link>, but the navigation lesson is simple:

**Do not return to the same route with the same plan.**

Add one improvement before trying again: Fins, Air Bladder, Standard Air Tank, a Beacon, a co-op guide, or a clearer exit rule.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Does Subnautica 2 have a map?

Do not rely on a traditional map in the opening route. The practical navigation system is made from Signals, landmarks, Beacons, bases, outposts, and your own route memory.

### How do I unlock Beacons in Subnautica 2?

Scan Beacon fragments and early route objects until the Beacon blueprint appears. Once unlocked, a Beacon costs **1x Titanium + 1x Copper**, must be placed with the **Habitat Builder**, can be renamed with **Set Label**, and can be dismantled with the Habitat Builder to recover materials.

### What should I name my first Beacon?

Name your first base Beacon `BASE-01`. After that, use functional names like `SILVER-CAVE`, `O2-STOP`, `DEEP-ENTRY`, or `DANGER-PREDATOR`.

### Is Pathfinder better than Dash?

Pathfinder is better when you get lost in caves because it can leave a visible return trail. Dash is better for speed and escaping danger. Use Pathfinder for confusing routes and Dash for general exploration.

### How do I stop getting lost in co-op?

Use shared Beacon names, call out routes by function, and avoid everyone using different names for the same place. If someone gets lost, guide them back to a named marker instead of sending the whole team searching.
