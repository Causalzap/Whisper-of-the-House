<h2 id="how-baseball-cards-work">
  How Baseball Cards work in MOUSE: P.I. For Hire
</h2>

Baseball Cards matter because they do not just fill a checklist.

They also connect to:

- the **Baseball Cards minigame**
- **Prize Tokens**
- the **Spike-D’s Token Prize D-Spenser**
- the **X1 D-Mousifier** reward path
- **100% completion routing**

That means Baseball Cards matter for both **collectible completion** and **reward progression**.

### Total cards and where you play

The current launch-version public total is **41 Baseball Cards**.

You play the minigame at:

- **Little & Big Bar** in the main hub
- **Roadhouse bars** later in the run

If you want more cards through the hub shop, the correct store name is the **Soup to Nuts store**.

### Match format

A Baseball Cards match is much shorter and more tactical than real baseball.

Each side always gets:

- **5 batting turns**
- then **5 pitching turns**

The biggest beginner mistake is assuming the game follows real baseball’s three-out structure.

It does not.

**Outs do not end the half.** Even if you make several outs quickly, the other side still gets all **5 turns** before the match flips.

### Your hand and your turn

At the start of play, you draw up to **5 cards**.

Each turn, you can use:

- **1 player card**
- **1 Tactics card**

After the turn resolves, your hand refills back to 5.

If your hand is weak, you can **discard and redraw up to 2 times while batting**, and then later **up to 2 more times while pitching**.

### The two stat checks that matter

The Baseball Cards system really works through **two checks**, not one.

When you are **batting**, the first thing that matters is whether your batter’s **swing power** beats the pitcher’s **pitch focus**.

- if **swing power** is higher, you get a hit
- if **pitch focus** is higher, you are out
- if the values are equal, the result is decided randomly
- if swing power beats pitch focus by **3 or more**, the hit becomes a **home run**

That is only the first half of the play.

The second part is where the real running value happens:

- the batter’s **speed**
- versus the pitcher’s **fielding**

That is why speed and fielding should not be treated as minor side stats.

### Why base-running matters

Once there are runners on base, the game stops being just “did I get a hit?”

Now it becomes “how much does this hit actually convert?”

That is where **speed** and **fielding** become critical.

A fast runner already on base can keep advancing on later turns.

A slow runner can bottleneck the whole sequence and reduce the value of a successful hit behind them.

And on defense, **fielding matters most when runners are already on base**, because that is the stat that helps stop them from continuing to turn pressure into runs.

The cleanest way to explain the whole system is:

**swing power wins the hit, but speed and fielding decide how much that hit is really worth.**

### Tactics Cards matter more than most players expect

Tactics Cards are not filler.

They are how you turn a borderline hand into:

- a safe hit
- a stronger pitching stop
- better speed conversion
- safer fielding
- a more reliable home run push

Because you only use one Tactics Card on a turn, the best habit is not to burn them automatically.

Use them when they help you:

- secure a hit you would otherwise lose
- force a stop on defense
- or create a big scoring swing

### Ties and late-game deck management

Tied values are **not safe**.

If the important values match exactly, the result is random.

Later, once your deck gets large enough, the game also introduces a **Banning Phase**.

When that happens, the usual best move is to cut weaker player cards first so your future draws stay cleaner.

<h2 id="how-to-get-more-baseball-cards">
  How to get more Baseball Cards
</h2>

There are four practical sources for cards, and they should not be blended together.

### 1. Story pickups

Some Baseball Cards are normal mission collectibles.

You find them in side rooms, secret paths, or off the main route.

### 2. Shop cards

Some cards are sold in the **Soup to Nuts store** and are not really buy-back cards at all.

They are simply part of the shop inventory.

The currently known shop-sold set is:

- **8 total cards**
- **4 cost 500 Coins**
- **4 cost 1000 Coins**

### 3. Recovery after a mission miss

Many standard mission cards can appear later in the shop if you missed them.

That is the forgiving part of the system.

### 4. Side Job reward cards

This is the dangerous bucket.

Some cards come from Side Jobs, not from normal pickups or shop inventory.

Those should **not** be treated as safe cleanup.

Known confirmed examples include:

- **Oh Yes... They Float** → **Jeremiah Curd Baseball Card**
- **Friends in Deep Places** → **Jacob “Cheesehead” McQueen Baseball Card**

<h2 id="how-to-win-more-baseball-card-matches">
  How to win more Baseball Card matches
</h2>

The best way to improve is not to grind immediately.

It is to understand the short match structure and stop wasting turns.

### Play later, not earlier

The minigame gets much easier once you have:

- more cards
- better cards
- better Tactics cards
- more coins to absorb losses

That is why a lot of players do better when they leave the real grind for later in the run, but still **before** the Jack Squat cutoff.

### Use redraws aggressively on weak hands

Do not keep bad hands out of habit.

If your opening five cards do not support the current half well, use your discard/redraw option.

You get:

- **up to 2 redraws while batting**
- **up to 2 redraws while pitching**

### Win the hit first, then maximize the run

When batting, the safer priority is usually:

1. make sure the batter actually beats **pitch focus**
2. then care about **speed** and extra-base value

That is why a pure speed bonus is not always the best first choice if you are still not sure the hit lands at all.

### Defense matters more once runners are on base

When pitching, the goal is not just to beat the batter’s swing power.

You also care about whether your **fielding** can stop runners from turning contact into more progress.

### Cut weak player cards in the Banning Phase

Once your deck grows large enough to trigger bans, remove low-tier player cards first.

That improves average draw quality far more than cutting strong Tactics cards.

### Think in match math, not just single turns

Because each side only gets **5 batters**, Baseball Cards is a short-match math game.

If you are already ahead, do not save your best defensive turn for later unless you truly need to.

Often the better question is:

**Do I win faster by slamming the door right now?**

### Coin planning matters

The clean public-facing rule is:

- **the first match costs 50 Coins**
- **each successful game raises the price to play again**

That means **1500 Coins** should only be treated as the **perfect-run minimum** for a long win grind, not as the safe real budget.

<GuideCallout title="Best win-rate summary">

  </div>
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="prize-tokens-and-the-prize-d-spenser">
  Prize Tokens and the Prize D-Spenser
</h2>

Prize Tokens are the reward currency tied to the Baseball Cards system.

### Main token source

The main source is still the minigame itself.

Each win gives you **1 Prize Token**.

Losses and draws give you nothing.

There is **no known token cap**, so you do not need to worry about overfarming them.

Matches are also short, usually around **3 to 4 minutes each**, which makes the grind much more manageable than it first sounds.

### Other token sources

Baseball wins are not the only source.

Prize Tokens can also come from:

- **some Side Jobs**
- **a known Wallop Bay NPC interaction**

One currently useful example is:

- in **Wallop Bay**, speaking through the window behind the frog group and listening through the full dialogue rewards **66 Coins and 1 Prize Token**

### The machine and the reward

The full machine name is the **Spike-D’s Token Prize D-Spenser**, and it is in **Little & Big Bar**.

You need:

- **20 Prize Tokens**

The reward is:

- **X1 D-Mousifier**

This is the **final bonus weapon** tied to the Baseball Cards system, and it is one of the main reasons the token grind matters in a 100% file.

<h2 id="baseball-cards-for-100-percent-completion">
  Baseball Cards for 100% completion
</h2>

If you care about a clean 100% route, the card system should be treated as part of your normal run planning, not as a postgame afterthought.

### Best route order

The safest order is:

1. **pick up mission cards as you go**
2. **buy strong shop cards when affordable**
3. **check shops for recoverable standard misses**
4. **finish Side Jobs that reward cards before Jack Squat**
5. **build a better deck**
6. **grind wins and tokens before the hub cutoff**
7. **claim the Prize D-Spenser reward while Little & Big is still comfortably accessible**

### The actual risk point

The clean warning here is:

- **starting Jack Squat is the P.I. Office point of no return**
- **the final two missions are too late to assume relaxed shop cleanup still exists**

That is why Baseball Cards are not a safe “deal with it later” system.

Normal mission-card cleanup is flexible.

Side Job reward cards and late-game cleanup are not.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### How many Baseball Cards are in MOUSE: P.I. For Hire?

The current public launch-version total is **41 Baseball Cards**.

### Where do you play Baseball Cards?

At **Little & Big Bar** in the main hub and at **Roadhouse bars**.

### How does the minigame work?

A match is split into **5 batting rounds** and **5 pitching rounds**. You draw to **five cards**, then use **1 player card** plus **1 tactics card** each turn.

### Do outs end the half early?

No. Each side still gets all **5 turns**, even if multiple batters are retired early.

### Can you redraw weak hands?

Yes. You can **discard and redraw up to 2 times while batting**, and later **up to 2 times while pitching**.

### What happens on a tie?

A tied matchup is decided randomly.

### Can you buy missed Baseball Cards later?

Many **standard mission cards** can be bought later through shop recovery, but **Side Job reward cards should not be treated as safe cleanup**.

### Is the Baseball Cards cost always 50 Coins?

No. The first match costs **50 Coins**, but each successful game raises the price to play again.

### How many coins should you budget for a long grind?

Treat **1500 Coins** as the clean perfect-run minimum, not the safe real budget.

### Do Prize Tokens have a cap?

Current public guidance says **no**.

### Do Prize Tokens only come from winning matches?

No. Wins are the main source, but some **Side Jobs** and at least one known **Wallop Bay interaction** also award Prize Tokens.

### What do you get from the Prize D-Spenser?

After collecting **20 Prize Tokens**, the **Spike-D’s Token Prize D-Spenser** gives you the **X1 D-Mousifier**.

### What is the biggest Baseball Card mistake for 100% completion?

Assuming all cards are equally recoverable. **Standard missed mission cards** are much safer than **Side Job reward cards**.
