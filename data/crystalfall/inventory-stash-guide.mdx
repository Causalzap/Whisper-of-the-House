<h2 id="why-inventory-pressure-happens-so-fast-in-crystalfall">
  Why inventory pressure happens so fast in Crystalfall
</h2>

One of the most immediate friction points in Crystalfall is that your bag stops feeling roomy almost immediately.

That happens because the game starts throwing several item categories at you at once:

- equippable gear
- gems / cores
- crafting materials
- extra skills
- backup weapons
- utility items and scroll-like clutter

That combination is what creates the real pressure.

It is not just that “loot drops a lot.”

It is that too many different things all feel vaguely important at the same time.

<GuideCallout title="The real issue">
Crystalfall inventory management is not hard because sorting is complicated.

It is hard because the game makes too many early items feel potentially relevant.
</GuideCallout>

---

<h2 id="inventory-vs-stash-what-each-one-should-do">
  Inventory vs stash: what each one should do
</h2>

A lot of players get slowed down because they treat inventory and stash like two versions of the same space.

They should not.

### What inventory is for

Your inventory should mainly hold:

- what you are actively wearing
- one or two plausible near-term upgrades
- items you intend to sell in the next town stop
- a very small number of test pieces you actually plan to use soon

### What stash is for

Your stash should absorb the things that are probably useful, but not useful **right now**:

- gems / cores
- crafting materials
- extra skills you may test later
- backup weapons for a future swap
- build pieces that are clearly not for your current route

That split matters because once your bag starts doing stash work, pacing collapses.

---

<h2 id="what-to-stash-first">
  What to stash first
</h2>

This is the cleanest beginner rule in the whole guide.

### First stash priority

If you are filling up again and again, the first things that should leave your inventory are:

- **gems / cores**
- **crafting materials**
- **future-use skills**
- **backup weapons**
- **specialized pieces you are not wearing yet**

That is the pattern the playthrough keeps returning to, and it is also the easiest way to reduce friction fast.

### Why these go first

Because these are exactly the categories most likely to be:

- useful later
- not useful right this second
- too annoying to keep comparing in the field

Once you accept that, stash discipline gets much easier.

<h2 id="what-to-keep-in-your-inventory">
  What to keep in your inventory
</h2>

This is where most players overdo it.

### Keep only near-term value

A good beginner inventory should mostly contain things that pass one of these tests:

1. **I am wearing this**
2. **I am about to equip this**
3. **I am about to sell this**
4. **I am very likely to test this before my next town stop**

If an item does not fit one of those, it probably does not belong in your bag.

### Why this matters

A lot of players keep too many things because they are scared of making the wrong call.

That instinct is understandable.

But in practice, carrying too many “maybe later” items hurts you more than making the occasional minor sell mistake.

<h2 id="what-to-sell-what-to-stash-and-what-to-stop-babysitting">
  What to sell, what to stash, and what to stop babysitting
</h2>

Here is the easiest way to think about it.

### Sell

Sell or dump obvious wrong-build gear:

- weapons for a route you are not actually playing
- bad rares with no realistic use case
- filler drops that are only “technically maybe usable”
- duplicate low-value gear you have no serious plan for

### Stash

Stash the pieces that are not current-use, but are still plausibly valuable:

- fire crests or other future-use build pieces
- gems / fusion materials
- socketables
- crafting items
- backup skills you may pivot into later

### Stop babysitting

This is the hardest category, but also the most important.

Do not waste field time endlessly comparing items that are:

- clearly not upgrades
- clearly not for your current build
- only interesting in the abstract
- not worth interrupting map flow for

That time tax adds up fast.

<h2 id="why-gems-and-cores-become-the-biggest-clutter-source">
  Why gems and cores become the biggest clutter source
</h2>

If you had to name one category that turns stash management into a real chore, it is probably this one.

### Why they pile up so fast

Gems / cores are awkward because they hit three bad conditions at once:

- they seem too useful to throw away
- you do not always want to process them immediately
- they stack into their own mini-management game

The playthrough hits this point directly: there are so many gem-like items that the player repeatedly postpones dealing with them because they would rather keep playing.

That is a very important signal.

It means the problem is not only storage.

It is also **timing**.

### The right beginner response

Do not force full gem optimization every time you go to town.

The better early pattern is:

- dump them in stash
- keep moving
- process them when you have a real reason to engage with fusion or sockets

That is much healthier than letting gem clutter hijack every play session.

<h2 id="pickup-flow-manual-loot-and-why-pace-matters">
  Pickup flow, manual loot, and why pace matters
</h2>

One underrated inventory problem is that Crystalfall’s loot flow does not always feel smooth enough.

The run explicitly complains about not being able to pick up loot for too long in some moments, and later clearly values pickup-radius-style quality of life when discussing passives.

That matters because inventory pressure is not just about what sits in your bag.

It is also about:

- how often you have to stop
- how much you have to hover / compare
- how much the game interrupts your movement rhythm

### What we can safely say right now

Launch-period public discussion is already full of complaints about manual pickup friction and missing inventory-quality-of-life expectations.

So even without overclaiming a final “no loot filter exists” statement, the safer beginner takeaway is:

- do not expect the loot flow to fully manage itself
- do not assume inventory friction is only your fault
- treat pickup-radius or similar quality-of-life gains as genuinely valuable if your build path can access them

### Why this changes how you should play

If pickup flow is already clunky, then inventory discipline matters even more.

The more disciplined you are about what actually deserves attention, the less the game’s rougher loot flow can slow you down.

<GuideCallout title="Pace rule">
A lot of early inventory pain is really pacing pain.

If your whole run keeps stopping for loot you do not even want, that is an inventory problem.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="storage-space-feels-small-and-that-changes-how-you-should-manage-loot">
  Storage space feels small, and that changes how you should manage loot
</h2>

Here the launch-period numbers actually help.

Public demo coverage reported that players start with:

- **54 inventory slots**
- **126 stash slots**

Those are not microscopic numbers in the abstract.

The reason they feel small is that Crystalfall asks you to care about too many item categories too early.

That is why “storage feels small” is not just a vague complaint.

It is a real consequence of limited starting space colliding with:

- gear
- gems / cores
- crafting materials
- extra skills
- future-use build pieces

### What this changes

When space is tight, your standard should become:

- keep what is current
- stash what is future-relevant
- sell what is vague
- stop preserving every maybe-item forever

If you do not adopt that mindset, your stash will feel “full” long before your build actually needs most of what is in it.

<h2 id="how-to-get-more-space">
  How to get more space
</h2>

This is where inventory friction turns into a monetization question.

### What public launch-period information suggests

Public launch-week discussion shows two things:

1. players reported shop listings for extra storage
2. the community specifically called out **inventory** expansion as a paid pain point

The most cited launch-week shop numbers were:

- **1 Inventory Tab = 150 credits**
- **1 Stash Tab = 30 credits**
- **4 Stash Tabs = 100 credits**

That does **not** translate into one universal dollar price everywhere, because local currency value depends on which credit bundle a player is using.

But the practical conclusion is still clear:

**extra space appears tied to the shop, and inventory-tab pricing is part of why this topic gets so much backlash.**

### What I would tell a beginner right now

If you are only trying to get through the early game cleanly:

- first fix your stash habits
- then decide whether the storage pressure is still unbearable
- do not confuse bad sorting discipline with a space problem
- but also do not pretend the space conversation is purely imaginary

If you want the broader monetization argument, that is better handled in a dedicated **Is Crystalfall Pay to Win?** article rather than inside this guide.

<GuideCallout title="Expansion rule">
Before you spend money on space, make sure your current bag is not full of things you should have sold or stashed ten minutes ago.

After that, it is fair to treat storage pressure as a real design and monetization issue.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="salvage-all-is-not-your-friend-if-you-are-not-careful">
  Salvage all is not your friend if you are not careful
</h2>

This is one of the easiest mistakes to make.

At one point in the run, the player realizes they no longer have Fireball because a previous salvage action also got rid of skills they did not mean to lose.

That matters because skills can sit in inventory like ordinary items.

So if you are not paying attention to what category something belongs to, a bulk salvage habit can absolutely sweep up something build-critical.

### What beginners should do instead

Before bulk salvage, make sure you are not accidentally scooping up:

- a skill you still want
- a future pivot piece
- a gem / core you meant to keep
- a backup weapon you were saving for a swap

### The real rule

Bulk actions are only safe when your filtering discipline is already good.

If you are still learning the item ecosystem, bulk salvage is where carelessness gets punished.

<GuideCallout title="Salvage rule">
If you are even slightly unsure whether something matters, do not trust a bulk salvage habit yet.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="best-inventory-rules-for-the-first-few-hours">
  Best inventory rules for the first few hours
</h2>

If you want the cleanest real-world rules, use these:

1. **stash gems, cores, and crafting materials early**
2. **do not carry more than a tiny number of “maybe later” items**
3. **sell obvious wrong-build gear instead of dragging it around**
4. **do not pause every screen for endless comparison**
5. **treat inventory pressure as a pace problem, not just a sorting problem**
6. **be careful with bulk salvage**
7. **accept that some useful things belong in stash, not in your bag**
8. **when in doubt, keep only what helps now or soon**

These rules are not fancy.

They are just the rules that stop Crystalfall from becoming a bag-management simulator.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What should I stash first in Crystalfall?

Start with **gems / cores, crafting materials, extra skills, and backup weapons**. Those are the items most likely to matter later without helping you immediately.

### What should I keep in my inventory?

Keep what you are currently using, what you are about to equip, what you are about to sell, and only a very small number of realistic near-term test items.

### Why does my inventory fill so fast?

Because Crystalfall throws several kinds of “possibly important” loot at you very early: gear, gems, materials, skills, and backup items all start competing for the same space.

### How much starting space do you get?

Launch-period public coverage reported **54 inventory slots** and **126 stash slots** at the start.

### How do you get more inventory space?

Public launch-week discussion indicates that extra storage is tied to shop tabs, with players reporting **1 Inventory Tab at 150 credits** and **stash tabs sold separately**.

### Is stash management actually important early?

Yes. In Crystalfall, stash discipline is an early progression habit, not just late-game neatness.

### Are gems really the biggest clutter source?

Usually yes. They pile up quickly, they seem too useful to throw away, and many players do not want to stop and process them constantly.

### Does storage space feel small in Crystalfall?

Yes. Not only because the starting space is limited, but because the game asks you to care about too many different item categories too early.

### Is salvage all safe?

Only if you are already very disciplined. Skills can sit in inventory like ordinary items, and the run shows that bulk salvage can delete things you actually meant to keep.

### Is inventory pressure really a gameplay problem?

Yes. In Crystalfall, inventory clutter breaks momentum so often that it becomes a real pacing issue, not just a neatness issue.
