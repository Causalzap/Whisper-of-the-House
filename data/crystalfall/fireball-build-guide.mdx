<h2 id="why-this-build-is-worth-playing">
  Why this build is worth playing
</h2>

The reason this build works is simple:

**Fireball solves early progression better than most alternatives.**

You do not need a flashy theorycraft build in the first few hours.

You need a build that:

- clears normal packs quickly
- stays at range
- does not collapse when Aether gets tight
- scales cleanly once the rest of the game opens up

That is exactly why Fireball is the best beginner build right now.

It is not the only possible skill.

It is just the one that asks the least from the player while still rewarding good scaling decisions.

<h2 id="best-class-for-a-fireball-build">
  Best class for a Fireball build
</h2>

The cleanest answer is still:

**Technomancer.**

This is not because other classes are hard-locked out of the build.

It is because Technomancer reaches the right support layers faster:

- Aether sustain
- cast comfort
- projectile-friendly scaling
- elemental support
- smoother early ranged play

That makes the build easier to stabilize and easier to trust.

### Can Knight or Rogue use Fireball?

**Yes, in theory.**

Crystalfall’s class system is flexible enough that class choice does not completely hard-lock you out of every other style. Community class guidance explicitly frames the game as allowing deeper crossover than the starting archetypes suggest. But Fireball itself still has a **Rod** requirement, and Technomancer reaches caster / Aether support more naturally than Knight or Rogue do.

That means Knight and Rogue can use the skill, but they are not the cleanest way to start this build.

<h2 id="first-priority-fix-aether-before-damage">
  First priority: fix Aether before damage
</h2>

This is the most important rule in the whole build.

If your Fireball setup feels weak, clunky, or fake-expensive, the answer is usually **not** “add more damage first.”

It is:

**fix Aether first.**

### Why this matters so much

A Fireball build only feels good when you can actually cast it at a comfortable pace.

If your resource economy is bad, the whole build collapses into frustration:

- your skill feels too expensive
- your real DPS drops, even if the tooltip looks better
- your clear rhythm breaks
- you start blaming the skill instead of the sustain

That is why early gains in this build come from:

- Aether regeneration
- max Aether
- Aether on hit / on kill
- practical gear that stabilizes the skill loop

Once those are working, **then** damage starts mattering much more.

### When to refund into damage

One of the smartest parts of the play pattern behind this build is:

- take sustain first
- make the build playable
- refund excess sustain later
- move those points into projectile / fire damage once the build no longer struggles

That is the beginner-safe way to scale Fireball.

<h2 id="where-fireball-upgrades-actually-come-from">
  Where Fireball upgrades actually come from
</h2>

This is the biggest mechanical clarification most players need.

### Fireball power does not come from one place

Skill progression for Fireball comes from four layers:

1. **Upgrade Skill Rarity** in the Tinkerbox
2. **Shuffle Skill Row**
3. spend **Skill Keys** to level selected nodes
4. insert a **Crest** into the top node

That means upgrades like split, pierce, extra projectile, burn, and other premium effects are not something you should think of as “just passive tree bonuses.”

They belong to the broader **skill progression system**.

### Practical meaning for beginners

If your Fireball does not have split yet, that does **not** mean the build is dead.

It means:

- keep progressing the skill
- keep upgrading rarity
- keep checking row options
- keep spending Skill Keys intelligently
- keep using the skill’s progression systems instead of assuming one drop decides everything

That is the real mindset shift.

<h2 id="best-fireball-upgrades-split-pierce-extra-projectile-and-burn">
  Best Fireball upgrades: split, pierce, extra projectile, and burn
</h2>

This is where the build starts getting exciting.

### Why these upgrades matter more than small raw numbers

For this build, some of the highest-value upgrades are not boring white-text damage bumps.

They are upgrades that change **how the projectile behaves**.

That includes:

- **split**
- **pierce**
- **extra projectile**
- **burn**

The reason is simple:

these upgrades can multiply real output in a way small generic stat gains often do not.

### Split is a premium upgrade

Split is one of the biggest reasons Fireball starts feeling outrageous in the right setup.

It is not just a crowd-clear upgrade.

In practice, it can dramatically increase how much useful work each cast does, which is why it often feels better than just chasing a slightly bigger damage number.

### Pierce is also excellent

Pierce matters because it improves how much work each cast does through real packs.

It fits the same logic as split:

one cast doing more meaningful projectile work.

### Extra projectile is never a dead stat here

If your build is already committed to Fireball, extra projectile is exactly the kind of modifier that usually feels good immediately.

### Burn is the best “simple” support layer

Burn is not as flashy as split, but it is still one of the cleanest upgrades because it adds pressure without forcing you into a weird new play pattern.

<h2 id="what-to-do-if-you-do-not-have-split-yet">
  What to do if you do not have split yet
</h2>

Do **not** panic and do **not** brick the build trying to force one modifier too early.

If split has not shown up yet, your fallback priority is:

1. **keep Fireball playable**
2. **keep Aether comfortable**
3. **take pierce / extra projectile / burn if they appear**
4. **upgrade skill rarity and revisit the node rows later**
5. **use Skill Keys and Crests intelligently instead of rerolling blindly**

That gives you a build that still works well while you keep improving it.

This matters because launch-period Crystalfall is not a game where you should assume one missing upgrade means the whole build is failed.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What is the best class for a Fireball build in Crystalfall?

**Technomancer** is the cleanest and safest class for a beginner Fireball build.

### Can Knight or Rogue use Fireball?

Yes, in theory. Crystalfall’s class framework is flexible enough to allow crossover builds, but Fireball still requires a **Rod**, and Technomancer reaches the right caster / Aether support much more naturally.

### Is Fireball or Firebolt better?

For a beginner page, the answer is simple: either works as the core of the build, and both follow the same starter logic. The important part is that this fire projectile route feels much smoother than weaker early alternatives.

### Is Fireball better than Ice Shards?

For real early progression, **yes**. Ice Shards can feel better on some bosses, but Fireball is better for most of the actual game you are playing minute to minute.

### What should I prioritize first in a Fireball build?

**Aether sustain first, damage second.** Make the skill playable before you try to make it flashy.

### How should I path my early passive tree?

The cleanest early Technomancer-side route is: **Timekeeper -> Caster -> Meditation Technique -> nearby Aether connectors -> Aeronautics -> Elementalist**.

### Where do split, pierce, and other Fireball upgrades come from?

They come from the skill progression systems around the skill itself: **Tinkerbox rarity upgrades, row shuffles, Skill Keys, and Crests**.

### Is split really that good on Fireball?

Yes. It is one of the highest-value upgrade types because it changes how much useful work each cast does instead of only adding a small generic damage bump.

### Can I play this build without split?

Yes. If split has not shown up yet, keep the build stable with Aether sustain, then lean on pierce, extra projectile, burn, and later skill progression.

### What weapon should I use for Fireball?

A **Rod**. For this build, you should treat Fireball like a caster projectile skill, not a generic ranged weapon build.

### Is this an endgame Fireball build?

No. This is a **starter and early progression build** built for beginners and Early Access launch-period play.
