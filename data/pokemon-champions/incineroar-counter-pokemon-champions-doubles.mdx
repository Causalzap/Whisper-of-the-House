<h2 id="why-incineroar-is-so-hard-to-deal-with">
  Why Incineroar Is So Hard to Deal With
</h2>

A lot of players lose to Incineroar because they focus too much on what it does by itself.

That is not the real problem.

The real problem is that Incineroar is one of the best **pace-control** Pokémon in the format.

It does three things extremely well:

1. **it weakens physical attackers with Intimidate**
2. **it steals first-turn tempo with Fake Out**
3. **it resets the board with Parting Shot**

That means a lot of games against Incineroar do not feel bad because it KOs you directly.

They feel bad because it makes your good turns weaker and your bad turns harder to recover from.

<h2 id="what-incineroar-is-actually-trying-to-do">
  What Incineroar Is Actually Trying to Do
</h2>

If you want to counter Incineroar well, you need to understand what the opponent is usually trying to get out of it.

### Fake Out to steal the first turn

Incineroar's first job is often to buy one clean turn for its partner.

That partner might want to:

- set Trick Room
- use Tailwind
- fire off a strong attack
- reposition safely
- force you into Protect or a passive line

A lot of players misplay this by focusing only on the Incineroar slot.

But in many games, the partner is the real threat.

### Intimidate to make physical pressure awkward

Intimidate matters because it changes damage math before the turn even starts.

That is especially annoying if your team leans on physical attackers to win neutral midgames.

If your best lines involve repeated physical chip, Incineroar often makes the game feel much slower and much worse than it should.

### Parting Shot to reset the board

Parting Shot is one of the biggest reasons Incineroar feels oppressive.

It lets Incineroar lower your damage output and then bring in a better board state behind it.

That means even if you positioned well for one turn, the opponent can often turn that advantage into a worse board on the next one.

<GuideCallout title="What usually matters most">
In many games, the real issue is not **Fake Out** or **Intimidate** on their own.

It is the sequence of:

**switch in → Fake Out or pressure → Parting Shot → bring in the real win condition**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="the-best-practical-counters-to-incineroar">
  The Best Practical Counters to Incineroar
</h2>

There is no single perfect answer, but some Pokémon and tool types are clearly better than others.

### Milotic

Milotic is one of the cleanest answers because it punishes Intimidate instead of merely surviving it.

That matters because Incineroar usually expects its switch-ins to create free value.

Milotic can flip that interaction and make the Incineroar player think twice before cycling.

### Kingambit

Kingambit is one of the best practical answers because it turns Intimidate into an actual risk.

It also does not mind slower board states as much as many other attackers, which makes it much harder for Incineroar to grind value out of the game.

### Dragapult

Dragapult is useful because it applies pressure without caring as much about the usual slow, bulky pivot patterns.

It also gives teams a way to keep tempo instead of letting Incineroar dictate every reset turn.

### Sneasler

Sneasler is a strong answer because it pressures Incineroar offensively and can make the first turn much less comfortable.

It is one of the best examples of a Pokémon that does not let Incineroar assume it owns the opening exchange.

### Taunt and anti-pivot utility

This is the most under-discussed answer type.

**Taunt** matters because it can shut off **Parting Shot** and other utility turns, which means Incineroar sometimes has to stay on the field and play fair instead of taking a free reset.

That is a much better result than merely surviving the turn.

<GuideCallout title="Most practical answer type">
The best Incineroar answers usually do one of two things:

- **punish Intimidate**
- **stop Incineroar from cycling safely**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="how-to-handle-fake-out">
  How to Handle Fake Out
</h2>

A lot of anti-Incineroar play starts here.

The biggest mistake players make is acting like Fake Out is the problem by itself.

It is not.

The real question is:

**What does Fake Out allow the partner to do?**

### Read the partner slot first

Before the turn starts, ask:

- is the partner trying to set Trick Room?
- is the partner trying to use Tailwind?
- is the partner trying to fire off one huge attack?
- is the partner trying to pivot or protect a more valuable line?

Once you answer that, the Fake Out turn becomes much easier to understand.

### Do not burn your defensive options with no follow-up

Protecting the Fake Out target can be fine.

But if that means the partner still gets a perfect turn, you did not actually stop anything important.

### Make Fake Out awkward, not just survivable

The best anti-Fake Out play is often not:

**"I lived the turn."**

It is:

**"That Fake Out did not create a winning board for you."**

<h2 id="how-to-stop-parting-shot-from-winning-the-turn">
  How to Stop Parting Shot From Winning the Turn
</h2>

This is where Incineroar becomes much more annoying than a normal bulky support.

Parting Shot is dangerous because it turns a stable position into a worse one very quickly.

### Why Parting Shot is so strong

It does two things at once:

- lowers your pressure
- brings in a better partner

That means the turn does not really end when Incineroar leaves the field.

The turn ends when the new Pokémon comes in on favorable terms.

### Do not only react to the Incineroar that is in front of you

The better habit is to ask:

**"If this Incineroar leaves right now, what comes in, and am I ready for it?"**

That question is more important than squeezing a little extra damage into Incineroar just because it is on the field.

### Taunt is one of the cleanest ways to punish lazy pivoting

If your team has Taunt, it can do more than stop obvious support Pokémon.

It can also make Incineroar's most comfortable utility turn much worse by turning off Parting Shot.

That forces the Incineroar player into a more honest board state.

### The goal is not always to stop Parting Shot completely

Sometimes you can stop it.

Sometimes you cannot.

But you can still reduce its value by making sure the switch-in is uncomfortable.

That might mean:

- keeping damage on the right slot
- not overcommitting your best attacker too early
- preserving your best answer to the likely incoming Pokémon
- positioning for the next turn instead of this one only

<GuideCallout title="Parting Shot rule">
A good anti-Incineroar line is not just one that pressures Incineroar.

It is one that also makes the **incoming switch** awkward.
</GuideCallout>

  Why Bulky Incineroar Cores Are So Annoying

Incineroar is at its most frustrating when it is not alone.

It becomes much more oppressive next to bulky partners that reward repeated switching and low-tempo games.

One of the clearest examples is **Incineroar + Sinistcha**.

This kind of core is annoying because it gives value on both sides of the reset:

- Incineroar gives Intimidate, Fake Out pressure, and Parting Shot
- the partner makes the next switch safer, bulkier, or harder to punish
- your good attacks often get split across two turns instead of one clean breakthrough

That is why a lot of players feel like they are not losing to Incineroar itself.

They are losing to the fact that Incineroar keeps buying one more annoying turn for the whole team.

### Common partner examples

These are the kinds of pairings that usually make Incineroar much more annoying:

- **Incineroar + Farigiraf**
- **Incineroar + Sneasler**
- **Incineroar + Sinistcha**
- **Incineroar + slow bulky attackers**
- **Incineroar + Trick Room support**

In all of these cases, the important question is not just whether Incineroar is on the field.

It is whether Incineroar is creating the exact turn its partner wants.

<h2 id="when-you-should-attack-incineroar-and-when-you-should-not">
  When You Should Attack Incineroar — and When You Should Not
</h2>

This is the biggest decision point in the matchup.

### Attack Incineroar when it is the board-control piece

You usually want to pressure Incineroar directly when:

- it is about to get a free Parting Shot turn
- it is the only thing stopping your main attacker from taking over
- your current line punishes it harder than its partner
- it has already done its job once and you do not want it cycling back in later

### Ignore Incineroar when the partner is the real problem

You usually do **not** want to tunnel Incineroar when:

- the partner next to it is the actual win condition
- Fake Out only matters because of what the partner does after it
- you can force a better trade by attacking the more valuable slot
- Incineroar is not threatening meaningful damage this turn

A classic mistake is trying to "solve" Incineroar while its partner gets exactly the turn it needed.

That is how you lose to a support Pokémon without realizing the support Pokémon was not the real target in the first place.

<h2 id="common-mistakes-players-make-into-incineroar">
  Common Mistakes Players Make Into Incineroar
</h2>

### Tunneling the Incineroar slot

Sometimes Incineroar is worth hitting immediately.

Sometimes it is not even the second-most-important Pokémon on the field.

### Overvaluing physical pressure into obvious Intimidate lines

If your whole game plan is repeated physical chip, Incineroar will usually get more value than you want to give it.

### Treating Fake Out like a standalone problem

Fake Out is only as scary as the turn it creates for the partner next to Incineroar.

### Ignoring the next switch

A lot of bad anti-Incineroar turns happen because players focus only on the current board and not the board that comes after Parting Shot.

### Letting bulky support cores drag the game exactly where they want it

If your team only functions in straightforward damage races, Incineroar will usually feel much stronger than it actually is.

<h2 id="what-kinds-of-teams-handle-incineroar-best">
  What Kinds of Teams Handle Incineroar Best
</h2>

You do not need a full anti-Incineroar team to improve this matchup.

In practice, teams usually feel much better into Incineroar if they have at least two of these three tools:

- one **Intimidate-punishing** Pokémon
- one reliable **special attacker** or stat-drop-resistant attacker
- one way to make **Fake Out or Parting Shot** uncomfortable

That is why Pokémon like **Milotic**, **Kingambit**, and **Sneasler** are so useful here.

They do not just beat Incineroar on paper.

They make Incineroar's most comfortable turns less automatic.

  Final Thoughts

Incineroar is one of the most annoying Pokémon in **Pokemon Champions Doubles**, but it is not unbeatable.

If you can read the partner slot first, respect Fake Out for what it enables, punish Parting Shot when possible, and bring at least one real anti-Intimidate or anti-pivot tool, the matchup becomes much easier to manage.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What are the best counters to Incineroar in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

The best practical answers are usually **Milotic, Kingambit, Dragapult, and Sneasler**, plus teams that can punish Intimidate or stop Parting Shot from generating free tempo.

### Does Taunt stop Parting Shot in Pokemon Champions?

Yes. Because Parting Shot is a status move, Taunt can prevent Incineroar from using it.

### Is Milotic the best Incineroar counter in Pokemon Champions?

Milotic is one of the cleanest answers because it punishes Intimidate directly, but it is not the only good answer. Kingambit, Dragapult, and Sneasler are also strong depending on your team structure.

### Should I always target Incineroar first?

No. A lot of the time, the partner next to Incineroar is the real threat, and Fake Out only matters because of what that partner gets to do.

### Why is Incineroar so much better in doubles than singles?

Incineroar is vastly superior in doubles primarily because its core mechanics scale significantly better when facing two opponents:

* **AoE Intimidate:** This is the most crucial factor. Unlike in singles, Intimidate lowers the Attack stat of **both** opposing Pokémon simultaneously, instantly crippling the opponent's physical offensive pressure the moment it hits the field.
* **Fake Out Utility:** It can flinch one opponent to completely stop their turn, allowing Incineroar's partner to safely attack or set up crucial board states (like Trick Room or Tailwind).
* **Pivoting with Parting Shot:** This allows Incineroar to lower an opponent's offensive stats and safely switch out. This means it can continually cycle in and out of the battle to trigger Intimidate and Fake Out all over again.

Through this cycle of debuffing, flinching, and pivoting, Incineroar provides unmatched pace control for its team.
