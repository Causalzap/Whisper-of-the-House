A lot of doubles games are not decided by raw damage alone. They are decided by **turn order**, **first-turn tempo**, and whether one side gets the exact board state it needs before the other side can respond. That is why Tailwind, Trick Room, Fake Out, and speed-based pressure matter so much.

This guide explains the core speed-control framework first, then links you out to the matchup pages that go deeper.

<h2 id="what-speed-control-means-in-pokemon-champions-doubles">
  What Speed Control Means in Pokemon Champions Doubles
</h2>

In singles, speed often decides which Pokémon attacks first.

In doubles, speed control does much more than that.

It decides:

- who gets the first clean attack
- who forces the first Protect
- who converts support into damage
- who gets to reposition safely
- who snowballs the board on turn one and turn two

That is why speed control is not just about “being fast.”

It is about **making your team act first at the exact moment that matters most**.

<GuideCallout title="Best way to think about speed control">
Speed control is not only about stats.

It is about **who gets the first useful turn**.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="the-four-main-ways-teams-control-speed">
  The Four Main Ways Teams Control Speed
</h2>

There are a lot of ways to influence turn order, but most real games come back to four core ideas.

### 1. Tailwind

Tailwind is the most straightforward form of team-wide speed control.

Once it goes up, one side gets four turns of doubled Speed. That means many games turn into a race to see whether the Tailwind user can immediately convert that advantage into knockouts, positioning, or tempo.

### 2. Trick Room

Trick Room does not just make one side faster.

It flips the whole logic of the game.

This is why Tailwind teams and Trick Room teams often ask completely different questions even when both are technically “speed control” structures.

### 3. Fake Out-supported setup turns

A lot of speed control is not about the move itself.

It is about how the move gets protected.

That is why **Fake Out** matters so much. A speed-control move that goes up freely is one thing. A speed-control move that goes up behind Fake Out is much more dangerous.

### 4. Speed control after the setup turn

A lot of players think the matchup ends once Tailwind or Trick Room goes up.

That is not really true.

Many games are actually decided by what happens **immediately after** the setup turn.

That is why speed control is always tied to board position, target priority, and partner pressure.

<h2 id="tailwind-vs-trick-room">
  Tailwind vs Trick Room
</h2>

This is the most important speed-control divide in the format.

### Tailwind asks:
- can I create one fast winning window?
- can I win the first boosted turn?
- can I stop the opponent from stabilizing before the four turns matter less?

### Trick Room asks:
- can I reverse the speed order completely?
- can I make slow attackers become the real win condition?
- can I keep control even after the first room turn?

This is why the two matchups feel so different.

Tailwind usually pressures you by making the opponent act first.  
Trick Room pressures you by making your normal speed assumptions wrong.

<h2 id="why-fake-out-matters-so-much">
  Why Fake Out Matters So Much
</h2>

Fake Out is one of the most important moves in doubles because it often decides whether speed control goes up safely.

That matters in all of these cases:

- Whimsicott + Fake Out support
- Trick Room setter + Fake Out support
- fast offense trying to steal the first turn
- pivot teams trying to create one clean support window

This is also why so many “speed control” matchups are not really only about Tailwind or Trick Room.

They are also about whether the support turn was protected.

<h2 id="the-first-turn-question-that-decides-many-games">
  The First-Turn Question That Decides Many Games
</h2>

A lot of doubles games are quietly decided by one question:

**Should you hit the setter, or should you hit the partner?**

This is true against:

- Tailwind leads
- Trick Room leads
- Fake Out leads
- aggressive pressure leads like Sneasler
- tempo control leads like Incineroar

The reason this question matters so much is simple:

sometimes the support Pokémon is the real problem, but sometimes the support Pokémon only matters because of what the partner gets to do next.

That is why good doubles play is rarely just:

**stop the move**

It is more often:

**stop the board state that the move is trying to create**

<h2 id="common-speed-control-combinations-you-should-recognize">
  Common Speed-Control Combinations You Should Recognize
</h2>

You do not need to memorize every team, but you do need to recognize the most common structures.

### Tailwind offense
Usually built around:
- a fast Tailwind setter
- one or two immediate attackers
- strong first-turn pressure

### Trick Room balance
Usually built around:
- a setter
- a slow attacker or two
- enough support to survive the setup turn

### Fake Out plus setup
Usually built around:
- Fake Out pressure
- one support move that matters
- one slot that converts the free turn into damage or control

### Fast pressure plus item-based speed swings
Usually built around:
- naturally fast attackers
- Fake Out or status pressure
- item interactions that change the next turn

<h2 id="how-to-build-a-team-that-does-not-auto-lose-speed-wars">
  How to Build a Team That Does Not Auto-Lose Speed Wars
</h2>

You do not need every speed-control tool on the same team.

But most teams feel much better if they have at least two of these three things:

### 1. A way to stop setup turns
Examples:
- Taunt
- Fake Out pressure
- direct pressure into fragile setters

### 2. A way to survive the first boosted turn
Examples:
- better Protect timing
- bulkier balance structures
- smarter target priority
- not overcommitting your key slot too early

### 3. A way to answer the speed war directly
Examples:
- your own Tailwind
- Trick Room
- strong positioning that makes the speed advantage less meaningful

<GuideCallout title="Best team-building question">
Do not only ask:

**"Does my team have speed control?"**

Ask:

**"What does my team do when the other side controls speed first?"**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="where-most-players-actually-lose-speed-control-games">
  Where Most Players Actually Lose Speed-Control Games
</h2>

A lot of players think they lose because the opponent’s speed control is too strong.

That is sometimes true.

But more often, they lose because of one of these mistakes:

### Treating the move as the only threat
Tailwind or Trick Room often matters because of the partner next to it.

### Respecting the wrong slot
Sometimes the setter is the problem. Sometimes the partner is.

### Failing to plan for the next turn
A lot of games are decided by the **first boosted turn**, not by the setup turn itself.

### Using the same answer into every team
Not all Tailwind teams are the same.  
Not all Trick Room teams are the same.  
Not all Fake Out leads ask the same question.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What is speed control in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

Speed control is any tool or structure that changes turn order or makes one side more likely to act first at the important moment.

### What are the main types of speed control in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

The most important ones are **Tailwind**, **Trick Room**, **Fake Out-supported setup turns**, and **post-setup positioning**.

### Is Tailwind better than Trick Room in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

Not always. Tailwind and Trick Room ask different questions, and the better one depends on your team structure and the matchup.

### Why does Fake Out matter so much in speed-control games?

Because it often determines whether a support move like Tailwind or Trick Room goes up safely.

### What is the most important turn in speed-control matchups?

Very often, it is **the first turn after speed control goes up**, not the setup turn itself.
