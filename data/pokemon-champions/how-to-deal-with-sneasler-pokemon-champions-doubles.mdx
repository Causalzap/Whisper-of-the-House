# How to Deal With Sneasler in Pokemon Champions Doubles

If you are looking for **how to deal with Sneasler in Pokemon Champions Doubles**, the short answer is this: the best counters are usually **Ghost-types that blank Fake Out and Close Combat**, plus teams that apply enough pressure to stop **Unburden** from activating on good terms.

In practice, that also means understanding **Sneasler weakness in Pokemon Champions**, recognizing when it is holding **White Herb** or **Focus Sash**, and knowing whether you should attack Sneasler itself or the partner next to it.

<GuideCallout title="Who this guide is for">
This guide is for players who keep losing early-game control to **Sneasler** in **Pokemon Champions Doubles** and want a practical way to manage the matchup better.

It is **not** a Sneasler build guide.  
It is a **counterplay guide** focused on why Sneasler feels so hard to deal with, how Unburden changes the matchup, when you should attack it, and which mistakes usually make it look stronger than it really is.

If Incineroar is also giving you trouble, see our <Link href="/pokemon-champions/how-to-counter-incineroar-pokemon-champions-doubles/">How to Counter Incineroar in Pokemon Champions Doubles</Link> guide.
</GuideCallout>

<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
**The best counters for Sneasler in Pokemon Champions Doubles are Ghost-types that blank Fake Out and Close Combat, strong Psychic- or Ground-based pressure that punishes its real weaknesses, and teams that do not give it a free Unburden trigger.**

In practice, that usually means:

- **Ghost-types** are great because they shut off two of Sneasler’s easiest buttons
- **Psychic, Flying, and Ground pressure** matter because those are Sneasler’s main weaknesses
- **good first-turn positioning** matters because many players lose by activating Unburden without actually solving the board
- **utility awareness** matters because not every Sneasler is the exact same standard four-move attacker
</GuideQuickAnswer>

<h2 id="why-sneasler-is-so-hard-to-deal-with">
  Why Sneasler Is So Hard to Deal With
</h2>

Sneasler is not just another fast attacker.

It is one of the most frustrating early-format threats because it compresses several annoying jobs into one slot:

1. **it is naturally fast**
2. **it hits hard enough to demand respect**
3. **it can steal the first turn with Fake Out**
4. **it can snowball into an even faster threat once Unburden activates**
5. **it adds status pressure through Dire Claw**

That is why Sneasler does not feel like a normal glass cannon matchup.

It often threatens both **tempo** and **damage** at the same time.

<h2 id="what-makes-sneasler-so-annoying-in-practice">
  What Makes Sneasler So Annoying in Practice
</h2>

A lot of players know Sneasler is strong, but they still misread **why** it is so hard to play into.

### Dire Claw creates pressure even when the damage is manageable

Dire Claw is one of the most annoying attacks in the format because it adds status pressure to a fast physical attacker.

That means even turns where Sneasler is not taking a knockout can still become bad turns for you.

### Fake Out gives it real first-turn value

This is a huge reason Sneasler is not just a cleaner or revenge killer.

Fake Out lets it support a partner, steal one turn of control, or create a board state where the next turn becomes much harder to answer.

### Close Combat means you cannot ignore it

Even if Sneasler is annoying because of utility, it is still a real damage threat.

That matters because some support-style threats can be ignored for a turn. Sneasler usually cannot.

<h2 id="how-unburden-changes-the-matchup">
  How Unburden Changes the Matchup
</h2>

This is the single most important thing to understand.

A lot of players do not actually lose to base Sneasler.

They lose to **activated Sneasler**.

### White Herb Sneasler

White Herb versions are dangerous because they can turn **Close Combat** into both a damage button and a speed trigger.

That means the Sneasler player is not only trying to hit you.  
They may also be trying to enter the speed tier that wins the next turn.

### Focus Sash Sneasler

Focus Sash versions are dangerous because they punish players who think they solved the problem by almost KOing it.

If Sneasler survives at 1 HP, activates Unburden, and still has pressure next turn, your “good” turn may have actually made the game harder.

### White Herb and Focus Sash do not create the same problem

White Herb Sneasler usually punishes you for letting it choose the trigger itself.

Focus Sash Sneasler usually punishes you for giving it just enough damage without actually removing it.

That means your answer to Sneasler should always include one extra question:

**What happens after the item is gone?**

### The important question

Before you choose your line, ask:

- has Unburden already activated?
- what item is the Sneasler most likely holding?
- does my attack solve the position, or does it just give Sneasler the trigger it wanted?

<GuideCallout title="Unburden rule">
The matchup changes the moment Sneasler loses its item.

Do not treat pre-Unburden and post-Unburden Sneasler like the same threat.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="what-actually-checks-sneasler-best">
  What Actually Checks Sneasler Best
</h2>

There is no single perfect answer, but some checks are much cleaner than others.

### Gengar and other Ghost-types

Ghost-types are some of the best practical answers because they make two of Sneasler’s most common buttons much worse:

- **Close Combat** does not hit them
- **Fake Out** does not work into them

That is a huge deal because it removes a lot of Sneasler’s easiest tempo lines.

### Strong Psychic pressure

Psychic is Sneasler’s biggest weakness on paper, so clean Psychic pressure is one of the fastest ways to stop it from snowballing.

This is especially useful before Unburden activates, when Sneasler is still much easier to pin down.

### Fast pressure that punishes bad item timing

Some teams do not need a perfect hard counter. They just need enough speed and board control to stop Sneasler from getting value out of White Herb or Focus Sash turns.

### Ground and Flying pressure

Ground and Flying attacks also matter because they punish real weaknesses instead of trying to brute-force through neutral trades.

That can be especially useful when you are dealing with non-standard Sneasler lines.

<GuideCallout title="Most practical answer type">
The best Sneasler answers usually do one of two things:

- **remove its best first-turn buttons**
- **punish it before or after Unburden activates**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="when-to-hit-sneasler-and-when-to-hit-its-partner">
  When to Hit Sneasler — and When to Hit Its Partner
</h2>

This is where most players throw the matchup.

### Turn 1: should you hit Sneasler or its partner?

If Sneasler is only dangerous because Fake Out is creating a free turn for the partner, the partner is often the real target.

If Sneasler is one clean trigger away from activating Unburden and taking over speed control, then Sneasler itself becomes the priority.

The key is not to attack Sneasler automatically. The key is to identify which slot actually wins the board on that turn.

### Hit Sneasler when it is the real damage or speed problem

You usually want to pressure Sneasler directly when:

- it is one clean trigger away from taking over the speed war
- your line can actually remove or cripple it instead of barely missing
- its partner is not the main threat this turn
- you can deny both damage and Unburden value at once

### Hit the partner when Sneasler is only scary because of the turn it creates

You should not automatically target Sneasler first if:

- Fake Out only matters because the partner needs one clean turn
- the partner is the actual setup or KO threat
- Sneasler is currently trying to support a stronger mode rather than sweep by itself
- attacking Sneasler would only trigger its item and make the next turn worse

That means the matchup is often not really:

**you vs Sneasler**

It is:

**you vs the board Sneasler is trying to create**

But that does not mean every Sneasler is the same.

Some Sneasler variants use tools like **Taunt**, **U-turn**, **Fake Out**, and **Protect**, and those sets can punish players who only prepare for Close Combat and Dire Claw.

If you assume every Sneasler is just trying to click damage, you may:

- protect too early
- respect the wrong slot
- miss a utility line
- get caught by Taunt or pivoting
- underestimate how much support value Sneasler still offers without taking a KO

If the standard line does not explain the board, start asking what utility variant would.

<h2 id="common-mistakes-players-make-into-sneasler">
  Common Mistakes Players Make Into Sneasler
</h2>

### Accidentally triggering Unburden without solving the board

This is probably the most common mistake.

A lot of players think they made progress because Sneasler lost its item.

But if that item loss turns on its best speed mode and the board is still bad for you, then you did not really solve anything.

### Treating Fake Out like the whole problem

Fake Out is only scary because of the turn it creates.

If you do not ask what Sneasler’s partner is getting out of that turn, you will keep defending the wrong thing.

### Forgetting that Dire Claw can still make neutral turns bad

Some players only respect Sneasler when it is threatening a KO.

That is not enough.

Dire Claw means Sneasler can still generate ugly board states even when the damage itself looks manageable.

### Playing as if all Sneasler sets are identical

Standard sets are common, but utility variants exist.

If you never consider Taunt, U-turn, Coaching, or niche support lines, you will eventually lose to something that looked “wrong” only because you were too rigid.

### Overcommitting to physical answers

Sneasler is not just an Attack stat problem.

It is also a speed-control and status-pressure problem, which means some purely physical answers still end up awkward once the matchup gets messy.

Do not ask:

**"How do I survive Sneasler?"**

<h2 id="what-kinds-of-teams-handle-sneasler-best">
  What Kinds of Teams Handle Sneasler Best
</h2>

You do not need a hard Sneasler counter on every team.

In practice, teams usually feel much better into Sneasler if they have at least two of these three things:

- one way to stop or punish **early Fake Out turns**
- one reliable answer to **post-Unburden speed pressure**
- one Pokémon or board state that makes **Close Combat and Fake Out** much less comfortable

That is why Ghost-types, strong special pressure, and teams with cleaner first-turn plans usually feel much better into Sneasler than passive balance lines that just hope to trade evenly.

<h2 id="final-thoughts">
  Final Thoughts
</h2>

Sneasler is one of the strongest pressure pieces in **Pokemon Champions Doubles**, but it is not unbeatable.

The players who usually lose to it are not the ones without a hard counter.

They are the ones who let Sneasler choose the item trigger, the speed state, and the partner turn that matters most.

If you can track whether Unburden is live, respect Fake Out for what it enables, and avoid turning a manageable board into an activated Sneasler board, the matchup becomes much easier.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Why is Sneasler so good in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

Sneasler is strong because it combines high Speed, high Attack, Fake Out, Dire Claw status pressure, and item-based Unburden activation in one slot.

### What is the best way to deal with Sneasler in Pokemon Champions?

The best way is to avoid giving it a free Unburden trigger, respect what Fake Out is enabling, and pressure either Sneasler or its partner based on which one actually matters that turn.

### Does Unburden make Sneasler much harder to check?

Yes. Once its item is consumed or lost, Sneasler becomes a much harder speed-control problem. That is why pre-Unburden and post-Unburden Sneasler should not be treated like the same threat.

### What are Sneasler’s main weaknesses in Pokemon Champions?

Sneasler is weak to **Psychic**, **Flying**, and **Ground**, with **Psychic** being the biggest type weakness.

### Are Ghost-types good against Sneasler?

Yes. Ghost-types are some of the cleanest answers because they blank Close Combat and stop Fake Out from working into them.

### Should I always target Sneasler first?

No. In many games, the more important question is what Sneasler’s partner gets if Sneasler is allowed to take the first turn cleanly.
