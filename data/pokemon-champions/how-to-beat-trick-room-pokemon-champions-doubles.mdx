<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
**The best way to beat Trick Room in Pokemon Champions Doubles is to identify the setter early, pressure the partner slot instead of tunneling the setter every game, use Fake Out and Taunt to deny clean setup, and plan for the turn order after Trick Room expires.**

If you only want the short version:

- **Do not assume** every Trick Room team is pure hard Trick Room.
- **Identify** core setters like Farigiraf, Oranguru, and Sinistcha early in the match.
- **Utilize** Fake Out as one of your best first-turn disruption tools.
- **Apply** Taunt as a clean, direct answer to deny the setup if your team supports it.
- **Leverage** Imprison to block their Trick Room or stop reset lines completely.
- **Pressure** slow sweepers like Kingambit to avoid being punished for passive play.
- **Prepare** for secondary setup attempts, as surviving the first Trick Room is often not enough.

<h2 id="the-trick-room-setters-you-should-expect">
  The Trick Room setters you should expect
</h2>

If you want to beat Trick Room consistently, the first step is knowing **which setter actually changes the matchup**.

### Farigiraf

Farigiraf is one of the cleanest Trick Room setters because **Armor Tail** changes how early turns work.

That means you often cannot play into it the same way you would into a more fragile setter.

Farigiraf also fits best next to partners that want a safe first turn, whether that means setup, pivoting, or immediate damage pressure.

### Oranguru

Oranguru is annoying because Trick Room is only part of the problem.

The real pressure often comes from **Instruct**, which turns the partner slot into the actual win condition.

A good example is **Oranguru + Torkoal**. If Trick Room goes up cleanly, Oranguru does not need to deal damage itself. It only needs one clean support turn to keep Torkoal's pressure rolling.

That is why the right question into Oranguru is often not **"Can I KO Oranguru right now?"** but **"Which slot actually wins the game for them if Oranguru gets one clean support turn?"**

### Sinistcha

Sinistcha is one of the most awkward setters to play into because its typing gives it a very different kind of protection.

It naturally avoids some of the pressure that other setters hate, and it also brings extra utility instead of being a pure one-button setter.

That makes it much better on mixed-mode teams than players often expect.

<GuideCallout title="Preview rule">
When you see a Trick Room setter in preview, ask:

**Is this the real problem, or is the real problem the partner that gets rewarded if Trick Room goes up?**
</GuideCallout>

### Mega Golurk

Mega Golurk is one of the clearest examples of a Trick Room sweeper that can end games very quickly.

It hits hard, it pressures Protect turns better than many players expect, and it can turn one clean Trick Room cycle into a huge advantage.

### Kingambit

Kingambit is a huge deal in this matchup.

It is slow enough to feel comfortable in Trick Room, bulky enough to survive awkward turns, and threatening enough that passive counterplay often gives it too much room.

It is one of the main reasons **"just stall out Trick Room"** is not always a complete answer.

### Sylveon

Sylveon gives Trick Room teams a more reliable special pressure option.

It does not need quite as much setup help as some other spread attackers, which makes it harder to dismiss as a niche pick.

### Torkoal

Torkoal is still a real threat, but it usually looks better when it is part of a more complete team shell instead of being thrown into Trick Room by itself.

That matters because if you overprepare for pure hard Trick Room, you can still get run over by a sun or mixed-mode finish.

<h2 id="how-to-stop-trick-room-from-going-up">
  How to stop Trick Room from going up
</h2>

Stopping Trick Room is not just about hitting the setter as hard as possible.

In practice, the better question is usually:

**What does my opponent need this turn in order to get a clean Trick Room?**

### 1. Use Fake Out with a plan

**Fake Out** is still one of the best first-turn tools against Trick Room.

But Fake Out only matters if it supports something bigger:

- damage into the setter
- damage into the partner
- your own setup
- or a stronger board position for turn two

### 2. Taunt is one of the cleanest direct counters

If your team can fit **Taunt**, it deserves to be part of almost any anti-Trick Room conversation.

Taunt is one of the standard ways to stop support-based Trick Room turns because it blocks status moves, including Trick Room itself, unless the target is protected by something like **Mental Herb**.

That makes Taunt especially useful when:

- the opposing setter is passive
- the partner slot is trying to buy time rather than deal immediate damage
- you can safely threaten the Taunt user’s other target with damage

The limit is obvious: **Taunt is not a guaranteed answer into every setter**.  
Some setters can shrug off the first denial line better than others, and some boards punish a low-pressure Taunt turn very hard. Smogon also highlights **Fake Out + Taunt** as one of the most common anti-Trick Room combinations, which is a good reminder that Taunt is strongest when it is part of a two-slot plan rather than a solo button.

### 3. Do not auto-double the setter

Sometimes the setter is the right target.

Sometimes the real problem is the partner next to it.

If Oranguru is next to a sweeper that can immediately cash in on **Instruct**, or if Farigiraf is next to a Pokémon that gets a free setup turn from **Armor Tail** support, tunneling the setter can still leave you in a losing position.

### 4. Respect Imprison and counter-Trick Room lines

Imprison matters because a Pokémon that knows both **Imprison** and **Trick Room** can stop the opposing side from using Trick Room while it remains on the field.

That means some games are not really about stopping the first Trick Room at all.  
They are about recognizing when the opponent is trying to bait you into an obvious Trick Room turn and punishing that setup instead.

<GuideCallout title="Most important first-turn mistake">
The biggest early-game error in this matchup is:

**trying to win the Trick Room turn instead of trying to win the position after the Trick Room turn.**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="how-to-stall-out-trick-room-turns-without-losing-anyway">
  How to stall out Trick Room turns without losing anyway
</h2>

Yes, stalling out Trick Room is still part of the answer.

No, it is not enough by itself.

A lot of players hear **"stall Trick Room turns"** and translate that into **"click Protect and pray."**

That works only if the opposing team does not punish passive turns well.

The better way to think about it is:

- **waste their best turns**
- **preserve your real answers**
- **do not give free setup to their slow attacker**
- **prepare the board for the turn Trick Room ends**

That last point matters a lot.

If you survive Trick Room but end up with terrible positioning, you did not really solve the problem.

### Do not just survive the room — prevent the second Trick Room

A lot of players think they have solved the matchup once they survive the first Trick Room cycle.

That is only half the job.

One of the most important anti-Trick Room habits is identifying **what has to happen for your opponent to set Trick Room a second time**. VGC Guide calls out this exact decision point when playing against Trick Room: first ask whether you can stop it from going up, then ask how bad it is if it does, and plan around the follow-up rather than only the first cycle. 

Before the first Trick Room ends, ask yourself:

- which setter is still alive?
- can it safely re-enter the field?
- does the partner next to it still force respect?
- if Trick Room goes up again, do I still have a winning endgame?

This is why board position matters so much.

If you burn four turns and then leave the opponent with a healthy setter plus a protected reset turn, you usually did not beat Trick Room at all — you only delayed it.

<GuideCallout title="Stall rule">
Stalling Trick Room is good.

**Stalling Trick Room while handing a sweeper a free setup turn is not.**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="common-mistakes-players-make-into-trick-room">
  Common mistakes players make into Trick Room
</h2>

### Treating every Trick Room team like the same team

Some teams are pure hard Trick Room.

Some are mixed.

Some are really just standard teams with a Trick Room option.

If you play every version of the archetype the same way, you will bleed free losses.

### Overcommitting to the setter

Not every game is won by KOing the setter immediately.

Sometimes the correct answer is to pressure the damage slot and force an awkward Trick Room turn instead.

### Forgetting the backup mode

A lot of current teams do not collapse just because Trick Room is denied once.

They still have another way to win the game.

### Playing too passively once Trick Room is active

Once the room goes up, a lot of players mentally check out and start clicking defensive moves with no real plan.

That is how you lose the game before Trick Room even expires.

### Ignoring reset turns

The first Trick Room cycle is rarely the whole matchup.

If your opponent still has a setter, a healthy support piece, and a clean re-entry path, you are often still in the danger zone.

<GuideCallout title="Best mindset for this matchup">
Do not ask:

**"How do I stop Trick Room every game?"**

Ask:

**"How do I make Trick Room bad for them, even when it goes up?"**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="what-kinds-of-teams-handle-trick-room-best">
  What kinds of teams handle Trick Room best?
</h2>

You do not need a full anti-Trick Room team to improve this matchup.

In practice, teams usually feel much better into Trick Room if they have at least two of these three tools:

- a reliable **Fake Out** user
- access to **Taunt**, **Imprison**, or another direct setup deterrent
- one Pokémon that still functions well into slower board states or can punish passive Trick Room turns

That is one reason Pokémon like **Incineroar** and **Kingambit** are so valuable in early doubles play: they do not only help in this matchup, but they also remain useful in normal games where Trick Room never appears.

<h2 id="final-thoughts">
  Final thoughts
</h2>

Trick Room in **Pokemon Champions Doubles** is worth respecting, but it is not a matchup you should panic over.

The players who usually lose this matchup are not the ones without anti-Trick Room tech.

They are the ones who misread **what actually matters on the board**.

If you can learn the setters, identify the real sweeper, use Fake Out and Taunt with a real plan, and avoid giving away free reset turns, this matchup becomes much easier to manage.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Is Trick Room good in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

Yes. It is not the easiest archetype in the format, but it is still a real ladder and tournament threat.

### What is the best way to beat Trick Room in Pokemon Champions?

The best answer is to combine **preview recognition**, **Fake Out pressure**, **Taunt**, **smart targeting**, and **good positioning after Trick Room goes up**.

### Does Taunt stop Trick Room in Pokemon Champions?

Usually, yes. Taunt prevents status moves like Trick Room, although it can fail as a full answer if the setter is protected by Mental Herb or if the board state still favors the Trick Room player. 

### What is Imprison Trick Room in Pokemon Champions?

Imprison Trick Room is a counterplay line where a Pokémon uses both **Imprison** and **Trick Room**. Once Imprison is active, opposing Pokémon cannot use Trick Room as long as the Imprison user remains on the field.

### How do you stop a second Trick Room setup?

The best way is to think about the reset before the first Trick Room ends. Track which setter is still alive, whether it can re-enter safely, and whether you have enough pressure to stop a second setup turn.

### Who are the main Trick Room setters in Pokemon Champions right now?

The main setters you should prepare for first are **Farigiraf, Oranguru, and Sinistcha**.

### Is Kingambit good against Trick Room?

Yes, but it is also strong on Trick Room teams themselves, which is part of why the matchup gets complicated.
