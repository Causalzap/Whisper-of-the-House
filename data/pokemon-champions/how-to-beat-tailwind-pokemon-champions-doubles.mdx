That is what makes this matchup tricky. Beating Tailwind is not only about stopping the move itself. It is about making sure Tailwind does **not** create the exact turn or board state your opponent wants.

<h2 id="why-tailwind-is-so-hard-to-deal-with">
  Why Tailwind Is So Hard to Deal With
</h2>

Tailwind is one of the strongest speed control tools in **Pokemon Champions Doubles** because once it goes up, it doubles the Speed of the user’s side for four turns.

That means a lot of games stop being normal damage races and become **speed races** instead.

The problem is not only that Tailwind makes fast teams faster.

The real problem is that Tailwind often gives a whole team one clean window to:

- attack first with both slots
- stack pressure behind Fake Out or Encore
- force Protects
- take a knockout before you can stabilize
- snowball into a winning board immediately

That is why a lot of players do not really lose to Tailwind itself.

They lose to the **first boosted turn** that Tailwind creates.

<GuideCallout title="Best way to frame the matchup">
Do not ask:

**"Can I stop Tailwind?"**

Ask:

**"If Tailwind goes up, what happens on the very next turn?"**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="recognize-the-most-common-tailwind-setters">
  Recognize the Most Common Tailwind Setters
</h2>

Not every Tailwind team plays the same way.

That matters because the right answer into **Whimsicott** is not always the same as the right answer into **Aerodactyl** or **Talonflame**.

### Whimsicott

Whimsicott is one of the most important Tailwind setters because **Prankster** gives priority to status moves.

That means Tailwind often goes up even in positions where normal fast setters would be pressured first.

It also means Whimsicott teams often care a lot about **Encore**, **Fake Out support**, and stealing the first clean turn of the game.

### Other setter types you should recognize

Not every relevant Tailwind setter is pure speed control.

#### Pelipper: Tailwind plus rain

Pelipper is dangerous because it does more than set Tailwind.

It also brings **Drizzle**, which means you are often fighting both **speed control** and **weather control** at the same time.

That changes the matchup a lot, because stopping Tailwind is not enough if the rain board is still great for the opponent.

#### Corviknight: bulky Tailwind

Corviknight is slower and bulkier than the other common setters.

That means it is less about explosive turn-one speed control and more about giving bulkier teams a repeatable way to stay ahead in the midgame.

It is a very different matchup from Whimsicott or Talonflame, and you should not treat them the same way.

<h2 id="the-best-ways-to-stop-tailwind-from-going-up">
  The Best Ways to Stop Tailwind From Going Up
</h2>

There is no single universal answer, but a few answers are clearly better than others.

### Taunt

Taunt is one of the cleanest direct answers to Tailwind because it stops the setter from using status moves.

This is especially strong into support-oriented setters that rely on getting Tailwind up more than they rely on dealing immediate damage.

### Fake Out pressure

Fake Out matters because many Tailwind leads want exactly one free turn.

If the setter needs help surviving or the partner needs one safe turn to convert Tailwind into pressure, Fake Out can disrupt the whole line.

### Direct pressure into the setter

Sometimes the cleanest answer is still just threatening enough damage that the setter cannot comfortably stay on the field.

This works best when the setter is fragile or when your line also remains good if the setter Protects.

### Mirror Tailwind

You do not always have to stop Tailwind.

Sometimes the better answer is to answer Tailwind with your own Tailwind and prevent the game from becoming a one-sided speed war.

### Trick Room

This is one of the most punishing answers because it flips the speed order entirely.

If the Tailwind team is too committed to fast-mode play, Trick Room can immediately turn the whole position against them.

<h2 id="if-tailwind-does-go-up-do-not-lose-the-next-turn">
  If Tailwind Does Go Up, Do Not Lose the Next Turn
</h2>

This is where a lot of games actually get thrown.

Players often treat Tailwind like an all-or-nothing move:

- either they stop it and feel safe
- or they fail to stop it and assume the turn is lost

That is too simple.

The better question is:

**What is the opponent trying to do with the first Tailwind turn?**

That might be:

- double attack for a knockout
- force a Protect and gain board control
- click Encore or another support move
- pivot into a stronger damage board
- turn one tempo into a two-turn snowball

If you can survive the first boosted turn without losing the board, Tailwind becomes much more manageable.

### Good post-Tailwind habits

- preserve your most important defensive slot
- identify which opponent actually matters this turn
- do not waste both slots trying to respect everything
- make the opponent prove they can convert Tailwind into damage
- think about turns two and three, not just turn one

<GuideCallout title="Do not panic rule">
A Tailwind that goes up is not automatically a losing board.

A Tailwind that goes up **and** gives your opponent the exact first turn they wanted is the real problem.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="turn-1-should-you-hit-the-setter-or-the-partner">
  Turn 1: Should You Hit the Setter or the Partner?
</h2>

This is the most important practical question in the matchup.

### Hit the setter when Tailwind itself is the real problem

You usually want to pressure the setter directly when:

- your line can actually stop Tailwind from going up
- the setter is fragile enough to punish immediately
- the partner is not the main threat on turn one
- the setter is the reason the whole line works

### Hit the partner when Tailwind only matters because of what comes next

You should not automatically tunnel the setter if:

- the partner is the real damage threat
- Tailwind is only useful because the partner gets one clean turn
- the partner is the slot that turns tempo into an immediate KO
- your line into the setter still loses to the partner’s action

### A common example: Whimsicott + Incineroar

This is a classic example of why the board matters more than the species name.

If the opening is **Whimsicott + Incineroar**, the real danger is often not Whimsicott by itself. The real danger is that **Fake Out + Prankster Tailwind** gives the partner side a perfect tempo lead.

In that kind of position, you should not always think:

**"I must hit Whimsicott."**

Sometimes the better question is:

**"If Tailwind goes up here, which slot actually wins the next turn?"**

That is often the more important target.

<h2 id="what-kinds-of-teams-handle-tailwind-best">
  What Kinds of Teams Handle Tailwind Best
</h2>

This matchup gets much easier once you stop thinking only in terms of single moves.

Some team structures are naturally better into Tailwind than others.

### Trick Room balance

This is one of the cleanest anti-Tailwind structures because it does not try to win the same speed war.

Instead, it flips the speed order entirely.

A team built around **Farigiraf + Ursaluna** is a good example of the kind of structure that can punish Tailwind teams for overcommitting to fast-mode play.

### Bulky balance with Fake Out or Taunt

These teams are good into Tailwind because they do not need to fully deny the move to stay playable.

They are built to survive the **first boosted turn** more comfortably, then stabilize.

Cores involving Pokémon like **Incineroar**, **Rotom-Wash**, and sometimes **Corviknight** fit this style well because they can slow the game back down after Tailwind goes up.

### Mirror Tailwind offense

Sometimes the cleanest answer is not to stop Tailwind at all.

It is to make sure the game does not become a one-sided speed war.

That is why hyperoffense or aggressive balance teams using **Whimsicott**, **Talonflame**, or **Aerodactyl** often handle opposing Tailwind much better than teams that have no direct speed answer of their own.

<GuideCallout title="Better team-building question">
Do not only ask:

**"Can my team stop Tailwind?"**

Ask:

**"If Tailwind goes up anyway, what does my team still do well?"**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="common-mistakes-players-make-into-tailwind">
  Common Mistakes Players Make Into Tailwind
</h2>

### Treating Tailwind as the only threat

Sometimes Tailwind is just the support move behind the real problem.

### Overcommitting to the setter

If the partner is the actual win condition, you can lose the turn even while “correctly” targeting the Tailwind user.

### Failing to plan for the first boosted turn

This is the biggest one.

Many players think the matchup ends the moment Tailwind goes up or fails.

In reality, the game is often decided by what happens immediately after.

### Using the same answer into every setter

Whimsicott, Aerodactyl, Talonflame, Pelipper, and Corviknight are not the same matchup.

### Forgetting that Trick Room flips the whole problem

Some players treat Tailwind like only another fast team problem.

But if your team already has a stable Trick Room line, that may be your cleanest answer.

Tailwind is one of the strongest speed control tools in **Pokemon Champions Doubles**, but it is not unbeatable.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What is the best way to beat Tailwind in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

The best answers are usually **Taunt, Fake Out pressure, mirror Tailwind, and Trick Room**, plus good positioning after Tailwind goes up.

### Does Trick Room counter Tailwind in Pokemon Champions?

Yes. Trick Room is one of the cleanest ways to punish Tailwind because it flips the speed order entirely.

### Can Fake Out stop Tailwind?

Sometimes. Fake Out is strongest when the Tailwind setter needs a free turn from its partner or when breaking that turn stops the whole line from working.

### Should I always try to stop Tailwind from going up?

No. Sometimes the better question is whether you can stop the **first boosted turn** from turning into a winning board. That matters more than the move by itself.

### What happens when both sides have Tailwind?

The game usually becomes a **speed war** instead of a one-sided control game. That is why mirror Tailwind is often a valid answer rather than a desperate fallback.

### Is Whimsicott the best Tailwind setter in Pokemon Champions?

Whimsicott is one of the most important setters because **Prankster** gives Tailwind priority, but it is not the only strong Tailwind option.

### Is Aerodactyl a good Tailwind setter in Pokemon Champions?

Yes. Aerodactyl is important because it is one of the fastest non-Prankster Tailwind setters and still applies real pressure.

### Is Talonflame a good Tailwind setter in Pokemon Champions?

Yes. Talonflame is strong because **Gale Wings** can turn Tailwind into a priority setup turn as long as Talonflame stays at full HP.

### Is Tailwind broken in Pokemon Champions Doubles?

Tailwind is one of the strongest speed control tools in the format, but it still has clear weaknesses. **Taunt, Fake Out pressure, mirror Tailwind, and Trick Room** can all keep it from snowballing as easily as it wants.

### What are Tailwind’s biggest weaknesses in Pokemon Champions?

Its biggest weaknesses are **Taunt, Fake Out pressure, mirror Tailwind, and Trick Room**, plus any line that stops the first boosted turn from converting into a real advantage.
