<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
For **Romestead dungeons and crypts**, do not treat every run as a generic loot trip. A **Twin Crypt** is usually a short combat check with spawner pressure. A trap or puzzle room tests patience and recovery planning. An artifact dungeon may be tied directly to a god objective or building unlock.

Before entering, make sure your town can survive without you, your backpack is not full of rare items, and you understand your current death setting. If a crypt spawner wakes up, stop looting and deal with the source of the pressure first.
</GuideQuickAnswer>

<h2 id="twin-crypt">
  Twin Crypt: how to approach the first crypt-style challenge
</h2>

Twin Crypt is a good example of why dungeons need their own strategy. The entrance looks manageable, but the danger ramps quickly once the spawner starts turning the room into a gauntlet.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/romestead/romestead-twin-crypt-entry.webp"
    alt="Romestead player approaching the Twin Crypt entrance before a crypt run."
    width={400}
    height={300}

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Treat Twin Crypt like a short combat challenge. Check your food, inventory, and exit route before committing.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Before stepping in, ask four questions: can your town handle you being gone, are you carrying anything too valuable, do you have enough food to recover between fights, and can you retreat if the room turns into a gauntlet?

If the answer is no, leave and return later. Crypts do not disappear because you waited.

<h2 id="crypt-spawner">
  Crypt Spawner: the real threat in gauntlet rooms
</h2>

The spawner is the part that turns a normal room into a problem. If you only fight the enemies and ignore the source, the room keeps draining your food, health, and patience.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

Spawner behavior can change during Early Access, so do not memorize an exact trigger distance. Play around the safer rule: **assume the spawner can activate before you stand directly on top of it**.

Do not use a spawner room as a place to test new weapons, sort inventory, or read menus. Clear the threat first, then loot.

<h2 id="death-recovery">
  Death recovery: fix the cause, not only the corpse run
</h2>

Death recovery is not just “run back and grab your stuff.” If you died because the room was still active, the same room can kill you again.

<GuideTable
  caption="Death recovery plans by cause of death."

The important idea is to identify why you died. A spawner death, a trap death, a no-food death, and an under-geared death are different problems. Recover your items first, then decide whether the dungeon deserves a second attempt.

<h2 id="coop">
  Co-op dungeon roles
</h2>

Romestead supports co-op, and dungeons become cleaner when players stop doing the same job.

<GuideTable
  caption="Simple co-op roles for Romestead dungeons."
  columns={coopColumns}
  rows={coopRows}
/>

In co-op, the biggest upgrade is discipline. Do not have every player loot at the same time while a spawner is active. One player should control the fight, one should focus the spawner, and one should secure important loot or retreat if the run turns bad.

If your town is still weak, leaving one player near town can be stronger than sending everyone into a dungeon.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What is the safest first dungeon type in Romestead?

A short crypt-style location like Twin Crypt is a reasonable early target, but only after your town has Food Storage, a worker, food, and a clear recovery plan.

### How do I deal with Crypt Spawners?

Treat the spawner as the main objective. Back up, control the first wave, then destroy or disable the pressure source before looting. Do not stand in the center while enemies keep appearing.

### Why did the Crypt Spawner trigger so early?

Spawner trigger distance can change during Early Access. Play safely by assuming the spawner may activate before you are directly beside it.

### What loot should I expect from dungeons?

Useful dungeon rewards can include weapons, armor, rings, bones, monster loot, artifacts, quest items, and cleared location progress. Always check whether unusual items connect to the altar.

### Are dungeons required for god progression?

Some god objectives can point you toward dungeon artifacts. The Moonstring is one example because it asks for a dungeon artifact and unlocks Well.

### What happens when I die in a dungeon?

It depends on your death setting. Normal death can drop carried inventory while keeping equipped items. Softcore is safer because it only drops money.

### Should I use Softcore?

Use Softcore if you want to learn dungeon layouts without painful recovery runs. Use normal death drops if you want more survival pressure.

### How do I recover dropped items from a trap room?

Return with full health, only the gear needed for recovery, and a plan for the exact hazard that killed you. Grab the items and leave instead of continuing the dungeon.

### Should I keep exploring after finding a good reward?

Only if your food, health, and inventory risk are still under control. If the reward is rare, an artifact, or a major gear upgrade, bank it first.

### How should co-op groups handle dungeons?

Split roles. One player controls enemies, one focuses spawners or ranged threats, one secures loot, and one may stay near town if the settlement is still unstable.
