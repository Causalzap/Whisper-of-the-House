<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
**Trade Stations in Shapez 2 are conversion buildings: they consume exact input shapes and output refined shapes that feed later tiers, rockets, and eventually the Grand Vortex Assembler.**

The Manufacture Mode chain is:

**Dimensional Waste → Polishing / first refined output → Trade Station tiers → Donation Station + Research Station research infrastructure → alternate recipes → Grand Vortex Assembler**

At base speed, useful starting numbers are:

- a normal belt is commonly **120 shapes/min**
- a Space Belt lane is commonly **480 shapes/min**
- the early Polishing Station example processes **60 shapes/min**
- the first starter scale-up target is **at least 8 Polishing Stations**
- some Manufacture Mode Trade Station blocks consume / produce about **half a Space Belt**
- later refined chains can require **multiple copies of earlier stations** to keep one final output block supplied

Always confirm the exact rate in your own save. Global speed upgrades, station tiers, and mode differences can change the values you should build around.
</GuideQuickAnswer>

  Trade Station system at a glance
</h2>

<GuideDataTable title="" rows={tradeStationSystemRows} />

A Trade Station is best understood as a permanent exchange block. You give it a precise input shape, it produces a refined output shape, and that refined output becomes a requirement for later production.

This is why a Trade Station factory should be designed more like a supply chain than a one-off milestone build.

<h2 id="key-ratios-and-throughput-numbers">
  Shapez 2 Trade Station ratios and throughput numbers
</h2>

The biggest weakness of most Trade Station explanations is that they say "watch your ratio" without giving any numbers.

The values below are a mix of base-speed constants, in-game tooltip examples, and tested-layout planning examples. Treat them as starting points, then verify the exact values in your own save by hovering the relevant station, belt, or Space Belt.

<GuideTable
  caption="Useful Shapez 2 Trade Station, Space Belt, and Manufacture Mode throughput numbers. Values are planning references, not universal rules."
  columns={knownRateColumns}
  rows={knownRateRows}

A good rule is:

- use **normal belt speed** to understand local machine modules
- use **Space Belt capacity** to understand station inputs and outputs
- use **station processing rate** to decide how many station copies you need
- use **refined output stability** to decide whether the next tier can run continuously

Trade Stations often connect directly to Space Belts, and their ratios are easiest to understand relative to saturated Space Belts rather than single normal belts.

<h2 id="manufacture-mode-progression-map">
  Manufacture Mode progression map
</h2>

Players often get stuck because they understand the station but not the full Manufacture Mode structure.

<GuideTable
  caption="Shapez 2 Manufacture Mode progression from dimensional waste to the Grand Vortex Assembler."
  columns={progressionColumns}

The short version:

1. You start with a special source instead of a normal Vortex loop.
2. You process that source into refined shapes.
3. Each Trade Station tier depends on earlier refined output.
4. Donation Stations and Research Stations form the research infrastructure.
5. Alternate recipes let you reroute or simplify Trade Station chains.
6. The Grand Vortex Assembler is the long-term destination.

That is why deleting an early station in Manufacture Mode can hurt later. It may still be feeding the chain several tiers downstream.

<h2 id="when-do-trade-stations-unlock">
  When do Trade Stations unlock?
</h2>

In **Manufacture Mode**, Trade Stations appear early and become the backbone of the campaign. Your factories are meant to stay alive, keep feeding station chains, and scale upward over time.

In **Classic Mode**, Trade Stations are an endgame addition. By the time they appear, the game expects you to understand belts, platforms, Space Belts, trains, colors, crystals, and high-throughput production.

<GuideTable
  caption="Classic Mode Trade Station order in Shapez 2 1.0."
  columns={classicOrderColumns}
  rows={classicOrderRows}
/>

If you are new, learn Classic Mode first. If you already understand factory routing and throughput, Manufacture Mode is where Trade Stations become the main game.

For the broader route, start with the <Link href="/shapez-2">Shapez 2 Guide Hub</Link> or read the dedicated <Link href="/shapez-2/manufacture-mode-guide">Manufacture Mode Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="how-to-supply-a-trade-station">
  How to supply a Trade Station in Shapez 2
</h2>

To supply a Trade Station, build the requested input shape as a separate module, verify the output preview, then route it into the station with enough Space Belt capacity.

<GuideTable
  caption="Best early Trade Station setup."
  columns={earlySetupColumns}
  rows={earlySetupRows}
/>

The station footprint matters more than it first appears. Some stations have unusual shapes, extra output pieces, or front attachments that make routing harder. Leave room for input Space Belts, refined output Space Belts, cleanup access, parallel copies, train loaders or unloaders, and future alternate recipes.

Before copying the block, watch it run. The best sign of a good Trade Station setup is not that the input is full. It is that the station processes continuously and the refined output leaves continuously.

<h2 id="alternate-recipes-and-when-to-use-them">
  Shapez 2 alternate Trade Station recipes: how to choose
</h2>

Manufacture Mode includes alternate Trade Station recipes for early tiers. These are unlocked through research and act as alternate manufacturers: they require different inputs while giving you a variant of that tier's refined output.

This is a major mid-game decision because an alternate recipe can either simplify your factory or make it worse.

<GuideTable
  caption="How to decide whether to use an alternate Trade Station recipe."
  columns={alternateRecipeColumns}
  rows={alternateRecipeRows}
/>

The best alternate recipe is not always the one with the easiest shape on paper.

Choose the recipe that makes the whole chain cleaner:

- fewer long-distance imports
- fewer awkward Space Belt crossings
- easier refined output routing
- better station footprint
- less pressure on scarce raw shapes or paint
- easier downstream connection to the next tier

If an alternate recipe solves one input but creates three new train routes, it may not be an upgrade.

<h2 id="donation-station-and-research-station">
  What do Donation Stations and Research Stations do in Shapez 2?
</h2>

Trade Stations do not exist alone. In Manufacture Mode, **Donation Stations** and **Research Stations** are what let the system expand.

A **Donation Station** accepts shapes, increases operator progress, and grants research points. Those research points are important because they unlock alternate manufacturers and other station-related upgrades.

A **Research Station** works similarly, but it can also output shapes or byproducts. This matters because it can help scale the earlier waste-processing chain instead of leaving your whole factory limited by the first source.

A simple mid-game research loop:

1. Stabilize your first refined chain.
2. Add a Donation Station for research points.
3. Unlock a useful alternate recipe or Research Station.
4. Feed refined shapes into the Research Station.
5. Route the byproducts back into earlier production.
6. Use the extra output to support more Trade Station chains.

Do not rush this before your first station chain is stable. Research infrastructure is powerful, but it can also steal shapes from the station line that is supposed to advance the campaign.

<h2 id="grand-vortex-assembler">
  What is the Grand Vortex Assembler in Shapez 2?
</h2>

The Grand Vortex Assembler is the reason the Trade Station chain matters.

In Classic Mode endgame examples, the Vortex Grand Assembler appears after refined Trade Station tiers and consumes high-tier refined shapes to produce the final scenario shape. In Manufacture Mode, the long-term goal is even clearer: you are producing the pieces needed to rebuild the Vortex platform.

This means a good Trade Station build is not just a way to clear the current milestone. It is infrastructure for the final objective.

When you reach assembler planning, check three things:

1. Which refined shapes does the assembler consume?
2. Which earlier stations supply those refined shapes?
3. Can every upstream station run continuously at the assembler's demand rate?

If the answer to the third question is no, the assembler will starve even when the final belts look clean.

<h2 id="common-trade-station-mistakes">
  Common Trade Station mistakes
</h2>

<GuideTable
  caption="Common Shapez 2 Trade Station mistakes and what to do instead."
  columns={commonMistakeColumns}
  rows={commonMistakeRows}
/>

Most station failures come from one of four mistakes: guessing ratios, ignoring research infrastructure, choosing alternate recipes too early, or polluting the refined-shape bus.

A good station block should answer four questions at a glance:

1. What exact shape goes in?
2. How fast does it go in?
3. What refined shape comes out?
4. Where does that refined shape go next?

If you cannot answer those quickly, the design is too hard to debug.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What does a Trade Station do in Shapez 2?

A Trade Station consumes exact input shapes and outputs refined shapes for later Trade Station tiers, rockets, or the Grand Vortex Assembler.

### What is the most important Trade Station ratio?

The most important ratio is the one shown in the tooltip for the station you are using. As a planning baseline, normal belts are commonly 120 shapes/min at base speed, Space Belt lanes are commonly 480 shapes/min, and early Manufacture Mode polishing examples process 60 shapes/min per station.

### How many Polishing Stations do I need early in Manufacture Mode?

The early guidance is to scale to at least 8 Polishing Stations when your first refined-output step is too slow. Build one first to prove the route, then scale the block.

### Why is my Trading Rocket not launching?

The rocket usually has not received enough valid payload. Check the required shape, upstream Trade Stations, Space Belt connection, station output, and whether wrong shapes have polluted the line.

### Why is my Trade Station backed up?

If the input is full but the station is not processing, the output may be blocked or the station may be missing another required input. If the output is clear but processing pulses, the input rate may be too low.

### What are alternate recipes?

Alternate recipes are research-unlocked Trade Station variants that require different inputs while producing a same-tier refined output variant. Use them when they simplify routing or input supply.

### How do I unlock alternate Trade Stations?

Use Donation Stations to generate research points, then invest those points into alternate manufacturers or other research options.

### What does a Donation Station do?

A Donation Station accepts shapes, increases operator progress, and grants research points. It is the main bridge between production and research unlocks.

### What does a Research Station do?

A Research Station is similar to a Donation Station, but it can also provide shape output or byproducts. This can help scale earlier production loops.

### What is the Grand Vortex Assembler?

The Grand Vortex Assembler is a late-game / final objective building that consumes refined Trade Station outputs to produce Vortex-related final products.

### Should I use the normal recipe or an alternate recipe?

Use the route that makes your entire chain easier. Input distance, station footprint, Space Belt routing, and downstream demand matter more than the recipe looking simple in isolation.

### What guide should I read next?

If you are learning the full 1.0 mode, read the <Link href="/shapez-2/manufacture-mode-guide">Manufacture Mode Guide</Link>. If your issue is sorting, signals, or automatic routing, read the <Link href="/shapez-2/wires-belt-filter-guide">Wires and Belt Filter Guide</Link>.
