Companions in **Lucky Tower Ultimate** are not just followers for one tower run. They affect routing, combat safety, hidden quest lines, village progression, achievement cleanup, and even the true ending path. If you want the short version, the most important companions to learn first are **Sobu**, **Princess**, **Alva**, **Hubert**, **Marisha and Louise**, and **the Bard**.

<h2 id="quick-answer">
  <a href="#quick-answer">Quick Answer</a>
</h2>

<div>
  The best utility companion is <strong>Sobu</strong>. The strongest combat companion is <strong>Princess</strong>.
  The most important progression companion is <strong>Alva</strong>. The clearest long-term rescue companion is
  <strong>Hubert</strong>. And the two best system companions for long-run planning are
  <strong> Marisha and Louise</strong>, the pipefitters, plus <strong>the Bard</strong>.
</div>

<div>
  If you are a newer player making one rescue decision in one run, the safest practical priority is usually:
  <strong> Alva or Sobu first</strong>, then <strong>Hubert</strong>, then <strong>Princess</strong> only if you
  already have a feeding plan.
</div>

<h2 id="how-companions-work">
  <a href="#how-companions-work">How Companions Work</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Companions in Lucky Tower Ultimate matter on three levels:
</div>

<ul>
  <li>they help or complicate the current tower run,</li>
  <li>they unlock or simplify achievements,</li>
  <li>and they feed into village-side progression after escape.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  That is why companion management is one of the game’s deepest systems. You are not just choosing who follows you.
  You are choosing which long-term routes stay open.
</div>

<h2 id="where-to-find-companions">
  <a href="#where-to-find-companions">Where to Find Companions</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Named companions such as <strong>Hubert</strong>, <strong>Sobu</strong>, and <strong>Alva</strong> can appear as
  ordinary chance encounters, in cages, or in more specialized rescue situations.
</div>

<div>
  Other important companion sources include:
</div>

<ul>
  <li>random room encounters,</li>
  <li>special rescue rooms,</li>
  <li>the torture chamber,</li>
  <li>vending machines that sell companions,</li>
  <li>transformation-based setups that restore recruitable people.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="companion-loyalty-system">
  <a href="#companion-loyalty-system">Companion Loyalty System</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Loyalty, morale, and disposition are best understood as one shared system.
</div>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Coins improve loyalty.</strong> If you want companions to like you, give them gold.</li>
  <li><strong>Bad treatment lowers loyalty.</strong> Stealing gear, shaking them down, and using them as disposable bait works against you.</li>
  <li><strong>Glowing eyes signal very high devotion.</strong> That is the clearest visual sign of a very loyal companion.</li>
  <li><strong>Escaping with loyal companions matters.</strong> Those are the rescues that feel truly “kept” rather than temporarily dragged along.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  The exact coin threshold is still not cleanly quantified for every named companion, so the practical rule is simple:
  reward useful followers often, and stop treating them like free armor.
</div>

<h2 id="sobu-guide">
  <a href="#sobu-guide">Sobu Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Sobu is the best route-reading companion in the game, but his system is not passive. His prophecy is primarily an
  <strong>active question mechanic</strong>.
</div>

<h3>How Sobu’s prophecy works</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>You ask Sobu directly.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Each question lowers his morale.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>His answer is only about 50% reliable by design.</strong></li>
</ul>

<div>
  That means Sobu is not a guaranteed solver. He is a finite-information resource. The real skill is deciding when a
  half-reliable prophecy is worth spending morale on.
</div>

<h3>Sobu’s long-term value</h3>

<div>
  Sobu matters far beyond doors. Once the castle route opens, Sobu is the key figure who recognizes Von Wanst as the
  <strong>Chosen One</strong> and reveals the darker truth of the tower. That makes him one of the most important
  named rescues in the full game.
</div>

<h2 id="princess-guide">
  <a href="#princess-guide">Princess Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Princess begins as a disguised enemy, not a normal ally. To recruit her, you have to
  <strong> feed her enemy corpses</strong> until she flips into companion mode.
</div>

<h3>How Princess joins</h3>

<ul>
  <li>She starts as a fake-princess enemy encounter.</li>
  <li>You convince her by feeding her corpses.</li>
  <li>Once she has been fed enough, she joins the party.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  Public guides agree on the corpse-feeding rule, but they do not all give one universally confirmed body-count number.
  In practice, the safe rule is: keep feeding corpses until her state changes.
</div>

<h3>Why Princess is so strong</h3>

<div>
  Princess has one of the highest combat ceilings in the game because once she joins, she will devour nearby enemies on
  her own. That includes dangerous targets like hammerers and bees, which is why she powers several high-value
  achievement routes.
</div>

<h3>Princess hunger and betrayal</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Her loyalty is tied to hunger.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>Hunger drains as the run goes on.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>If she is not fed often enough, she turns hostile again.</strong></li>
  <li><strong>When she flips, she becomes your problem immediately.</strong></li>
</ul>

<div>
  Princess is the ultimate snowball companion: the strongest ally in the room when handled correctly, and one of the
  fastest ways to throw a run if you recruit her without a feeding plan.
</div>

<h2 id="alva-guide">
  <a href="#alva-guide">Alva Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Alva is much more dangerous than a simple “helpful witch” description suggests. She is one of the most valuable
  progression companions in the game, but she is also a real combat actor with real offensive threat.
</div>

<h3>What Alva does in combat</h3>

<ul>
  <li>She throws <strong>explosion potions</strong> at enemies as ranged support.</li>
  <li>She provides <strong>antidote support</strong> when poison becomes a problem.</li>
  <li>If you anger her or push too hard for potions, she can throw those same dangerous potions at <strong>you</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  This is important because an unarmored or lightly armored player can die instantly to Alva’s bad side. She is not a
  passive support NPC. She is an armed alchemist.
</div>

<h3>Why Alva matters long-term</h3>

<ul>
  <li>She always carries an Antidote item.</li>
  <li>She can repeatedly heal poison once her support loop is established.</li>
  <li>After rescue, her hut becomes a key potion and cauldron space.</li>
  <li>She is part of the line leading to the legendary potion and the final Evelius route.</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Alva clone clue</h3>

<div>
  One of the strangest worldbuilding hints comes from repeated Alva rescues. Village-side Alva can comment on another
  duplicate showing up and exploding, which strongly suggests the tower’s named companions are not unique bodies in the
  usual sense, but magical copies or doppelgängers.
</div>

<h2 id="hubert-guide">
  <a href="#hubert-guide">Hubert Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Hubert matters for both utility and long-term payoff.
</div>

<h3>What Hubert does</h3>

<ul>
  <li>He can help scout dangerous doors.</li>
  <li>Giving him enough money makes this role more rewarding long-term.</li>
  <li>He also anchors one of the clearest recurring family quest lines in the village.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  Hubert is one of the companions who most clearly proves that repeated rescues are not wasted effort.
</div>

<h3>Hubert’s long-term value</h3>

<ul>
  <li><strong>True friend</strong> is tied to giving Hubert enough money to save his family.</li>
  <li><strong>Avenger</strong> is tied to killing Hubert’s killer with Hubert’s club or corpse.</li>

<h2 id="pipefitter-guide">
  <a href="#pipefitter-guide">Pipefitter Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  <strong>Marisha and Louise</strong>, the pipefitters, are one of the most important 1.0-era companion systems because
  they partially break the normal rule that the best tower items are hard to preserve.
</div>

<h3>How the Pipefitter system actually works</h3>

<ul>
  <li>You work with Marisha and Louise in the village.</li>
  <li>You gradually repair the tower’s broken pipe network.</li>
  <li>Inside the tower, each segment has a pipe slot that can move one item forward to the <strong>next segment</strong>.</li>
  <li>Once an item reaches the outside village end of the network, you can buy it back from the pipefitters.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  This is not a normal “cross-run storage chest.” It is better understood as an <strong>in-run transport network</strong>
  that can eventually push valuable gear all the way out to the village buyback point.
</div>

<div>
  That makes the pipe system extremely strong for segment planning, emergency item preservation, and setting up better
  outcomes when you actually survive the tower.
</div>

<h2 id="bard-guide">
  <a href="#bard-guide">Bard Guide</a>
</h2>

<div>
  The Bard is one of the strangest companion systems in the game because his value is tied as much to his corpse as to
  his living quest line.
</div>

<h3>How the Bard quest works</h3>

<ul>
  <li>The Bard wants instruments from the tower.</li>
  <li>Instrument drops are especially linked to the musical enemy trio.</li>
  <li>After his quest advances, the Bard enters the tower and usually dies in a random place each run.</li>
  <li>His corpse can be looted for instruments and other useful items.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Why the Bard corpse matters</h3>

<ul>
  <li>You can use the corpse as a <strong>thrown body</strong> to trigger traps safely.</li>
  <li>That is what people mean when they say the Bard corpse can “disarm traps.” It does not magically disable them; it sets them off for you.</li>
  <li>His corpse is also a loot source, not just a joke prop.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  That makes the Bard much more practical than he first appears: part collection quest, part expendable utility tool.
</div>

<h2 id="best-companion-combos">
  <a href="#best-companion-combos">Best Companion Combos</a>
</h2>

<h3>Princess + Alva</h3>
<div>
  Princess is strongest when constantly fed, while Alva’s potion utility helps stabilize dangerous runs that would
  otherwise collapse. This is one of the best sustain-heavy snowball combinations in the game.
</div>

<h3>Sobu + Hubert</h3>
<div>
  Sobu gives you costly prophecy-level guidance, while Hubert adds safer practical scouting and follow-up value. Together
  they create one of the safest low-greed routing styles.
</div>

<h3>Marisha and Louise + any high-roll combat run</h3>
<div>
  The pipe network turns good items into recoverable value if you can keep them moving toward the village end of the
  tower.
</div>

<h2 id="clone-theory-and-worldbuilding">
  <a href="#clone-theory-and-worldbuilding">Clone Theory and Worldbuilding</a>
</h2>

<div>
  One of the most interesting ways to read the companion system is through the “clone” idea.
</div>

<ul>
  <li>Repeated rescues can produce duplicate named characters.</li>
  <li>Alva directly comments on repeated copies showing up and exploding.</li>
  <li>Blue human-restoring transformations turn monsters or brooms back into near-identical peasants.</li>
  <li>The tower keeps generating bodies, copies, and reruns in ways that do not feel natural.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  Put together, that strongly supports the darker reading that the tower is less a normal dungeon and more a magical
  meat grinder full of copied, recycled, or transformed people.
</div>

<h2 id="village-progression-and-citizenship">
  <a href="#village-progression-and-citizenship">Village Progression and Citizenship</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Saving companions and building village progression are linked, but not in the same way.
</div>

<ul>
  <li><strong>Saving peasants</strong> improves village life, activity, and tavern population.</li>
  <li><strong>Spending money</strong> is what actually pushes core unlock thresholds like housing upgrades and citizenship.</li>
</ul>

<div>
  The main long-term goal is getting <strong>citizenship</strong>. Once you have that, the castle route opens, Sobu’s
  Chosen One line becomes relevant, and the game starts moving toward the deeper Evelius / true ending path.
</div>

<div>
  There is also an alternate route: the castle area opens not only for players with citizenship, but also for players
  who have <strong>unleashed Princess on the guards</strong>. That means Princess is not just a combat companion. She is
  also a chaos-based shortcut into endgame progression.
</div>

<h2 id="new-player-priority">
  <a href="#new-player-priority">New Player Priority</a>
</h2>

<div>
  If you are new and only want a practical rescue order, use this:
</div>

<ol>
  <li><strong>Alva</strong> if you are playing for long-term progression or safety</li>
  <li><strong>Sobu</strong> if wrong doors are killing your runs</li>
  <li><strong>Hubert</strong> if you want a safe named rescue with strong follow-up value</li>
  <li><strong>Marisha and Louise</strong> if you are already thinking in segment planning</li>
  <li><strong>Bard</strong> if you are doing collection and utility routes</li>
  <li><strong>Princess</strong> only when you can actually feed her</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="faq">
  <a href="#faq">FAQ</a>
</h2>

<h3>How do Sobu’s hints actually work?</h3>
<div>
  Ask Sobu directly. Each question lowers his morale, and his predictions are only about half-reliable by design.
</div>

<h3>How do you recruit Princess?</h3>
<div>
  Princess starts as a disguised enemy. Feed her corpses until she flips into companion mode.
</div>

<h3>Why is Princess so dangerous?</h3>
<div>
  Because her loyalty is tied to hunger. She is one of the strongest combat allies in the game, but she can revert into
  a threat if she is not fed often enough.
</div>

<h3>Why is Alva so important?</h3>
<div>
  Because she is both a combat-capable potion user and a key structural NPC for poison recovery, potion access, and the
  endgame route.
</div>

<h3>What do Marisha and Louise actually do?</h3>
<div>
  They help build a pipe network that moves items from one tower segment to the next. If an item reaches the village end
  of the network, you can buy it back there.
</div>

<h3>Why does the Bard matter if he is often dead?</h3>
<div>
  Because the Bard’s corpse is both a loot source and a throwable utility object for safely triggering traps.
</div>

<h2 id="final-verdict">
  <a href="#final-verdict">Final Verdict</a>
</h2>

<div>
  Companions are one of the systems that make Lucky Tower Ultimate feel much bigger than a normal slapstick roguelite.
  Sobu teaches you that information has a morale cost. Princess teaches you that overwhelming power comes with upkeep.
  Alva teaches you that some companions are really progression systems in disguise. Hubert proves the game remembers your
  rescues. Marisha and Louise turn item routing into strategy. The Bard proves even a corpse can be a useful resource.
  Once you start reading companions this way, the whole game opens up.
</div>
