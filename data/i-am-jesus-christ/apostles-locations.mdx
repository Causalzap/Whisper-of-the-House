<h2 id="quick-apostles-overview">
  Quick apostles overview
</h2>

The 12 apostles in **I Am Jesus Christ** do not all join in the same way.

Some are:

- effectively part of the main story flow
- tied to obvious miracle scenes
- grouped together in clean early recruitment chains

Others are:

- hidden behind secondary triggers
- easy to leave unfinished by accident
- more important for follower-heavy progression than they first appear

That is why this page is not just a list of names. It is a **recruitment order, anti-miss, and progression guide**.

---

<h2 id="how-apostle-recruitment-works">
  How apostle recruitment works
</h2>

The game usually recruits apostles through one or more of these patterns:

- a **story encounter**
- a **miracle demonstration**
- a **follow-up conversation after the miracle**
- a **second visit or return trigger**
- a **moral or theological dialogue choice**
- a **hidden or low-visibility first location**

That means “I found the apostle” is not always the same as “the apostle has joined.”

In practical terms, the safest way to think about apostle recruitment is:

1. find the apostle
2. trigger the scene properly
3. finish the miracle or linked objective
4. talk again if needed
5. wait for the actual join confirmation

<GuideCallout title="Common recruitment mistake">
A lot of players assume a conversation or miracle automatically finishes the recruitment. In several cases, the real join point happens only after a second interaction or a return trigger.
</GuideCallout>

---

<h2 id="apostles-and-miracle-scenes">
  How apostles affect miracle and scene progression
</h2>

Apostles do not just function as collection targets. In practice, they often matter because they:

- unlock or point you toward new miracle scenes
- add follow-up dialogue that confirms a route is actually complete
- make follower-heavy progression feel smoother
- connect one miracle scene to the next major story beat

This is especially noticeable with routes like:

- **Philip → Nathanael**, where one recruit opens the next
- **Thomas → widow’s son → riddles**, where a miracle scene is not the end of the chain
- **Thaddaeus → <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#exorcism">Exorcism</Link>**, where the miracle is part of the recruitment logic
- **Peter and Andrew → <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#transformation">Miraculous Catch of Fish</Link>**, where the miracle itself is the recruitment proof

This is one reason the apostles page and miracles page should be read together, not as separate topics.

<h2 id="recruitment-triggers">
  Recruitment triggers
</h2>

<h3 id="peter-and-andrew">
  Peter and Andrew
</h3>

Peter and Andrew are the cleanest early recruitment pair.

**Trigger chain:**
- finish **Wedding at Cana**
- reach the Sea of Galilee
- talk to Peter on the beach
- perform the <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#transformation">Miraculous Catch of Fish miracle sequence</Link>
- return to shore
- talk again to trigger the full “fishers of men” scene

This is one of the clearest examples of why a second interaction matters. The miracle alone is not the whole recruitment.

<h3 id="james-and-john">
  James and John
</h3>

These two follow immediately after Peter and Andrew.

**Trigger chain:**
- finish the fishing miracle
- move down the shore to Zebedee’s dock
- speak to James and John
- let the farewell animation with their father resolve fully

Do not sprint away too early during the join scene. This group is easy, but it still works better if you let the transition finish cleanly.

<h3 id="philip">
  Philip
</h3>

Philip is more about proof than spectacle.

**Trigger chain:**
- recruit the first fisherman group
- find Philip in the busier inland crowd
- demonstrate divine power in front of him
- complete the local miracle proof scene

He matters because he leads naturally into Nathanael.

<h3 id="bartholomew-nathanael">
  Bartholomew / Nathanael
</h3>

This is one of the first recruitment chains where **another apostle directly opens the route**.

**Trigger chain:**
- recruit Philip first
- go to the fig tree area he points out
- use <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#divine-vision">Divine Vision</Link>
- trigger the “I saw you under the fig tree” line
- complete the follow-up conversation

This is an excellent example of how Divine Vision can function like a hidden recruitment key.

<h3 id="matthew-levi">
  Matthew / Levi
</h3>

Matthew is one of the most valuable early-mid recruits because his route is tied to crowd and city-flow control.

**Trigger chain:**
- return to Capernaum’s tax area
- trigger the crowd-pressure or conflict scene
- calm the situation through miracle use or dialogue control
- follow Matthew into the meal or house sequence
- finish the defensive or parable-style response scene

Matthew is one of the best apostles to prioritize if you want the midgame follower wall to feel less punishing.

Thomas is one of the easiest apostles to leave unfinished.

**Trigger chain:**

- return to Thomas afterward
- complete the three riddles
- wait for the actual join confirmation

If you do not come back after the miracle scene, Thomas can feel like unfinished flavor dialogue rather than a completed recruit.

<h3 id="james-son-of-alphaeus">
  James son of Alphaeus
</h3>

This is a quieter recruitment route.

**Trigger chain:**
- reach the countryside preaching and travel phase
- find him near the field-side area
- finish the dialogue and support interaction
- resolve the small local need or symbolic task

He is not as flashy as Thomas or Matthew, but he fits well into the later cleanup structure.

<h3 id="thaddaeus-jude">
  Thaddaeus / Jude
</h3>

Thaddaeus is closely tied to a synagogue-centered exorcism path.

**Trigger chain:**
- find the debate in the synagogue courtyard
- talk through the setup
- go inside or into the linked exorcism encounter
- finish the <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#exorcism">Exorcism</Link> properly
- return and confirm the follow-up conversation

This is one of the cleanest “miracle directly unlocks apostle” routes.

<h3 id="simon-the-zealot">
  Simon the Zealot
</h3>

Simon is one of the most sensitive recruitment routes because tone and values matter.

**Trigger chain:**
- unlock the Jericho / Judea route
- find the hidden rebel-style camp
- talk through the political tension
- choose the calmer, peace-aligned answers
- let Simon shift toward your vision of change

The safest way to recruit him is to stay consistently nonviolent in tone.

<h3 id="judas-iscariot">
  Judas Iscariot
</h3>

Judas is much closer to the Jerusalem-heavy late game.

**Trigger chain:**
- move into the stronger Jerusalem-facing story phase
- find him in the temple or nearby money-focused environment
- finish the stewardship / donation / teaching-themed sequence
- let the story fold him into the group

Judas feels much less like a hidden recruit and much more like a late-stage structural apostle.

<h2 id="missable-apostles">
  Most missable apostles
</h2>

If you are trying to avoid cleanup pain, these are the ones to watch most carefully.

### Thomas

The biggest trap is assuming the widow’s son miracle automatically finishes his route. It does not. You need to **come back** and finish the riddle chain.

### Bartholomew / Nathanael

The biggest trap is going to the fig tree too early or failing to use <Link href="/i-am-jesus-christ/miracles-list/#divine-vision">Divine Vision</Link> correctly. Philip is part of the route logic.

### Simon the Zealot

The biggest trap is treating the conversation like a generic rebel scene and leaning too hard into violent answers. The safest path is the peace-aligned one.

### Thaddaeus

The biggest trap is finishing the exorcism setup but not returning to resolve the follow-up conversation.

### Matthew

The biggest trap is treating the booth encounter like a quick pickup instead of following it through the meal and argument sequence.

<GuideCallout title="Hardcore rule for avoiding misses">
Whenever an apostle route includes a miracle, a crowd conflict, or a return visit, assume the route is not complete until the actual join confirmation is obvious.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="best-recruitment-order">
  Best recruitment order
</h2>

If your goal is the smoothest overall run, this order makes the most sense.

### Tier 1 — Core setup group

**Peter, Andrew, James, John**

This is the best opening group because they are:

- tightly clustered
- easy to follow from the main flow
- foundational for your early team
- the cleanest way to get your apostolic structure moving

The big practical lesson here is simple: do not delay Galilee shore recruitment.

### Tier 2 — Midgame momentum group

**Matthew, Philip, Thaddaeus**

This is one of the best midgame efficiency clusters because:

- Matthew is one of the strongest apostles for follower-focused progression
- Philip cleanly opens Nathanael’s route
- Thaddaeus fits naturally into miracle-linked regional progress

If you want the middle of the game to stop feeling slow, this is the group to prioritize.

### Tier 3 — Missables and condition-heavy routes

**Thomas, Bartholomew/Nathanael, Simon the Zealot**

These are the apostles most likely to punish sloppy routing.

Recruit them with intention, not passively. These are exactly the kind of routes players think they finished when they actually only triggered the setup.

### Tier 4 — Late-flow fillers

**James son of Alphaeus, Judas Iscariot**

These are less urgent for early stability and fit more naturally into the later story flow.

They still matter, but they are not where you should spend your earliest effort if your main concern is smoother progression.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Where do you find Peter and Andrew in I Am Jesus Christ?

You first find Peter and Andrew on **Capernaum Beach** after **Wedding at Cana**, near the fishing boats and shoreline work area. To actually recruit them, perform the Miraculous Catch sequence, return to shore, and speak again to trigger the full join scene.

### Where do you find James and John?

James and John appear further down the Galilee shore near **Zebedee’s dock** after the Peter and Andrew sequence. Let the farewell scene with their father finish before assuming the recruitment is complete.

### Where do you find Philip?

Philip appears around the busier inland market space after the early fisherman group is established. His route works best when you follow through the miracle-proof scene instead of treating him like an instant recruit.

### Where do you find Bartholomew / Nathanael?

You find Bartholomew (Nathanael) under the **fig tree** outside Cana, but his route works best only after recruiting Philip first. Use Divine Vision at the fig tree scene to trigger the recognition dialogue instead of treating it like a simple walk-up recruit.

### Where do you find Matthew?

Matthew first appears at the **tax collector’s booth** in Capernaum, near Roman guards and a crowd-pressure scene. To recruit him properly, stabilize the conflict and then follow the house or meal sequence through to the actual join point.

### Where do you find Thomas?

Thomas first appears in the **carpenter’s workshop** in **Nain**. To actually recruit him, finish the widow’s son sequence, return to the workshop, and complete his three riddles. If you do not come back after the miracle, his route can feel unfinished.

### Which apostle is easiest to miss?

Thomas is usually the most missable because his route depends on returning after the resurrection-linked story sequence and then completing the riddle chain instead of walking away after the first big miracle.

### What is the best recruitment order?

The cleanest order is:
- Peter
- Andrew
- James
- John
- Matthew
- Philip
- Nathanael
- then your missable or condition-heavy routes

This gives you the strongest early base before the follower-heavy middle of the game.

### Which apostles matter most for follower progression?

Matthew is one of the strongest momentum recruits, and apostles tied to miracle-heavy or crowd-heavy routes matter most once follower requirements start tightening.

### Does every apostle have a clear map marker?

No. Some do, but several of the most valuable or easiest-to-miss apostles rely more on proximity triggers, follow-up dialogue, or trigger-area logic than on a permanently obvious marker.

### Is Judas missable?

Judas is less “secret missable” than apostles like Thomas and more closely tied to the later Jerusalem-facing story flow, so he is usually safer to leave until later.

### Do I need all 12 apostles before the follower-heavy midgame?

Not always, but the stronger your recruitment foundation is before the follower wall, the less painful the middle of the game usually feels.
