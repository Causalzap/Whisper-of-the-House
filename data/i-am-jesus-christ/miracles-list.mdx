<h2 id="how-the-miracle-system-works">
  How the miracle system works
</h2>

The miracle system in **I Am Jesus Christ** is not just a giant checklist of isolated powers. In practice, it feels like a mix of:

- **miracle categories**
- **story set pieces**
- **interactive puzzles**
- **healing or restoration tasks**
- **combat or banishment encounters**
- **movement or environmental miracle sequences**

That is why scenes like **Water into Wine**, **Feeding the 5,000**, and **Walking on Water** should not be treated the same way as **Healing** or **Exorcism** on a mechanics page. Some are **categories**, while others are **story miracle scenes built on top of them**.


<h3 id="holy-spirit-and-prayer">
  Holy Spirit and prayer
</h3>

One of the most important systems tied to miracle use is **Holy Spirit**.

In practical terms:

- miracle-heavy scenes consume **Holy Spirit**
- if you empty the bar too early, later inputs become harder
- some combat or banishment encounters punish bad resource timing
- **prayer** is the main way to restore your miracle resource

That means miracles are not just about “which power is unlocked.” They are also about **how well you manage Holy Spirit under pressure**.

A simple rule works for most players:

- do not panic-spam miracle use
- recover before the bar is almost empty
- step out of danger if the scene allows it
- re-enter the miracle interaction only when you actually have control again

<h3 id="divine-vision">
  Divine Vision
</h3>

**Divine Vision** is not a miracle category in the same sense as Transformation or Healing, but it is still one of the most important support abilities in the game.

It helps you:

- reveal clues and trails
- identify interactive objects
- spot miracle-related targets more quickly
- move through puzzle or investigation sections without wandering blindly

In other words, Divine Vision is often the bridge between **finding the next miracle scene** and **actually completing it**.

### Unlocks are not always just raw combat powers

Some miracle progression is gated behind:

- story scenes
- puzzle sequences
- Bible-knowledge or heavenly-question style sections
- scene-specific interactions rather than freeform spell selection

That is one reason the game can feel confusing if you expect a normal RPG skill tree. Miracle progression here is more narrative and scene-based than that.

<h2 id="transformation">
  Transformation miracles
</h2>

**Transformation** is one of the clearest core miracle categories in the game, and **Water into Wine** is the best early example of how it works.

This category makes the most sense for moments where:

- an object changes state
- the scene depends on setup and interaction
- the player completes a transformation-style miracle input
- the miracle is tied to a story event rather than free-cast combat

That is why the famous scenes that players remember most strongly are often not separate skill slots at all. They are **Transformation-based story miracle scenes**.

### Water into Wine

At **Wedding at Cana**, the miracle is not just a cutscene. You need to:

- set up the scene properly
- find and interact with the correct jars
- hold the miracle input steadily
- wait for the transformation to fully register

If you stop too early, the game may not count the interaction even if it looks partially finished.

### Miraculous Catch of Fish

This scene is best treated as another **Transformation / utility** miracle moment rather than a completely unrelated power.

The practical logic is:

- interact with the correct boat or miracle trigger
- begin the fish-related miracle sequence
- complete the scene cleanly so the recruitment story can move forward

### Feeding the 5,000

This is still best understood as a **Transformation-based crowd miracle**.

In practical terms, the scene is not just “watch bread appear.” You are usually doing some combination of:

- advancing the crowd setup
- handling the loaves and fish interaction
- placing or using the correct containers
- opening or resolving the multiplied food scene correctly

So Transformation is not just about one object becoming another. It is one of the core ways the game turns iconic story moments into interactive miracle sequences.

<h2 id="healing-miracles">
  Healing miracles
</h2>

**Healing** is best understood as a miracle family rather than a single one-off event.

This category includes scenes like:

- **Peter’s mother-in-law**
- wounded or sick civilians
- lepers
- blind or disabled people
- other story-linked illness and injury scenes

### How Healing actually works

Healing is one of the categories where players most often want **practical instructions**, not just story description.

For ordinary healing scenes, the basic loop is usually:

- stabilize the camera on the afflicted target
- focus on the actual wounds, sores, or affected areas
- clear them one by one instead of sweeping around randomly
- keep the interaction precise until the healing state completes

That is why “targeted interaction” is not just vague flavor text. In practice, sloppy cursor movement or rushing the interaction is what makes these scenes feel harder than they really are.

### Healing crippled or more severe conditions

Some of the higher-pressure healing or life-restoration scenes feel more advanced than basic wound cleanup. These are closer to the game’s later miracle logic, where the interaction becomes more symbolic or more timing-sensitive.

If a scene feels more demanding than simple wound removal, it usually means the game is treating it as a higher-stakes miracle sequence rather than a routine support heal.

### When Healing is required

Healing is clearly required when it is tied to:

- a story NPC
- apostle progression
- a specific city or household milestone

### When Healing is optional

Healing leans optional when it is tied to:

- generic town cleanup
- broader follower grinding
- completionist progression
- regional support content

If your goal is only the shortest story route, you can skip some healing content. If your goal is smoother progression, Healing is one of the best categories to keep using.

**Exorcism** becomes available after the **Wilderness / Satan** sequence, and it is one of the most obviously game-like miracle categories in the whole campaign.

Its main uses are:

- banishing darkness
- clearing possession
- handling specific story exorcism scenes
- supporting follower gain and route stability

### How Exorcism actually works

Exorcism is more active than many Healing or Transformation scenes. The core loop usually involves:

- managing **Holy Spirit** under pressure
- keeping the target or darkness pattern under control
- interrupting the encounter cleanly instead of just free-firing
- completing the banishment sequence rather than only surviving it

That is why Exorcism feels like the most “gameplay-heavy” miracle category. It is not just about selecting the right power. It is about staying stable long enough to finish the miracle properly.

### Why players fail Exorcism

The most common problems are:

- draining Holy Spirit too early
- overreacting instead of controlling the pace
- treating the scene like raw DPS instead of a banishment mechanic
- losing track of the actual objective while under pressure

If you want a miracle category that matters for both story progression and follower efficiency, Exorcism is probably the best example.

<h2 id="resurrection">
  Resurrection
</h2>

**Resurrection** is one of the most important late miracle categories because it is tied to the game’s biggest emotional and narrative peaks.

The clearest examples include:

- the **widow’s son** sequence in **Nain**
- the **Lazarus** sequence in **Bethany**

### How Resurrection actually works

Compared to Healing or Transformation, Resurrection scenes are much higher pressure.

The gameplay logic is closer to:

- beginning the resurrection sequence
- protecting or guiding the miracle under time pressure
- avoiding the failure pressure from darkness or loss of control
- finishing the miracle before the scene collapses

In practical terms, players should think of Resurrection as a **guided completion minigame**, not just a button press.

If a resurrection scene feels difficult, the usual reason is not that the game is bugged. It is that the scene is intentionally designed to feel more dramatic and less forgiving than ordinary healing.

### Why Resurrection matters so much

Resurrection is one of the game’s strongest proof-of-power systems. It is used when the story wants to move from “teacher and healer” into something much more climactic and unmistakable.

So even though you will not use Resurrection as often as Healing, it is one of the most important miracle categories in the entire game.

<h2 id="feeding-the-5000">
  Feeding the 5,000
</h2>

**Feeding the 5,000** is one of the clearest examples of a miracle that is both:

- a major biblical set piece
- a practical progression milestone
- a follower-heavy crowd miracle

In story terms, it is one of the most memorable public miracle scenes in the game. In gameplay terms, it is important because it helps scale the campaign up from local healing and apostle interactions into something much bigger.

It is also one of the best examples of why “miracle scenes” and “miracle categories” should be separated. This is a major miracle moment, but it fits more naturally inside **Transformation / crowd miracle logic** than as its own permanent top-level category.

### Why it matters for progression

This scene is useful because it is tied to:

- crowd progression
- follower growth
- midgame momentum
- the move into the Sea of Galilee sections

If you are trying to break through the follower-heavy middle part of the game, this is one of the most important miracle scenes to understand.

  Walking on Water and storm miracles
</h2>

**Walking on Water** is a major story miracle, but it also has a strong gameplay identity because it changes how movement and route progression work in the sea section.

That makes it useful to think about in two layers:

### Walking on Water

This is the obvious headliner scene. It is tied to:

- locating and reaching the disciples’ boat
- surviving the sea route
- finishing one of the game’s most recognizable miracle set pieces

The most important practical point is that this is not just a dramatic animation. It is a playable movement sequence.

### Storm and sea miracle logic

The sea sections also include broader miracle-driven environmental pressure:

- weather
- danger visibility
- route reading
- survival pacing

That is why “Calming the Storm” makes more sense as a **sea miracle set piece** than as a separate top-level category.

### Why this matters for players

If you get stuck here, the problem is usually not theological confusion. It is practical gameplay:

- reading the environment
- staying patient
- not charging straight into hazards
- keeping control during a visually noisy sequence

So this whole cluster belongs in a miracle guide even if it is not a classic spell-slot category.

Because it becomes a core tool after the Wilderness and stays relevant for both story and followers.

Because it anchors some of the biggest late-story miracle scenes.

If you only want the shortest possible story path, prioritize the miracle categories that are obviously tied to hard story scenes instead of broad optional cleanup.

<h2 id="what-30-plus-miracles-probably-means">
  What “30+ miracles” probably means
</h2>

The phrase **“30+ miracles”** is easy to misunderstand if you read it like a normal skill list.

The most practical reading is:

- not necessarily **30+ major miracle categories**
- but rather **many miracle scenes, miracle moments, or meaningful miracle events** across the campaign

That matches the structure of the game much better:

- a smaller number of core miracle categories
- a larger number of story scenes that use them

That also explains why some famous biblical moments feel mechanically related even though they are very different in the story.
