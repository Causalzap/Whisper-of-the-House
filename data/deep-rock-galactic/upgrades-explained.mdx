<h2 id="upgrades-overview">
  Rogue Core Upgrades Explained
</h2>

Rogue Core upgrades are not just bigger numbers.

They are how your run becomes a build.

A good upgrade can make your weapon easier to control, give your Reclaimer ability a real role, fix ammo problems, help against armor, turn crits into crowd control, or make a risky floor safer. A bad upgrade can look powerful and still do almost nothing for the way you are actually playing.

This guide explains how to read upgrades like a player:

- how Expenite costs scale,
- when the upgrade event pauses the action,
- how 4-player upgrade choices work,
- how to read weapon stats,
- when to focus one weapon,
- when a new weapon is worth it,
- how Workbenches differ from Expenite upgrades,
- how Bio Boosters fit into your build,
- and how upgrade synergy changes the value of later picks.

If you are still learning the full run loop, start with the <Link href="/deep-rock-galactic/beginner-guide">Rogue Core beginner guide</Link>. If you are choosing upgrades around a specific Reclaimer, use the <Link href="/deep-rock-galactic/best-class">Rogue Core best class guide</Link> too.

<h2 id="how-expenite-upgrades-work">
  How Expenite Upgrades Work
</h2>

<GuideTable
  caption="How the Expenite upgrade loop works in Rogue Core."
  columns={upgradeLoopColumns}
  rows={upgradeLoopRows}
/>

The most important habit is simple:

**Deposit before the run gets messy.**

If you are carrying Expenite and the team is close to an upgrade, stop delaying it. Call the drone, deposit, gather, and take the upgrade. An upgrade waiting in your pocket does nothing when the next wave hits.

<GuideTable
  caption="Current Early Access Expenite cost curve for standard upgrades."
  columns={expeniteCostColumns}
  rows={expeniteCostRows}
/>

In a steady run, you can expect roughly a dozen standard upgrade events per player, but that depends on how cleanly your team mines, deposits, and keeps the run moving. The exact number is less important than the pattern: early upgrades are cheap, later upgrades punish scattered choices.

<h2 id="upgrade-choice-screen">
  How to Read an Upgrade Choice
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/deep-rock-galactic/rogue-core-bio-booster-choice.webp"
    alt="Upgrade choices showing Vital Impact and Lucky Shot in Deep Rock Galactic Rogue Core."
    width={1600}

    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Choices like Vital Impact and Lucky Shot are only great if your build can trigger crits often enough.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

When an upgrade choice appears, slow down for a second.

Ask these questions:

1. **Does this help my current weapon?**
2. **Does this help my Reclaimer ability?**
3. **Does this fix my biggest problem right now?**
4. **Can I actually trigger this bonus?**
5. **Will this still matter on the next floor?**

For example, **Vital Impact** says critical hits stun enemies for **+2s**. That is not just a damage upgrade. It turns crits into crowd control.

That makes it much better when you already have:

- weakpoint damage,
- crit chance,
- Spotter support,
- accurate weapons,
- or a build that keeps landing critical hits.

If you are missing weak points and panic-firing into armor, the same upgrade becomes much weaker.

Rogue Core gives you real numbers, but you should read them as playstyle clues.

A **BRT7 Burst Fire Gun** roll with **+40% Recoil Control** and **+30% Rate of Fire** is not telling you the same thing as a **"Hammer Caster" 58MM** with **+20% Damage**. One is leaning toward smoother handling and faster output. The other is more about raw hit value.

A **Zhukov NUK17** with **+1 Penetration** and **+200% Armor Breaking** is a different kind of answer. That roll is attractive when armored or dense enemies are slowing you down.

<h2 id="focus-one-weapon">
  Focus One Weapon Before Spreading Upgrades
</h2>

One common mistake is upgrading everything a little.

In Rogue Core, one reliable upgraded weapon is usually better than two weak weapons that both feel unfinished. If your current gun already handles the run well, push it toward Epic or Legendary-level value before splitting upgrades across another weapon.

<GuideTable
  caption="When to focus one weapon, split upgrades, or take a new weapon."
  columns={focusColumns}
  rows={focusRows}
/>

The exception is when your current weapon has a clear blind spot. If it cannot handle armor, range, crowds, or ammo pressure, a second weapon can be worth taking because it solves a problem your main weapon cannot.

<h2 id="choose-upgrades-by-problem">
  Choose Upgrades by the Problem You Are Solving
</h2>

The best upgrade is not always the highest damage upgrade.

It is the upgrade that solves the thing currently hurting your run.

<GuideTable
  caption="Which upgrade type to pick based on your current run problem."
  columns={problemColumns}
  rows={problemRows}
/>

A simple example:

If you are dying because enemies surround you, **movement, stun, pushback, penetration, or area damage** may be better than more single-target damage.

If you are losing long fights because your gun is empty, **ammo recovery, magazine size, reload speed, or a second weapon** may be better than a damage roll.

If you are already landing weakpoint shots, **+40% Weakpoint Damage** can be huge. If you are not hitting weak points at all, that upgrade is mostly wishful thinking.

A good upgrade can be wrong if you take it at the wrong time.

Early upgrades should make your run stable. Mid-run upgrades should strengthen the direction you already started. Late upgrades should solve the thing most likely to kill the run.

  Workbenches: Ability, Weapon Upgrade, or New Weapon?
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

  </figcaption>
</figure>

A Workbench often asks you to choose between three different build directions:

<GuideTable
  caption="How to choose between Workbench options in Rogue Core."
  columns={workbenchColumns}
  rows={workbenchRows}
/>

A Workbench is not free value. It often asks you to find a toolbox first, bring it back, and then choose the upgrade.

That means the real question is not only:

**Which option is strongest?**

It is also:

**Is the toolbox close enough, and is the floor stable enough, for this upgrade to pay back the time?**

Use a Workbench when:

- the toolbox is close,
- the room is safe enough,
- your build has a clear weakness,
- and the offered choice fixes that weakness.

Skip or delay it when:

- the toolbox is far,
- the aggression meter is already high,
- ammo or armor is low,
- or your current build is strong enough to leave.

A **Crit Darts** ability upgrade like **+1.25m Crit Dart Area Radius** makes sense when you are playing around Spotter marks and team focus.

A **P-96 Mag Blaster** weapon upgrade with **-35% Spread**, **+200% Armor Breaking**, and **+40% Damage as Acid** pushes the weapon toward accuracy, armor handling, and damage-over-time value.

Use the same upgrade-choice checklist from earlier, then add one Workbench-only question:

**Did the toolbox trip cost less than the upgrade gives back?**

That is the part normal Expenite upgrades do not ask. A Workbench can be excellent, but only if the toolbox route, room safety, and current floor pressure make sense.

If the ability upgrade improves a tool you are already using, take it. If your current weapon is already carrying fights, upgrade it. If the new weapon fixes a missing answer like armor, range, crowds, or ammo pressure, consider switching.

If none of the three options changes the next floor, leave with your current build.

Bio Boosters are different from Expenite upgrades.

They require a hack, then give you a personal upgrade choice. Because the choices are tied to your character, you do not need to worry about stealing the option from someone else.

A Bio Booster can look tempting because both options are usually useful. Still, use the same build-fit logic:

- **Vital Impact** is better when critical hits are already happening often.
- **Lucky Shot** style ammo recovery is better when you land crits and your weapon runs dry.
- **Movement speed** is better when survival and repositioning are the real problem.
- **Ability cooldown** is better when your Reclaimer ability is already saving fights.

Take the Bio Booster that solves the next floor, not the one that only looks exciting.

<h2 id="solo-upgrade-priority">
  Solo Upgrade Priority
</h2>

Solo uses the same upgrade framework as the rest of this guide, but the punishment for a bad choice is higher because no teammate can cover your missing piece.

That makes **upgrade synergy** more important in solo. If you already picked weakpoint damage, the next crit-based upgrade becomes more valuable. If you already picked magazine size, rate of fire or reload speed may become safer. If you are building Slicer around Slice damage, kill-chain duration or melee safety can matter more than a random weapon roll.

The solo rule is:

**Do not leave a major weakness uncovered.**

A co-op team can sometimes cover your bad armor handling, weak crowd clear, or low ammo. Solo cannot. Prioritize upgrades with simple, reliable value: armor, damage reduction, movement, ammo help, and damage triggers you can actually activate.

For the full solo decision framework, read the <Link href="/deep-rock-galactic/solo-guide">Rogue Core solo guide</Link>.

<h2 id="coop-upgrade-priority">
  Co-op Upgrade Priority
</h2>

Co-op upgrade choices are not only about your own build. They also involve pick order and team value.

<GuideTable
  caption="How upgrade choices work in a 4-player Rogue Core squad."
  columns={coopDraftColumns}
  rows={coopDraftRows}
/>

In co-op, lean into role value:

- Spotter gets more from crit and weakpoint triggers when teammates focus marked targets.
- Guardian turns stun, armor, and space-control upgrades into safer team fights.
- Falconer gets more from electric and revive reliability when teammates can play around those windows.
- Slicer can go harder into melee if the team creates space.
- Retcon gets more from planned burst windows when teammates understand the timing.

The main co-op mistake is not “picking selfish upgrades.” It is picking upgrades that neither help your own build nor solve a team problem.

<h2 id="when-to-skip-upgrades">
  When to Skip a Reward or Leave It Behind
</h2>

Not every reward is worth the time.

A Workbench is great when it is close and the team is stable. It is much worse when the toolbox is far away, ammo is low, and the aggression meter is already ugly.

A Bio Booster is great when the room is safe enough to hack. It is a bad idea when enemies are already breaking the team apart.

An Expenite event can be worth a lot, but it can also stack more enemies on top of pressure that is already building.

Use this rule:

**If the reward costs more health, ammo, and time than it gives back, leave.**

Rogue Core is not about collecting every possible upgrade. It is about getting enough power to survive the next floor.

<h2 id="simple-upgrade-rules">
  Simple Upgrade Rules
</h2>

Use these rules when you do not have time to think:

1. **If you are dying, take survival or movement.**
2. **If enemies live too long, take damage you can actually trigger.**
3. **If you are always empty, take ammo or magazine help.**
4. **If your weapon is working, upgrade it instead of replacing it.**
5. **If your ability is carrying fights, upgrade the ability.**
6. **If a stat depends on crits or weak points, only take it when you can trigger it.**
7. **If armor is slowing the run down, take armor breaking or acid-style pressure.**
8. **If reloads get you hit, take magazine size or reload speed.**
9. **If the Workbench toolbox is far and pressure is high, skip it.**
10. **If an upgrade works with what you already picked, its value goes up.**

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

<h3 id="how-do-upgrades-work-in-rogue-core">
  How do upgrades work in Rogue Core?
</h3>

Mine Expenite, call the R.E.P.D. / processor drone, deposit the Expenite, fill the upgrade bar, gather near the drone, and choose a permanent upgrade for the current run.

<h3 id="how-much-expenite-do-upgrades-cost">
  How much Expenite do upgrades cost?
</h3>

The first standard upgrade costs 120 Expenite. Early upgrade thresholds then increase by 10 each time until 230, after which costs scale harder.

<h3 id="does-the-game-pause-during-upgrade-selection">
  Does the game pause during upgrade selection?
</h3>

Yes. When the upgrade threshold is reached and players gather near the R.E.P.D., the upgrade choice pauses the action so you can read the options.

<h3 id="should-i-always-pick-the-rarest-upgrade">
  Should I always pick the rarest upgrade?
</h3>

No. Rarity matters, but build fit matters more. A lower-rarity upgrade that supports your weapon, class ability, or survival problem can be better than a rare upgrade that does not fit.

<h3 id="should-i-upgrade-one-weapon-or-spread-upgrades">
  Should I upgrade one weapon or spread upgrades?
</h3>

Usually focus one reliable weapon first. A strong upgraded weapon is often better than two weak weapons, unless your second weapon clearly solves a missing problem like armor, range, crowds, or ammo pressure.

<h3 id="when-should-i-deposit-expenite">
  When should I deposit Expenite?
</h3>

Deposit often, especially when the team is close to filling the upgrade bar. Holding Expenite too long delays power and can make the next wave harder than it needs to be.

<h3 id="are-workbenches-worth-it">
  Are Workbenches worth it?
</h3>

Usually, yes, but only when the team has time and space. A Workbench can give ability upgrades, weapon upgrades, or a new weapon, but chasing the toolbox during heavy pressure can cost the run.

<h3 id="should-i-take-a-new-weapon-or-upgrade-my-current-one">
  Should I take a new weapon or upgrade my current one?
</h3>

Upgrade your current weapon if it is already working. Take a new weapon if your current setup has a clear weakness, such as poor range, bad crowd control, weak armor handling, or ammo problems.

<h3 id="are-bio-boosters-better-than-normal-upgrades">
  Are Bio Boosters better than normal upgrades?
</h3>

Bio Boosters can be stronger and more personal than normal Expenite upgrades, but they require a hack and time. They are usually worth checking when the room is safe.

<h3 id="one-upgrade-rule">
  If I only remember one upgrade rule, what is it?
</h3>

Pick the upgrade that makes your next floor safer or faster. That usually means fixing your current bottleneck: damage, ammo, armor, movement, crowd control, weakpoint triggers, or ability uptime.

<h3 id="how-do-coop-upgrade-choices-work">
  How do co-op upgrade choices work?
</h3>

In a 4-player squad, players can see multiple upgrade options and choose in a random order. Most picks are exclusive, so a build-defining option may disappear if another player takes it first. Health Reward is a special shared-style option that can be available to everyone.

<h3 id="is-ammo-boost-worth-taking">
  Is Ammo Boost worth taking?
</h3>

Yes, if your run keeps running out of ammo. A simple ammo upgrade can be better than a flashy weapon when ammo pressure is the real reason the run is falling apart.

<h3 id="is-weakpoint-damage-good">
  Is weakpoint damage good?
</h3>

Weakpoint damage is good when you can actually hit weak points or your team can mark targets. If you are panic-firing into armor or crowds, take a more reliable upgrade first.
