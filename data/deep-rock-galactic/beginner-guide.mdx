<h2 id="beginner-guide-overview">
  Rogue Core Beginner Guide Overview
</h2>

Deep Rock Galactic: Rogue Core is not impossible for beginners, but it is easy to play it the wrong way if you come straight from classic Deep Rock Galactic.

The biggest difference is that Rogue Core is a run-based roguelite. You do not enter with a complete fixed build and slowly clear a normal mission. You pick weapons during the run, mine Expenite for upgrades, choose temporary power spikes, and keep moving before the floor pressure gets out of control.

If you already play DRG, your movement, aiming, revive sense, and cave awareness help. The habit you need to change is pace: Rogue Core rewards useful progress, not full-clearing every corner.

<h2 id="is-rogue-core-hard-for-beginners">
  Is Rogue Core Hard for Beginners?
</h2>

Yes, Rogue Core can feel hard for beginners because the game pushes you faster than regular DRG.

The difficulty is not only enemy damage. It comes from several things stacking together:

- you start with basic gear,
- weapons and upgrades are drafted during the run,
- Expenite upgrades need to be triggered on time,
- the aggression meter punishes slow play,
- events can stack extra enemies onto existing pressure,
- and Risk Vectors can make later floors harder.

The good news is that early runs get much easier once you stop trying to do everything. Your job is to get enough power to survive the next floor, not collect every reward on the map.

Do not just click the nearest mission and launch. Read the run first.

For your first few runs, choose the mission that lets you learn cleanly:

**low Depth, short Facility Length, low Complexity, and manageable Risk Vectors.**

That gives you more room to understand Expenite, upgrades, Workbenches, Bio Boosters, crates, and elevator defense without the run collapsing before you learn anything.

<h2 id="mine-and-deposit-expenite">
  Mine and Deposit Expenite Early
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

  </figcaption>
</figure>

Expenite is the green mineral you should care about immediately.

The basic loop is:

1. Mine Expenite.
2. Call the processor drone.
3. Deposit Expenite.
4. Fill the upgrade bar.
5. Gather near the drone.
6. Pick a permanent upgrade for the run.

The first upgrade costs **120 Expenite**. After that, the early thresholds increase by **10 Expenite** each time until they reach **230**.

<GuideTable
  caption="Early Expenite upgrade cost curve for Rogue Core beginners."
  columns={expeniteColumns}
  rows={expeniteRows}
/>

This is why early Expenite matters so much. The first few upgrades are cheap enough that ignoring nearby Expenite usually makes the run harder than it needs to be.

Do not carry Expenite forever. If your team is near a threshold, call the drone and deposit before starting a risky event or pushing into the next major fight.

Some floors may also give you Expenite-focused events or side opportunities. Treat those as upgrade accelerators, not mandatory objectives. They are worth doing when the team has ammo, armor, space, and a clear route back. They are not worth starting while the aggression meter is already high or while the team is sitting on enough Expenite to trigger an upgrade.

<h2 id="choose-upgrades-by-problem">
  Choose Upgrades by the Problem You Are Solving
</h2>

Do not think of upgrades as “best” or “bad” in isolation.

Think of them as answers.

<GuideTable
  caption="How beginners should choose upgrades based on the current run problem."
  columns={upgradeColumns}
  rows={upgradeRows}
/>

A weakpoint upgrade is strong if you can hit weak points. Ammo recovery is strong if you are running dry. Armor Breaking is strong if armored enemies are wasting your shots. Ability upgrades are strong if your Reclaimer tool is already carrying fights.

For deeper examples, read <Link href="/deep-rock-galactic/upgrades-explained">Rogue Core upgrades explained</Link>.

  Watch the Aggression Meter
</h2>

The aggression meter is Rogue Core’s pressure system.

When you mine, explore, hack, repair, or fight on a floor, pressure keeps rising. The meter does not mean you must sprint past everything. It means every side objective needs a reason.

Use this check before staying longer:

- Are you close to an Expenite upgrade?
- Is a Workbench or Bio Booster nearby?
- Does the team have enough ammo and armor?
- Is an event about to stack enemies on top of an incoming wave?
- Can you still reach the elevator cleanly?

If the answer is mostly no, stop adding risk.

<h2 id="use-workbenches">
  Use Workbenches When the Route Is Safe
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/deep-rock-galactic/rogue-core-workbench.webp"
    alt="Workbench upgrade choice in Deep Rock Galactic Rogue Core."
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Workbenches are one of the best places to fix a weak build or double down on a strong one.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Workbenches are one of your biggest beginner advantages because they are easy to understand once you know the flow.

The process is:

1. Find the Workbench.
2. Locate the toolbox.
3. Bring the toolbox back.
4. Choose between an ability upgrade, weapon upgrade, or new weapon.

This is one of the clearest decisions in Rogue Core.

Take an **ability upgrade** if your Reclaimer tool is already important. Take a **weapon upgrade** if your current weapon feels good and needs more power. Take a **new weapon** if your current setup has a real gap, such as bad armor handling, weak crowd control, poor range, or ammo problems.

Do not drag the toolbox across a collapsing floor just because the Workbench exists. Use it when the route is safe enough for the upgrade to pay back the time.

  Bio Boosters Are Strong Personal Upgrades
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">

Bio Boosters require a short hack, then give you a personal upgrade choice.

Because the upgrade is personal, you do not need to worry about taking someone else’s option. The main question is whether the room is safe enough to hack.

Good Bio Booster choices usually match your current problem:

- crit effects if you can trigger crits,
- movement if you keep getting trapped,
- ability cooldown if your class tool is carrying fights,
- ammo or recovery if long floors are draining you.

Do not start the hack just as a wave is about to hit unless the team is ready to defend the room.

<h2 id="risk-vectors">
  Risk Vectors: Read the Downside Before the Reward
</h2>

Risk Vectors appear between stages and change the direction of the run.

They usually give you a downside and a reward. The important part is that the downside can persist and stack through the rest of the mission, while the reward may only help the next stage or a specific part of the run.

That means Risk Vectors are not just flavor text.

Beginner rule:

**Do not pick the reward until you understand the downside.**

A heavy weapon crate sounds great, but if the attached downside makes the rest of the run much harder, it may not be worth it. Early on, choose Risk Vectors that make the next floor easier to understand, not ones that create complicated pressure you are not ready to handle.

<h2 id="know-when-to-start-the-elevator">
  Know When to Start the Elevator
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/deep-rock-galactic/rogue-core-elevator-defense.webp"
    alt="Elevator defense event in Deep Rock Galactic Rogue Core."
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    The elevator is a defense event, not a free exit. Start it when you are ready to commit.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The elevator starts a timer and triggers a Core Spawn attack.

You need to defend the generator until the elevator arrives. If enemies damage it too much, someone has to repair it, which gets stressful when ammo and armor are already low.

Before starting the elevator, make sure:

- everyone is close enough,
- weapons are reloaded,
- armor is not empty,
- the important reward is already taken,
- and the route back to the elevator is clear.

Once the elevator arrives, leave. The goal is to reach the next floor with enough ammo and armor to keep playing.

<h2 id="what-to-do-after-a-run">
  What to Do After a Run
</h2>

A failed run is not wasted.

After each run, return to the ship and check:

- completed directives,
- intel task progress,
- clearance unlocks,
- enhancement slots,
- Reclaimer upgrades,
- cosmetics,
- and loadout changes.

New players often lose progress speed by skipping terminals after a run. If the game is telling you to claim something, spend something, or unlock a new option, do it before launching again.

<h2 id="rogue-core-vs-drg-for-beginners">
  Rogue Core vs Deep Rock Galactic for Beginners
</h2>

If you are coming from Deep Rock Galactic, the biggest adjustment is not aiming or mining. It is tempo.

In DRG, you can often stabilize a messy mission by gathering Nitra, kiting, mining carefully, and recovering. In Rogue Core, the run keeps asking you to make decisions faster:

- which weapon solves the next floor,
- whether to deposit Expenite now,
- whether the Workbench is worth the time,
- whether the Risk Vector downside is acceptable,
- and when to leave instead of full clearing.

DRG experience helps, but Rogue Core needs different habits.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

<h3 id="is-rogue-core-hard-for-beginners">
  Is Rogue Core hard for beginners?
</h3>

Yes, it can feel hard at first because Rogue Core is faster, more pressure-based, and more dependent on upgrade decisions than classic Deep Rock Galactic. It gets easier once you learn when to mine, deposit, upgrade, and leave.

<h3 id="what-should-i-do-first-in-rogue-core">
  What should I do first in Rogue Core?
</h3>

Pick a low-depth mission, choose a simple Reclaimer, take a controllable weapon, mine and deposit Expenite early, and leave the floor before the aggression meter turns the run against you.

<h3 id="how-much-expenite-does-the-first-upgrade-cost">
  How much Expenite does the first upgrade cost?
</h3>

The first Expenite upgrade costs 120. Early upgrade thresholds then increase by 10 Expenite until they reach 230.

<h3 id="what-is-the-best-beginner-class">
  What is the best beginner class in Rogue Core?
</h3>

Guardian is the safest beginner class because it has crowd control, protection, armor support, and space-making tools. Falconer is a strong flexible option if you want damage and team utility.

<h3 id="what-weapon-should-beginners-pick">
  What weapon should beginners pick?
</h3>

Pick the weapon that solves your current problem. Recoil control and simple damage are good while learning. Armor breaking is better when armored enemies slow the run down. Ammo or magazine bonuses are better when you keep running dry.

<h3 id="what-are-risk-vectors">
  What are Risk Vectors in Rogue Core?
</h3>

Risk Vectors are between-stage choices that add a downside and a reward direction to the run. Read the downside before picking the reward because bad modifiers can make later floors harder.

<h3 id="are-rare-upgrades-always-better">
  Are rare upgrades always better?
</h3>

No. A rare upgrade can be worse than a common upgrade if it does not fit your weapon, Reclaimer, or current problem.

<h3 id="should-i-full-clear-every-floor">
  Should I full clear every floor?
</h3>

Usually no. Take useful rewards, mine nearby Expenite, and use important side systems, but leave before the aggression meter makes the floor too dangerous.

<h3 id="should-drg-players-play-rogue-core-differently">
  Should DRG players play Rogue Core differently?
</h3>

Yes. DRG habits help with movement, mining, and teamwork, but Rogue Core is more about run tempo, upgrade drafting, Expenite timing, and knowing when to leave.
