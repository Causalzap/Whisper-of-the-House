<h2 id="rogue-core-vs-drg-overview">
  Rogue Core vs Deep Rock Galactic Overview
</h2>

Deep Rock Galactic: Rogue Core feels familiar, but it does not play like a normal Deep Rock Galactic mission.

You are still a dwarf. You still go into dangerous caves. You still fight bugs, mine resources, shout Rock and Stone, and rely on co-op chaos. But the structure is different enough that old DRG habits can get you killed.

The biggest difference is simple:

**Deep Rock Galactic is mission-first. Rogue Core is run-first.**

In DRG, you usually enter with your class loadout, complete a mission objective, mine what you need, call the drop pod, and leave. In Rogue Core, you enter a dive, pick weapons, collect Expenite, take upgrades, push through floors, and build enough power to survive the boss.

<h2 id="is-rogue-core-a-sequel">
  Is Rogue Core a Sequel to Deep Rock Galactic?
</h2>

Rogue Core is better understood as a spin-off, not a direct replacement for Deep Rock Galactic.

That matters for expectations.

If you go in expecting another normal DRG mission pack, Rogue Core may feel strange. The game is built around run decisions: which weapon you take, which upgrade you pick, whether you risk another side reward, when you deposit Expenite, and whether your build can handle the next floor.

It is still DRG in spirit.

It is not DRG in structure.

This is the first place DRG players need to reset their thinking.

In classic DRG, you usually pick a mission type and prepare a loadout. In Rogue Core, the mission screen already tells you how the run may feel:

- **Depth** tells you difficulty.
- **Facility length** tells you how many floors you need to push through.
- **Complexity** tells you how annoying the cave may be to read and move through.
- **Biome** changes the environment.
- **Boss icon** tells you what the run is building toward.

That means the mission choice is not just “where are we going?”

It is also:

**How long is this run, how hard is it, and can my build survive it?**

Rogue Core uses Reclaimers instead of the classic DRG class structure.

That is a big shift for returning players.

Do not try to force the old four-class logic onto the new roster. The Reclaimers are built around new tools and different run roles:

- **Guardian** is the safest frontline and protection pick.
- **Spotter** is the team support and crit setup pick.
- **Falconer** is flexible offense with drone utility.
- **Slicer** is aggressive melee pressure and movement.
- **Retcon** is high-skill timing and risk control.

If you loved Scout, Engineer, Gunner, or Driller, you may still find a Reclaimer that matches your habits, but the match is not exact.

In DRG, a lot of your identity comes from the loadout you bring into the mission.

In Rogue Core, your build starts forming after the run begins.

That changes the way you make decisions. You are not just asking:

**What weapon do I like?**

You are asking:

**What does this run need right now?**

A weapon with recoil control may be better while learning. A weapon with armor breaking may be better when armored enemies slow the team down. A weapon with more ammo may be better when you keep running dry before the elevator.

That is the roguelite part of Rogue Core: the build is not only something you prepare before launch. It is something you assemble under pressure.

DRG players already understand that mining matters.

Rogue Core changes what mining is for.

Expenite is not just money or crafting material. It fuels your run upgrades. You mine it, deposit it into the processor drone, fill the upgrade bar, and choose upgrades that shape your current dive.

That means mining has a different question attached to it:

**Will this resource make us stronger before the floor gets too dangerous?**

If yes, mine it and deposit.  
If no, keep moving.

Rogue Core still rewards exploration, but it punishes players who treat every cave like a slow full clear.

<h2 id="tempo-and-pressure">
  Rogue Core Is Faster and Less Forgiving
</h2>

Rogue Core has a different pressure curve from classic DRG.

Ghost Ship has described Rogue Core's baseline difficulty as roughly equivalent to a **Hazard 3** experience in the original Deep Rock Galactic, scaling upward from there. That is a useful reference point if you are wondering whether Rogue Core is harder than DRG.

The difficulty is not only about enemy damage. It also comes from run pressure:

- you start with a smaller kit,
- weapons and upgrades are drafted during the dive,
- Expenite timing matters,
- side rewards compete with the aggression meter,
- and bad upgrade choices can make later floors feel much worse.

That does not mean you should sprint blindly.

It means you should stop treating every side reward as mandatory.

A good Rogue Core habit is:

**Take the reward that matters, then leave before the floor becomes the real enemy.**

<h2 id="solo-differences">
  Solo: Bosco vs Cooper

Solo is another place where returning players need to adjust.

In DRG, Bosco gives solo players a reliable helper. Rogue Core has Cooper, but Cooper exists inside a different kind of game. Your run still depends on upgrade timing, Expenite deposits, route discipline, and knowing when to leave.

Cooper helps with solo pressure, but it does not replace smart solo play.

<h2 id="is-rogue-core-worth-it-for-drg-players">
  Is Rogue Core Worth It for DRG Players?
</h2>

Rogue Core is worth trying if what you want is a different way to use the DRG setting.

It is not the best pick if you only want more classic DRG missions. It is a much better fit if you like the idea of:

- choosing weapons during the run,
- stacking upgrades,
- adapting to imperfect options,
- pushing through shorter, more intense floors,
- and turning failures into better next runs.

The best expectation is:

**Rogue Core is familiar enough to feel like DRG, but different enough that you need to learn it on its own terms.**

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

<h3 id="is-rogue-core-like-deep-rock-galactic">
  Is Rogue Core like Deep Rock Galactic?
</h3>

Yes, but only in some ways. It keeps the co-op caves, dwarven tone, mining pressure, and bug fights, but changes the structure into roguelite runs with weapons, upgrades, Expenite, Reclaimers, and bosses.

<h3 id="is-rogue-core-a-sequel-to-drg">
  Is Rogue Core a sequel to DRG?
</h3>

Rogue Core is better treated as a spin-off, not a direct sequel or replacement for Deep Rock Galactic.

<h3 id="is-rogue-core-dlc">
  Is Rogue Core a DLC for Deep Rock Galactic?
</h3>

No. Rogue Core is a standalone game, not DLC for Deep Rock Galactic. You do not need to own the original DRG to buy or play Rogue Core.

<h3 id="what-is-the-biggest-difference-between-rogue-core-and-drg">
  What is the biggest difference between Rogue Core and DRG?
</h3>

The biggest difference is structure. DRG is built around missions, while Rogue Core is built around runs where you gain weapons and upgrades during the dive.

<h3 id="does-rogue-core-have-the-same-classes-as-drg">
  Does Rogue Core have the same classes as DRG?
</h3>

No. Rogue Core uses Reclaimers instead of the classic DRG classes. They have their own abilities, roles, and upgrade interactions.

<h3 id="is-mining-important-in-rogue-core">
  Is mining important in Rogue Core?
</h3>

Yes, but the focus is different. Expenite is especially important because it fuels run upgrades. You mine it, deposit it, and use it to make your build stronger during the dive.

<h3 id="can-you-play-rogue-core-solo">
  Can you play Rogue Core solo?
</h3>

Yes. Rogue Core supports solo play with Cooper as your helper, but solo still requires good routing, upgrade choices, Expenite deposits, and survival decisions.

<h3 id="should-drg-players-buy-rogue-core">
  Should DRG players try Rogue Core?
</h3>

DRG players should try Rogue Core if they want a faster, run-based version of the DRG idea with roguelite upgrades and new class identities. If you only want more classic DRG missions, it may not be what you expect.

<h3 id="is-rogue-core-harder-than-deep-rock-galactic">
  Is Rogue Core harder than Deep Rock Galactic?
</h3>

It can feel harder because the tempo is faster and bad build choices matter more. DRG experience helps, but Rogue Core still asks you to learn new habits.

<h3 id="do-i-need-to-play-drg-before-rogue-core">
  Do I need to play DRG before Rogue Core?
</h3>

No. DRG experience helps you understand the tone, movement, co-op pressure, and cave chaos, but Rogue Core has its own systems and should be learned as its own game.
