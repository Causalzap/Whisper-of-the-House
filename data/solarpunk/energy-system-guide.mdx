<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
If your **Solarpunk** energy system is not working, check your **Network Display** first. In the current build covered here, a **Solar Panel gives 10 power**, a **Battery stores 95**, a **Drill uses 120**, a **Sprinkler uses 40**, and the **Advanced Dock uses 100**. That means one drill is a heavy early load, and three sprinklers can drain as much power as a drill.

Your first stable route should be: **Energy Workbench → Solar Panels → Cables → Network Display → Drill → Battery**. Keep solar panels in open sky, use batteries for night and rain, put sprinklers on a switch, and prioritize Windmill after Tier 3 if your base still loses power.

<h2 id="solarpunk-energy-system-guide">
  Solarpunk energy system guide
</h2>

The energy system in **Solarpunk** is easy to underestimate. At first, power looks simple: build a generator, add fuel, connect a machine, and move on. Then you unlock solar panels, drills, sprinklers, batteries, switches, windmills, and powered docks, and your base starts shutting things off for reasons that are not obvious.

The most common mistake is treating power as a yes-or-no system. It is not. Your grid has four separate questions:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>How much power are you generating?</li>
  <li>How much power are your machines consuming?</li>
  <li>How much energy can your batteries store?</li>
  <li>Are all powered devices connected to the right network?</li>
</ol>

That is why the **Network Display** matters so much. Without it, you are guessing. With it, you can see whether the problem is low generation, too much consumption, no stored power, or a machine sitting on the wrong cable network.

If you are still in the first hour and have not unlocked the Energy Workbench yet, read the <Link href="/solarpunk/beginners-guide">Solarpunk Beginner Guide</Link> first. If you are still trying to reach wheat, quartz, or cobalt, use the <Link href="/solarpunk/airship-guide">Airship Guide</Link>.

That does not mean you must build 12 panels immediately. It means you should not connect a drill to a tiny starter grid and expect it to run all day.

<h2 id="what-to-build-first">
  What to build first for power
</h2>

Your early energy route should not start with a huge solar field. It should start with the unlocks that let you understand the grid.

<GuideTable
caption="Best early Solarpunk energy unlock order."
columns={unlockColumns}
rows={unlockRows}
/>

The important part is this: **do not skip the Network Display**. It is tempting to build another solar panel or another drill first, but the display is what tells you what your setup is actually doing.

A clean early route looks like this:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Trade for the Energy Workbench blueprint.</li>
  <li>Build the Energy Workbench at your base.</li>
  <li>Research or craft solar panels.</li>
  <li>Make enough cables to connect your first powered area.</li>
  <li>Add a Network Display before expanding the grid.</li>
  <li>Only then add heavier machines like drills and sprinklers.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="why-power-stops">
  Why your power stops working
</h2>

Most power problems in Solarpunk are not bugs. They usually come from night, rain, overloaded machines, bad placement, or messy cable routing.

<GuideTable
caption="Common Solarpunk power problems and fixes."
columns={problemColumns}
rows={problemRows}
/>

If your setup works during the day and fails at night, your solar panels are doing exactly what solar panels do. Early on, sleeping is the easiest fix because it skips you back to daytime. Later, that is what batteries are for.

If your setup works in clear weather but drops during rain, do not only add more solar panels. You need storage, switches, and later another generation type such as wind power.

<h2 id="early-best-energy-setup">
  Best early energy setup
</h2>

Your first serious setup should be small and readable. Do not try to power every machine at once.

<GuideTable
caption="Recommended Solarpunk energy setups by progression stage."
columns={setupColumns}
rows={setupRows}
/>

For the first real base grid, aim for:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>one central cable line,</li>
  <li>solar panels in an open area,</li>
  <li>a Network Display near your crafting area,</li>
  <li>only one or two important powered machines,</li>
  <li>and enough room to add batteries later.</li>
</ul>

Do not put your first solar setup in a cramped corner. You will expand this grid later, so leave space for more panels, batteries, windmills, and switches.

<h2 id="solar-panel-placement">
  Solar panel and windmill placement rules
</h2>

Bad placement can make a power setup feel weaker than it should.

<GuideTable
caption="Solarpunk solar panel and windmill placement rules."
columns={placementColumns}
rows={placementRows}
/>

For solar panels, the safe rule is **open sky first**. Do not build them under roofs, behind tall walls, or in tight corners where your base structures can block them. Staggering panels also makes future expansion cleaner.

For windmills, plan a separate open area instead of jamming turbines directly into your crowded crafting zone. A rough 5-10 meter spacing rule gives you room to expand and keeps the energy area readable.

<h2 id="solar-panel-vs-generator">
  Solar panel vs generator
</h2>

Generators are useful early, but they are not where you want to stay. They need fuel, and fuel costs resources you could use elsewhere.

Solar panels are cleaner and easier during the day, but they have two big weaknesses:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>They stop at night.</li>
  <li>They become unreliable during poor weather.</li>
</ol>

So the real answer is not “generator or solar forever.” The answer is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Use generators as a temporary bridge.</li>
  <li>Move to solar panels as soon as the Energy Workbench allows it.</li>
  <li>Add batteries once your base depends on powered machines.</li>
  <li>Add windmills later so bad solar conditions do not cripple the whole base.</li>
</ul>

If you still need fuel, wax briquettes are much better than dumping wood into everything. Wood disappears fast once you are building, crafting, expanding, and running furnaces.

<h2 id="sprinklers-power">
  Sprinklers and farm power
</h2>

Sprinklers are great because they remove a boring daily job. They are also dangerous because they make your farm part of your power grid.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/solarpunk/solarpunk-sprinkler-eight-plots.webp"
    alt="Solarpunk sprinkler layout watering eight nearby farm plots"
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    A compact sprinkler layout saves time, but every sprinkler adds another 40 power load.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

A safe farming setup is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>keep farm plots close to the sprinkler,</li>
  <li>avoid spreading sprinklers across a huge messy field,</li>
  <li>check the Network Display after each new sprinkler,</li>
  <li>use switches when sprinklers are not needed,</li>
  <li>and add batteries before the farm depends on automation full time.</li>
</ul>

If it rains, turn sprinklers off when your grid is busy. Rain helps with watering, but weak solar and active sprinklers can drain your stored power at the same time.

<h2 id="switches-logic">
  How to stop sprinklers from wasting power
</h2>

Switches are one of the easiest ways to make a weak grid feel smarter. A switch lets you shut off one branch of your grid instead of letting every machine fight for the same power.

<GuideTable
caption="How to control power with switches in Solarpunk."
columns={switchColumns}
rows={switchRows}
/>

Start simple:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Put sprinklers on their own cable branch.</li>
  <li>Add a switch before that branch.</li>
  <li>Turn the sprinkler line off during rain or low-power periods.</li>
  <li>Keep your drill on a separate controllable line if it competes with the farm.</li>
  <li>Use sensors and logic later when you want automation instead of manual switching.</li>
</ol>

This is the part many players miss. More power is good, but **controlling demand** is just as important.

Once you find a good ore island, do not keep flying back just to hand-mine the same resource. That is exactly what drills are for.

For a basic remote mining outpost, bring:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>a drill,</li>
  <li>enough local power generation for the drill,</li>
  <li>cables,</li>
  <li>a battery if you have unlocked it,</li>

Keep the outpost on its own local network. It is easier to diagnose one drill, one battery bank, and one small power setup than a giant cable chain running back to your main base.

At first, you can visit the island and collect ore manually from the drill. Later, transport drones can turn this into a real automated supply chain.

<h2 id="windmill-priority">
  When to build windmills
</h2>

Windmills are not your first energy fix. They are your answer when your base has grown past simple solar.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image

    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

  </figcaption>
</figure>

Prioritize Windmill when:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>your farm uses multiple sprinklers,</li>
  <li>you have batteries but they keep draining,</li>
  <li>your drills compete with base machines,</li>
  <li>your Advanced Dock is part of the grid,</li>
  <li>and rain or night still interrupts your routine.</li>
</ul>

Lights, decorations, and comfort unlocks are nice, but power stability affects everything. If your base is still unstable, Windmill comes before cosmetic upgrades.

If you follow that order, Solarpunk's energy system becomes much less frustrating. You will still need to expand, but you will know why each machine stops and what to build next.


<h2 id="faq">
  Solarpunk energy system FAQ
</h2>

### Why is my Solarpunk power not working?

Most of the time, your grid is either out of sunlight, overloaded, missing stored power, or split into separate cable networks. Build a Network Display, check generation and consumption, then turn off non-essential machines.

### Why do solar panels stop at night?

Solar panels need sunlight. At night, they stop producing energy. Sleep through the night early on, then build batteries later so your machines can keep running after sunset.

### Why are my solar panels not producing enough?

Check placement first. Solar panels should be in open sky, away from roofs, tall walls, and cramped blocked corners. Staggering panels also makes expansion cleaner.

### How much power does a solar panel make?

In the current build covered here, one solar panel gives 10 power. Check your Network Display after placing panels because weather and time of day still matter.

### How much power does a drill need?

A drill uses 120 power. If one solar panel gives 10 power, a drill equals 12 panels before you count weather, night, batteries, or other machines.

### How many solar panels do I need for a drill?

For clean math, a 120-power drill needs the same output as 12 solar panels if each panel gives 10 power. In practice, use the Network Display, add batteries, and avoid running sprinklers on the same weak grid.

### How much power does a sprinkler use?

A sprinkler uses 40 power. Three sprinklers use 120 power, the same load as one drill.

### How much storage does a battery have?

One battery stores 95 energy. Two batteries store 190, and four batteries store 380.

### Should I build Battery or Animal Transport first?

If your machines already shut off, build Battery first. Animal Transport is useful, but Battery solves the power instability that affects drills, sprinklers, docks, and other powered systems.

### What does the Network Display do?

The Network Display helps you see how much power your grid is generating, consuming, and storing. Build it early so you stop guessing why machines turn off.

### How do I stop sprinklers from wasting power?

Put sprinklers on their own cable branch with a switch. Turn them off during rain or when your battery storage is low.

### How do I control which machines get power?

Use switches to separate branches of your grid. Put sprinklers, drills, or optional devices like the Advanced Dock on controllable lines so they do not all compete for power at once.

### Is there water power in Solarpunk?

Yes, Solarpunk supports water power as part of its renewable energy systems. Do not delay your early base waiting for it, though. Solar panels, batteries, switches, and later windmills are the practical route for most early and mid-game power problems.

### How do I set up a mining outpost in Solarpunk?

Bring a drill, local generation, cables, a battery if unlocked, food, water, and repair supplies. Keep the outpost on its own local grid so the drill is easier to power and troubleshoot.

### Is the Advanced Dock worth it?

Yes, but not before your base has stable power. The Advanced Dock uses 100 power, so adding it too early can make an already strained grid worse.

### When should I build windmills?

Build windmills after Tier 3 when your base is too large for simple solar and batteries. Windmills help diversify your energy sources and reduce bad-weather problems.

### Should I use one big power grid or separate networks?

Use one main grid early because it is easier to read and troubleshoot. Split into separate networks later when you know exactly what each area needs.
