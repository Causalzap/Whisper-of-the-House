<h2 id="why-blade-mammoth-is-one-of-the-first-bosses-worth-studying">
  Why Blade Mammoth is one of the first bosses worth studying
</h2>

Not every GRIME II boss needs a full standalone strategy page.

Blade Mammoth does because it feels like one of the first fights where raw aggression stops being enough. This boss asks you to read spacing, recognize layered follow-up pressure, and keep enough control that your punish windows still matter.

That is why Blade Mammoth is worth breaking down properly:

- it has enough screen control to punish lazy positioning
- it mixes obvious threat with delayed follow-up danger
- it pressures both your movement and your Force management
- its Phase 2 attacks become wide enough that old “safe” habits stop working

This is not just a large boss.  
It is a boss that starts punishing impatience in a very visible way.

<GuideCallout title="Best way to think about Blade Mammoth">
Blade Mammoth is easier once you stop trying to “win the exchange” every time it moves.

The fight becomes much more manageable when your goal is to survive the dangerous sequence cleanly, then take one short punish instead of forcing a combo.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="blade-mammoth-boss-summary">
  Blade Mammoth boss summary
</h2>

Blade Mammoth is a large pressure boss built around three main problems:

- fast space-closing attacks
- heavy commitment attacks that punish bad recovery
- a Phase 2 escalation where the attack coverage becomes much harder to ignore

The fight feels threatening because the boss does not just hit hard.  
It takes away room.

A lot of failed attempts come from the same pattern:

1. the player survives the obvious opening hit
2. the player assumes the danger is over
3. the follow-up or expanded hitbox catches them while they try to re-enter

That is the core rhythm you need to beat.

### What this fight really tests

- whether you can read commitment instead of just reacting to red flashes
- whether you can keep enough Force for meaningful punishes
- whether you can stay calm once the arena starts feeling smaller in Phase 2

<GuideCallout title="What makes Blade Mammoth harder than it first looks">
The boss does not need a huge move list to be dangerous.

A few strong attack patterns are enough if they cover space well, punish panic, and force you to respect the whole sequence instead of the first hit only.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="best-preparation-before-the-fight">
  Best preparation before the fight
</h2>

The best preparation for Blade Mammoth is not a gimmick setup.

It is going in with a clear rule for how much risk you are willing to take.

### 1. Use a weapon you can disengage from quickly
This fight is much easier if your weapon lets you take one or two hits and move immediately.

A strong heavy weapon can still work, but only if you already understand the boss well enough not to trap yourself in recovery.

### 2. Keep enough Force to make punish windows matter
Blade Mammoth is one of those fights where bad Force management makes everything worse.

If you spend too much Force on weak openings, the next real punish feels much less valuable.

### 3. Expect the charge and slam patterns to punish early movement
This boss rewards patience more than panic movement.

A lot of bad deaths happen because the player reacts to the first half of the animation instead of waiting for the actual committed threat.

### 4. Decide your punish limit before the fight starts
A simple rule is enough:

- one hit on uncertain openings
- two hits on obvious recovery
- no greed unless you already know exactly what comes next

<GuideCallout title="Simple preparation rule">
If you go into Blade Mammoth already committed to short punishes, the whole fight gets cleaner.

The boss becomes much more dangerous when you keep trying to squeeze medium-value damage out of unsafe space.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="blade-mammoths-main-attacks-and-how-to-read-them">
  Blade Mammoth’s main attacks and how to read them
</h2>

The names below are descriptive names for clarity.

You do not need official move names to beat this fight.  
You just need to recognize the patterns that actually matter.

### Red blade slam into ground wave
This is the most dangerous and most important sequence in the fight.

Blade Mammoth raises the blade arm with a strong red telegraph, slams down, and then sends a fast ground wave forward.

This is the move that traps players who think “I dodged the slam, so I am safe now.”

**Why it is dangerous:**

- the first hit makes people panic early
- the follow-up arrives quickly
- close range gives you less reaction time
- bad timing gets punished twice

**How to handle it:**

- do not react too early just because the arm is raised
- respect the whole sequence, not just the slam
- if you are close, plan around the follow-up wave immediately

### Low-stance sweep
Blade Mammoth lowers its body and prepares a wide horizontal swing.

This is the kind of move that punishes players who stand at a comfortable-looking mid-range and assume they can react late.

**How to handle it:**

- read the lowered body as a real commitment signal
- do not drift backward lazily and assume that is enough
- if you are unsure, choose survival first and punish later

### Double-blade slam
This is one of the clearest heavy-commitment attacks in the fight.

Blade Mammoth brings both front limbs together and slams down hard into the area in front of it.

This is dangerous if you freeze, but it is also one of the cleaner attacks to punish if you survive it properly.

**How to handle it:**

- do not panic backward by default
- move with intention toward the safe side, not just away from the boss
- wait for the full threat to resolve before stepping back in

### Burst roar / transition pressure
At transition points, Blade Mammoth can force space with a large burst-like effect.

This matters because it resets your comfort and stops you from treating the fight like a simple repetition loop.

**How to handle it:**

- respect the reset
- do not treat the visual break as a free punish moment
- use it to re-center mentally instead of rushing forward

<h2 id="how-to-handle-phase-2-without-panicking">
  How to handle Phase 2 without panicking
</h2>

Phase 2 is where Blade Mammoth starts feeling much less forgiving.

The biggest difference is not just that the boss becomes louder or flashier.  
It is that attacks which were previously manageable from a certain distance start reaching farther and controlling more space.

### The real Phase 2 change is coverage
The widened attack trails and longer blade reach make old “safe” reactions worse than they were in Phase 1.

That means a step-back answer that worked earlier may stop working later.

### The spin / extended sweep pressure is the real panic check
This is where many attempts collapse.

If you overreact, you give up too much space and lose control.  
If you underreact, you get clipped by more of the attack than you expected.

### Ground danger matters more once the pace rises
Once the floor itself starts feeling less comfortable, panic movement gets punished much harder.

You are no longer just avoiding the boss.  
You are also managing where you can afford to stand after the boss moves.

### Keep the same punish discipline from Phase 1
One of the worst mistakes in Phase 2 is changing your whole plan and suddenly trying to become more aggressive.

That is usually backwards.

The correct adjustment is normally:

- be more selective
- respect larger hitboxes
- only take the punishes you can still exit cleanly

Your real job is to stay calm through the ugly sequence, then punish when the fight becomes stable again.

<h2 id="best-punish-windows-and-most-common-mistakes">
  Best punish windows and most common mistakes
</h2>

Blade Mammoth is beatable once you stop confusing “the boss has moved” with “the boss is punishable.”

Those are not the same thing.

### Best punish windows

#### After the full double-blade slam recovery
This is one of the cleanest real punish moments in the fight.

The key is to wait for the whole threat to end, not the first impact only.

#### After a fully committed forward sequence
If Blade Mammoth clearly spends itself on a charge-style action and leaves extension in front of you, that can be a strong short punish window.

#### After the boss overextends into a stable reset
Some punishes are less about one move and more about the boss finally ending a full sequence in a position you can trust.

Those are often better than trying to force damage off a flashy but uncertain opening.

### Most common mistakes

#### Dodging the first hit but forgetting the follow-up
This is the biggest one.  
The slam is not the whole problem. The ground wave and extended pressure are what finish people off.

#### Emptying Force on bad openings
This makes later punishes weaker and turns good reads into low-value damage.

#### Assuming dash-through solves everything
Sometimes it does.  
But Blade Mammoth covers too much space to reduce the whole fight to one default answer.

#### Panicking in Phase 2
Once the hitboxes expand and the floor feels less safe, panic movement creates more deaths than the boss’s raw damage does.

<GuideCallout title="Best punish rule">
If you are not sure whether the punish is real, assume it is not.

Blade Mammoth becomes much easier when you stop taking medium-value risks just because the animation looks tempting.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="faq">FAQ</h2>

### Is Blade Mammoth one of the harder GRIME II bosses?

It is one of the first bosses that feels substantial enough to reward a dedicated strategy page. The difficulty comes from how it controls space and punishes impatience, not just how much damage it deals.

### What is the biggest mistake against Blade Mammoth?

The most common mistake is treating the first successful dodge as the end of the danger. This fight often kills players with the follow-up, not the obvious opener.

### Why does Phase 2 feel so much worse?

Because the attack coverage expands and your old spacing becomes less reliable. The fight starts punishing habits that felt safe in Phase 1.

### Should you always counter Blade Mammoth?

No. You should only counter the parts of the fight you are actually reading clearly. Uncertain counter attempts are one of the fastest ways to lose control of the fight.
