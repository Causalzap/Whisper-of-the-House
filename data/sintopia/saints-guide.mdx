**Saints** are one of the strangest traffic types in **Sintopia**.

At first glance they look harmless, because they come from very low-Sin Souls. In practice, though, they are one of the most awkward things you can send through Hell because they do **not** behave like normal profitable traffic.

This guide focuses on one question:

> When a Soul becomes a Saint, should you send it away immediately, or should you deliberately break its sainthood?

That is the real Saint-management decision in **Sintopia**.

<h2 id="quick-answer">
  <a href="#quick-answer">Quick Answer</a>
</h2>

If you want the short version:

- **Saints do not give Hearos**
- they also do not pay out normal Hell-building currencies while they remain Saints
- they do not behave like ordinary Souls in your normal punishment economy
- they stay bad for your economy until you either **route them out** or **break their sainthood**
- **Holy Patience** is the meter that keeps them Saints
- the best ways to reduce Holy Patience are **Waiting Lines**, **Waiting Booth support**, failure exposure, and later tools like **Time Dilatator**
- **Attrition Warfare** is one of the best Saint-support upgrades once Saints become a recurring problem

So the core Saint strategy is simple:

- if Saints are rare, get them out of your main lane
- if Saints are common, build a way to break them on purpose

<h2 id="what-saints-do-in-sintopia">
  <a href="#what-saints-do-in-sintopia">What Saints Do In Sintopia</a>
</h2>

A Soul resurrected with a very low Sinometer can become a **Saint**.

That sounds good at first, but Saints are not just “extra clean Souls.” They are a special state with different rules.

While a Soul remains a Saint:

- it does **not** give **Hearos**
- it does **not** generate normal Hell-building currencies
- it does **not** function like an ordinary punishable Soul
- it becomes a traffic problem instead of a money-making one

That is why Saints are so deceptive.  
They look clean, but they are **economically dead** until something changes.

<h2 id="why-saints-are-bad-for-your-hell-economy">
  <a href="#why-saints-are-bad-for-your-hell-economy">Why Saints Are Bad For Your Hell Economy</a>
</h2>

Saints are bad for your Hell economy for a simple reason:

> they consume route capacity without acting like profitable Souls

That hurts in three ways.

### 1. They waste your best lanes
If a Saint is sitting on a route that should be processing regular Souls, that route is losing value.

### 2. They delay better traffic
Every Saint standing in line is space that could have gone to a Soul that might produce Purgadollars, Faithcoins, or Hearos.

### 3. They make your system look cleaner than it really is
A Saint-heavy line can feel “safe” because the traffic is low-Sin, but your economy still suffers because the line is not paying out.

That is why Saints are not a bonus.  
They are a **routing decision**.

<h2 id="how-holy-patience-works">
  <a href="#how-holy-patience-works">How Holy Patience Works</a>
</h2>

**Holy Patience** is the meter that keeps a Saint in its Saint state.

When **Holy Patience** reaches **zero**, the Saint loses sainthood and becomes a normal Soul again.

That is the whole trick behind Saint handling.

A Saint stops being dead lane-weight only when you make Holy Patience collapse.

The main ways to reduce Holy Patience are:

- waiting in Waiting Lines
- witnessing staff mistakes and critical failures
- later support such as Time Dilatator
- support upgrades like Attrition Warfare

So when you ask, “How do I deal with Saints?”  
the real answer is:

> either avoid them, or attack their Holy Patience on purpose

If you are seeing them often, build a dedicated answer.

<h2 id="waiting-lines-are-the-foundation-of-saint-handling">
  <a href="#waiting-lines-are-the-foundation-of-saint-handling">Waiting Lines Are The Foundation Of Saint Handling</a>
</h2>

A **Waiting Line** is the foundation of almost every good Saint strategy.

Why?

Because it solves two problems at once:

### It protects Souls from Infernal Corruption
Souls in a proper waiting line are not gaining random extra Sin from road exposure.

### It slowly reduces Holy Patience
A Saint kept waiting long enough will eventually become easier to break.

That does not mean Waiting Lines are fast.  
They are actually the **slow** Saint solution.

But they are still the safest baseline tool because they work passively and they fit into normal Hell routing much more easily than some late-game alternatives.

A **Waiting Booth** is what makes a Saint lane feel practical instead of theoretical.

A long line by itself can work, but it is slow.  
Once you add a Waiting Booth, the line becomes much more useful because you are no longer just stalling traffic — you are processing it deliberately.

That is why the best Saint route usually looks like this:

1. separate Saints from your profitable lane
2. send them into a dedicated queue
3. support that queue with a Waiting Booth
4. let Holy Patience drop until they break

This is also why Saints deserve their own route instead of sharing your main Hearos lane.

<h2 id="when-to-route-saints-out-immediately">
  <a href="#when-to-route-saints-out-immediately">When To Route Saints Out Immediately</a>
</h2>

Sometimes the best Saint strategy is not to “solve” Saints at all.

You should usually route Saints out immediately when:

- your Hell is still small
- your line is already crowded
- your current mission is about survival, throughput, or speed
- you do not yet have a decent waiting setup
- you cannot afford to spend your best route space on non-paying traffic

This is especially common in the early game.

A lot of players overcomplicate Saints too early.  
If your system is not stable yet, do not build a whole Saint economy around one or two annoying Souls.

Just get them out of the way.

<h2 id="when-to-break-sainthood-on-purpose">
  <a href="#when-to-break-sainthood-on-purpose">When To Break Sainthood On Purpose</a>
</h2>

Breaking sainthood becomes worth it once Saints stop being rare.

You should usually build a real Saint lane when:

- Saints are appearing often enough to matter
- your main routing is already stable
- you have Waiting Lines and a Waiting Booth available
- your Hell can afford to dedicate space to a low-speed process
- you want long-run efficiency instead of short-term simplicity

This is where Saints stop being an annoyance and start becoming an optimization problem.

Once you can reliably break them, Saints become much less dangerous because they rejoin the normal economy instead of permanently clogging it.

Once unlocked, **Time Dilatator** becomes the cleanest late-game Saint answer. It can strip away the need for long queues as your main solution and is the most efficient way to finish off Holy Patience in a single pass.

- you are in Sandbox or a long mission where system efficiency matters more than quick patch fixes

If Saints are rare, Attrition Warfare is not urgent.  
If Saints are common, it becomes one of the most sensible support buys in the tree.

<h2 id="saints-vs-hearos-what-actually-matters">
  <a href="#saints-vs-hearos-what-actually-matters">Saints Vs Hearos: What Actually Matters</a>
</h2>

A lot of players assume Saints should be good for Hearos because they are low-Sin Souls.

That is the trap.

Saints do **not** pay out Hearos while they remain Saints. So from a Hearos perspective, they are not “clean wins.” They are stalled value.

That means your real Hearos question is not:

> “How many Saints do I have?”

It is:

> “How much route capacity are my Saints wasting right now?”

If the answer is “a lot,” your Hearos economy is weaker than it looks.

That is why Saint handling connects so closely to your other two big systems:

- your **Hell layout**
- your **Hearos spending priorities**

<h2 id="best-saint-handling-in-campaign-vs-sandbox">
  <a href="#best-saint-handling-in-campaign-vs-sandbox">Best Saint Handling In Campaign Vs Sandbox</a>
</h2>

### Campaign
Campaign is objective-driven, so Saint handling should stay practical.

That usually means:
- route Saints out quickly if they are not central to the mission
- only build a Saint lane once the mission economy can support it
- avoid overbuilding for rare edge cases

### Sandbox
Sandbox rewards long-term control.

That usually means:
- build a dedicated Saint lane earlier
- invest in Waiting Booth support sooner
- value Attrition Warfare more highly
- optimize for stable conversion instead of short-term survival

Campaign asks:
“What helps me finish this scenario?”

Sandbox asks:
“What makes my whole Hell more efficient over time?”

<h2 id="faq">
  <a href="#faq">FAQ</a>
</h2>

### Are Saints good in Sintopia?

Not really. Saints look clean, but they are economically bad while they remain Saints because they do not generate Hearos or normal Hell-building currencies.

### How do you break Saints in Sintopia?

You break Saints by reducing Holy Patience to zero. The most practical ways are long Waiting Lines, Waiting Booth support, witnessing staff mistakes and critical failures, and later Saint-support tools.
### Do Saints give Hearos?

No. A Saint does not give Hearos while it remains a Saint.

### What is Holy Patience?

Holy Patience is the meter that keeps a Soul in its Saint state. When it reaches zero, the Saint loses sainthood and becomes a normal Soul again.

### Is Attrition Warfare worth taking?

Yes, once Saints are common enough to be a real routing problem. It is much less important if Saints only appear occasionally.

### Should I build a Saint lane early?

Usually no. Early on, it is often better to route Saints away quickly unless they are already becoming a real bottleneck.

### Is Time Dilatator the best late-game Saint answer?

In most cases, yes. Once you unlock it, it becomes the most efficient way to finish off Holy Patience without relying on very long queues.

<h2 id="final-verdict">
  <a href="#final-verdict">Final Verdict</a>
</h2>

The best Saint strategy in **Sintopia** is not “always keep them” or “always get rid of them.”

It is:

- remove them quickly when they are rare
- break them deliberately when they are common
- use Waiting Lines as your foundation
- use Waiting Booths to make the line practical
- use Attrition Warfare when Saints become a real economic drag
- move to Time Dilatator once your run is advanced enough

That is what turns Saints from a confusing gimmick into a manageable system.

They are not a bonus.  
They are not a prize.  
They are a **routing problem** — until you break them.
