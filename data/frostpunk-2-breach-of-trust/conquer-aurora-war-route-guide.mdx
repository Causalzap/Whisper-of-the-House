<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
To **conquer Aurora** in **Frostpunk 2: Breach of Trust**, choose the force route, build **Barracks**, grow your platoons through soldier hiring or conscription, and secure **Heavy Weaponry** as a real military resource before raids and final attacks.

The war route is not just “train troops and click attack.” Hiring soldiers costs heat stamps and support capacity, while conscription can save money but requires a difficult law and can hurt trust. Heavy Weaponry also has to be gathered or produced before some raids and conquest actions. I attack only when my army advantage is strong, my shelters and food are stable, and I can afford the occupation that begins after Aurora falls.

<h2 id="conquer-aurora-war-route">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust conquer Aurora war route guide
</h2>

The war route in Breach of Trust is tempting because Aurora is the reason New Edinburgh is starving. If the colony will not cooperate, taking it back sounds direct.

That is exactly why the route is dangerous.

War solves one political problem by creating several others. I still have to keep New Edinburgh fed. I still need to lower steam output before tremors hit. I still need Emergency Shelter Hubs. I still need trust before major votes. On top of all that, I now need soldiers, Heavy Weaponry, attack timing, and post-war occupation control.

My route is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Choose the force route at the opening council decision.</li>
  <li>Stabilize New Edinburgh enough that war spending does not collapse food, heat, or shelters.</li>
  <li>Research and build Barracks.</li>
  <li>Grow platoons through hiring or conscription, depending on money and political support.</li>
  <li>Secure Heavy Weaponry as a resource before raids and major attacks.</li>
  <li>Weaken Aurora through outposts only when the city can afford the cost.</li>
  <li>Attack when the army advantage is strong, not simply when the button appears.</li>
  <li>After conquest, treat Aurora as a broken second city that must be rebuilt and governed.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="opening-choice">
  Choose war only if you can afford the pressure
</h2>

The first force decision improves my position with the faction pushing for a hard line against Aurora, but I do not treat that approval as free.

The war route spends four resources at once:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li><strong>food</strong>, because New Edinburgh still needs to survive while the army is forming,</li>
  <li><strong>workers</strong>, because Barracks, industry, housing, and repairs all compete for people,</li>
  <li><strong>heat stamps</strong>, because hiring soldiers and raising the war economy is expensive,</li>
  <li><strong>trust</strong>, because conscription, occupation, and harsh post-war choices can all create political debt.</li>
</ul>

This is the real question behind the route:

<strong>Can I win the war without losing the city before the victory matters?</strong>

Before I commit too hard, I check the next tremor timer, food stockpile, prefab reserve, and whether Emergency Shelter Hub is on schedule. Barracks can wait a few weeks if uncovered housing is about to get smashed.

<h2 id="barracks-soldiers">
  Barracks, mercenaries, and conscription
</h2>

Barracks starts the military route, but soldiers are not just a simple “train civilians” button.

In my run, I could spend heat stamps and support capacity to hire soldiers quickly. That was useful when I needed platoons now, but it hurt the economy because heat stamps were already competing with shelters, laws, repairs, and city expansion.

There was also a stronger long-term option: **conscription**. The catch was political. The conscription law sat under governance and needed strong support. It could give me platoons without the same heat stamp pressure, but it came with trust cost and vote difficulty.

My rule is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>hire soldiers when I need a fast push and can afford the heat stamps,</li>
  <li>use conscription when I have enough political support and need to preserve money,</li>
  <li>avoid draining workers if shelters, food, or prefab production are already unstable,</li>
  <li>and never build the army so fast that the city loses the ability to repair after tremors.</li>
</ul>

This is what makes the war route feel brutal. Every soldier choice is also an economic and political choice.

<h2 id="heavy-weaponry">
  Heavy Weaponry is a resource, not just a tech unlock
</h2>

Heavy Weaponry is easy to misunderstand.

It is not only a research checkbox that makes attacks stronger. I treat it like a military resource that has to be gathered, produced, and spent. Some outpost actions and war actions need Heavy Weaponry before I can use them.

In one run, taking an outpost with Heavy Weaponry reduced Aurora's strength by **10**, which improved my position. That kind of raid can be worth it, but it is not automatically correct.

I spend Heavy Weaponry when it does at least one of these things:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>reduces Aurora's strength before a major attack,</li>
  <li>secures food, coal, goods, or materials I urgently need,</li>
  <li>unlocks a route I cannot pressure with platoons alone,</li>
  <li>or prepares the final conquest window.</li>
</ul>

I do not spend it just because a raid is available. If the reward is small and the city is already short on prefabs, food, or workers, I hold the weaponry for the next decisive push.

<h2 id="attack-timing">
  Decide when to attack and when to wait
</h2>

The safest attack is not the earliest attack. It is the attack that New Edinburgh can survive afterward.

I wait for a strong army advantage before attacking Aurora. The route can require multiple assaults, and every bad attack threatens two goals at once: the conquest timer and the achievement route. If I am chasing **Flawless Victory**, I cannot afford to lose a battle at all.

Before I attack, I want most of these answers to be good:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Do I have a clear army advantage?</li>
  <li>Do I have enough platoons left for follow-up actions?</li>
  <li>Do I have enough Heavy Weaponry for the attack or next raid?</li>
  <li>Is the next tremor covered by shelters and prefab reserves?</li>
  <li>Can New Edinburgh survive several more weeks of food pressure?</li>
  <li>Do I have a plan for Aurora heat, housing, and transfer capacity after victory?</li>
</ul>

I wait if two or more of these are bad:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>the next tremor is close and housing is uncovered,</li>
  <li>New Edinburgh is nearly out of food,</li>
  <li>the attack would leave too few platoons for the next push,</li>
  <li>Heavy Weaponry is too low for the follow-up,</li>
  <li>I have no prefab reserve for damage repairs,</li>
  <li>or Act 2 would begin before I have any plan for Aurora housing and food transfer.</li>
</ul>

This is also where the war route's emotional pressure matters. I may want to punish Aurora, but the city does not care about that feeling. The city cares whether people are fed, sheltered, and politically calm enough to survive the victory.

<h2 id="after-conquest">
  After Aurora falls, the real war route begins
</h2>

The biggest trap is thinking “Aurora conquered” means “food solved.”

It means I now control a broken, hostile second city. Aurora needs heat, housing, workers, Fishery Hubs, resource transfer, and political repair. New Edinburgh still needs food. Both cities can fail each other if transfer capacity is weak.

My first Act 2 checks are:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>turn on or stabilize Aurora's heat source,</li>
  <li>repair or build Fishery Hubs,</li>
  <li>expand housing before people die from exposure,</li>
  <li>start moving food back to New Edinburgh,</li>
  <li>prepare Skyways or Freight Docks if transfer capacity is too low,</li>
  <li>and build security before resistance shuts down key districts.</li>
</ul>

If you need the food route itself, use the <Link href="/frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust/aurora-negotiation-fishery-hub-guide">Aurora Negotiation and Fishery Hub Guide</Link>. The war route still needs the same food math; it just adds occupation pressure on top.

<h2 id="resistance">
  Resistance is the occupation tax
</h2>

After conquest, Aurora is not loyal just because the front line moved.

Resistance can shut down districts, interrupt food production, and force me to spend guards, heat stamps, and attention in places I wanted to ignore. If resistance hits a Fishery Hub, heat support, housing district, or transfer route at the wrong moment, New Edinburgh can start starving again.

I handle resistance in layers:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>build guards and patrol capacity before shutdowns stack up,</li>
  <li>protect food, heat, housing, and transfer districts first,</li>
  <li>avoid using Heavy Weaponry casually if it makes occupation harder,</li>
  <li>and choose post-war punishments only when the reward is worth the added resistance or trust loss.</li>
</ul>

One event can offer harsh punishment, war reparations, or a softer reconciliation path. The hard option may give heat stamps when I desperately need them, but it can also raise resistance. The softer option can reduce resistance, but it may cost trust or delay economic recovery.

That is why I do not treat post-war events as roleplay only. They are part of the occupation economy.

<h2 id="post-war-laws">
  Auroran Suffrage and Generator Relocation can block the recovery
</h2>

In my Act 2 route, the major post-war political blockers were **Auroran Suffrage** and **Generator Relocation**. I use those names because they appeared in my run; exact wording can change with route, localization, or patches.

These are not small convenience votes. They sit at the intersection of food, trust, Aurora relations, and long-term survival. If people are hungry, recent deaths are high, or factions are angry, the votes can be nearly impossible.

My rule is to fix the visible pressure before forcing the law:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>reduce hunger first,</li>
  <li>repair housing and food infrastructure after tremors,</li>
  <li>stabilize Aurora enough that people stop dying there,</li>
  <li>use faction promises only when I can actually keep them,</li>
  <li>and push the law when the council is angry but not hopeless.</li>
</ul>

This is another reason war is costly. The army opens Aurora. The council decides whether the post-war city can actually function.

<h2 id="achievements">
  War route achievements to keep in mind
</h2>

If I am achievement-hunting, I watch two war-route achievements while playing this page.

**Flawless Victory** is the clean-war challenge: conquer Aurora without losing a battle. That changes attack timing. I do not click a risky attack just to move the story forward. I wait for advantage, enough platoons, enough Heavy Weaponry, and enough city stability to support the next push.

**We're So Back** points toward regaining access to Aurora. That matters because the route can temporarily feel like access is solved, then become blocked again by resistance, politics, or post-war instability. If I lose practical access to Aurora's food or governance path, I check resistance, laws, transfer capacity, and housing before assuming the conquest route is broken.

I do not think these need a separate achievements page yet. They fit naturally inside the war guide because the actual solution is better route control, not a hidden collectible step.

<h2 id="faq">
  Conquer Aurora war route FAQ
</h2>

### How do I start the war route?

Choose the force route at the opening council decision, then stabilize New Edinburgh enough to afford Barracks, soldiers, Heavy Weaponry, and later occupation control.

### Are platoons trained or conscripted?

Both can matter. In my run, I could hire soldiers with heat stamps and support capacity, while conscription required a governance law and political support. Hiring is faster but expensive; conscription can save money but costs trust and votes.

### What is Heavy Weaponry?

Heavy Weaponry is a military resource, not just a passive upgrade. You need it for some outpost raids, pressure actions, and later combat steps.

### Should I hire mercenaries or pass conscription?

Hire when I need speed and have heat stamps. Use conscription when I have enough political support and need to preserve money. Do not force conscription if the vote will damage trust at the wrong time.

### When should I attack Aurora?

Attack when the army advantage is strong, food is stable for the next few weeks, shelters are ready for tremors, and I have enough platoons and Heavy Weaponry for follow-up actions.

### What is Flawless Victory?

Flawless Victory is tied to conquering Aurora without losing a battle. If I am chasing it, I wait for strong advantage instead of taking risky attacks.

### Does conquering Aurora solve food?

No. It gives control of Aurora, but I still need Fishery Hubs, heat, housing, workers, and transfer capacity to feed New Edinburgh.

### What is We're So Back?

We're So Back is tied to regaining access to Aurora. In practice, I watch for the route states where Aurora access, food transfer, or post-war control becomes blocked by resistance or political instability.

### How do I reduce resistance after conquest?

Use guards, patrols, stable food, housing, and reconciliation laws. Protect food, heat, housing, and transfer districts first.

### What happens if I fail the Auroran Suffrage vote?

Failing the Auroran Suffrage vote slows down the post-war recovery because Aurora remains harder to integrate politically. I treat it as a timing problem: stabilize food, housing, heat, and resistance first, then bring the law back when trust and council support are stronger.

### Is the war route harder than negotiation?

For a first clear, yes. War adds soldier supply, Heavy Weaponry, attack timing, resistance, and occupation laws on top of the normal food, tremor, and evacuation problems.
