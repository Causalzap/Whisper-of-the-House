In Act 2, turn Aurora into a real food base with **Fishery Hubs** and transfer capacity. <h2 id="walkthrough">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust walkthrough
</h2>

Breach of Trust is different from a normal Frostpunk 2 start because the city can fail politically before it looks dead economically.

New Edinburgh begins hungry, damaged, and politically unstable. Aurora has cut off the food supply. The mountain is already building seismic pressure. The council does not simply hand me power and disappear. I have to keep enough approval to survive the next **Vote of Trust**, while also keeping people fed, warm, sheltered, and alive.

The safest way to read the scenario is this:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>The city needs food from Aurora.</li>
  <li>The city needs heat, but too much steam output makes tremors worse.</li>
  <li>The council needs approval, not just efficiency.</li>
  <li>Every faction promise is a timer.</li>
  <li>Every tremor can create a prefab, housing, and trust crisis.</li>
  <li>Act 3 stops being city-building and becomes evacuation logistics.</li>
</ol>

That is why I do not start by building randomly. I pause, read the political promises, check the food timer, and decide whether the first run will be negotiation or war.

<h2 id="act-1-pause">
  Act 1: pause before you build
</h2>

After the council approves me as First Citizen, I pause before unpausing the city for real.

The opening pressure comes from four directions at once:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>New Edinburgh has food pressure immediately.</li>
  <li>Several districts are damaged or inefficient.</li>
  <li>Steam output is pushing seismic stress upward.</li>
  <li>The first political promise already points me toward negotiation or war.</li>
</ul>

The worst opening mistake is treating this like a normal build order. If I rush whatever looks useful without checking the timers, I can easily end up with Barracks but no shelters, food but no approval, or heat that accelerates the next tremor.

My first check is always the same:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>How many weeks of food are left?</li>
  <li>How high is steam output?</li>
  <li>How close is the next tremor?</li>
  <li>Which damaged districts are worth repairing?</li>
  <li>How many prefabs can I spend without losing repair safety?</li>
  <li>Which faction did I just please, and what will they expect next?</li>
</ul>

If a damaged district has no useful deposit left, I consider demolishing it instead of repairing it. If a district is warm beyond what it needs, I lower heat before I build new systems.

<h2 id="politics-vote-of-trust">
  Vote of Trust, factions, and heat politics
</h2>

The key system in Breach of Trust is approval.

Officially, Breach of Trust adds five new communities and factions. In my run, the opening council shows three early route-facing groups: **Brokers** push negotiation with Aurora, **Four-Fingers** push force, and **Founders** sit closer to a cautious swing position. I use these names for the decisions I saw in-game, not as a complete official list of every community that can appear later.

What matters for the walkthrough is the role each group plays:

caption="Political pressure points in Frostpunk 2: Breach of Trust."

rows={politicalRows}
/>

Council timing matters because a bad law window can trap me. In Frostpunk 2, the council rhythm is usually built around roughly **10-week** voting windows. If I spend that window on a low-impact law, then suddenly need food, shelters, workers, or a major governance change, I may not be able to fix the problem immediately.

That changes how I use promises.

A promise is good when it lines up with something I already need. If I already plan to research Barracks, promising military research to a forceful faction can make sense. If I already need a food law, housing law, or worker law, I can turn that into political support. But I do not promise a law just because it wins today’s vote if it damages the next 30 to 50 weeks of the run.

Heat also becomes political. Warm districts help trust, and trust helps votes. But too much steam output brings tremors closer. So I treat heat like a limited political resource: useful, but dangerous if I buy approval by destabilizing the ground.

<h2 id="tremor-vs-volcanic-night">
  Tremor and Volcanic Night are not the same problem
</h2>

Breach of Trust uses two different environmental threats.

**Tremors** are recurring, predictable pressure checks. I can see the timer, reduce steam output, build Emergency Shelter Hubs, and keep prefabs ready for repairs. Tremors break districts and can injure or kill people, but they are part of the ongoing Act 1 and Act 2 management loop.

**Volcanic Night** is a phase change. It happens later, after the volcano erupts and ash darkens the region. At that point, squalor rises, trust becomes harder to hold, crime can increase, and the route turns into evacuation logistics. New Edinburgh is no longer a city I am trying to perfect. It is a city I am trying to empty.

This distinction changes what I build.

For tremors, I build prevention and repair.
For Volcanic Night, I build evacuation capacity and Aurora-side survival.

This is the core tension of Act 1: I need enough warmth for approval and safety, but not so much steam that the ground punishes me before shelters are ready.

<h2 id="emergency-shelters">
  Research Emergency Shelter Hub early
</h2>

Emergency Shelter Hub is one of the safest early technologies.

If I am on the negotiation route, I usually research it before getting fancy with the economy. If I am on the war route, I still have to be careful. Barracks helps against Aurora, but it does not protect housing from seismic damage.

The hub is expensive enough that I plan around it. The build screen can show **50 workforce**, **40 materials demand**, and a construction cost of **50 heatstamps**, **40 materials**, and **100 prefabs**. I recheck these numbers in the build screen before publishing patch-sensitive updates, because costs can change with difficulty and balance.

My shelter priority is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Housing districts first.</li>
  <li>Prefab or industrial production second.</li>
  <li>Research districts if the next tech is urgent.</li>
  <li>Food and transfer infrastructure if they are exposed.</li>
</ol>

Covered districts can still be damaged, so I keep a prefab reserve for repairs after the hit.

<h2 id="aurora-choice">
  Choose negotiation or war based on how much pressure you want
</h2>

The first Aurora decision is not only a story choice. It changes the shape of Act 1.

### If I negotiate with Aurora

Negotiation is the cleaner first clear. I trade resources, manage defiance, deliver promised shipments, and later build a food base around Aurora's fishing grounds.

The main risk is timing. Talks take time, Aurora can counteroffer, and food only helps after I send the promised resources. I do not wait until New Edinburgh is already starving before opening talks.

A strong early trade can be coal for food, because New Edinburgh starts with coal and Aurora values it. If a deal asks for **40,000 coal** for food, I only accept when I know I can deliver it and still keep the city warm enough.

For delivery buttons, territory deals, Fishery Hubs, and transfer sliders, use the <Link href="/frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust/aurora-negotiation-fishery-hub-guide/">Aurora Negotiation and Fishery Hub Guide</Link>.

### If I reclaim Aurora by force

War is playable, but it is not easier.

I need Barracks, platoons, attack timing, and sometimes Heavy Weaponry. I also still need food during the buildup. After Aurora falls, the city is not a working prize. It is a damaged, angry second city that needs heat, housing, food production, transfer capacity, and resistance control.

For the full military route, use the <Link href="/frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust/conquer-aurora-war-route-guide/">Conquer Aurora War Route Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="act-2-aurora">
  Act 2: Aurora is a food base, not an instant solution
</h2>

Act 2 is where the walkthrough changes from emergency survival into two-city logistics.

Whether I negotiated access or conquered Aurora, the same food truth appears: Aurora can feed New Edinburgh, but only if I build the route correctly.

The key building is the **Fishery Hub**. It is not a normal Food District. It needs fishing-ground placement, heat, workers, materials, and enough space after frostbreaking.

My Act 2 order is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Stabilize Aurora's heat source.</li>
  <li>Build or repair the first Fishery Hubs.</li>
  <li>Expand housing before moving too many people.</li>
  <li>Send food back to New Edinburgh.</li>
  <li>Improve transfer capacity with Skyways or similar infrastructure when food cannot move fast enough.</li>
  <li>If Aurora was conquered, add guards and resistance control before shutdowns hit the food route.</li>
</ol>

The important distinction is production versus delivery. If Aurora produces food but New Edinburgh still starves, I do not build randomly. I check the transfer route first.

<h2 id="act-3-volcanic-night">
  Act 3: Volcanic Night turns the city into an evacuation machine
</h2>

When Volcanic Night begins, I stop improving New Edinburgh like it has a future.

The old city is doomed. Ash pressure increases squalor, trust becomes harder to hold, crime can rise, and the main work becomes evacuation speed, prefab production, food, coal, and Aurora housing.

The first Act 3 priority is **Evacuation Hub** research. I also start raising prefab production because the evacuation loop eats prefabs repeatedly.

If I build four Evacuation Hubs, one full wave can move **4,000 people**, but it also needs **20,000 food**, **20,000 coal**, and **2,000 prefabs**. That is why I do not wait until the final warning to build the evacuation economy.

At this point, I start dismantling buildings that no longer matter. I do not spend resources on comfort if those resources could load an airship, build housing in Aurora, or keep the final workforce alive.

<h2 id="generator-core">
  Time the Generator Core, do not click it immediately
</h2>

The Generator Core objective is the final timing trap.

The game warns that extracting the core releases underground pressure and pushes the volcano toward full eruption. Anyone left in New Edinburgh shortly after extraction is in danger.

If core extraction takes **30 weeks**, I compare that timer against:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>how many people are still in New Edinburgh,</li>
  <li>how many airships are loaded or almost loaded,</li>
  <li>whether enough workers remain to load the next cycle,</li>
  <li>whether Aurora can house the next arrivals,</li>
  <li>and how close the eruption timer is.</li>
</ul>

I do not start the core while airships are idle or resources are missing. If the timer is tight, I stop all non-evacuation spending and focus only on launches.

<h2 id="faq">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust walkthrough FAQ
</h2>

### How many factions are there in Breach of Trust?

Breach of Trust adds five new communities and factions overall. In the opening route I played, the important early council groups were Brokers, Four-Fingers, and Founders. I treat those as the route-facing groups for the opening decision, while later Aurora-side groups can appear as the scenario develops.

### What is the main mechanic in Breach of Trust?

The main mechanic is the link between survival and approval. Food, heat, trust, faction promises, Vote of Trust pressure, tremors, and Aurora decisions all affect whether the city accepts your rule.

### What should I do first after becoming First Citizen?

Pause, check food, lower unnecessary steam output, inspect damaged districts, and decide whether your first route is negotiation or war. Do not spend prefabs before you know the tremor and shelter situation.

### How often can I pass laws?

Frostpunk 2 council timing is built around roughly 10-week law windows. In Breach of Trust, that matters because a badly timed vote can leave you unable to fix food, shelters, workers, or governance pressure right when a Vote of Trust is approaching.

### Is Vote of Trust important?

Yes. You can lose politically even if your economy looks workable. Keep approval stable through food, warmth, fulfilled promises, and careful law timing.

### Should I use Guided Voting?

Use it only when the law matters for survival or route progress. Guided Voting can help pass a law, but the political cost is not worth it for low-impact convenience choices.

### Should I negotiate or reclaim Aurora on the first run?

Negotiation is safer for a first clear. War is playable, but it adds Barracks, platoons, conquest timing, resistance, and occupation rebuilding.

### What is the difference between Tremors and Volcanic Night?

Tremors are recurring damage events that you manage with lower steam output, shelter coverage, and repairs. Volcanic Night is the later eruption phase where ash, squalor, crime, evacuation, and Aurora housing become the main problem.

### Why does steam output matter?

Steam output increases seismic pressure. Lowering it toward the 200 target buys time before tremors hit, but you still need enough heat to keep people stable.

### Why is food still a problem after reaching Aurora?

Aurora needs Fishery Hubs, heat, workers, and transfer capacity. One Fishery Hub is not enough, and food must still move back to New Edinburgh.

### How many Evacuation Hubs should I build?

More than one. Each airship saves only 1,000 people and needs 5,000 food, 5,000 coal, and 500 prefabs.

### When should I extract the Generator Core?

Extract it after the evacuation system is already moving and the 30-week core timer lines up with the eruption and airship cycles.

### Can I use this walkthrough for Pure Dead Brilliant?

Use it to learn the route first. For Pure Dead Brilliant, replay with stricter shelter coverage, no starvation, no crime deaths, stable Aurora housing, and cleaner final evacuation timing.
