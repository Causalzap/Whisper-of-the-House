<h2 id="frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust-guide">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust guide
</h2>

Breach of Trust looks like a food scenario at first, but it quickly becomes a political and route-management scenario.

Aurora stops feeding New Edinburgh. The city starts with damaged districts, political pressure, food shortages, and a new seismic problem tied to steam output. If I only chase food, tremors punish me. If I only prepare for tremors, hunger punishes me. If I ignore the council, the **Vote of Trust** can punish me before the economy is fully repaired.

My first goal is not to “win” Aurora immediately. My first goal is to keep New Edinburgh stable long enough for Aurora to become useful.

The clean early order is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Pause and inspect food, housing, prefabs, steam output, damaged districts, and approval.</li>
  <li>Stabilize Vote of Trust pressure before forcing risky laws or promises.</li>
  <li>Reduce obvious survival leaks before committing to diplomacy or war.</li>
  <li>Start Aurora negotiations or military preparation before food hits zero.</li>
  <li>Research Emergency Shelter Hub early enough for the next tremor.</li>
  <li>Keep prefabs for repairs instead of spending everything on expansion.</li>
  <li>Turn Aurora into a working food route through Fishery Hubs and transfer capacity.</li>
  <li>Prepare Volcanic Night before the eruption timer becomes urgent.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="before-you-buy-or-start">
  Before you buy or start Breach of Trust
</h2>

Breach of Trust is not a new standalone game. It is a Frostpunk 2 DLC scenario, so I only recommend jumping in if I either already like Frostpunk 2's council-and-city pressure or specifically want a harder story scenario built around political distrust.

At launch, the Steam page listed Breach of Trust as a paid DLC released on **June 23, 2026** at **$12.99**. It requires the base game Frostpunk 2, and availability can differ by storefront, platform, region, and sale timing.

The reason I would play it is not because it adds a relaxed city-builder map. It adds a crisis route where every big decision has a second cost:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>negotiation can save food but create delivery pressure,</li>
  <li>war can reclaim Aurora but create occupation pressure,</li>
  <li>lower steam output can reduce tremors but make heat harder,</li>
  <li>Emergency Shelter Hubs can save lives but drain prefabs,</li>
  <li>and evacuation can work only if food, coal, prefabs, and timing line up.</li>
</ul>

If you are still deciding whether to play it, the best fit is simple: Breach of Trust is for players who enjoy Frostpunk 2 when politics, logistics, and moral pressure all collide at once. If you only want a clean economy sandbox, this DLC is much more stressful than that.

<h2 id="first-moves">
  What I do first after the scenario starts
</h2>

I do not unpause and build blindly.

The opening is dangerous because several systems are already close to failure. New Edinburgh needs food, but the city also needs shelter coverage, heat, repairs, and political control. If I spend everything on one system, another one usually breaks.

My first pause checklist is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>check the Vote of Trust and approval situation,</li>
  <li>check how long the food stockpile lasts,</li>
  <li>look for damaged housing and production districts,</li>
  <li>check prefab stockpile before placing expensive buildings,</li>
  <li>open the steam output objective and see how far above the safe target I am,</li>
  <li>start research toward Emergency Shelter Hub if the next tremor is a real threat,</li>
  <li>and decide whether Aurora will be handled through diplomacy or force.</li>
</ul>

The biggest early mistake is treating the Aurora choice as the only urgent issue. The route matters, but New Edinburgh has to survive the weeks before the route pays out.

<h2 id="vote-of-trust">
  Vote of Trust changes how you play
</h2>

Vote of Trust is not just story flavor. It changes how I evaluate almost every early decision.

In a normal city route, I might choose the most efficient building, law, or resource play. In Breach of Trust, I also ask whether that decision keeps the council from turning against me. A slightly slower economic route can be better if it preserves approval, avoids broken promises, and gives me time to pass the laws I actually need.

The key lesson is:

<strong>approval can matter more than efficiency.</strong>

I use that rule in three places:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>Before promises:</strong> I only promise a faction something if I can deliver it before the next crisis steals my attention.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Before laws:</strong> I avoid forcing a major law when hunger, cold, or deaths are already pushing trust down.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Before Aurora choices:</strong> I check whether negotiation or war will give me enough political support to survive the next phase.
  </li>
</ul>

This is why Breach of Trust can feel confusing on a first run. A city can look economically recoverable and still be politically fragile. If I rush the “correct” economic answer while ignoring trust, the council can stop me before that answer pays off.

<h2 id="steam-output-tremors">
  Lower steam output without freezing the city
</h2>

Breach of Trust ties tremor danger to steam pressure. In my runs, the early objective pushed me toward reducing steam output to around **200** or lower.

That target is useful, but I do not hit it by shutting everything down instantly. If I cut too hard, cold housing and workplace problems can kill people before the next tremor does.

<h2 id="aurora-negotiation">
  Negotiate with Aurora if you want the safer first clear
</h2>

The negotiation route is my preferred first-clear path because it keeps the scenario closer to food logistics instead of adding a full military layer.

That does not mean it is easy.

Aurora can accept a deal and New Edinburgh can still starve if I forget the delivery step. After a proposal succeeds, I always check the objective panel and send the promised resources. The negotiation screen is not the end of the trade.

<h2 id="war-route">
  Conquer Aurora only if you can afford the occupation
</h2>

The force route is stronger on paper than it feels in practice. It promises control, but it also forces me to build a war economy while the city is already hungry and unstable.

Barracks starts the military route, but soldiers are not just a free training queue. In my run, I had to think about hired soldiers, heat stamp pressure, support capacity, and conscription laws. Hiring gives speed. Conscription can preserve money, but it needs political support and can damage trust.

Heavy Weaponry is also not just a passive upgrade. I treat it as a military resource that has to be gathered or produced, then spent on raids, outposts, and combat pressure.

The war route is worth considering when:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>food is stable enough to survive several more weeks,</li>
  <li>the next tremor is covered by shelters and prefabs,</li>
  <li>I can afford Barracks without killing the economy,</li>
  <li>I can grow platoons through hiring or conscription,</li>
  <li>and I have enough Heavy Weaponry for more than one action.</li>
</ul>

I do not attack simply because the attack option appears. I attack when the army advantage is strong and the city can survive after victory. Conquest starts the occupation stage, where Aurora still needs heat, housing, food production, guards, patrols, and transfer capacity.

For the full military route, use the <Link href="/frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust/conquer-aurora-war-route-guide">Conquer Aurora War Route Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="emergency-shelter-hub">
  Research Emergency Shelter Hub before the tremor becomes a disaster
</h2>

Emergency Shelter Hub is the building I do not want to remember too late.

Its job is not to prevent every point of damage. Its job is to stop tremors from turning damaged housing into mass deaths. Covered districts can still need repairs afterward, so I still keep prefabs ready.

In one run, the build panel showed a meaningful upfront cost in heat stamps, materials, prefabs, and workforce. That is why I do not spend my last prefabs right before researching it. I want both the shelter and the repair reserve.

My placement priority is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>housing districts,</li>
  <li>prefab and material production,</li>
  <li>food or transfer infrastructure,</li>
  <li>research and council support buildings,</li>
  <li>then anything else the route depends on.</li>
</ol>

If I am forced to choose, I protect people first. A damaged workplace is bad. Dead citizens and collapsed trust are worse.

<h2 id="fishery-hubs-transfer">
  Fishery Hubs solve food only when transfer works
</h2>

Aurora's food route is not a normal New Edinburgh food district route.

Once I gain access to Aurora-side fishing territory, I need **Fishery Hubs**. In my runs, early Fishery Hub output appeared around **50–66 food**, with a significant heat requirement in at least one panel. That is useful, but it is not enough by itself.

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

The food route has two halves:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>produce enough food on the Aurora side,</li>
  <li>then transfer enough of it back to New Edinburgh.</li>
</ul>

If food exists on the Aurora side but New Edinburgh is still starving, I check the transfer screen before building another hub. Later, the scenario can ask for sustained high food transfer, such as a **300 food output** route for multiple weeks. That is when Skyways or better transfer capacity becomes more important than another small production gain.

Do not look for a fixed third-city name. Focus on the Aurora-side settlement or territory you control, the Fishery Hubs placed there, and the transfer route back to New Edinburgh.

<h2 id="volcanic-night-evacuation">
  Volcanic Night changes the scenario into an evacuation race
</h2>

Tremors are recurring damage events. **Volcanic Night** is the phase change.

Once Volcanic Night begins, I stop thinking only about “hold the city” and start thinking about evacuation throughput. The final objective can show a run-specific eruption timer, population target, and casualty cap. Those numbers depend on the run, so I read my own objective panel instead of copying a fixed number.

In one run, an Evacuation Hub launch required **5,000 food**, **5,000 coal**, and **500 prefabs** to save **1,000 people**. I use that as a planning reference, not a universal rule.

Before the ending, I want:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>multiple evacuation launches planned,</li>
  <li>food and coal stockpiles for repeated airships,</li>
  <li>prefabs reserved for launch costs and repairs,</li>
  <li>Aurora-side housing ready for people who cannot stay in New Edinburgh,</li>
  <li>and Generator Core extraction timed so it finishes near the final evacuation window.</li>
</ul>

Do not start Generator Core extraction just because it appears. In my runs, it used a long timer, and starting too early can create a window where the city is weaker while evacuation is still underbuilt.

<h2 id="faq">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust FAQ
</h2>

### What is Frostpunk 2: Breach of Trust?

Breach of Trust is a paid Frostpunk 2 DLC scenario set in New Edinburgh, where Aurora cuts off food while the city deals with political distrust, tremors, Volcanic Night, and evacuation planning.

### Do I need Frostpunk 2 to play Breach of Trust?

Yes. Breach of Trust is DLC and requires the base game Frostpunk 2.

### When did Breach of Trust release?

The Steam listing launched Breach of Trust on June 23, 2026. Store availability, discounts, and regional pricing can change.

### What should I do first in Breach of Trust?

Pause, check Vote of Trust pressure, food, damaged districts, prefabs, steam output, and the next tremor timer. Do not rush only toward Aurora. New Edinburgh needs enough stability to survive until Aurora can actually help.

### What is Vote of Trust?

Vote of Trust is the political pressure that makes approval and council support central to the DLC. It means I cannot judge decisions only by economic efficiency; I also need to avoid broken promises, badly timed laws, and trust collapse.

### Can I lose even with a good economy?

Yes. Breach of Trust can punish political instability even when the economy looks recoverable. If approval collapses, promises fail, or key laws become impossible to pass, the run can fall apart before the food route or evacuation plan pays off.

### Should I negotiate with Aurora or conquer Aurora?

For a first clear, I prefer negotiation. It still has food and transfer problems, but it avoids the extra pressure of Barracks, soldiers, Heavy Weaponry, resistance, and occupation laws. Conquest is viable when the city can afford the war and the recovery afterward.

### Why did my Aurora deal not give me food?

After Aurora accepts a deal, you still need to send the promised resources from the objective panel. If you miss that delivery action, the trade can stall even though the negotiation succeeded.

### How do I reduce tremor danger?

Lower excess steam output, especially geothermal pressure, while keeping enough heat for housing and key workplaces. Then cover housing with Emergency Shelter Hubs and keep prefabs for repairs.

### What does Emergency Shelter Hub do?

Emergency Shelter Hub reduces the risk of deaths from tremor damage around covered districts. It does not make districts immune to all damage, so repairs and prefab reserves still matter.

### What are Fishery Hubs?

Fishery Hubs are the main food buildings for Aurora-side fishing territory. They need heat, space, workers, and materials, then their food has to be transferred back to New Edinburgh.

### What is the Aurora-side settlement?

It is the territory New Edinburgh gains access to after progressing Aurora negotiations or control. The exact settlement name can depend on your run, so focus on fishing grounds, Fishery Hubs, and the transfer screen rather than a fixed name.

### Does conquering Aurora solve food?

No. Conquering Aurora gives control, but the food system still needs Fishery Hubs, heat, workers, transfer capacity, and resistance control.

### What is Heavy Weaponry used for?

Heavy Weaponry is a military resource used for war-route pressure, raids, outposts, and later combat steps. Do not treat it as only a passive technology.

### When should I prepare for Volcanic Night?

Start before the eruption timer feels urgent. Build the food route, store coal and prefabs, prepare evacuation launches, and make sure the Aurora-side route can support people and resources.

### When should I extract the Generator Core?

Extract the Generator Core after evacuation is already functioning and the timer lines up with the final eruption window. Starting it too early can weaken the city before airships are ready.
