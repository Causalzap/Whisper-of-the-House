<h2 id="aurora-negotiation-guide">
  Frostpunk 2 Breach of Trust Aurora negotiation guide
</h2>

Aurora is the first big economic problem in Breach of Trust, and it is easy to misunderstand the route.

New Edinburgh starts hungry because Aurora has cut off food. Local food deposits buy time, but they do not solve the scenario. That means I need Aurora one way or another: through trade, territory access, or conquest.

This guide focuses on the negotiation and Fishery Hub route. If you chose to retake Aurora by force, use the <Link href="/frostpunk-2-breach-of-trust/conquer-aurora-war-route-guide">Conquer Aurora War Route Guide</Link> instead. The food systems overlap later, but the political and resistance problems are different.

My safe route is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Start Aurora talks before New Edinburgh runs out of food.</li>
  <li>Use coal as the cleanest early bargaining chip.</li>
  <li>Watch Aurora strength and defiance before pushing aggressive choices.</li>
  <li>Send the promised resources after the deal is accepted.</li>
  <li>Negotiate or unlock land with fishing grounds.</li>
  <li>Build Fishery Hubs on the Aurora-side settlement.</li>
  <li>Move food back to New Edinburgh through the transfer screen.</li>
  <li>Upgrade transfer capacity when production exists but still does not reach the city.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="first-trade">
  Start the first Aurora trade before starvation peaks
</h2>

The first mistake is waiting too long.

When I open the negotiation screen, I think about two timers at once: the food stockpile in New Edinburgh and the time it takes for Aurora to answer. A deal is not instant. Aurora can accept, reject, or counteroffer, and then I still need to send my side of the shipment.

For the first deal, coal is usually the cleanest offer. New Edinburgh starts with a large coal stockpile, and Aurora values coal because its own heat situation is fragile. A strong opening proposal can be something like **35,000–40,000 coal** for a large food shipment if the acceptance chance is still reasonable.

I do not treat that number as a fixed recipe. If coal is already falling, I lower the offer or prepare a new coal source first. If food is about to run out, I may accept a worse deal rather than let hunger turn into trust loss before the economy stabilizes.

<h2 id="strength-defiance">
  Strength and defiance decide how painful the deal feels
</h2>

Aurora has two pressure stats that matter during talks.

**Strength** is Aurora's leverage. When strength is high, Aurora can demand better trades because it has more economic and military weight behind it.

**Defiance** is how willing Aurora is to refuse you. If defiance rises too much, negotiations become harder and can break down. High defiance can also make a later force route or occupation route more painful.

This is why I avoid reckless event choices during a diplomacy run. If an event gives me quick food but pushes Aurora toward higher defiance, I ask whether I am buying a short-term rescue at the cost of several harder deals later.

A good diplomacy route keeps Aurora talking while New Edinburgh builds coal, prefabs, housing stability, and enough approval to survive the next political window.

<h2 id="send-promised-resources">
  If the deal is accepted, send the promised resources
</h2>

This is the most common real blocker.

The negotiation can succeed and still not feed New Edinburgh immediately. After the proposal is accepted, I check the objective panel and look for the delivery action. The shipment does not complete just because the talk went well.

I do this right after the deal resolves:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>open the objective panel,</li>
  <li>confirm the remaining delivery timer,</li>
  <li>send the promised coal, goods, or materials,</li>
  <li>then check the food stockpile after Aurora's shipment arrives.</li>
</ul>

If I cannot deliver the promised coal, I start fixing coal production immediately. A bad trade can still be survivable. A missed delivery can turn into defiance, distrust, and another food panic.

<h2 id="counteroffers">
  When should you accept a bad counteroffer?
</h2>

Aurora can counteroffer, and some counteroffers feel ugly.

I do not automatically accept every demand. If Aurora asks for too much coal while I have no coal production ready, I pause and check three things:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>How many weeks of food are left?</li>
  <li>Can I build or expand coal production before delivery?</li>
  <li>Will turning steam or geothermal output back up buy heat at the cost of a worse tremor timer?</li>
</ul>

If food is stable for a while, I reject or renegotiate. If New Edinburgh is about to starve, I may accept the bad deal and immediately pivot into coal recovery.

The wrong move is accepting a coal-heavy deal, spending the coal elsewhere, and then realizing the shipment timer is still waiting.

<h2 id="territory">
  Negotiate fishing-ground territory when trades stop being enough
</h2>

Trading food buys time, but it does not create a permanent food base.

Eventually, I need access to Aurora land with fishing grounds. In one route, New Edinburgh was granted control over part of Aurora with fish harvesting access and land for development, then the game treated it as an Aurora-side settlement area.

That matters because this is not a separate fixed third city. When this guide mentions the Aurora-side settlement, I mean the territory New Edinburgh controls on Aurora's side of the route.

One territory deal can be expensive. In my run, a deal involved letting **600 New Edinburgh citizens** into the Aurora-side settlement and having the settlement provide **90 more heat** to Aurora for **30 weeks**.

<h2 id="fishery-hub">
  Build Fishery Hubs, not normal Food Districts
</h2>

Once I reach Aurora-side fishing territory, the food solution looks different from New Edinburgh.

The game does not give me a normal food district over the fishing grounds. I need **Fishery Hubs**. They are awkward because they need space, heat, workers, materials, and enough nearby layout room to place correctly.

In one run, a Fishery Hub showed about **50 food**. In another screenshot, I saw **66 food** with a **40 heat** requirement. I treat that as a practical range, not a permanent universal value. Difficulty, route state, workforce, heat, and patches can change what your own panel shows.

The first time I build one, I do not expect it to fix the whole famine. I treat it as the start of a food network.

My Fishery Hub rules are:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>frostbreak the full placement area before trying to place the hub,</li>
  <li>check heat before committing,</li>
  <li>do not forget housing for the workers,</li>
  <li>build more than one hub if New Edinburgh still has a large deficit,</li>
  <li>and keep materials stable because hubs and support buildings are not free.</li>
</ul>

If the placement preview says no, I usually need more frostbreaking or a cleaner angle around the fishing ground.

<h2 id="transfer-food">
  Move the food back to New Edinburgh
</h2>

Building Fishery Hubs is only half the solution.

If the Aurora-side settlement has food but New Edinburgh still shows a deficit, the problem is transfer. I open the resource transfer screen and set food to move back toward New Edinburgh. I do not wait for the stockpile to look perfect. I start moving food as soon as the deficit can be reduced.

This is where I stop looking at food as one number. I check both sides:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>New Edinburgh food deficit,</li>
  <li>Aurora-side food surplus,</li>
  <li>coal and heat on the Aurora side,</li>
  <li>materials for hub upkeep,</li>
  <li>and available transfer capacity.</li>
</ul>

If I send too much food away, the Aurora-side settlement can start failing. If I send too little, New Edinburgh keeps starving. The best setting is the one that reduces New Edinburgh's deficit without emptying the food city.

<h2 id="skyways-transfer">
  Use Skyways when transfer capacity becomes the blocker
</h2>

After the first food base is stable, the next objective can ask for a much larger transfer. In my run, the objective asked me to transfer **300 food output** from Aurora to New Edinburgh and keep that supply moving for **25 weeks**.

That is when a basic route may no longer be enough. Skyways or extra transfer capacity become more important than another small production boost.

I research or build transfer upgrades when:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Aurora-side food exists but New Edinburgh still loses food,</li>
  <li>the transfer slider cannot cover enough demand,</li>
  <li>coal must move one way while food moves the other,</li>
  <li>or the next objective explicitly asks for sustained food output.</li>
</ul>

Do not wait until Act 3 to care about this. The evacuation phase also wants food, coal, prefabs, and people moving in large chunks. A weak transfer setup makes the ending harder.

<h2 id="when-not-to-buy-or-build">
  When I stop buying, building, or negotiating
</h2>

The easiest way to lose this route is to solve one problem so hard that another one collapses.

I stop negotiating if the price would empty the resource I need for the next promise. I stop building Fishery Hubs if the settlement cannot heat or staff them. I stop expanding Aurora-side territory if New Edinburgh is about to face a tremor and I need prefabs for repairs.

I also avoid taking territory deals just because they look permanent. A fishing-ground deal that adds heat demand, workers, and housing pressure can hurt if I am not ready to support it.

My checkpoint before any big Aurora decision is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Can I pay the promised resource on time?</li>
  <li>Will this make Aurora or the settlement harder to heat?</li>
  <li>Do I have workers for the new hub?</li>
  <li>Can food actually move back to New Edinburgh?</li>
  <li>Am I spending prefabs I need for tremor repairs?</li>
</ul>

If two of those answers are bad, I wait or take a smaller step.

  Common Aurora mistakes
</h2>

These are the mistakes I would check first:

  <li>

  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Forgetting the delivery action:</strong> an accepted deal still needs the promised shipment.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Ignoring strength and defiance:</strong> high strength makes deals worse, and high defiance can break negotiations.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Looking for a fixed third-city name:</strong> focus on the Aurora-side settlement or territory you control, not on a specific settlement name.

<h2 id="faq">
  Aurora negotiation and Fishery Hub FAQ
</h2>

### How do I negotiate with Aurora?

Open the Aurora negotiation screen, set what New Edinburgh wants on the demand side, then offer resources Aurora values. Coal is usually the easiest early offer because New Edinburgh starts with a large coal stockpile.

### Why did Aurora accept but my food did not arrive?

You probably still need to send the promised resources. After the deal is accepted, check the objective panel and use the delivery action.

### What do strength and defiance do?

Strength affects Aurora's leverage in negotiations. Higher strength can make deals more expensive. Defiance affects whether Aurora keeps talking or refuses you.

### Should I accept Aurora's counteroffer?

Accept only if food is urgent and you can deliver the requested resources on time. If the counteroffer empties your coal or materials without a recovery plan, it can create the next crisis.

### What is the Aurora-side settlement?

The Aurora-side settlement is the territory New Edinburgh gains access to after progressing Aurora negotiations or territory control. Its exact name can depend on your run, so focus on the fishing-ground territory, Fishery Hubs, and transfer screen rather than a fixed settlement name.

### How do I get fishing grounds?

Progress Aurora negotiations or territory access until New Edinburgh gains control of land with fishing grounds. Be ready for extra heat, housing, and workforce demands after the deal.

### Why can I not build a normal Food District in Aurora?

Aurora's fishing grounds use Fishery Hubs. Frostbreak enough space, place the hub around the fishing ground, and provide heat and workers.

### Is one Fishery Hub enough?

Usually no. In my runs, early Fishery Hub output appeared around 50–66 food, and one hub was not enough for both New Edinburgh and the Aurora-side settlement.

### How do I send food from Aurora to New Edinburgh?

Use the resource transfer screen and set food to move from the Aurora-side settlement back to New Edinburgh. If the route cannot move enough, research Skyways or add transfer capacity.

### When should I research Skyways?

Research Skyways when food exists on the Aurora side but New Edinburgh still cannot receive enough, or when the objective asks for sustained high food transfer.

### Does the war route still need Fishery Hubs?

Yes. Conquering Aurora changes the political problem, not the food math. You still need Fishery Hubs, heat, housing, transfer capacity, and resistance control.
