<h2 id="supermarket-chaos-beginner-guide">
  Supermarket Chaos beginner guide
</h2>

Supermarket Chaos starts with a simple job: put every product back where it belongs.

The problem is scale. The store is not asking me to fix one aisle. It is asking me to sort thousands of products across hundreds of shelves. That changes how I play. If I pick up whatever looks familiar, I make progress for a few minutes, but I also start forgetting which shelves were incomplete, which items already failed, and where my temporary piles are.

My first-run rule is:

<strong>Do not solve the store one product at a time. Solve it one section family at a time.</strong>

That means I focus on:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>learning the big section signs,</li>
  <li>placing obvious products first,</li>
  <li>using shelf feedback when an item does not fit,</li>
  <li>buying early carry upgrades,</li>
  <li>and saving confusing products for the section map instead of forcing guesses.</li>
</ul>

This page is for the first hour. If you are already chasing 100%, use the <Link href="/supermarket-chaos/achievements-guide">achievements guide</Link>. If you mainly need upgrade order, use the <Link href="/supermarket-chaos/best-upgrades-skills">best upgrades guide</Link>.

<h2 id="what-kind-of-game-this-is">
  What kind of game is this?
</h2>

Supermarket Chaos is not a full supermarket management sim. I do not set prices, order stock, hire staff, manage cashiers, or run a deep business loop.

The core game is classification and placement: pick up products from the floor, read the shelf family, and put each product back where it belongs. That is why this guide focuses on sorting habits instead of business strategy. If you came here expecting a deep store-management game, set that expectation early. If you came here because the floor is covered in products and you want a cleaner route, this guide is for you.

The early loop is simple:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">

  <li>Watch the shelf feedback.</li>
  <li>Earn money from correct placements.</li>
  <li>Buy upgrades once walking starts costing more time than sorting.</li>

I do not worry about perfect shelf styling at the start. Correct placement matters more than making every shelf look neat. The late run is when I clean up layout, missing copies, and awkward shelves.

<h2 id="keyboard-mouse-controller">
  Keyboard, mouse, or controller?
</h2>

Supermarket Chaos works with either keyboard and mouse or controller, but I choose based on the task.

For normal sorting, I care more about comfort than raw speed. Controller movement can feel better for long cleanup sessions because I spend a lot of time walking between aisles. Keyboard and mouse feels cleaner when I need faster item selection, quick throwing, or precise shelf testing.

The difference shows up in specific moments:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>Use controller</strong> when you are doing relaxed long-session cleanup and mostly moving through known sections.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Use keyboard and mouse</strong> when you are testing several nearby shelves, picking individual products from clutter, or throwing items quickly.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Switch temporarily</strong> if one action feels awkward. The clearest example is Do Not Throw, where fast repeated throws can feel easier on keyboard and mouse.
  </li>
</ul>

The input method does not change the sorting logic. The real beginner skill is still reading section signs, using shelf feedback, and avoiding random mixed piles.

<h2 id="best-sections-to-clear-first">
  Best sections to clear first
</h2>

I like starting with categories that are easy to recognize from a distance. That does not mean they are always the fastest to complete, but they are safer for a first run.

<GuideTable
  caption="Beginner-friendly section families in Supermarket Chaos."
  columns={earlySectionColumns}
  rows={earlySectionRows}
/>

Books, electronics, home decoration, drinks, and frozen food are good early choices because I can recognize many of those products before picking them up. Toilet and cleaning products are also useful, but I double-check the sign because personal care and housekeeping can split in ways that are easy to confuse.

The main thing I avoid is forcing food categories too early. Breakfast, sweets, tea, candies, bento, meat, frozen, and ramen can overlap visually. I still pick those products up, but I do not treat every food item as obvious.

<h2 id="use-shelf-feedback">
  Use shelf feedback instead of guessing
</h2>

The fastest way to learn the store is to let the shelves correct me.

If a product does not belong, I do not keep testing every nearby shelf. I step back and ask which section family makes more sense. A product like sugar is a good early example: it looks easy, but it can sit near categories that overlap in the player's head, such as breakfast, sweets, tea, or candy.

<h2 id="how-to-handle-temporary-piles">
  How I handle temporary piles
</h2>

Temporary piles are useful only if they stay organized.

I do not make one huge pile of random products. That only moves the chaos from the floor to a new location. Instead, I use small piles by section family:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>unknown drinks near the drink sections,</li>
  <li>uncertain food near the food aisles,</li>
  <li>personal care near toilet products,</li>
  <li>cleaning supplies near housekeeping,</li>
  <li>books and electronics near their own shelves.</li>
</ul>

If a pile starts mixing three or four unrelated categories, I stop and place what I already understand. A temporary pile should reduce decisions, not create more of them.

<h2 id="faq">
  Supermarket Chaos beginner FAQ
</h2>

### What should I do first in Supermarket Chaos?

Walk the store, learn the big section signs, then place obvious products first. Do not start by trying to identify every product on the floor.

### Is Supermarket Chaos a management game?

Not really. The core loop is sorting and placement. There is no deep pricing, restocking, cashier, staffing, or business-management layer in the early route.

### How many products are in Supermarket Chaos?

The launch objective is to place **4,668 products** back onto the correct shelves.

### How many shelves are there?

The run tracks **528 shelves**, so it is better to think in section families than individual products.

### Is keyboard and mouse or controller better?

Both work. I use controller for relaxed long sorting and keyboard and mouse for quicker item handling, precise shelf tests, or fast throw timing.

### What changes after I buy Extra Carry?

The route feels less stop-start. I can carry more products from the same aisle, finish small section passes faster, and avoid walking back and forth for every few items.

### Should I clean one shelf perfectly before moving on?

Not at the very start. I care more about correct section placement first. Perfect cleanup matters more later, when the store is easier to read.

### What should I do with confusing products?

Test the most likely section once. If it fails, move by category family or use the section map. Do not brute force every shelf nearby.

### Are sugar, coffee, shampoo, wine, and bento items beginner traps?

Yes, they can be. They are not hard once you know the section logic, but they are easy to misread early because several categories look related.

### Should I use an all-items list?

No. For a normal clear, an all-items list is unnecessary. Learn section rules, keep small temporary piles, and use finder upgrades later.

### When should I start achievement hunting?

Do Music Duty and Do Not Throw early if you want 100%. Then follow the normal cleanup route until the dedicated achievement milestones become relevant.
