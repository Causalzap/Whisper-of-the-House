Galactic War is ZeroSpace's persistent cooperative strategy mode. Every
completed mission affects two different layers: it advances my own commander
and System Mastery while also adding Capture Points to an alliance-controlled
galaxy map.

That combination creates most of the early confusion. Winning a mission does
not always capture a system, an occupation bonus is not the same as permanent
Mastery, and the resources shown on the Galactic War map do not all perform the
same job.

This guide explains how I choose an alliance, select a useful first system,
spend Jump Fuel, read Capture Points, launch missions with players or AI, and
turn mission rewards into lasting progression.

<h2 id="what-is-galactic-war">
  What is Galactic War in ZeroSpace?
</h2>

Galactic War is a persistent cooperative PvE mode built around a shared galaxy
map.

Instead of completing an isolated match and returning to an unchanged menu, I
select a system whose strategic balance can be affected by the mission result.

Three layers are active at once:

1. **Mission objective:** complete the current PvE scenario.
2. **System conflict:** add Capture Points toward alliance control.
3. **Personal progression:** earn Commander XP, Mastery, items, Credits, and
   Influence.

This explains why a successful mission can still leave the system under enemy
control. The mission succeeded, but the wider Capture Point balance may still
favor another alliance.

<h2 id="capture-points">
  How do Capture Points work in ZeroSpace?
</h2>

Completing a mission adds Capture Points for my alliance in the selected
system.

  <Image
    src="/images/zerospace/zerospace-galactic-war-capture-points.webp"
    alt="ZeroSpace Galactic War Capture Points tutorial explaining mission contributions and system ownership"
    width={1600}
    height={900}

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-500">
    The mission contributes to the system from which it was launched. One
    victory may reduce an enemy lead without immediately changing ownership.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The alliance with the strongest Capture Point position can take control and
receive the occupation bonus.

That produces three possible results after a win:

1. my alliance gains ground but remains behind;
2. the system becomes closely contested;
3. the mission provides enough progress to change ownership.

When ownership changes, the accumulated Capture Point totals are significantly
reduced.

This creates a strategic opening:

- the previous owner may counterattack;
- the new owner may need immediate defense;
- a third alliance may enter while both totals are weakened.

A newly captured system is therefore not automatically safe.

<h3 id="why-cant-i-capture-a-system">
  Why can’t I capture a system after winning?
</h3>

One mission contributes points; it does not guarantee control.

I check:

- the remaining point difference;
- whether allied players are completing missions in the same system;
- whether another alliance has recently captured it;
- and whether I accidentally launched from a different system.

When the enemy lead is large, several alliance victories may be required.

<h2 id="occupation-bonus">
  How occupation bonuses work
</h2>

The alliance controlling a system receives its occupation bonus.

Visible examples include:

- **10% maximum unit energy and regeneration**;
- **+10 maximum Supply**.

These values are examples from individual systems, not bonuses shared by every
planet.

I compare the bonus with the missions I am currently playing:

- maximum Supply helps army-heavy scenarios;
- energy and regeneration support ability-heavy armies;
- production bonuses help missions with weak starting economies.

When another alliance captures the system, the occupation bonus can disappear.
That does not remove personal Mastery already earned there.

<h2 id="galactic-resources">
  ZeroSpace Galactic War resources explained
</h2>

The Galactic War interface shows several resources that perform unrelated
jobs.

The cleanest distinction is:

| Resource | Used for |
| --- | --- |
| Jump Fuel | Travelling between systems |
| Credits | Improving personal Arsenal items |
| Influence | Supporting alliance buffs |
| Hexite and Flux | Building and producing units inside missions |

Hexite and Flux belong to the RTS mission economy. They are not used to improve
items or support the alliance map.

1. my current system;
2. the intended destination;
3. the displayed Jump Fuel cost;
4. whether enough fuel is available;
5. whether the travel action was confirmed;
6. whether the interface has entered a travel or matchmaking state.

Repeatedly selecting different systems while waiting makes the current
destination harder to identify.

<h3 id="credits">
  What are Credits used for?
</h3>

Credits improve items equipped through the Arsenal.

I do not spend them across every early reward.

My order is:

1. choose the commander I use most often;
2. open the Arsenal;
3. compare the available items;
4. equip the item that supports that commander;
5. spend Credits on that item.

This gives me one functional setup sooner than partially upgrading several
items I may never use.

<h3 id="influence">
  What does Influence do?
</h3>

Influence supports alliance buffs through the planet or system menu.

It is not an item-upgrade currency.

I spend Influence when:

- the system is on an active front;
- the alliance is close to capturing or losing it;
- other allied players are completing missions there;
- and the selected buff has a clear effect.

I hold it when the system is uncontested or the alliance has moved to a
different front.

<h3 id="mission-resources">
  Are Hexite and Flux used on the Galactic War map?

Hexite and Flux are primarily mission resources.

Inside an RTS scenario:

- bases generate Hexite;
- Harvesters gather Flux;
- Supply limits army size;
- and those resources pay for units, buildings, and upgrades.

<h2 id="choose-first-system">
  Which Galactic War system should I choose first?
</h2>

My first system meets three conditions:

1. it is reachable without spending most of my Jump Fuel;
2. my alliance is already attacking or defending it;
3. the mission is understandable at Normal difficulty.

A defensive mission is useful when:

- the system is alliance controlled;
- the Capture Point balance is close;
- and the occupation bonus is worth preserving.

An offensive mission is useful when:

- the destination is nearby;
- the enemy lead is recoverable;
- and allied players are already contributing there.

A distant system with no allied activity is usually a poor first investment,
even when its occupation bonus looks strong.

I start on **Normal**.

Before launching, I identify:

1. what structure or unit must survive;
2. what enemy mechanic blocks progress;
3. whether the mission contains simultaneous objectives;
4. when I need to abandon a side objective;
5. whether the selected commander can handle the mission role.

One Siege Walker scenario illustrates this clearly:

- the ZeroSpace Relay must survive;
- shield generators protect the walkers;
- attacking shielded walkers wastes time;
- the generators must be destroyed first.

The largest enemy on the screen is not always the first objective.

<h2 id="first-mission-checklist">
  My first Galactic War mission checklist
</h2>

### What must survive?

The failure condition may be:

- a Relay;
- an Operating Tower;
- a settlement;
- a Fabricator;
- or a timed extraction target.

### What prevents progress?

The blocker may be:

- shield generators;
- portals;
- artillery;
- anti-air defenses;
- or an enemy production source.

### Should the players split?

Splitting works when one army can defend while the other removes a blocker.

Both armies stay together when:

- the mission starts with few units;
- a major wave is already active;
- or the objective requires concentrated damage.

### When should I return from a side objective?

I turn back when:

- the protected structure is taking sustained damage;
- a countdown begins;
- the ally repeatedly pings the main objective;
- or the return route is becoming unsafe.

### When should I increase the difficulty?

I move above Normal after completing the same mission type without losing the
protected objective or misunderstanding the target order.

<h2 id="system-mastery">
  How does System Mastery work?
</h2>

Each system has a Mastery track containing **four levels**.

Earned levels provide permanent personal bonuses that continue applying even
if another alliance later captures the system.

Visible examples include:

- **+1% building production**;
- **+1% unit health and shields**.

  <Image

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-500">
    Mastery is personal and persistent. The occupation bonus belongs to the
    alliance currently controlling the system.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

For early progression, I concentrate on a small group of connected systems.

That approach:

- reduces travel costs;
- makes mission objectives familiar;
- builds Mastery more consistently;
- and supports the same alliance front repeatedly.

I move to another system when:

- the next useful Mastery threshold has been reached;
- the alliance no longer needs pressure there;
- or a nearby system offers a more valuable permanent bonus.

<h3 id="does-mastery-reset">
  Does System Mastery reset when a planet is captured?
</h3>

No. Ownership and Mastery are separate.

A system changing hands can remove the alliance occupation bonus, but previously
earned Mastery remains part of personal progression.

<h2 id="commander-xp">
  How Commander XP works
</h2>

Mission experience levels the selected commander and unlocks new units,
upgrades, and play styles.

I develop one default commander first.

I switch when:

- the mission requires a role the current commander handles poorly;
- a newly unlocked unit is worth testing;
- or the first commander's basic roster is already functional.

Constantly changing commanders can leave several options partly developed
without one dependable setup for harder missions.

<h2 id="items-and-arsenal">
  Items and the Arsenal
</h2>

Mission completion can award items for the Arsenal.

I check the Arsenal after several missions because an item reward may not be
equipped automatically.

My first upgrade goes to an item that:

- supports my main commander;
- affects a unit used in most missions;
- or improves a weakness I repeatedly encounter.

I avoid investing Credits in an item merely because it was the first one
awarded.

<h2 id="after-mission">
  What to check after completing a mission
</h2>

After a victory, I return to the galaxy map and check:

1. how the Capture Point balance changed;
2. whether the system became contested;
3. whether ownership changed;
4. which occupation bonus is active;
5. whether System Mastery advanced;
6. whether the commander gained a level;
7. whether an item or Credits were awarded;
8. how much Jump Fuel remains.

A mission can provide the final contribution that changes ownership.

- remain and help defend the new ownership;
- or move with the allied front toward another nearby system.

<h2 id="coop-or-ai">
  Should you choose Play Co-op or Play with AI?
</h2>

I use **Play with AI** when:

- learning an unfamiliar mission;
- testing a new commander;
- checking the objective order;
- or practising Normal difficulty.

I use **Play Co-op** when:

- the mission contains simultaneous objectives;
- one army can defend while another removes a blocker;
- a higher difficulty requires better coordination;
- or the map contains several permanent attack sources.

In co-op, I choose one clear responsibility:

- defend the fixed objective;
- destroy shield generators;
- close portals;
- secure an expansion;
- handle a side route;
- or reinforce the final attack.

Following the other player with the entire army often leaves one objective
uncovered.

<h3 id="ship-will-not-move">
  Why won’t my ship move in Galactic War?
</h3>

The usual causes are:

- the destination is selected but travel is not confirmed;
- the destination costs more Jump Fuel than is available;
- the current location is unclear;
- or the interface is already searching for a mission.

I select one destination, read its cost, confirm the action, and wait before
clicking another system.

<h3 id="mission-did-not-capture">
  Why did my mission not capture the planet?
</h3>

A victory adds Capture Points rather than granting automatic ownership.

The remaining enemy lead may still be larger than the mission contribution.
Allied players need to continue completing missions in the same system.

<h3 id="occupation-bonus-disappeared">
  Why did my occupation bonus disappear?
</h3>

Another alliance probably captured the system.

Occupation bonuses belong to the current owner. Earned System Mastery remains
personal and does not disappear with the bonus.

<h3 id="influence-not-personal-upgrade">
  Why can’t I use Influence to upgrade an item?
</h3>

Influence and Credits have different purposes.

Credits improve personal Arsenal items. Influence supports alliance buffs
through the planet or system menu.

<h3 id="third-alliance-capture">
  Why can a third alliance take a system so quickly?
</h3>

Ownership changes significantly reduce the existing Capture Point totals.

That leaves the new owner and previous owner with a weaker point position,
allowing the third alliance to enter the contest more easily.

<h2 id="faq">
  ZeroSpace Galactic War FAQ
</h2>

### How do Capture Points work in ZeroSpace Galactic War?

Each completed mission adds Capture Points to the alliance in the system from
which the mission was launched. One win may only reduce the enemy lead. A
system changes ownership when the overall point balance is sufficient, not
simply because one mission was completed.

### Does System Mastery reset when another alliance captures the system?

No. Earned Mastery is permanent personal progression. The occupation bonus may
disappear when ownership changes, but the Mastery levels already earned remain
active.

### What is Jump Fuel used for?

Jump Fuel pays for travel between Galactic War systems. It is separate from
Hexite and Flux used inside RTS missions. A distant route can consume enough
fuel to delay the next strategic move, so I work through connected systems
first.

### What does Influence do in Galactic War?

Influence supports alliance buffs through the selected planet or system menu.
It does not improve Arsenal items. Credits are the currency used for personal
item upgrades.

### Why did I win but not capture the system?

The mission added Capture Points, but another alliance may still have the
stronger total. The result may also have changed the system from enemy
controlled to contested without transferring ownership.

### Can I play Galactic War without another player?

Yes. Select Play with AI from the mission panel. I use it to learn objective
order, test commanders, and practise Normal difficulty before choosing Play
Co-op.

### Does my alliance lock the faction I can play?

No. Alliance membership controls strategic contributions on the galaxy map.
The commander and combat faction used inside missions remain separate choices.

### Why did a third alliance capture a system after my alliance won it?

When ownership changes, stored Capture Point totals are significantly reduced.
That weaker post-capture balance allows the third alliance to enter and contest
the system quickly.

### Should I always travel to the system with the best occupation bonus?

No. Travel cost, current location, alliance activity, mission difficulty, and
the current Capture Point balance may matter more than the bonus itself. A
nearby contested system usually provides more immediate value.
