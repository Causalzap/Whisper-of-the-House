<h2 id="how-tanker-truck-actually-works">
  How the Tanker Truck scenario actually works
</h2>

The Tanker Truck scenario becomes much easier once you stop treating it like a normal static colony map.

What makes it hard is not just scarcity. It is the combination of:

- **limited opening resources**
- **fuel-gated movement between zones**
- **temporary scavenging windows**
- **the need to repair and expand while still saving enough to leave**
- **a population that can quietly outgrow your production**

That is why the observed run keeps returning to the same idea: **stabilize an equilibrium first, then move**.

In practical terms, the first successful loop is:

- get storage online
- stabilize **wood**
- convert wood into **water**
- stabilize **food**
- open **boat scavenging**
- add **powered junk income**
- pivot into **fuel production**
- only then start acting like you have freedom

If you skip those steps and start improvising with too many side systems, the scenario tends to punish you with fuel starvation rather than immediate collapse.

<h2 id="all-will-fall-tanker-truck-best-build-order">
  All Will Fall Tanker Truck best build order
</h2>

For most players, the strongest opening from the observed run looks like this:

### 1. Build Storage, Water Storage, and Food Storage first

This opening is not glamorous, but it is correct. The run starts by getting the ship unloaded and immediately creating stable drop-off points before trying to expand anywhere fancy.

That matters because the scenario wastes your time fast if you gather before you can properly hold and route materials. It also matters because **storage pressure arrives early** in this scenario. If storage fills too fast, your gather loop stalls, floating value gets missed, and the run can look “resource rich” while actually choking.

### 2. Build the first research structure immediately

The observed route immediately pushes into early research so it can stop improvising and start using math.

The whole guide really starts once the player can say:

- Wood Catcher output is visible
- Wood Boiler cost is visible
- food equilibrium can be planned instead of guessed

### 3. Build the Wood Catcher first

The early argument is simple: **wood unlocks the rest of the opening**.

In the observed run, the Wood Catcher is treated as the first true stabilizer because it shows **1.5 wood per hour**, which then supports the next water step.

### 4. Build the Wood Boiler second

Once wood is not purely scavenged, the next goal is turning that into survivable water. The Wood Boiler is the first serious water bridge in the observed route, and its visible cost is **20 wood**.

The practical lesson here is that the Tanker Truck opening is not “whatever I can afford first.” It is a dependency chain.

### 5. Add two Fishing Huts for the first equilibrium

The run explicitly frames **2 Fishing Huts** as the early answer for feeding the opening 12-population setup.

That does not mean fishing is the best forever food solution. It means fishing is the first clean way to stop bleeding out while you are still fuel-poor.

### 6. Add a Simple Boat as soon as junk pressure starts

Once the base materials are barely stable, the guide pivots into the first boat because too much of the scenario economy is floating outside your core structure.

That is the point where the map starts opening up.

If you want the shortest possible principle, it is this:

**Do not jump from “I can barely survive” to “I want advanced systems.” Build the first equilibrium first.**

<h2 id="resource-math-and-why-fuel-is-the-real-bottleneck">
  Resource math and why fuel is the real bottleneck
</h2>

The smartest part of the observed run is that it does not hide behind vague advice like “just gather more resources.” It repeatedly frames the scenario as a **math problem**.

The most important opening math is:

- **Wood Catcher = 1.5 wood per hour**
- **Wood Boiler = early water bridge**
- **2 Fishing Huts = first food equilibrium target**
- **movement between zones costs fuel**
- **a visible move cost reaches 60 fuel**
- **wood-to-fuel becomes the real economic pivot**

That changes how you should think about almost every early decision.

### Why wood matters more than it first looks

At first, wood feels like “just another basic material.” In Tanker Truck, it is much more than that:

- wood supports early construction
- wood supports early water
- wood supports some support pieces and ramps
- later, wood becomes part of the fuel solution

That is why the run keeps revisiting wood catchers, wall wood, and wood conservation.

### Why the scenario feels harder than it really is

Many failed runs are not losing because the player misunderstood one big mechanic. They lose because they drift into negative numbers on:

- fuel
- wood
- water
- or staffing efficiency

The scenario then becomes impossible to recover because leaving the current zone also costs fuel.

</h2>

The scenario feels dramatically better once you stop depending only on walkable scraps.

The observed route makes two major points:

### Simple boats are not optional for long

Once the first Simple Boat is live, the colony is no longer limited to purely local hand gathering.

That means you can:

- grab floating junk
- grab floating wood
- reduce how much your whole economy depends on one cramped build zone
- start preparing for future moves instead of barely surviving the current one

### Sailors are a real bottleneck

The run calls this out directly: sailors matter because boats matter, and boats are one of the cleanest ways to keep the scenario from choking.

That also explains why you cannot just accept every new person mindlessly. More people means more consumption, but the wrong staffing mix can also leave your best economic tools half-idle.

<h2 id="powering-the-first-junk-economy">
  Powering the first junk economy
</h2>

This is the first powered threshold that really matters in Tanker Truck.

The logic is straightforward:

- **Electric Junk Catcher needs 3 electricity**
- **Hamster Wheel provides 3 electricity**

That is one of the biggest early turning points in the whole scenario.

The wheel is small, simple, and good enough to unlock the first powered junk route.

That makes it strong even if it is not elegant.

That makes the practical lesson clear:

- Hamster Wheel = first bridge
- Wave Generator = first serious passive energy pivot

Do not wait for a perfect placement if the structure you have right now is already enough to hit the next useful threshold.

<h2 id="when-to-move-between-zones">
  When to move between zones
</h2>

This is the question that decides whether the run feels manageable or doomed.

A visible move in the observed run costs **60 fuel**, which means leaving is never a free reset button. Before you move, you want a simple decision framework.

### Move only when at least most of these are true

- **your current zone is no longer offering easy fuel, wood, or junk**
- **your food and water are stable enough that one bad event will not instantly break the run**
- **you can afford the next move without gutting your reserve**
- **you have already converted the best low-risk loot into actual stored materials**
- **your boats are working and not sitting idle for preventable reasons**

### Do not leave just because the map feels awkward

Tanker Truck often feels messy even when it is salvageable. A weird-looking zone is not the same thing as a dead zone.

### Do not stay just because the zone still has loot

If the remaining loot is expensive, awkward, or requires too much fuel or worker time to reach, it may already be worse than just moving on.

<GuideCallout title="A simple move rule that works well">
If leaving the zone will cost **60 fuel**, do not treat it like a casual travel action. Treat it like a midgame purchase.

A good practical question is:

**“If I leave right now, am I buying safety and future access — or am I just fleeing a problem I have not actually stabilized yet?”**
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="when-to-start-making-your-own-fuel">
  When to start making your own fuel
</h2>

This is the true scenario pivot.

The observed run is blunt about it: the early base can look stable and still be doomed if it cannot produce enough fuel to keep jumping.

That is why the guide eventually says the quiet part out loud:

- fuel is the hardest part
- once basics are established, the scenario becomes manageable
- the real problem is producing enough fuel to keep moving

### The wood-to-fuel turning point

The clearest planning note shown in the run is that **3 wood per hour** is treated as the key threshold for wood-to-fuel logic.

That is not the same thing as a universal final formula, but it is absolutely enough to guide early-midgame planning.

### What changes once fuel is online

Once your own fuel production works, the whole scenario changes:

- movement stops feeling like a countdown to failure
- you can evaluate zones more calmly
- you can prioritize higher-value scavenging
- you can afford to plan upgrades instead of only emergency exits

<h2 id="the-midgame-food-pivot-mushroom-farm-vs-fishing-huts">
  The midgame food pivot: Mushroom Farm vs Fishing Huts
</h2>

This is one of the most useful high-intent takeaways from the long run.

Early fishing is fine. But later, the player explicitly treats **1 Mushroom Farm as roughly equal to 3 Fishing Huts**.

That matters for two reasons:

### 1. It frees sailors

Fishing-heavy openings keep sailors busy, which can be awkward when boats are also valuable.

### 2. It turns food into a cleaner systems problem

The Mushroom Farm route is framed as a better long-run conversion because it uses resources the colony already wants to scale anyway.

So the practical progression becomes:

- survive with fishing
- stabilize wood and water
- move into fuel
- later replace sailor-heavy food with denser production

That is a much stronger Tanker Truck flow than trying to force fishing to carry the whole campaign forever.

<h2 id="common-tanker-truck-mistakes">
  Common Tanker Truck mistakes
</h2>

### 1. Expanding before the first equilibrium exists

If wood, water, and food are not stable yet, more buildings usually make the map feel worse, not better.

### 2. Treating boats as optional

They are not. Too much value floats outside your reach.

### 3. Solving the wrong bottleneck first

A layout can look terrible and still be fine if the economy is stable. A pretty base can still die instantly if fuel is negative.

### 4. Waiting for perfect passive placements

A usable 70% threshold can be better than a perfect theoretical build that comes too late.

### 5. Overcommitting to fishing forever

Fishing works as an opening stabilizer. It is not automatically the best forever plan.

### 6. Ignoring storage pressure

This is not just a late cleanup issue. It starts in the opening. If storage fills too fast, gather routes stall and your apparent surplus stops turning into real usable stock.

<GuideCallout title="The Tanker Truck mindset that actually works">
The most reliable mental model is not “How do I make this base look complete?” It is:

**What is the next threshold that stops the run from choking?**

Usually that threshold is one of these:

- enough wood to keep water and supports alive
- enough food to stop population pressure
- enough power to unlock passive junk
- enough fuel to leave safely
</GuideCallout>

  FAQ

### Is Tanker Truck the same as Broken Tanker in All Will Fall?

For practical search intent, yes — this guide is written around the scenario commonly referred to in creator coverage as **Broken Tanker**.

### What should I build first in the Tanker Truck scenario?

The safest observed opening is **Storage → Water Storage → Food Storage → early research → Wood Catcher → Wood Boiler → 2 Fishing Huts → Simple Boat**.

### What is the first useful production number in Tanker Truck?

The clearest early number shown in the observed run is **Wood Catcher = 1.5 wood per hour**.

### How much fuel does it cost to move between zones?

The clearest visible checkpoint in the run shows a move costing **60 fuel**. This page does not yet claim that 60 is the universal formula for every zone state.

### What powers the Electric Junk Catcher?

It needs **3 electricity**, and the first simple bridge shown in the run is **Hamster Wheel = 3 electricity**.

### Is Wave Generator better than Hamster Wheel?

In the long run, yes, because passive energy frees workers and supports a stronger economy. In the short run, Hamster Wheel is still a very practical bridge.

### When should I move to another zone?

Move when the current zone is no longer giving you cheap fuel, wood, or junk and when paying the next move cost is buying safety, not just covering for an unstable economy.

### When should I start making my own fuel?

As soon as your opening food-and-water equilibrium is real enough that the colony can survive the staffing shift. In the observed run, fuel production is treated as the true scenario pivot.

### Are Fishing Huts the best food source forever?

No. The observed run later treats **1 Mushroom Farm as roughly equal to 3 Fishing Huts**, which makes it a strong pivot once your economy can support it.
