<h2 id="what-makes-tornado-race-different">
  What makes Tornado Race different
</h2>

The central mistake players make in **All Will Fall Tornado Race** is bringing the wrong instinct from other scenarios.

In **Oil Rig**, the main question is whether you can keep raising the rig fast enough to stay ahead of the ocean. In **Tanker Truck**, the main question is whether your fuel economy can keep you moving between salvage zones. In **Tornado Race**, the colony is still important, but it only matters because it feeds one fixed evacuation objective.

That objective is simple on paper:

- survive long enough to keep producing
- find **4 Thruster Engines**
- finish the evacuation platform
- board the colony before **Day 60**

That is why Tornado Race should be played as a **deadline scenario** first and a **colony-builder** second.

<GuideCallout title="The mindset that wins this map">
If a build decision does not help the colony survive, help Sailors find Thruster Engines, help Engineers finish Power Grid, or help the final platform stay on schedule, it probably is not urgent enough for Tornado Race.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="best-current-timeline">
  Best current timeline for Tornado Race
</h2>

This is the most practical way to turn the current public notes into an actual run plan.

<GuideDataTable
  title="Best-current Tornado Race timeline"
  rows={tornadoRaceTimelineRows}
/>

### Days 1–10: stabilize survival and open exploration

The first 10 days are not about beauty, happiness-maxing, or elaborate city shape. They are about reaching a point where:

- food is no longer collapsing
- water is no longer collapsing
- research is active
- Sailor exploration is not being delayed into the second phase of the scenario

This is why “Day 1, send Sailors exploring” matters so much. If the scenario really needs 25–35 days to find all 4 Thruster Engines, late exploration is one of the easiest ways to lose before you even realize you are losing.

### Days 10–20: stop solving the wrong problems

By this point, the colony should be functional, not perfect. The main question is whether you are already transitioning into the real scenario bottlenecks.

That means:

- stop overbuilding long-term comfort
- stop treating Sailors as optional utility
- stop assuming the platform can wait until later
- start tracking the run against a real deadline

### Days 20–35: lock in the research and material path

This is the practical middle where Tornado Race starts punishing vague planning.

A good state here is:

- Thruster Engine progress is being measured
- platform materials are being watched actively
- research is no longer drifting
- your core routes look like something you can reinforce later, not something you will have to rebuild from scratch

### By Day 40: Power Grid should be finished

This is the strongest public timing benchmark on the page, and it matters because it stops Power Grid from becoming one of those “we will do it after this next comfort upgrade” unlocks that quietly destroys the run.

If you are around Day 40 and still talking about Power Grid like a future luxury, you are probably behind.

### Days 50–57: reinforce before panic

The public Tornado Race notes make a useful distinction here: the game starts selling you the storm pressure earlier, but the real building damage pressure does not spike until **Day 58**.

That means the smarter play is to reinforce **before** the panic window instead of during it.

### Days 58–59: common finish window

This is one of the most useful competitor facts because it tells you what “late but still normal” actually looks like.

If you are finishing on Day 58 or 59, you are not necessarily barely scraping by in some weird edge case. You are in the public common completion window.

### Day 60: hard fail

This is the simplest benchmark on the page and still the one that matters most. The storm does not care whether your colony looks almost ready.

<h2 id="what-good-enough-actually-means">
  What “good enough” actually means in Tornado Race
</h2>

A lot of bad Tornado Race advice says “do not optimize for perfection,” but then never defines what a usable target actually looks like.

This is the best current version of that answer.

### Happiness

The current public Tornado Race notes explicitly say to get **Workers to 60% happiness, not 80%**.

That is important because it gives you a real anti-overbuilding benchmark. In other words:

- you are not trying to build a dream colony
- you are trying to keep the colony stable enough that the evacuation plan can continue

### Sailors

Treat this as the most practical current benchmark:

- **2 Sailors minimum** if you are barely functional
- **3 to 4 Sailors** if your economy can support them without creating a food or water crash

This is one of the few scenarios where Sailors are close to a direct win-condition role rather than a nice logistics bonus.

### Food and water

If you want a usable “good enough” rule, aim for:

- a **small but real surplus**
- enough to absorb one bad event or one temporary misallocation
- not a giant late-game stockpile fantasy

Using the current public beginner-math benchmarks of **0.5 food** and **0.4 water** per colonist per day as a planning reference, the point is not to build massive abundance. The point is to avoid falling into deficit while platform work and exploration stay active.

### Structures

“Good enough” in Tornado Race does **not** mean “barely stable until the storm finally matters.”

It means:

- stable enough to survive the true late pressure
- not overengineered
- not beautiful
- but reinforced before Day 58 turns weakness into a real punishment

<h2 id="platform-checklist">
  Evacuation platform checklist (and what to prioritize first)
</h2>

Before looking at the checklist itself, use this priority order:

1. missing **Thruster Engines**
2. missing **Power Grid**
3. missing **steel / concrete**
4. colony improvements that do not help any of the above

This matters because Tornado Race is not lost when the checklist is unknown. It is lost when players focus on the wrong bottleneck first.

### Why Thruster Engines are the scariest requirement

Steel and concrete are still serious problems, but they are familiar production problems. The **Thruster Engines** are the uncomfortable part because they are currently described as **Sailor exploration drops**.

That means if exploration starts too late, you can lose time in a way normal production cannot easily repair.

Current known requirements:

- **4 Thruster Engines** (Sailor exploration drops)
- **20 steel**
- **40 concrete**
- **Power Grid**

<GuideCallout title="The real problem is not the checklist">
Most failed runs are not missing the requirements because they are unknown. They fail because players start preparing **too late** or **prepare in the wrong order**.
</GuideCallout>

### Thruster Engines (exploration bottleneck)

- Typically completed in **Day 25–35 window**
- Fully dependent on **Sailor uptime**

**If you do not have at least 2–3 Sailors active before Day 20, you are already late.**

### Steel and concrete (production bottleneck)

This is where most guides are too vague.

The correct way to think about it:

#### Phase 1 (before Day 20)

- do **not** rush steel or concrete
- focus on survival
- focus on Sailor setup
- focus on basic research uptime

#### Phase 2 (Day 20–35)

- begin **tracking** platform materials
- identify where steel and concrete will come from
- reserve space
- avoid wasting metal on non-essential builds

You do **not** need full production yet, but you do need a path.

#### Phase 3 (after Day 35)

This is the real start of platform preparation.

By now you should already:

- know where steel and concrete will come from
- have production partially running or ready to start immediately
- be treating materials as part of the platform timeline, not as future housekeeping

### Practical material timing rule

- **If you start serious steel/concrete production after Day 40, you are behind**
- **If you wait until Thruster Engines are done before starting materials, you will stall**

Correct approach:

- exploration and materials should **overlap**, not sequence

### What to do if you are short on materials

If you are missing steel or concrete late:

1. **Stop all non-essential builds immediately**
2. redirect workers away from:
   - comfort
   - decoration
   - overbuilt housing
3. prioritize:
   - platform materials
   - minimal survival loop

<GuideCallout title="Late-game rule">
At this stage, steel and concrete are not “resources.” They are **time converted into survival probability**.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="when-to-move-between-zones">
  When to move between zones (time-based decisions)
</h2>

Zone movement is not about “is there more loot here.”

It is about **whether staying delays your evacuation timeline**.

### Before Day 20

Stay if:

- you still need basic survival setup
- Sailor exploration is not fully online

Leave if:

- the current zone is slowing down Sailor deployment

### Day 20–35 (critical window)

This is the most important movement phase.

Leave your zone if:

- you are not accelerating Thruster Engine discovery
- you are spending time on inefficient resource gathering
- your next zone clearly improves exploration or materials

<GuideCallout title="Critical rule">
If you are still in the same zone at Day 35 and do not have at least 2 Thruster Engines, you are very likely behind.
</GuideCallout>

### After Day 40

Movement becomes riskier.

Leave only if:

- the new zone directly helps platform completion
- or your current zone cannot support required materials

Otherwise:

- stabilize
- commit
- finish

### After Day 50

Avoid movement unless absolutely necessary.

At this stage:

- time matters more than marginal efficiency
- stability matters more than expansion

<h2 id="phase-shift">
  Phase shift: when colony growth stops and evacuation starts
</h2>

This is the part many Tornado Race guides rush past, even though it is where runs are often won or lost.

<GuideDataTable
  title="Tornado Race phase shift"
  rows={tornadoRacePhaseRows}
/>

### What should happen in Phase 3

Once platform commitment begins, stop thinking like a general colony manager and start thinking like an evacuation coordinator.

#### 1. Freeze nonessential expansion

Do not start new comfort projects unless they solve a real bottleneck.

At this point, your build budget belongs mostly to:

- platform completion
- exploration continuation
- essential reinforcement
- minimum viable food and water stability

#### 2. Reassign labor aggressively

A good Phase 3 labor rule is:

- keep enough Workers on food and water to avoid collapse
- keep Sailors on the remaining exploration bottleneck
- keep Engineers on the research or infrastructure still blocking the platform
- cut any job that improves the colony but does not improve escape odds

#### 3. Reinforce only what truly matters

Do not try to harden the whole colony. Harden:

- routes needed for platform completion
- routes needed for critical material inputs
- structures that would cause a real cascade if they fail

#### 4. Spend like these are final-run materials

This is the right phase to ask one simple question before every expensive decision:

- does this make evacuation more likely?

If the answer is no, it needs a very strong excuse.

<h2 id="cross-scenario-comparison">
  How Tornado Race differs from Oil Rig and Tanker Truck
</h2>

This comparison matters because players coming from other scenarios often carry the wrong habits into Tornado Race.

### Compared with Oil Rig

In <Link href="/all-will-fall/oil-rig-guide/">Oil Rig</Link>, the core issue is surviving the rising-water platform economy. Vertical planning and raising the platform dominate the scenario.

In Tornado Race, the real center is not repeated platform raising. It is whether your colony can keep functioning while exploration, research, and evacuation all stay on schedule.

### Compared with Tanker Truck

In <Link href="/all-will-fall/tanker-truck-guide/">Tanker Truck</Link>, the key pressure is fuel continuity between zones.

In Tornado Race, fuel logic still matters, but the scenario pushes harder on:

- **Sailor exploration timing**
- **Power Grid timing**
- **late-stage reinforcement**
- **platform-first commitment**

The fastest mental adjustment is this:

- Sailors matter more here
- good enough matters more here
- deadline commitment matters more here

<h2 id="failure-snapshots">
  Failure snapshots (what a losing run actually looks like)
</h2>

Most Tornado Race failures come from the same underlying pattern:

- Sailors were late
- Power Grid was late
- platform commitment was late
- reinforcement was late

The problem is that these mistakes often do not look fatal while the colony still appears stable.

That is why this section is more useful than a generic mistake list: it shows what those mistakes look like **inside a real run**.

### Snapshot 1: Day 45 — everything looks fine, but you are already losing

Typical signs:

- food and water are stable
- buildings are not collapsing
- colony looks clean
- **only 1–2 Thruster Engines found**

This is a **false stability state**.

You are not failing because of survival. You are failing because **exploration started too late**.

### Snapshot 2: Day 40 — Power Grid still not started

Typical signs:

- research feels under control
- you are still unlocking mid-tier systems
- Power Grid is “next”

This is already behind schedule.

**Power Grid should be done or nearly done by Day 40.**

### Snapshot 3: Day 50 — strong colony, no platform progress

Typical signs:

- high happiness
- strong production
- no obvious material shortage
- platform barely started

This is the classic **wrong win condition** problem.

You built a good colony. But Tornado Race is not about building a good colony.

### Snapshot 4: Day 58 — panic mode

Typical signs:

- suddenly reinforcing everything
- moving workers randomly
- trying to finish the platform under pressure

At this point, most runs are already decided.

<GuideCallout title="Core lesson">
If your first panic moment happens after Day 55, the real mistake probably happened **20 days earlier**.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### How are Sailors used differently in Tornado Race than in other scenarios?

In Tornado Race, Sailors are unusually important because the current public platform requirements tie **Thruster Engines** to exploration drops. That makes them much closer to a direct win-condition role than in many other scenarios.

### When should I send Sailors exploring in Tornado Race?

The best current answer is **Day 1**. The strongest public benchmark says finding all 4 Thruster Engines typically takes **25–35 days**, which means late exploration is one of the easiest ways to fall behind the timer.

### What if I still have not found all 4 Thruster Engines by the midgame?

Cut nonessential expansion, keep Sailor uptime high, and make sure your zone movement decisions are still helping the exploration timeline instead of only polishing the colony.

### What counts as good enough food and water before I pivot harder into the platform?

Aim for a **small positive surplus** rather than a giant reserve. The point is to stop the colony from slipping into deficit while exploration, research, and platform work stay active.

### How much happiness do I really need in Tornado Race?

The strongest current public benchmark is **Workers at 60% happiness**, not 80%. This scenario rewards sufficiency more than optimization.

### When should Power Grid feel urgent?

By the time you are approaching the middle of the timer, Power Grid should already feel like an immediate target. The clearest current public benchmark is **completed by Day 40**.

### When does the tornado actually start damaging weak structures more seriously?

The current public notes say storm visuals and wind pressure build from **Day 50 onward**, but **actual damage pressure does not increase until Day 58**.

### What is the realistic finish window for a successful run?

The current public notes say **Day 58–59** is a common completion window, which makes it the most useful expectation-setting benchmark on the page.
