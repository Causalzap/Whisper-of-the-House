<h2 id="what-airships-are-in-shifting-sands">
  What airships are in Shifting Sands?
</h2>

Airships in **Shifting Sands** are not just background vehicles.

They are part of the DLC’s main progression identity.

The practical way to think about them is:

**you start with a useful ship shell, then turn it into a flying ship, and only after that do the larger ship goals start making sense.**

That matters because a lot of players assume “airship” means one late-game unlock.

That is not really how the route reads in practice.

The progression is closer to this:

1. **starter ship**
2. **first flight upgrade**
3. **airship camp encounters**
4. **medium / Falcon-class progression**
5. **later ship combat experimentation**

<GuideCallout title="What this page does and does not confirm">
This guide covers the **confirmed progression beats** from a full Shifting Sands run.

It does **not** pretend to lock in a final “best ship build” or a fully solved medium-ship material spreadsheet.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="do-you-start-with-a-ship-in-shifting-sands">
  Do you start with a ship in Shifting Sands?
</h2>

**Yes, effectively.**

Very early in the run, the player opens a chest containing **ship building materials and workbenches**, then immediately realizes the nearby hull is now their ship.

That starter ship is not just transportation.

It quickly becomes:

- your **first storage platform**
- your **first crafting cluster**
- your **first mobile safe point**
- your **first version of a moving base**

### Why the starter ship matters

A lot of early Shifting Sands systems feel overwhelming on purpose.

The starter ship helps stabilize that.

It gives you somewhere to:

- dump loot
- place workbenches
- test early build pieces
- move between river and delta points without fully committing to a land base yet

<h2 id="how-to-unlock-your-first-flying-ship">
  How to unlock your first flying ship
</h2>

The first big Airship breakthrough is tied to the **Sobec Crocodile** route.

That is the cleanest point where ship play stops being “boat plus storage” and starts being **true anti-gravity flight**.

### Step 1: Reach the Sobec Crocodile route

The run’s next ship milestone only opens after pushing the early progression to the **Sobec** fight.

That matters because tribe progression and ship progression overlap here.

You are not just killing a boss for loot.

You are killing a boss to unlock the next real ship layer.

### Step 2: Beat Sobec Crocodile

Once Sobec is defeated, the pyramid behind that route opens up.

That pyramid is the major turning point.

### Step 3: Loot the pyramid rewards

The key rewards shown in the run are:

- **marine anti-gravity skid**
- **wings**
- **engine parts**
- **6 power crystals**

That is the first fully confirmed flight package.

### Step 4: Install the parts on your small ship

The first real flight setup shown in the run is:

1. install the **marine anti-gravity skid** underneath the ship
2. place **a wing on each side**
3. place the **small anti-gravity engine**
4. load fuel

### Step 5: Fuel the ship

The run uses the **6 power crystals** from the pyramid loot.

That gives the ship about **12 hours of flight**.

### Step 6: Take off

This is the moment where the ship is no longer confined to waterways.

That is the practical unlock players are actually chasing.

<h2 id="what-first-flight-actually-changes">
  What first flight actually changes
</h2>

The first takeoff is not just cosmetic.

It changes how you move through the map.

Once the ship can fly, it becomes much easier to:

- land near points of interest
- skip pure river routing
- approach desert locations more flexibly
- use the ship as a repositioning tool instead of a water-only transport

That is the point where Shifting Sands starts feeling like an **airship DLC**, not just a new zone with boats.

### Why this matters more than raw speed

The main benefit is not just movement speed.

It is **routing freedom**.

That means:

- faster detours
- easier loot drops and pickups
- more flexible dungeon approach paths
- better control over where you stop and reset

<GuideCallout title="What the first ship is best at">
Your first flying ship is best used as a **mobility unlock and route shortcut**, not as a final combat flagship.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="airship-camps-and-why-they-matter">
  Airship camps and why they matter
</h2>

Once the ship is airborne, one of the clearest “this system is bigger than I thought” moments is the first set of **airship camps**.

These are not decorative locations.

They are progression tests.

### Small airship camp

The first small camp encounter shows:

- a parked airship in the center
- sand bandits guarding it
- explosive red barrels
- a dangerous giant ballista

That is a very clean signal that ship content is tied to combat spaces, not just crafting benches.

### Medium airship camp

The medium camp raises the bar immediately.

It has:

- a **much larger layout**
- a **massive force field**
- defenders behind protection
- more pressure on positioning and siege tools

In the run, a **battering ram** can damage the force field, but the ram also breaks after one hit, so the camp is not fully solved there.

That is exactly the kind of place where players get baited into thinking they are underprepared only because of combat, when the real issue is often **ship-stage progression and missing siege tools**.

<h2 id="can-you-turn-airships-into-a-floating-base">
  Can you turn airships into a floating base?
</h2>

Yes, that is part of the appeal.

The run explicitly treats the early ship as a **mobile base**, and later notes that ships can be customized into more of a **floating base** if you want to keep tribe workers and storage with you while traveling.

That does **not** mean you should force that design immediately.

### Best way to think about ship layouts

There are two clean directions:

1. **travel-first ship**
   - lighter
   - cleaner deck
   - better for fast map routing

2. **floating-base ship**
   - more storage
   - more utility placement
   - better for long expeditions

The right answer depends on your playstyle.

If your land base is already stable, the small ship works best as a **travel ship**.

If you want one all-purpose platform, you can lean more into the **mobile base** route.

<GuideCallout title="Design tip">
Do not confuse “can be a floating base” with “must be a floating base.”

A cleaner travel ship is often stronger than a cluttered all-in-one deck.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="falcon-class-airship-progression">
  Falcon-Class Airship progression
</h2>

This is where the Airship Guide stops being just about first flight and becomes a real **ship progression page**.

### When Falcon-Class progression starts

The next big breakpoint happens at **tribe level 3**.

That unlocks **premium ship building**, and the next ship goal becomes a **Falcon-class airship**.

### What is confirmed at this stage

The run confirms these progression beats:

- **tribe level 3 unlocks premium ship building**
- the quest points toward building a **Falcon-class airship**
- the body of the Falcon-class ship can be crafted
- the larger ship then still needs follow-up components like **wings, sails, rudder, and engine progression**

### Why players stall here

This is where the guide needs to stay honest.

The run repeatedly hits medium-tier bottlenecks such as:

- **metal plates**
- **iron wire**
- **large ingot costs**
- **medium anti-gravity engine requirements**
- **medium power crystal / ship fuel issues**
- **missing engine cores at the moment you want immediate takeoff**

So the clean takeaway is **not** “tribe level 3 instantly gives you a finished flying medium ship.”

The real takeaway is:

**tribe level 3 unlocks the serious ship game, but the larger ship still has its own parts-and-power wall.**

<h2 id="best-practical-airship-order">
  Best practical Airship order
</h2>

If you are trying to progress cleanly, this is the best practical order:

1. **Use the starter ship as your early mobile base**
2. **Stabilize your gear and tribe enough to beat Sobec**
3. **Loot the pyramid and install the first anti-gravity setup**
4. **Use the small flying ship to expand map control**
5. **Scout airship camps, but do not assume all of them are ready to clear immediately**
6. **Push tribe progression to level 3**
7. **Unlock premium ship building**
8. **Start Falcon-class progression**
9. **Treat medium-tier engine and fuel requirements as a real second wall**

This is the version of the route that keeps expectations realistic.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Do you get a ship early in Soulmask: Shifting Sands?

Yes. You get access to a practical starter ship very early, along with ship building materials and workbenches.

### When does your ship first become a flying airship?

After the **Sobec Crocodile** route, when you loot the pyramid parts and install the first anti-gravity setup.

### What parts are confirmed for the first flying ship?

The first confirmed setup uses a **marine anti-gravity skid**, **two wings**, and a **small anti-gravity engine**, then fuels the ship with power crystals.

### How much fuel did the first ship use?

In this run, **6 power crystals** gave about **12 hours of flight**.

### Are airship camps easy loot stops?

No. Even the first camps function more like progression checks than free chest rooms.

### What unlocks Falcon-class airship progression?

**Tribe level 3** and **premium ship building**.

### Does crafting the Falcon-class body mean the ship is ready to fly?

No. The larger ship still needs more components and can hit medium-tier bottlenecks around engines and power.

### Should your first ship be a floating base or a travel ship?

Usually a **travel-first ship** is cleaner early, but the system supports turning ships into a more complete floating base later.
