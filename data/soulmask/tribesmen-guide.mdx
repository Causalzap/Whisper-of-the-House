<h2 id="complete-loop-rule">
  The core rule: one Tribesman needs one complete loop
</h2>

The most important rule for Tribesmen is simple: **a job is only real when the full loop around that job works**.

A worker assigned to farming is not useful just because they are called a farmer. They need farmland, seeds, water, a Grainery, storage access, and a safe route between those pieces. A gatherer is not useful just because they have a gathering role. They need the right tool, a precise map marker, a reachable resource cluster, and somewhere to deposit materials.

This is why many “AI problems” are actually loop problems.

Use this mental model:

| Worker type | Complete loop means |
|---|---|
| **Gatherer** | Tool → resource marker → safe path → storage |
| **Farmer** | Seeds → field → water → Grainery → storage |
| **Animal worker** | Food → water → breeding settings → tools → population floor → storage |
| **Crafter** | Input materials → station → recipe → fuel or tool if needed → output storage |
| **Fighter** | Weapon → armor → food → bandages → deployment → commands |

Once you think this way, Tribesmen stop feeling random. You are no longer assigning jobs. You are building loops that workers can own.

<h2 id="what-tribesmen-actually-do-in-shifting-sands">
  What Tribesmen actually do in Shifting Sands
</h2>

Tribesmen matter because they remove repetition first and multiply output later. Early on, they take over boring work that repeatedly interrupts exploration, crafting, combat preparation, or base expansion. Later, they become specialists who can outperform your main character in specific roles.

That progression usually follows this path:

1. **Recruit safely**
2. **Read the Tribesman’s profession, proficiencies, talents, and defects**
3. **Assign one useful job**
4. **Complete the loop around that job**
5. **Train the worker through leveling, Training Ground, or direct control**
6. **Scale into farming, animals, crafting, combat, and outpost systems**

In other words, Tribesmen are not just workers. They are a progression layer. The better your roster becomes, the smoother your base economy becomes.

<h2 id="how-to-recruit-tribesmen-safely">
  How to recruit Tribesmen safely
</h2>

The first major breakpoint is unlocking **recruitment** early enough that enemies can become workers instead of only becoming loot. The system is easy to misunderstand because the game separates the takedown step from the actual conversion step.

The first half is **Deter**. You weaken an eligible target until the Deter prompt appears, then use it to force the target into a recruitable state. That does not finish recruitment. After that, you still need to keep the target alive until their **Recognition reaches 500**, which is when **Recruit** becomes available.

Once this distinction is clear, the system becomes much easier to manage:

| Step | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Weaken target** | Reduce health without killing them |
| **Deter** | Put the target into a recruitable state |
| **Stabilize** | Prevent bleeding, starvation, heat, or enemy danger |
| **Wait for 500 Recognition** | Let the target become recruitable |
| **Recruit** | Add them to your tribe |

The most common mistake is treating Deter as the end of recruitment. It is not. Deter is only the setup. The real challenge is keeping the unconscious NPC alive long enough to recruit them.

<GuideCallout title="Recruitment safety checklist">
After Deter, do not leave the target lying in the open. Put **bandages** or **broth / liquid food** directly into their inventory if they are bleeding or close to death, then move them to safety before waiting for 500 Recognition.
</GuideCallout>

You can move an unconscious target by carrying them on your shoulder. If you have a llama with an extended seat, you can also use it to move recruits over longer distances. This is especially useful when you Deter someone far from your base, inside a dangerous camp, or in a desert area where the environment itself can become a threat.

<h2 id="shifting-sands-recruitment-risks">
  Shifting Sands recruitment risks
</h2>

Recruitment is less forgiving in **Shifting Sands** because the desert can punish unconscious NPCs before you finish converting them.

A recruitable target lying on the ground is still vulnerable to:

| Threat | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Bleeding** | Can kill the target before Recognition reaches 500 |
| **Heat** | Dangerous when the target is left exposed in desert areas |
| **Sandstorms** | Can turn a safe-looking recruit location into a hazard |
| **Nearby enemies** | Camps and patrols can still threaten the body |
| **Long travel distance** | Makes it easier to lose the recruit before stabilizing them |

For that reason, Shifting Sands recruitment should be treated as a rescue operation, not just a combat action. Once the target is down, check their condition, treat bleeding, feed them if needed, and move them out of danger.

The simple rule is: **a deterred target is not secured until they are alive, fed, treated, and away from immediate danger**.

This is also why your first few recruitment runs should not be too ambitious. It is better to recruit a useful nearby worker safely than to Deter a better-looking target deep in hostile territory and lose them before the recruit step becomes available.

<h2 id="how-to-judge-a-good-tribesman">
  How to judge a good Tribesman: caps, profession, talents, and attributes
</h2>

A good Tribesman is not just the highest-level recruit in front of you. The best long-term recruits are the ones whose **profession, proficiency caps, talents, and attributes** all point toward the same job.

Before investing in a Tribesman, check these five things:

| Check | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Roster cap** | Your tribe size is limited, so every long-term slot matters. The initial Control node supports 3 Tribesmen, while a fully upgraded Connection Enhancement can raise the cap to 18. |
| **Proficiency cap** | 120+ is a major target because proficiency milestones can unlock useful buffs at 30, 60, 90, and 120. |
| **Profession match** | Profession affects which proficiencies are more likely to have high caps. |
| **Talents and Defects** | Tribesmen gain an Advantage Talent every 10 levels and have a chance to remove a Defect every 5 levels. |
| **Attributes** | Worker stats matter too: Strength supports gathering-focused roles, while Perception is especially useful for crafting and production efficiency. |

For practical evaluation, do not ask only “Is this recruit strong?” Ask:

1. What job would this Tribesman own?
2. Does their profession support that job?
3. Can the relevant proficiency reach 120+?
4. Do their talents improve the same role?
5. Are their defects tolerable or removable?
6. Are their attributes aligned with the job?

This is the difference between filling your tribe with bodies and building a roster of specialists.

### Profession and proficiency direction reference

Use profession as a **directional filter**, not as a guaranteed cap table. Professions influence where high caps are likely to appear, but individual recruits can still vary. Always confirm the actual numbers on the in-game character page before you invest rare gear, training time, or a permanent roster slot.

| Profession type | What to check first |
|---|---|
| **Warrior** | Melee weapon caps, survivability talents, combat defects |
| **Hunter** | Bow or spear potential, ranged combat talents, mobility or survival traits |
| **Guard** | Defensive talents, shield or frontline weapon potential, durability |
| **Laborer** | Logging, mining, planting, harvesting, Strength, gathering-related talents |
| **Porter** | Processing and support proficiencies, hauling value, base utility traits |
| **Craftsman** | Crafting, alchemy, weapon crafting, armor crafting, cooking, Perception |

This table should help you decide what to inspect first. It should not replace the character sheet. If the in-game page says a recruit has poor caps for the job you wanted, trust the page over the profession name.

For example, a Warrior with 120 potential in a weapon you actually use is much more valuable than a random high-level recruit with poor weapon caps. A Craftsman with strong production traits and high crafting potential can become part of your entire base economy. A Laborer with strong gathering caps and Strength investment can save more time than a better-looking combat recruit who does not fit your current needs.

### Why 120+ proficiency matters

Proficiency is not just a flavor stat. It controls how far a Tribesman can grow in a role. The 120 threshold matters because it is tied to the highest major proficiency milestone.

In simple terms:

| Proficiency profile | What it usually means |
|---|---|
| **Low current proficiency, low cap** | Poor long-term candidate for that role |
| **Low current proficiency, high cap** | Good Training Ground or direct control candidate |
| **High current proficiency, low cap** | Useful now, but limited later |
| **High current proficiency, 120+ cap** | Strong long-term specialist candidate |

This is why you should not judge a recruit only by current numbers. A high-level worker with strong starting proficiency can be immediately useful, but a lower-current recruit with better caps may become stronger after training.

<GuideCallout title="Recruit evaluation rule">
Do not judge a Tribesman only by current level. Check their profession, actual proficiency caps, talents, defects, attributes, and whether they have a clear long-term role in your base.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="quick-deter-check">
  How to quickly decide if a target is worth Deter
</h2>

You do not need to fully solve a recruit’s future before you Deter them. You only need to decide whether they are worth the risk of the capture attempt.

Use this quick filter before committing:

| Question | Good sign |
|---|---|
| **Are they safe to extract?** | The camp, patrol route, weather, and distance make rescue realistic |
| **Do they fill a current need?** | You need a worker, fighter, crafter, farmer, or gatherer right now |
| **Does their profession match the role?** | The profession points toward the job you want them to do |
| **Are they high enough level to inspect seriously?** | Higher-level targets are often more worth the risk than random early bodies |
| **Can you keep them alive after Deter?** | You have bandages, food, transport, and a safe place to wait for Recognition |
| **Is the area worth returning to?** | Even if this target fails, the camp has more possible recruits or useful loot |

If the answer is “no” to extraction safety, skip the target. A perfect-looking recruit is worthless if they bleed out, cook in the desert, or die beside hostile patrols before Recognition reaches 500.

For early runs, prioritize recruits who are **safe enough to capture** and **useful enough to assign immediately**. Save dangerous deep-camp recruitment for when you have better gear, mounts, supplies, and a clearer roster plan.

<h2 id="first-jobs-that-actually-remove-friction">
  First jobs that actually remove friction
</h2>

Your first recruit is usually most valuable as a **worker**, not a fighter. Early combat followers can help, but early base friction is usually the thing slowing your progression every few minutes.

The best early jobs are the boring ones because boring jobs are the ones that repeatedly interrupt progress. If a worker takes over the task you are most tired of doing by hand, that worker is already useful.

Strong first assignments usually include:

| Early job | Why it is useful |
|---|---|
| **Linen / cloth chain** | Reduces interruptions in crafting and gear preparation |
| **Planks / wood products** | Stabilizes common construction and production needs |
| **Basic gathering** | Keeps common resources moving while you explore |
| **Farm support** | Becomes strong once farmland, seeds, storage, and water are ready |
| **Simple production station ownership** | Helps one station stay supplied instead of becoming another chore |

Do not pick the first job from an abstract tier list. Pick it from your current pain point.

If cloth keeps blocking you, prioritize linen. If wood products keep disappearing, prioritize planks. If you keep stopping expeditions to gather basics, assign a gatherer. The best first Tribesman is the one who removes the most repeated manual work from your current route.

<h2 id="why-tribesmen-stop-working">
  Why Tribesmen stop working
</h2>

When a Tribesman stops working, the problem is usually not “bad AI” in the abstract. Most of the time, the worker is blocked by one missing requirement in the loop.

Use this troubleshooting order:

1. **Missing materials**
2. **Missing or broken tools**
3. **Wrong assignment**
4. **Wrong or imprecise map marker**
5. **Bad work area or pathing**
6. **No valid storage or output destination**
7. **Mood, food, water, or survival issue**

That order solves most false “AI problems” quickly. A Loom worker who stands still may simply be missing fiber. A gatherer may have the right job but no usable tool. A farm worker may be assigned correctly but unable to complete the route because field, storage, seeds, and water are not actually connected.

Gathering has one especially easy-to-miss problem: **the map marker**.

For gathering jobs, you often need to manually mark the resource location on the map and select that marker in the work settings. If the marker is too vague, too far from the resource cluster, or not the marker you intended, the Tribesman may technically have a gathering job but still stand around doing nothing.

<GuideCallout title="Gathering marker mistake">
If a gatherer has tools but still does nothing, move the map marker directly onto the resource cluster and reselect that marker in the work tab. A vague marker near the area can be enough to break the job.
</GuideCallout>

The important shift is to stop asking, “Why is this worker broken?” and start asking, “Which part of this loop is incomplete?”

That question usually leads you to the answer faster.

<h2 id="farming-and-gathering-loops">
  Farming and gathering loops that actually work
</h2>

Farming and gathering are the first systems where Tribesmen can start saving serious time. They only become reliable, however, when the support pieces exist around the job.

For farming, a stable early setup needs:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Farmland** | Gives the worker a valid field |
| **Assigned seeds** | Tells the system what to plant |
| **Grainery** | Gives crops and seeds a storage logic |
| **Water access** | Allows watering to happen consistently |
| **Water tanks** | Prevents the loop from stalling when water is not immediately available |
| **Valid worker assignment** | Connects the Tribesman to the farm role |
| **Reachable storage** | Lets outputs return to the base economy |

Gathering needs fewer buildings, but it is just as sensitive to setup:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Correct tool** | Lets the worker harvest the resource |
| **Precise map marker** | Tells the worker where to go |
| **Nearby or reachable storage** | Lets gathered materials return to the base |
| **Safe route** | Prevents interruptions or stuck movement |
| **Narrow task focus** | Avoids spreading one worker across too many vague jobs |

<h2 id="animal-loops-and-specialists">
  Animal loops and why specialists matter
</h2>

Animal management is one of the clearest points where Tribesmen stop being convenience tools and start becoming a real background economy.

A breeding-and-slaughter setup works because it turns repeated maintenance into a steady supply of resources, but only when the conditions are controlled.

A stable animal setup usually needs:

| Requirement | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Food** | Keeps animals alive and breeding |
| **Water** | Prevents the pen from stalling |
| **Correct worker assignment** | Lets a Tribesman maintain the job |
| **Butcher’s knives or required tools** | Allows slaughter and processing |
| **Minimum population floor** | Protects breeding stock from being wiped out |
| **Storage access** | Moves meat, bones, feathers, and other output into the economy |

In a chicken setup, for example, one worker can maintain the system as long as food, water, slaughter settings, tools, and population limits are already configured. The value is not only convenience. It is that meat, bones, feathers, and related outputs start arriving without constant manual supervision.

This is also where specialist investment starts to matter. A Tribesman who stays on a stable animal, crafting, or production job long enough can become meaningfully better than a generic worker. The job is no longer just being “done.” It is being improved by a worker whose proficiencies and talents match the task.

<h2 id="production-ownership-and-later-base-scaling">
  Production ownership and later base scaling
</h2>

Later production is where Tribesmen stop feeling like helpers and start feeling like a workforce. This shift happens when a worker no longer “occasionally assists,” but instead **owns a station or material role**.

That is especially clear with kiln and furnace work. Once later smelting chains appear, a base becomes easier to run when workers are divided by responsibility:

| Role example | What they own |
|---|---|
| **Tin worker** | Tin-related smelting and material flow |
| **Copper worker** | Copper production and input/output movement |
| **Bronze worker** | Bronze chain and related station work |
| **Wood specialist** | Planks, fuel support, and construction materials |
| **Textile specialist** | Linen, cloth, and gear-related inputs |
| **Farm worker** | Planting, watering, harvesting, and crop return |
| **Animal worker** | Food, slaughter settings, and animal output |

For production workers, pay attention to **Perception** as well as profession and crafting proficiency. A Tribesman with the right production profession, strong crafting caps, useful talents, and Perception investment is much more valuable than a random body assigned to a station.

Early workers reduce interruptions. Later workers let the base keep functioning at a scale that would otherwise require constant babysitting.

<h2 id="training-ground-priority">
  Training Ground: how to unlock it, build it, and choose candidates
</h2>

Training Ground is one of the most important **Shifting Sands** systems because it turns Tribesmen from “usable recruits” into long-term specialists.

Without Training Ground, a mediocre recruit often stays mediocre. With Training Ground, a recruit with good potential but imperfect current skills can still become valuable. Tribesmen can develop weapon proficiencies, learn from stronger tribe members, and become more specialized over time.

### How to unlock Training Ground

Training Ground is unlocked through the **Mask tech tree**, under the **Tribe / Management** side of progression. Treat it as a mid-game tribe development unlock rather than an early decoration piece.

As a practical benchmark, start looking for Training Ground once you have:

| Requirement | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **More than 5 Tribesmen** | You have enough roster depth to justify training instead of only recruiting |
| **Mid-game tribe technology** | Training Ground belongs to management progression, not the first survival steps |
| **Stable food and production loops** | Training is easier when your base already supports workers |
| **At least one high-potential recruit** | Training Ground is strongest when used on someone worth developing |

### How to check Training Ground materials

Check the **Building Workshop** in your current game version before farming materials for Training Ground. Recipes can vary across patches, settings, or server versions, so the safest workflow is:

1. unlock the relevant Mask technology
2. open the Building Workshop
3. find Training Ground
4. pin or screenshot the recipe
5. farm the exact materials shown in your game

This is more reliable than farming from an outdated recipe list.

### When should you build Training Ground?

Use this priority table:

| Situation | Training Ground priority |
|---|---|
| You only have 1–2 recruits | Low. Stabilize food, tools, and first jobs first. |
| You have 4–6 stable workers | Medium. Start preparing your best long-term specialists. |
| You found a recruit with strong caps but weak current skills | High. This is exactly the kind of recruit Training Ground can salvage. |
| You are building a combat party | High. Train weapon proficiencies and useful combat talents. |
| Your base already has stable production loops | Very high. Roster development becomes the next progression layer. |
| You have duplicate workers with similar jobs | Medium to high. Use training to separate them into clearer specialties. |

The best Training Ground candidates are not always the strongest recruits right now. They are the recruits with the best future use.

Good candidates include:

1. a high-cap worker with weak current proficiency
2. a combat recruit with strong survivability
3. a crafter with useful production traits
4. a worker with one major defect but strong long-term value
5. a specialist whose role your base will always need

<GuideCallout title="Training Ground priority">
Build Training Ground after your first production loops are stable and you have recruits worth developing. It is strongest for high-cap workers, combat followers, crafters, and Tribesmen with useful roles but imperfect current skills.
</GuideCallout>

Do not treat Training Ground as decoration. Treat it as the bridge between “this recruit is usable” and “this Tribesman is part of my long-term roster.”

<h2 id="direct-control-tribesman-training">
  Direct control: the fastest way to train the right body
</h2>

One of the most important Tribesman systems is **direct control**. After repairing the relevant Mask control node, you can put the mask on a Tribesman and control that body directly.

This matters for training because actions performed while controlling a Tribesman use that Tribesman’s skills and proficiencies. Body, weapon, and crafting experience gained during direct control goes to the controlled Tribesman.

There is also an extra benefit: **Awareness EXP still goes to the chieftain controlling the Tribesman**. That means direct control can improve the controlled body while still helping your overall account-style progression.

Use direct control when:

| Goal | Why direct control helps |
|---|---|
| **Level a future weapon specialist** | Fight with the weapon you want that Tribesman to master |
| **Build a better crafter** | Perform relevant crafting actions on the body you want to develop |
| **Test a combat recruit** | See whether their stats, talents, and weapon caps actually feel good |
| **Speed up a specialist** | Stop waiting for passive work and train the exact role manually |

The tradeoff is that your original character sleeps while you control the Tribesman, so do this from a safe place and avoid risking an important body without backup.

Direct control is especially useful when you find a recruit with excellent caps but weak current proficiency. Training Ground can develop them over time, but direct control lets you push progress toward the exact role you care about.

<h2 id="mood-and-happiness">
  Mood and happiness: why productive Tribesmen need comfort
</h2>

A Tribesman is not only limited by tools, materials, assignments, and proficiency. Their mood also affects how reliable they feel over time.

This is easy to ignore because supply problems are more visible. When a worker lacks fiber, a tool, or a route, the cause is obvious. Mood problems are quieter. They show up as a tribe that feels less stable, less efficient, or harder to retain.

To keep Tribesmen productive, build the base around comfort as well as logistics.

| Need | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Food and drinks** | Broth, juice, fruit skewers, liked foods, reliable meals |
| **Facilities** | Outhouse, bathtub, dining table, chairs |
| **Comfort objects** | Statues and other mood-supporting furniture |
| **Work readiness** | Proper clothes, correct tools, replacement gear |
| **Stable routines** | Jobs that can be completed without constant interruption |

This belongs in the same mental model as supply chains. Food, tools, storage, and comfort are all part of keeping a specialist stable.

A worker with the right assignment can still become a problem if the base does not support their needs. The more valuable the Tribesman, the more important this becomes. You do not want your best crafter, farmer, or fighter destabilized because the base lacks basic comfort.

<h2 id="war-party-and-field-use">
  War party and field use
</h2>

Tribesmen are not only labor. They can also function as a small combat party, which changes how much pressure you have to absorb alone.

The usable limit is **up to 3 deployed followers at once**, but the raw number matters less than how they are managed. Three badly equipped followers can die quickly. One or two well-prepared followers can make boss fights, camp clears, and dangerous routes much safer.

Do not bring three random Tribesmen. Bring roles that match the fight.

| Follower type | Best use |
|---|---|
| **Warrior / Guard** | Frontline pressure, boss distraction, holding enemies in place |
| **Hunter** | Safer ranged support and camp clearing |
| **High-Endurance recruit** | Longer fights where survival matters |
| **High weapon proficiency recruit** | Better damage if you can keep them alive |
| **Defensive talent recruit** | Useful when the goal is tanking pressure rather than burst damage |

Before leaving base, check:

1. weapons
2. armor
3. food
4. bandages
5. spare supplies for longer trips
6. mask energy supplies such as Sunstones when needed
7. deployment status after reload

Manual control also matters. Combat followers are strongest when you command them onto priority targets instead of hoping they improvise perfectly. Their best value is not perfect AI. Their best value is that they split pressure, create time to heal, distract bosses, and add enough damage that hard fights stop feeling like pure solo attrition.

<GuideCallout title="Combat control rule">
A war party is strongest when you gear them, supply them, and direct them toward priority targets instead of treating them like fully autonomous fighters.
</GuideCallout>

One small habit saves a lot of frustration: after reloading, check that your followers are actually deployed before walking into danger.

<h2 id="outpost-dispatches">
  Outpost dispatches and passive rewards
</h2>

Shifting Sands also gives Tribesmen value outside normal base jobs. Once your roster is large enough, you can send groups to outposts for rewards instead of keeping every recruit inside the same production setup.

This should not replace your core workforce too early. Your best early workers should usually stay on essential jobs like linen, planks, farming, animal care, or smelting. Those jobs keep the base alive.

Outpost dispatches become more attractive when:

| Situation | Why dispatches make sense |
|---|---|
| **Your core loops are stable** | You can spare people without breaking the base |
| **You have extra recruits** | Idle roster depth becomes useful |
| **You have specialized non-essential members** | They can generate value away from base |
| **You want passive rewards** | Dispatches give your tribe another progression outlet |
| **Your base economy no longer needs every worker present** | Extra manpower can be converted into outside gains |

Treat outpost dispatches as a mid-game and later use for roster depth. The stronger your core economy becomes, the easier it is to send a separate group away without hurting your base.

The rule is simple: **do not send away the person holding your food, wood, cloth, or smelting chain together**. Use dispatches when the base can function without that exact worker present.

<h2 id="airships-change-reach-not-base-economy">
  Airships change reach, not your base economy
</h2>

Airships improve travel, hauling, and field reach, but they do not replace the bonfire base that supports farming, storage, breeding, comfort, and long-term worker routines.

For Tribesmen, the practical rule is simple: **use the airship to go farther, but keep your core economy stable on land**.

For a deeper breakdown of ship progression, use the <Link href="/soulmask/airship-guide">Shifting Sands Airship Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="recommended-progression-order">
  Recommended progression order
</h2>

If you are unsure what to prioritize, use this order as a practical reference.

| Stage | Priority | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Unlock recruitment early | Turns enemies into labor instead of only loot |
| 2 | Recruit safely | Bandage, feed, and move unconscious targets before they die |
| 3 | Assign linen or planks | Removes the first repeated crafting bottleneck |
| 4 | Fix tools, materials, and storage | Prevents false “AI is broken” problems |
| 5 | Set precise gathering markers | Makes gathering jobs actually start |
| 6 | Build a farm setup | Farmland, seeds, Grainery, water, and tanks |
| 7 | Add animal setups | Food, water, knives, and population floors create passive output |
| 8 | Read profession, caps, talents, and defects | Shows who deserves long-term investment |
| 9 | Start Training Ground development | Turns good recruits into stronger specialists |
| 10 | Use direct control for key specialists | Trains the exact body and proficiency you care about |
| 11 | Improve mood and comfort | Keeps productivity stable |
| 12 | Create a war party | Uses up to 3 followers for bosses and dangerous camps |
| 13 | Use outpost dispatches | Converts extra roster depth into passive rewards |
| 14 | Scale airship and base logistics | Airship expands reach; bonfire base remains the economy |

This order is not a strict rule for every run. Base location, recruit quality, resource shortages, and combat style can change the details. But it gives you a stable foundation: secure workers first, complete loops second, then invest in specialization.

<h2 id="where-strict-optimization-ends">
  Where strict optimization ends and playstyle begins
</h2>

There is no single perfect staffing pattern that applies to every Shifting Sands run.

A desert base with weak water access has different priorities than a base with strong farming but poor mineral routes. A player who fights often will value Warriors, Guards, Hunters, and weapon talents earlier. A player focused on production may get more from crafters, laborers, farmers, and Training Ground development.

The point of this guide is not to force one roster template. The point is to give you a way to make better decisions:

1. Does this Tribesman have a clear role?
2. Does their profession or proficiency support that role?
3. Can the relevant proficiency reach 120+?
4. Do their talents and attributes support the job?
5. Does the base have the full setup needed to make them useful?
6. Can Training Ground or direct control improve their long-term value?
7. Are they more useful at base, in combat, or on dispatch?

Once you can answer those questions, Tribesmen stop feeling like random helpers and start feeling like the backbone of your tribe.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### How do I quickly decide if a recruit target is worth Deter?

Check extraction safety first. If the target is too deep in a hostile camp, too exposed to desert heat, or too hard to carry home, skip them unless you are prepared. After that, check whether their profession matches a current need and whether you have the supplies to keep them alive until Recognition reaches 500.

### Why does recruitment fail after I use Deter?

Deter only puts the target into a recruitable state. You still need to keep them alive until Recognition reaches 500. If they are bleeding, starving, exposed to heat, caught in a sandstorm, or left near enemies, they can die before you recruit them.

### Why does my gatherer stand still?

Check tools and assignment first, then check the map marker. Gathering often fails because the selected marker is not close enough to the intended resource node or because the wrong marker is selected in the work tab.

### What does 120+ proficiency mean?

It means the Tribesman has high long-term potential in that role. Proficiency milestones can unlock buff choices at 30, 60, 90, and 120, so a 120+ cap is one of the clearest signs that a recruit can become a true specialist.

### When should I build Training Ground?

Build Training Ground after your first production loops are stable, you have more than a few Tribesmen, and you have at least one recruit worth developing. It is strongest for high-cap workers, combat followers, and crafters with useful traits but imperfect current proficiency.

### Should I directly control Tribesmen?

Yes, when you want to train a specific body quickly. Direct control lets you fight, craft, or work as that Tribesman. Body, weapon, and crafting experience go to the controlled Tribesman, while Awareness EXP goes to the chieftain controlling them.

### Should I send Tribesmen to outposts?

Yes, but usually not before your core base jobs are stable. Do not send away the worker holding your food, wood, cloth, or smelting chain together. Use outpost dispatches when you have enough roster depth to spare a group.

### Do airships make the land base less important?

No. Airships improve reach and mobility, but the bonfire base still carries farming, smelting, storage, animal systems, mood support, and long-term worker logistics.
