<h2 id="what-tribesmen-actually-do-in-shifting-sands">
  What Tribesmen actually do in Shifting Sands
</h2>

Tribesmen matter in **Shifting Sands** because they remove repetition first and multiply output later. The early value is not “full automation.” The early value is that one correctly assigned worker can take a boring but constant task off your hands and keep the rest of the base from stalling.

That distinction is important because a lot of players expect the system to feel complete too early. It usually does not. At first, Tribesmen are only as good as the loop you give them: a worker with no inputs, no tool, or no clear route does not create automation, even if the assignment menu says everything is enabled. Once the chain around them is stable, though, the same worker can become the reason your base finally feels smooth.

A good way to think about the whole system is this: first you unlock labor, then you assign one meaningful job, then you tighten the supply chain around that job, and only after that do larger loops like farming, breeding, furnace work, or combat support start to scale properly.

<GuideCallout title="Best mindset for early Tribesmen">
Do not ask whether the entire system is automated yet. Ask whether **one worker now owns one repetitive problem** that used to slow you down.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="how-recruitment-actually-works">
  How recruitment actually works
</h2>

The first real breakpoint is unlocking **recruitment** early enough that enemies can become workers instead of only becoming loot. In practice, the system is easy to misunderstand because the game separates the takedown step from the actual recruit step.

The first half is **Deter**. You weaken an eligible target until the Deter prompt appears, then use it to force the target into a recruitable state. That is not the same thing as finishing recruitment. After that, you still need to wait until that tribe's **recognition reaches 500**, which is the point where **Recruit** becomes available.

Once that distinction is clear, the system stops feeling arbitrary. Deter is the setup; Recruit is the actual conversion.

What matters just as much is what you do next. Your first recruit is usually most valuable as a **worker who removes a bottleneck immediately**, not as a vague backup fighter. In most runs, the strongest first recruit is the one who takes over an annoying, repeatable task that has already started slowing your base down.

<h2 id="first-jobs-that-actually-remove-friction">
  First jobs that actually remove friction
</h2>

The best early jobs are usually the boring ones, because boring jobs are the ones that repeatedly interrupt progress. If a worker takes over the task you are most tired of doing by hand, that worker is already pulling real weight.

In practice, the strongest early assignments are the jobs that keep common materials moving: **linen**, **planks**, **basic gathering**, and later **farm support** once the underlying farm loop is ready. These jobs do not look glamorous, but they remove the kind of base friction that quietly wastes the most time.

That is why first-job choice should not be treated as an abstract tier list. It should be tied to your actual pain point. If cloth inputs keep stopping your crafting, automate linen. If you constantly run short on wood products, automate planks. If you keep interrupting yourself to collect basics, assign gathering. The right answer is usually the task you most want to stop babysitting.

<GuideCallout title="How to choose the first specialist">
Pick the job that saves you the most manual time right now. Early Tribesmen are strongest when they reduce friction, not when they merely look optimized on paper.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="why-workers-go-idle">
  Why workers go idle
</h2>

When a Tribesman stops working, the problem is usually not bad AI in the abstract. Most of the time, the worker is blocked by a broken loop rather than by a broken system.

The cleanest troubleshooting order is:

1. **missing materials**
2. **missing or broken tools**
3. **wrong assignment**
4. **bad work area or pathing**

That order matters because it solves most false “AI problems” quickly. A Loom worker who stands still may simply be missing fiber. A gatherer may have the right role but no usable tool. A farm worker may be assigned correctly but unable to complete the route because the field, storage, and water chain do not actually connect. In other words, a stationary worker often means the assignment is fine and the surrounding logistics are not.

The biggest mistake here is to diagnose the behavior too generally. “My worker is broken” is often less useful than “this loop is missing one requirement.”

<GuideCallout title="Fast troubleshooting rule">
If a worker looks idle, check **materials, tools, assignment, and work area** in that order before assuming the system itself is failing.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="when-farming-and-gathering-become-real-automation">
  When farming and gathering become real automation
</h2>

Farming and gathering are the first places where Tribesmen can start saving real time instead of merely looking promising. They only become reliable, however, when the full loop exists around them.

For farming, that means more than placing farmland and hoping a worker handles the rest. A stable early farm loop needs **farmland**, **assigned seeds**, **a Grainery**, **water access**, and **water tanks**. The point is not just that the worker has a field; the point is that planting, watering, harvesting, and returning output all belong to one connected setup. If one part of that chain is missing, the worker often begins the loop and then stalls.

Gathering works the same way. It becomes much more consistent when the worker has a tight area, the correct tool, and nearby storage. Without those supports, gathering feels random even when the system is technically doing what it was told. With them, it starts to feel predictable.

The deeper lesson is that automation in **Shifting Sands** does not come from turning on a role. It comes from building a loop the role can actually complete.

<h2 id="animal-loops-and-why-specialists-matter">
  Animal loops and why specialists matter
</h2>

Animal management is one of the clearest points where Tribesmen stop being convenience tools and start becoming a real background economy. A breeding-and-slaughter loop works because it turns repeated maintenance into a steady supply of useful resources, but only when the conditions are tightly controlled.

In the confirmed setup here, one worker can maintain a chicken loop as long as food, water, and slaughter settings are already configured, the worker has the necessary butcher's knives, and the pen keeps a **minimum population floor** so the breeding stock is not destroyed. The value of that loop is not just convenience. It is that meat, bones, feathers, and related outputs start arriving without constant manual supervision.

This is also a good place to understand why **stable specialists** matter more than constantly rotating staff. Once a worker remains on the same useful loop for long enough, profession bonuses begin to feel real instead of cosmetic. In one confirmed example, a plank-focused worker reached **+24% output**, which is exactly the kind of gain that turns a background assignment into a meaningful material multiplier.

That is why long-term investment belongs on workers attached to loops you know you will keep.

<h2 id="production-ownership-and-later-base-scaling">
  Production ownership and later base scaling
</h2>

Later production is where Tribesmen stop feeling like helpers and start feeling like a workforce. This shift happens when a worker no longer “occasionally assists,” but instead **owns a station or material role**.

That is especially clear with kiln and furnace work. Once later smelting chains appear, a base becomes much easier to run when one worker handles tin, another handles copper, another handles bronze, and the surrounding support roles keep inputs moving. The point is not that every worker needs a highly specialized late-game identity immediately. The point is that production scales more cleanly when responsibility is divided by role instead of left as a pile of loosely shared tasks.

This is one of the biggest transitions in the whole Tribesmen system. Early workers reduce interruptions. Later workers allow the base to keep functioning at a scale that would otherwise require constant babysitting.

<h2 id="war-party-and-field-use">
  War party and field use
</h2>

Tribesmen are not only labor. They can also function as a small combat party, and that changes how much pressure you have to absorb alone.

The usable limit here is **up to 3 deployed followers at once**, but the raw number matters less than how they are managed. A combat follower contributes best when equipped, provisioned, and actively directed. In practice, that means bringing followers with decent spare gear, food, and bandages, then manually commanding them onto priority targets instead of hoping they improvise well.

That is why deployed Tribesmen perform far better in boss fights than in messy, unmanaged situations. Their best value is not perfect AI. Their best value is that they distract enemies, split pressure, create time to heal or reposition, and add enough damage that harder encounters stop feeling like pure solo attrition.

One detail worth remembering is that follower state is not always as sticky as players expect. A reload can leave your group out of deployment, so it is worth checking that they are actually active before walking into danger.

<GuideCallout title="Combat control rule">
A war party is strongest when you **gear them, supply them, and tell them who matters** instead of treating them like fully autonomous fighters.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="airships-change-reach-not-base-economy">
  Airships change reach, not base economy
</h2>

This is one of the most important **Shifting Sands-specific** distinctions. Airships change your mobility, but they do not replace the bonfire base that keeps your tribe alive.

Early on, the ship works well as a mobile staging area. Later, though, once the DLC expects a wider set of workstations, farmland, storage, and production loops, the idea of keeping your whole economy on the ship stops being practical. The bonfire base remains the real center of farming, smelting, storage, breeding, and routine worker logic. The airship expands your operational reach; it does not replace the systems that sustain that reach.

That is exactly why Tribesmen become more important, not less important, once airships matter. The farther you travel, the more valuable it is that the land base continues producing while you are away.

<GuideCallout title="Airship rule">
Treat the airship as a **mobility and staging tool**, not as a full substitute for the bonfire-centered worker economy.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="where-strict-optimization-ends">
  Where strict optimization ends and playstyle begins
</h2>

A strong Tribesmen setup relies on the confirmed mechanics we have covered: understanding the two-step recruitment flow, assigning early workers to clear bottlenecks, scaling later production through role ownership, and keeping your bonfire base active even when flying airships. 

However, players often look for a single "perfect" late-game staffing pattern or a universally solved Training Ground priority list. In practice, because base locations, resource deficits, and combat styles vary so heavily between runs, that final layer of optimization is highly dynamic. 

Rather than chasing a rigid tier list, the goal is to build the stable foundation described here. Once your core loops are automated, your roster development and training priorities should adapt to whatever your specific base needs next.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Should my first recruit be a worker or a fighter?

Usually a worker. Early Tribesmen create the most value when they remove a repeated bottleneck that is already slowing the base down.

### Why do Tribesmen often feel unreliable even when the system is working?

Because the worker is only as reliable as the loop around them. Most apparent AI failures are really broken supply, tool, storage, or routing chains.

### Is one focused worker better than several flexible workers early on?

Most of the time, yes. One worker attached to a complete loop usually produces more value than several workers spread across half-finished setups.

### Do airships make the land base less important?

No. Airships improve reach, but the bonfire base still carries the economy that makes longer expeditions sustainable.

### Is there a single "best" Training Ground priority?

It is highly situational. What is clear is that it increases the long-term value of recruits and makes roster development matter more than it used to.
