<h2 id="what-tribesmen-actually-do-in-shifting-sands">
  What Tribesmen actually do in Shifting Sands
</h2>

Tribesmen matter because they remove repetition first and multiply output later. The early value is not that your base instantly becomes automated. The early value is that one correctly assigned worker can take over a boring, repeated task that used to interrupt everything else you were trying to do.

That distinction matters. A Tribesman with no tool, no material input, no storage access, or no valid work area is not “automation,” even if the role menu says they have a job. They only become reliable when the full loop around that job is complete.

The best way to understand the system is:

1. **Recruit safely**
2. **Read the Tribesman’s role and growth potential**
3. **Assign one meaningful job**
4. **Build the supply chain around that job**
5. **Train and maintain the worker over time**
6. **Scale into farming, animals, production, combat, and outpost systems**

In other words, Tribesmen are not just workers. They are a progression layer. The better your roster becomes, the smoother your base economy becomes.

<h2 id="how-to-recruit-tribesmen-safely">
  How to recruit Tribesmen safely
</h2>

The first major breakpoint is unlocking **recruitment** early enough that enemies can become workers instead of only becoming loot. The system is easy to misunderstand because the game separates the takedown step from the actual conversion step.

The first half is **Deter**. You weaken an eligible target until the Deter prompt appears, then use it to force the target into a recruitable state. That does not finish recruitment. After that, you still need to keep the target alive until their **Recognition reaches 500**, which is when **Recruit** becomes available.

Once this distinction is clear, the system becomes much easier to manage:

| Step | What it means |
|---|---|
| Weaken target | Reduce health without killing them |
| Deter | Put the target into a recruitable state |
| Stabilize | Prevent bleeding, starvation, heat, or enemy danger |
| Wait for 500 Recognition | Let the target become recruitable |
| Recruit | Add them to your tribe |

The most common mistake is treating Deter as the end of recruitment. It is not. Deter is only the setup. The real challenge is keeping the unconscious NPC alive long enough to recruit them.

<GuideCallout title="Recruitment safety checklist">
After Deter, do not leave the target lying in the open. Put **bandages** or **broth / liquid food** directly into their inventory if they are bleeding or close to death, then move them to safety before waiting for 500 Recognition.
</GuideCallout>

You can move an unconscious target by carrying them on your shoulder. If you have a llama with an extended seat, you can also use it to move recruits over longer distances. This is especially useful when you Deter someone far from your base, inside a dangerous camp, or in a desert area where the environment itself can become a threat.

<h2 id="shifting-sands-recruitment-risks">
  Shifting Sands recruitment risks
</h2>

Recruitment is less forgiving in **Shifting Sands** because the desert can punish unconscious NPCs before you finish converting them.

A recruitable target lying on the ground is still vulnerable to:

| Threat | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Bleeding | Can kill the target before Recognition reaches 500 |
| Heat | Dangerous when the target is left exposed in desert areas |
| Sandstorms | Can turn a safe-looking recruit location into a hazard |
| Nearby enemies | Camps and patrols can still threaten the body |
| Long travel distance | Makes it easier to lose the recruit before stabilizing them |

For that reason, Shifting Sands recruitment should be treated as a rescue operation, not just a combat action. Once the target is down, check their condition, treat bleeding, feed them if needed, and move them out of danger.

<GuideCallout title="Desert recruit rule">
In Shifting Sands, never treat an unconscious recruit as “secured” until they are fed, bandaged if needed, and moved away from heat, sandstorms, hostile patrols, and other environmental danger.
</GuideCallout>

This is also why your first few recruitment runs should not be too ambitious. It is better to recruit a useful nearby worker safely than to Deter a better-looking target deep in hostile territory and lose them before the recruit step becomes available.

<h2 id="how-to-judge-a-good-tribesman">
  How to judge a good Tribesman: level, profession, proficiencies, and talents
</h2>

A good Tribesman is not just the one with the highest current level. What matters is whether their long-term profile fits the role you want them to own.

Before investing heavily in a Tribesman, check four things:

| Category | What to look for |
|---|---|
| **Level** | Current power, growth stage, and how much development remains |
| **Proficiencies** | Logging, mining, planting, weaving, crafting, weapon skills, and other job-specific categories |
| **Profession / personality type** | Warrior, Guard, Laborer, Porter, Craftsman, Hunter, and similar role identities |
| **Talents** | Innate strengths, defects, title effects, tribe traits, and talents gained through leveling |

This is the real reason stable assignment matters. A worker who stays on one useful loop is not only saving time today. They are also becoming more valuable in the exact role you need them to keep performing.

For example, a plank-focused worker with a production bonus is not just “a worker assigned to planks.” They are a specialist whose output bonus turns a background job into a material multiplier. The longer you keep that kind of Tribesman on a stable loop, the more that loop becomes part of your base economy.

Talents also make recruitment more interesting. Some recruits are immediately useful. Others may look average at first but become excellent after leveling, Training Ground development, or defect removal. This is why it is worth reading the whole character sheet instead of judging only the first visible stat.

<GuideCallout title="Recruit evaluation rule">
Do not judge a Tribesman only by current level. Check their profession, job proficiencies, talent profile, and whether they have a clear long-term role in your base.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="first-jobs-that-actually-remove-friction">
  First jobs that actually remove friction
</h2>

Your first recruit is usually most valuable as a **worker**, not a fighter. Early combat followers can help, but early base friction is usually the thing slowing your progression every few minutes.

The best early jobs are the boring ones because boring jobs are the ones that repeatedly interrupt progress. If a worker takes over the task you are most tired of doing by hand, that worker is already useful.

Strong first assignments usually include:

| Early job | Why it is useful |
|---|---|
| **Linen / cloth chain** | Reduces interruptions in crafting and gear preparation |
| **Planks / wood products** | Stabilizes common construction and production needs |
| **Basic gathering** | Keeps common resources moving while you explore |
| **Farm support** | Becomes strong once farmland, seeds, storage, and water are ready |
| **Simple production station ownership** | Helps one station stay supplied instead of becoming another chore |

Do not pick the first job from an abstract tier list. Pick it from your current pain point.

If cloth keeps blocking you, prioritize linen. If wood products keep disappearing, prioritize planks. If you keep stopping expeditions to gather basics, assign a gatherer. The best first Tribesman is the one who removes the most repeated manual work from your current route.

<GuideCallout title="First recruit priority">
Your first recruit should solve a real bottleneck: linen, planks, basic gathering, or another repeated task that is already slowing your base down.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="why-tribesmen-stop-working">
  Why Tribesmen stop working
</h2>

When a Tribesman stops working, the problem is usually not “bad AI” in the abstract. Most of the time, the worker is blocked by one missing requirement in the loop.

The best troubleshooting order is:

1. **Missing materials**
2. **Missing or broken tools**
3. **Wrong assignment**
4. **Wrong or imprecise map marker**
5. **Bad work area or pathing**
6. **No valid storage or output destination**
7. **Mood, food, or survival issue**

That order solves most false “AI problems” quickly. A Loom worker who stands still may simply be missing fiber. A gatherer may have the right job but no usable tool. A farm worker may be assigned correctly but unable to complete the route because field, storage, seeds, and water are not actually connected.

Gathering has one especially easy-to-miss problem: **the map marker**.

For gathering jobs, you often need to manually mark the resource location on the map and select that marker in the work settings. If the marker is too vague, too far from the resource cluster, or not the marker you intended, the Tribesman may technically have a gathering job but still stand around doing nothing.

<GuideCallout title="Gathering marker mistake">
If a gatherer has tools but still does nothing, move the map marker directly onto the resource cluster and reselect that marker in the work tab. A vague marker near the area can be enough to break the job.
</GuideCallout>

The important shift is to stop asking, “Why is this worker broken?” and start asking, “Which part of this loop is incomplete?”

That question usually leads you to the answer faster.

<h2 id="farming-and-gathering-loops">
  Farming and gathering loops that actually work
</h2>

Farming and gathering are the first systems where Tribesmen can start saving serious time. They only become reliable, however, when the full support loop exists around the job.

For farming, a stable early loop needs:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Farmland** | Gives the worker a valid field |
| **Assigned seeds** | Tells the system what to plant |
| **Grainery** | Gives crops and seeds a storage logic |
| **Water access** | Allows watering to happen consistently |
| **Water tanks** | Prevents the loop from stalling when water is not immediately available |
| **Valid worker assignment** | Connects the Tribesman to the farm role |
| **Reachable storage** | Lets outputs return to the base economy |

The point is not just that a worker has a field. The point is that planting, watering, harvesting, and returning output all belong to one connected setup. If one part of that chain is missing, the worker often begins the loop and then stalls.

Gathering works the same way. A reliable gatherer needs:

| Requirement | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Correct tool** | Lets the worker harvest the resource |
| **Precise map marker** | Tells the worker where to go |
| **Nearby or reachable storage** | Lets gathered materials return to the base |
| **Safe route** | Prevents interruptions or stuck movement |
| **Narrow task focus** | Avoids spreading one worker across too many vague jobs |

<h2 id="animal-loops-and-specialists">
  Animal loops and why specialists matter
</h2>

Animal management is one of the clearest points where Tribesmen stop being convenience tools and start becoming a real background economy.

A breeding-and-slaughter loop works because it turns repeated maintenance into a steady supply of resources, but only when the conditions are controlled.

A stable animal loop usually needs:

| Requirement | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| **Food** | Keeps animals alive and breeding |
| **Water** | Prevents the pen from stalling |
| **Correct worker assignment** | Lets a Tribesman maintain the loop |
| **Butcher’s knives or required tools** | Allows slaughter and processing |
| **Minimum population floor** | Protects breeding stock from being wiped out |
| **Storage access** | Moves meat, bones, feathers, and other output into the economy |

In a chicken loop, for example, one worker can maintain the system as long as food, water, slaughter settings, tools, and population limits are already configured. The value is not only convenience. It is that meat, bones, feathers, and related outputs start arriving without constant manual supervision.

This is also where specialist investment starts to matter. A Tribesman who stays on a stable animal, crafting, or production loop long enough can become meaningfully better than a generic worker. The job is no longer just being “done.” It is being improved by a worker whose proficiencies and talents match the task.

<h2 id="production-ownership-and-later-base-scaling">
  Production ownership and later base scaling
</h2>

Later production is where Tribesmen stop feeling like helpers and start feeling like a workforce. This shift happens when a worker no longer “occasionally assists,” but instead **owns a station or material role**.

That is especially clear with kiln and furnace work. Once later smelting chains appear, a base becomes easier to run when workers are divided by responsibility:

| Role example | What they own |
|---|---|
| Tin worker | Tin-related smelting and material flow |
| Copper worker | Copper production and input/output movement |
| Bronze worker | Bronze chain and related station work |
| Wood specialist | Planks, fuel support, and construction materials |
| Textile specialist | Linen, cloth, and gear-related inputs |
| Farm worker | Planting, watering, harvesting, and crop return |
| Animal worker | Food, slaughter settings, and animal output |

The point is not that every worker needs a perfect late-game identity immediately. The point is that production scales more cleanly when responsibility is divided by role instead of left as a pile of shared tasks.

Early workers reduce interruptions. Later workers let the base keep functioning at a scale that would otherwise require constant babysitting.

<h2 id="training-ground-priority">
  Training Ground: when to build it and who should use it
</h2>

Training Ground is one of the most important **Shifting Sands** systems because it changes how you evaluate recruits.

Without Training Ground, a mediocre recruit often stays mediocre. With Training Ground, a recruit with good potential but imperfect current skills can still become valuable. Tribesmen can develop weapon proficiencies, learn from stronger tribe members, and become more specialized over time.

This matters because your character has limits, while strong Tribesmen can become exceptional in specific jobs or combat roles. A well-developed Tribesman can eventually outperform your main character in a narrow specialty, especially when their profession, proficiency caps, and talents all support the same role.

Use this priority table:

| Situation | Training Ground priority |
|---|---|
| You only have 1–2 recruits | Low. Stabilize food, tools, and first jobs first. |
| You have 4–6 stable workers | Medium. Start developing your best long-term specialists. |
| You found a recruit with strong caps but weak current skills | High. This is exactly the kind of recruit Training Ground can salvage. |
| You are building a combat party | High. Train weapon proficiencies and useful combat talents. |
| Your base already has stable production loops | Very high. Roster development becomes the next progression layer. |
| You have duplicate workers with similar jobs | Medium to high. Use Training Ground to separate them into clearer specialties. |

The best Training Ground candidates are not always the strongest recruits right now. They are the recruits with the best future use.

Good candidates include:

1. a high-cap worker with weak current proficiency
2. a combat recruit with strong survivability
3. a crafter with useful production traits
4. a worker with one major defect but strong long-term value
5. a specialist whose role you know your base will always need

<GuideCallout title="Training Ground priority">
Build Training Ground after your first production loops are stable and you have recruits worth developing. It is strongest for high-cap workers, combat followers, and recruits with useful roles but imperfect talents.
</GuideCallout>

Do not treat Training Ground as decoration. Treat it as the bridge between “this recruit is usable” and “this Tribesman is part of my long-term roster.”

<h2 id="mood-and-happiness">
  Mood and happiness: why productive Tribesmen need comfort
</h2>

A Tribesman is not only limited by tools, materials, and assignments. Their mood also affects how reliable they feel over time.

This is easy to ignore because supply problems are more visible. When a worker lacks fiber, a tool, or a route, the cause is obvious. Mood problems are quieter. They show up as a tribe that feels less stable, less efficient, or harder to retain.

To keep Tribesmen productive, build the base around comfort as well as logistics.

| Need | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Food and drinks** | Broth, juice, fruit skewers, liked foods, reliable meals |
| **Facilities** | Outhouse, bathtub, dining table, chairs |
| **Comfort objects** | Statues and other mood-supporting furniture |
| **Work readiness** | Proper clothes, correct tools, replacement gear |
| **Stable routines** | Jobs that can be completed without constant interruption |

This belongs in the same mental model as supply chains. Food, tools, storage, and comfort are all part of keeping a specialist stable.

A worker with the right assignment can still become a problem if the base does not support their needs. The more valuable the Tribesman, the more important this becomes. You do not want your best crafter, farmer, or fighter destabilized because the base lacks basic comfort.

<GuideCallout title="Mood maintenance">
A productive Tribesman needs more than tools. Keep liked food, drinks, comfort facilities, clothes, and usable work gear available so mood does not become the hidden bottleneck.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="war-party-and-field-use">
  War party and field use
</h2>

Tribesmen are not only labor. They can also function as a small combat party, which changes how much pressure you have to absorb alone.

The usable limit is **up to 3 deployed followers at once**, but the raw number matters less than how they are managed. Three badly equipped followers can die quickly. One or two well-prepared followers can make boss fights, camp clears, and dangerous routes much safer.

Do not bring three random Tribesmen. Bring roles that match the fight.

| Follower type | Best use |
|---|---|
| **Warrior / Guard** | Frontline pressure, boss distraction, holding enemies in place |
| **Hunter** | Safer ranged support and camp clearing |
| **High-Endurance recruit** | Longer fights where survival matters |
| **High weapon proficiency recruit** | Better damage if you can keep them alive |
| **Defensive talent recruit** | Useful when the goal is tanking pressure rather than burst damage |

Before leaving base, check:

1. weapons
2. armor
3. food
4. bandages
5. spare supplies for longer trips
6. mask energy supplies such as Sunstones when needed
7. deployment status after reload

Manual control also matters. Combat followers are strongest when you command them onto priority targets instead of hoping they improvise perfectly. Their best value is not perfect AI. Their best value is that they split pressure, create time to heal, distract bosses, and add enough damage that hard fights stop feeling like pure solo attrition.

<GuideCallout title="Combat control rule">
A war party is strongest when you gear them, supply them, and direct them toward priority targets instead of treating them like fully autonomous fighters.
</GuideCallout>

One small habit saves a lot of frustration: after reloading, check that your followers are actually deployed before walking into danger.

<h2 id="outpost-dispatches">
  Outpost dispatches and passive rewards
</h2>

Shifting Sands also gives Tribesmen value outside normal base jobs. Once your roster is large enough, you can send groups to outposts for rewards instead of keeping every recruit inside the same production loop.

This should not replace your core workforce too early. Your best early workers should usually stay on essential loops like linen, planks, farming, animal care, or smelting. Those jobs keep the base alive.

Outpost dispatches become more attractive when:

| Situation | Why dispatches make sense |
|---|---|
| Your core loops are stable | You can spare people without breaking the base |
| You have extra recruits | Idle roster depth becomes useful |
| You have specialized non-essential members | They can generate value away from base |
| You want passive rewards | Dispatches give your tribe another progression outlet |
| Your base economy no longer needs every worker present | Extra manpower can be converted into outside gains |

Treat outpost dispatches as a mid-game and later use for roster depth. The stronger your core economy becomes, the easier it is to send a separate group away without hurting your base.

<GuideCallout title="Outpost dispatch rule">
Do not send away the worker who holds your food, wood, or crafting loop together. Use outpost dispatches after the base can function without those specific Tribesmen present.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="airships-change-reach-not-base-economy">
  Airships change reach, not your base economy
</h2>

Airships are one of the biggest Shifting Sands features, but they do not replace the bonfire base that keeps your tribe alive.

Early on, the airship works well as a mobile staging area. It helps you travel, explore, haul, and operate farther from home. Later, once the DLC expects a wider set of stations, farmland, storage, animal systems, comfort facilities, and production loops, trying to keep your whole economy on the ship becomes much less practical.

The bonfire base remains the center of:

1. farming
2. smelting
3. storage
4. breeding
5. worker routines
6. mood and comfort
7. long-term production
8. roster development

The airship expands your operational reach. It does not replace the systems that sustain that reach.

This is exactly why Tribesmen become more important, not less important, once airships matter. The farther you travel, the more valuable it is that your land base keeps producing while you are away.

<GuideCallout title="Airship rule">
Treat the airship as a mobility and staging tool, not as a full substitute for the bonfire-centered worker economy.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="recommended-progression-order">
  Recommended progression order
</h2>

If you are unsure what to prioritize, use this order as a practical reference.

| Stage | Priority | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Unlock recruitment early | Turns enemies into labor instead of only loot |
| 2 | Recruit safely | Bandage, feed, and move unconscious targets before they die |
| 3 | Assign linen or planks | Removes the first repeated crafting bottleneck |
| 4 | Fix tools, materials, and storage | Prevents false “AI is broken” problems |
| 5 | Set precise gathering markers | Makes gathering jobs actually start |
| 6 | Build a farm loop | Farmland, seeds, Grainery, water, and tanks |
| 7 | Add animal loops | Food, water, knives, and population floors create passive output |
| 8 | Read talents and professions | Shows who deserves long-term investment |
| 9 | Build Training Ground | Turns good recruits into stronger specialists |
| 10 | Improve mood and comfort | Keeps productivity stable |
| 11 | Create a war party | Uses up to 3 followers for bosses and dangerous camps |
| 12 | Use outpost dispatches | Converts extra roster depth into passive rewards |
| 13 | Scale airship and base logistics | Airship expands reach; bonfire base remains the economy |

This order is not a strict rule for every run. Base location, recruit quality, resource shortages, and combat style can change the details. But it gives you a stable foundation: secure workers first, complete loops second, then invest in specialization.

<h2 id="where-strict-optimization-ends">
  Where strict optimization ends and playstyle begins
</h2>

There is no single perfect staffing pattern that applies to every Shifting Sands run.

A desert base with weak water access has different priorities than a base with strong farming but poor mineral routes. A player who fights often will value Warriors, Guards, Hunters, and weapon talents earlier. A player focused on production may get more from crafters, laborers, farmers, and Training Ground development.

The point of this guide is not to force one roster template. The point is to give you a way to make better decisions:

1. Does this Tribesman have a clear role?
2. Does their profession or proficiency support that role?
3. Does the base have the loop needed to make them useful?
4. Can Training Ground improve their long-term value?
5. Can mood, tools, and supplies keep them stable?
6. Are they more useful at base, in combat, or on dispatch?

Once you can answer those questions, Tribesmen stop feeling like random helpers and start feeling like the backbone of your tribe.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Should my first recruit be a worker or a fighter?

Usually a worker. Your first recruit should remove a repeated bottleneck such as linen, planks, or basic gathering. Combat followers become more valuable once your base can consistently support gear, food, bandages, and replacement supplies.

### Why does recruitment fail after I use Deter?

Deter only puts the target into a recruitable state. You still need to keep them alive until Recognition reaches 500. If they are bleeding, starving, exposed to heat, caught in a sandstorm, or left near enemies, they can die before you recruit them.

### How do I keep an unconscious recruit alive?

Check their condition immediately after Deter. Put bandages or broth / liquid food into their inventory if needed, then carry them or transport them with a suitable mount setup to a safer location.

### Why does my gatherer stand still?

Check tools and assignment first, then check the map marker. Gathering often fails because the selected marker is not close enough to the intended resource node or because the wrong marker is selected in the work tab.

### When should I build Training Ground?

Build it after your first production loops are stable and you have recruits worth developing. It is especially valuable for high-cap workers, combat followers, and Tribesmen with useful roles but imperfect talents.

### Are Tribesmen better than the main character?

They can be in specific roles. Your character has limits, while strong Tribesmen can exceed those limits in certain specialties depending on profession, proficiencies, and talents.

### How do I choose which Tribesmen to keep long term?

Look for a clear match between profession, proficiency, talents, and base need. A recruit with strong logging traits belongs on wood loops. A strong crafter belongs on production. A strong Warrior or Guard belongs in your combat roster.

### How do I keep Tribesmen productive?

Keep tools, materials, food, water, storage, comfort facilities, and mood support available. A worker with the right assignment can still become unreliable if the surrounding base does not support them.

### Should I send Tribesmen to outposts?

Yes, but usually not before your core base loops are stable. Do not send away the worker holding your food, wood, or smelting chain together. Use outpost dispatches when you have enough roster depth to spare a group.

### Do airships make the land base less important?

No. Airships improve reach and mobility, but the bonfire base still carries farming, smelting, storage, animal loops, mood support, and long-term worker logistics.
