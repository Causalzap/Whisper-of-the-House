<h2 id="why-tribesmen-matter-in-shifting-sands">
  Why Tribesmen matter in Shifting Sands
</h2>

Tribesmen matter in **Shifting Sands** because they remove repeat chores first, then become true production multipliers later.

The mistake most players make is expecting full automation too early.

The practical progression looks like this:

1. **unlock recruitment**
2. **recruit your first worker**
3. **assign one simple job**
4. **fix the missing inputs that stop that job**
5. **stabilize gathering or farming**
6. **add breeding or furnace work**
7. **claim profession bonuses on stable workers**
8. **build a 3-follower combat party**
9. **use airship progression to expand mobility without breaking your bonfire base**

Start small. One useful worker is better than three badly supplied ones.

<h2 id="how-to-unlock-and-recruit-tribesmen">
  How to unlock and recruit Tribesmen
</h2>

The first real breakpoint is unlocking **recruitment** early enough that enemies can become workers instead of just loot.

### Stage 1: Deter the target

The first part of recruitment is **Deter**.

In practical play, this means:

1. **unlock recruitment first**
2. **find an eligible enemy**
3. **drop them to low health**
4. **use Deter when the prompt appears**

That Deter step is the “knock them down” part of the process, not the full recruit.

### Stage 2: Wait for Recruit

After a successful Deter, wait until that tribe's **recognition reaches 500**, then use **Recruit**.

That is the important distinction:

- **Deter** gets the target into the recruitable state
- **recognition 500** is what unlocks the actual **Recruit** step afterward

If you mix those two together, the system feels confusing even when it is working correctly.

### What your first recruit should do

Do not treat the first recruit as a throwaway combat extra.

Your first Tribesman should immediately be turned into a **worker who removes one real bottleneck**, usually:

- linen
- planks
- gathering
- basic farm support

That is when the system starts paying you back.

<h2 id="best-first-tribesmen-jobs">
  Best first Tribesmen jobs
</h2>

The best first jobs are the jobs that unblock everything else.

### Best early jobs in order

For most runs, the cleanest early order is:

1. **Loom / linen**
2. **carpentry / planks**
3. **basic gathering**
4. **farm support**
5. **simple cooking or production support**

These jobs are strong because they solve base friction instead of adding complexity.

### Why linen and planks come first

Linen and planks are boring jobs, which is exactly why they should be automated first.

They create the most common early interruptions:

- you need more cloth inputs
- you need more planks again
- you cannot keep up with basic crafting materials

A worker who handles that bottleneck is more valuable than a flashy recruit who does not solve anything immediately.

### How to choose your first specialist

Use this rule:

- if crafting chains keep stopping, assign **linen or planks**
- if you keep running out of wood, bark, fiber, or basics, assign **gathering**
- if your fields are half-manual and inconsistent, assign **one dedicated farm worker**

Do not choose a first job based on what looks strongest on paper.

Choose the job you are most tired of doing by hand.

<h2 id="why-tribesmen-stop-working">
  Why Tribesmen stop working
</h2>

If a Tribesman is standing still in **Soulmask: Shifting Sands**, the problem is usually not “bad AI.”

Most of the time, it is one of four setup problems:

1. **missing input materials**
2. **missing or broken tools**
3. **wrong job assignment**
4. **work area or station access problems**

Use this checklist in order before assuming the worker system is bugged.

### The cleanest early example: the Loom worker

A Loom worker can look completely idle when the real problem is simple:

- the Loom job is assigned
- the worker exists
- but **fiber is missing**

That is not an AI problem. It is a supply problem.

Once fiber is added, the worker starts making linen immediately.

### What to check when a farm worker stops

If your farm worker stops, check these in order:

1. **seeds available**
2. **water source working**
3. **water tanks available**
4. **Grainery or farm storage supporting the loop**
5. **farmland inside the work area**
6. **the worker is actually assigned to farm actions**

### What to check when a gatherer stops

If a gatherer is doing nothing, check:

- the correct tool is equipped
- the gathering area still has resources in it
- there is nearby drop-off storage
- the job is tied to the correct work area

<GuideCallout title="Fast fix rule">
If a worker is idle, do not start by blaming the AI.

Start by checking **materials, tools, assignment, and work area** in that exact order.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="how-farming-actually-works">
  How farming actually works
</h2>

Farming is one of the first places where Tribesmen start saving real time instead of just looking useful.

A farm loop only works when the **entire chain** exists, not just the field itself.

### Minimum farm setup

A reliable early farm loop needs:

- **farmland**
- **seeds**
- **a Grainery**
- **water access**
- **water tanks**
- later, **fertilizer support** if you want a cleaner full cycle

If one of those is missing, the farm worker often starts part of the loop and then stalls.

### What one farm worker should actually do

A dedicated farm worker should cover one clear cycle:

1. **plant**
2. **water**
3. **fertilize**
4. **harvest**
5. **return output through the farm storage loop**

That is much better than giving several workers a vague farming role and hoping it works.

### How to think about the Grainery

The Grainery matters because it keeps the farm loop tight.

The important part is that:

- the worker can reach the field
- the worker can reach the Grainery
- seeds are assigned to the correct plots
- the well and water tanks can actually support watering

The farm only becomes truly self-sufficient once water tanks are added and the worker can refill and water on her own.

### Why farm loops usually fail

Most failed farm setups come from one of these problems:

- seeds are missing
- the wrong seeds are assigned
- water exists but the worker cannot complete the watering step
- storage is not supporting the loop properly
- farmland is outside the assigned work area
- the worker is trying to do too many unrelated jobs

<h2 id="wild-collection-and-basic-resource-loops">
  Wild collection and basic resource loops
</h2>

Once your first production bottleneck is solved, the next clean win is **basic gathering**.

### Why defined gathering areas matter

Gatherers work best when they have:

- **a clearly defined area**
- **the right tool**
- **a nearby place to drop materials**
- **one clear purpose**

Without that, gathering feels random even when the system is technically working.

### Best way to make gathering feel consistent

Do not assign gathering as a vague background role.

Instead:

1. define the work area
2. equip the correct tool
3. place nearby drop-off storage
4. make sure the worker is not overloaded with unrelated tasks

A collection yard can work, but one of the cleanest confirmed upgrades is using the **bonfire itself as the wild collection work location** and directing a gatherer to the nearby trees and basic resources around that base area.

That is when gathering starts feeling reliable instead of random.

<h2 id="breeding-farm-and-animal-automation">
  Breeding farm and animal automation
</h2>

Animal automation is where Tribesmen stop being convenience tools and start becoming a repeatable material engine.

### Why the chicken loop is strong

Once you have a breeding pair and the right management setup, chickens can become a steady source of:

- meat
- bones
- feathers
- related byproducts

That is valuable because it supports both survival and crafting.

### How to build the loop

The practical setup is:

1. **capture and place the animals**
2. **make sure food and water are available**
3. **assign one worker to breed and slaughter management**
4. **give that worker butcher's knives**
5. **set a population floor so the worker only kills surplus animals**

One confirmed setup kept the coop at **2 male and 2 female**, only slaughtering above that threshold.

### What makes this better than manual handling

A stable animal loop saves time in three ways:

- you stop checking the pen constantly
- you get repeatable materials
- the worker turns maintenance into background production

<h2 id="profession-bonuses-and-why-output-matters">
  Profession bonuses and why output matters
</h2>

A stable specialist is often worth more than a fresh recruit with prettier potential.

### Why output bonuses matter

Once a worker stays on the same discipline for long enough, the output gain stops feeling cosmetic and starts feeling real.

The practical rule is simple:

**stable specialists scale better than constantly rotating staff.**

One plank worker reached **wood and stone proficiency 68**, claimed two bonuses, and ended up at **+24% output**, effectively producing bonus planks over time.

### When to claim bonuses

Claim profession bonuses once a worker has already proven that they belong in a stable loop.

That usually means they are:

- permanently handling a useful station or activity
- consistently supplied with inputs
- no longer an experimental recruit you might replace tomorrow

### Which workers deserve long-term investment

The best long-term workers are the ones attached to loops you know you will keep:

- planks
- linen
- farming
- furnace roles
- other consistent high-use stations

<h2 id="position-based-production-and-furnace-work">
  Position-based production and furnace work
</h2>

This is where the base starts feeling responsive instead of clumsy.

### Why position-based jobs are better

Once you start using real station positions instead of one-off manual orders, the worker system becomes easier to scale.

That is the big shift with roles like:

- kiln work
- furnace work
- other dedicated production positions

The important difference is ownership.

A worker is no longer “sometimes helping.” They are now **responsible for that role**.

### How to assign later production cleanly

The cleanest way to scale later production is to split work by station or material instead of making every worker do everything.

A practical example looks like:

- one worker on **tin ingots**
- one worker on **copper ingots**
- one worker on **bronze**
- others on support stations feeding the chain

This is how you stop stalling out on later smelting.

### Why furnaces matter so much

Later production breaks down fast when no one owns it.

In practice, furnace work is one of the clearest examples because a furnace effectively needs a Tribesman operating it constantly.

If those stations are still relying on manual babysitting, the whole base feels slower than it should.

<h2 id="airship-tribesmen-roles-in-shifting-sands">
  Airship Tribesmen roles in Shifting Sands
</h2>

This is the clearest reason a **Shifting Sands** Tribesmen guide should not read like a base-game guide.

The important point is not just that airships exist.

It is that **airships change your logistics, but they do not replace your real worker base**.

### When do you actually get a flying ship?

For a new Shifting Sands player, the important distinction is this:

- you can start with a **small ship-style mobile setup**
- your **first real flying upgrade** comes later, after the Sobec route and the ship parts unlocked from that progression

If you want the full first-airship route, parts order, and ship progression, use the <Link href="/soulmask/airship-guide">Shifting Sands Airship Guide</Link>.

### What changes once airships matter

Early on, the ship works as a mobile staging area.

Later, that stops being enough.

A key Shifting Sands breakpoint is that once tribe progression starts requiring a **bonfire, farmland, and a wider spread of workstations**, your main base cannot stay on the boat forever. you eventually need to move onto land and build a real bonfire-centered base.

That is the real shift:

- the airship improves **mobility**
- the bonfire base still handles **farming**
- the bonfire base still handles **storage**
- the bonfire base still handles **smelting and workstation growth**

### Best way to think about the airship

Treat the ship as:

- a **mobile staging platform**
- a **transport and resupply tool**
- a way to **park near points of interest, jump out, and clear content**

Do not treat it as a full replacement for your entire tribe economy.

### Why this matters for Tribesmen

Airships make Tribesmen more valuable because your base now has to keep working while you roam farther.

That means your real specialists matter even more:

- plank production
- gathering
- farming
- breeding
- furnace work
- other support loops that keep your expeditions supplied

<GuideCallout title="Airship rule">
Airships should expand your reach, not replace the bonfire base loops that keep your tribe alive.
</GuideCallout>

Training Ground matters in **Shifting Sands** because it changes how you evaluate long-term recruits.

The key idea is simple:

**a mediocre recruit is more worth keeping if your tribe can teach and improve that body over time.**

That matters more in a DLC built around a wider roster and longer logistics loops than it did in older early-access thinking.

What this guide can say confidently right now is:

- Training Ground is **not** just a flavor feature
- it changes how valuable borderline recruits can become
- it gives you a stronger reason to care about roster development instead of disposable workers

<h2 id="best-way-to-build-a-war-party">
  Best way to build a war party
</h2>

Tribesmen are not only for labor.

You can also deploy them as a small combat party.

### War party limit

You can have up to **3 Tribesmen** deployed with you at the same time.

That only matters if they are actually equipped, brought along, and used correctly.

### How to build a usable war party

To deploy a Tribesman, approach them and hold **E**, then select **Deploy** from the work menu. Once deployed, you can command them to follow, attack a specific target, or hold position.

The practical process is:

1. **recruit bodies you actually want to field**
2. **deploy up to 3 of them**
3. **give them the best spare gear you can afford**
4. **load them with food and bandages**
5. **bring them into fights where distraction matters**
6. **manually command them onto the boss target when the fight starts**

That last point matters a lot.

Boss fights go much more smoothly when deployed followers are **commanded to target the boss from the start** instead of being left to freelance.

### What makes a good combat recruit

The best combat followers do not need to be perfect.

They need to do useful things consistently:

- draw enemy attention
- survive long enough to split pressure
- contribute damage
- carry usable gear
- help you avoid pure solo pressure in harder fights

Things that start to matter more later include:

- combat talents
- combat proficiencies
- max proficiency caps
- role fit
- whether you can actually keep them geared

### Common war party failure points

Followers perform much worse when:

- they are undergeared
- they are not fed or supplied
- they get left behind
- you expect perfect AI instead of giving target priority
- you reload and do not realize they are no longer deployed

One especially painful confirmed failure point is that a reload can silently take your group **out of deployment**, which can leave them absent or exposed if you assume they are still active.

### What deployed Tribesmen are best at

Deployed followers are strongest when they:

- distract enemies
- create breathing room in boss fights
- buy you time to bandage, reposition, or shoot
- carry spare gear and supplies
- contribute enough damage that focusing the boss becomes realistic

<GuideCallout title="Combat control rule">
A war party is strongest when you **gear them, provision them, and manually point them at the right target** instead of letting them improvise every fight.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### How do you recruit Tribesmen in Soulmask: Shifting Sands?

Recruitment is a two-step flow. First, weaken an eligible enemy until **Deter** becomes available and use it. After that, wait for the target to reach the point where **Recruit** becomes available. A visible gate in this flow is **recognition 500** before **Recruit** appears.

### What are the best first jobs for Tribesmen?

The best first jobs are the ones that remove your current bottleneck. For most players, that means:

1. **Loom / linen**
2. **carpentry / planks**
3. **basic gathering**
4. **farm support once seeds and water are stable**

Do not start with “best worker overall.” Start with the job that saves you the most manual time right now.

### Why is my Tribesman not working?

Check four things in order:

1. **input materials**
2. **tool equipped and not broken**
3. **correct job enabled**
4. **work area includes the station**

Most “idle Tribesman” problems are caused by missing fiber, seeds, fuel, or a tool that broke and was never replaced.

### Can one Tribesman run the farm?

Yes, but only if the full farm loop already exists. One worker can handle planting, watering, fertilizing, and harvesting when the farm has farmland, assigned seeds, a Grainery, a water source, water tanks, and a work area that actually covers the field.

### Can Tribesmen automate breeding and slaughter?

Yes. A worker can maintain a breeding-and-slaughter loop if food, water, animal settings, and slaughter conditions are already configured correctly. The most important safeguard is setting a minimum population floor so the worker only kills surplus animals.

### Are profession bonuses worth taking?

Yes. Profession bonuses matter most on workers who already have a stable long-term role. One plank worker reached reached **+24% output**, which is the kind of gain that turns a background worker into a real material multiplier.

### How many Tribesmen can fight with you at once?

You can deploy up to **3 Tribesmen** to fight alongside you at one time.

### How do I make Tribesmen fight better in boss fights?

Deploy them before you leave, give them decent gear, food, and bandages, and manually command them onto the boss instead of letting them roam freely. Boss fights improve sharply once the followers are ordered to focus the boss from the start.

### Can deployed Tribesmen help revive you?

Yes. One confirmed combat example showed that while downed, you can ask your allies to help you back up with **F7**.

### When do I actually get a flying ship in Shifting Sands?

The ship becomes a true airship later in progression, after the Sobec route and the parts unlocked from that path. For the full first-airship route and ship progression, use the <Link href="/soulmask/airship-guide">Shifting Sands Airship Guide</Link>.

### Do airships make Tribesmen less important in Shifting Sands?

No. Airships make Tribesmen more valuable because they improve your mobility, but your real farming, gathering, smelting, and storage loops still belong at a bonfire base.

### Can my main base stay on the boat forever?

Not realistically. Early on, the ship works as a mobile staging area, but once tribe progression requires a bonfire, farmland, and more workstations, you need a proper land base to keep your worker economy running.

### Is Training Ground worth using in Shifting Sands?

Yes. Training Ground lets Tribesmen teach skills to each other, which makes borderline recruits more viable long term. The full best-priority order still needs more route testing, but it already changes how you judge whether a mediocre recruit is worth keeping.
