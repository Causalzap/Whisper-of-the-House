<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
To keep crops watered in **Timberborn**, plan irrigation around **water depth, coverage, and target land**, not only around stored water.

Use these core numbers: a beaver drinks about **2.1–2.5 water per day** for planning, a **Small Tank stores 30**, a **Large Tank stores 100**, Water Pumps need about **0.3 m** to operate, exposed water evaporates at about **0.045 m per surface tile per day**, and a reservoir should usually reach **2.0 m+** before you rely on it through long droughts.

For farms, water depth matters because irrigation coverage shrinks as water level drops. A one-tile canal is usually the most efficient soil-irrigation shape; wider canals are better only when you also need storage. Aquatic crops such as Cattails and Spadderdock need direct water coverage, not just green land nearby. 
</GuideQuickAnswer>

<h2 id="timberborn-irrigation-reservoir-guide">
  Timberborn irrigation and reservoir guide
</h2>

This **Timberborn irrigation and reservoir guide** is for the point where drinking water is stable, but crops, trees, or farm districts still dry out during longer droughts.

The important distinction is:

**Water in storage keeps beavers alive. Water near soil keeps land green.**

A full tank does not automatically irrigate a farm. A full reservoir does not help crops unless the water is close enough, deep enough, and connected to the land you want to protect.

/>

If crops dry only on the outer edge, the issue is often irrigation radius. If the entire farm dries, the source, outlet, or reservoir may be too weak. If aquatic crops die, the tile itself probably lost water coverage.

<h2 id="coverage-and-water-depth">
  Irrigation coverage: water depth changes the green radius
</h2>

A common beginner confusion is: **why do crops beside the water survive while crops slightly farther away dry out?**

The answer is that irrigation coverage depends on water depth and local terrain. As drought lowers the water level, the green area can shrink. The farthest tiles from the water source usually dry first.

<GuideTable
caption="Timberborn irrigation coverage mechanics."
columns={coverageColumns}
rows={coverageRows}
/>

This changes how you should design farms:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Put critical crops closer to the canal, river, basin, or tunnel.</li>
  <li>Use less important crops or spare land on the outer edge.</li>
  <li>Check green coverage late in drought, not only during flow season.</li>
  <li>If the edge dries first, fix depth or route position before adding more drinking-water tanks.</li>
</ul>

  Reservoir before tunnel: choose a real water source
</h2>

Before digging complicated tunnels, make sure you have a water source worth feeding from.

A good reservoir answers four questions:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>How does water enter?</li>
  <li>How does water leave?</li>
  <li>Which exact farm, forest, or aquatic-crop zone does it protect?</li>
  <li>Can beavers build every wall, gate, valve, and path?</li>
</ul>

If those answers are unclear, fix the reservoir before building the irrigation route.

<h2 id="reservoir-design-checks">
  Reservoir design checks
</h2>

A reservoir should be deep enough for storage, close enough for coverage, controlled enough for release, and safe enough that badwater cannot hijack the route.

caption="Timberborn reservoir design checks for irrigation."
columns={reservoirColumns}
rows={reservoirRows}
/>

A reservoir that stores water but fails coverage is not finished. The real test is whether the target land stays green late in drought.

<h2 id="depth-over-width">
  Depth over width: storage math, not just a slogan
</h2>

Depth matters because exposed surface evaporates. A wide shallow pond exposes more water surface, while a compact deep basin can store the same or greater volume with less surface loss.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/timberborn/timberborn-drought-proof-water-hole-depth.webp"
    alt="Deepened drought-proof water hole in Timberborn"
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Deepening a water hole increases useful storage without spreading the same water over a huge exposed surface.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

Use this split:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>If the goal is **storage**, go deeper and more compact.</li>
  <li>If the goal is **soil coverage**, place water closer to the land that dries.</li>
  <li>If the goal is **aquatic crops**, keep the crop tiles directly covered by water.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="canal-width">
  Canal width: one tile is usually enough for irrigation
</h2>

A wider canal does not automatically mean a better farm. For soil irrigation, a **one-tile-wide canal** is usually the most space-efficient shape because it saves farmland while still bringing water close to both sides.

Use wider canals only when you need something beyond coverage:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>more storage volume in the canal itself,</li>
  <li>more flow capacity,</li>
  <li>a wider river replacement,</li>
  <li>a safer buffer before a pump or basin,</li>
  <li>room for aquatic crops or water routes.</li>
</ul>

If the question is “should I make my canal wider?”, ask what problem you are solving. For farm coverage, move a narrow canal closer. For storage, increase depth or controlled volume.

<h2 id="filling-a-reservoir">
  Fill the reservoir before drought
</h2>

A reservoir that fills too late is only a hole. The best time to fill it is during flow season, before the drought warning becomes urgent.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/timberborn/timberborn-water-diverter-filling-pit.webp"

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

  </figcaption>
</figure>

Check the fill route before trusting it:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Does the inlet open from a reliable water source?</li>
  <li>Can the reservoir reach useful depth before drought?</li>
  <li>Does the outlet release water somewhere safe?</li>
  <li>Will the route stay clean during badwater or badtide pressure?</li>
</ol>

Once water starts moving into an irrigation tank, tunnel, canal, or basin, control the release. Full flow is rarely the safest first setting.

  <li>watch the water level,</li>
  <li>check whether the target land turns green,</li>
  <li>increase flow only if the land stays dry,</li>
  <li>lower flow if the tank drains too quickly or the route floods.</li>

<h2 id="aquatic-crops">
  Aquatic crops: Cattails and Spadderdock need covered tiles
</h2>

Aquatic crops are not normal irrigated crops. They need water on the growing tile itself. A canal beside the tile is not enough if the crop tile is dry.

<GuideTable
caption="Normal crops, trees, Cattails and Spadderdock during drought."
columns={aquaticColumns}
rows={aquaticRows}
/>

This is why aquatic crop beds should be planned like small reservoirs. Give them a basin, enough depth, and controlled refill so drought does not turn the growing tiles into dry ground.

<h2 id="do-not-refill-everything">
  Do not refill the whole river unless you mean to
</h2>

You can use stored water to refill a river section, but that does not mean it is efficient.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/timberborn/timberborn-underground-irrigation-flow.webp"
    alt="Stored water flowing back into a river section for irrigation in Timberborn"
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    In our test, refilling the river restored irrigation, but it used far more water than feeding a smaller underground route.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The problem is surface area and target size. If your goal is only to keep one crop block or forest strip alive, refilling every tile of the old river can drain storage faster than expected.

Use whole-river refill only when:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>it is a short emergency,</li>
  <li>the river also supports pumps or other key systems,</li>
  <li>you have enough stored water to spend,</li>
  <li>you are testing flow before building the final route.</li>
</ul>

For a long drought, a smaller controlled irrigation path is usually safer.

  Keep badwater out of irrigation routes
</h2>

Clean irrigation and toxic-water routing should not share an uncontrolled channel. If badwater can reach your farm route, solve that before expanding the irrigation system.

Most irrigation failures come from solving the wrong problem: storing water without moving it to soil, moving water without enough depth, widening canals without a storage reason, or planting aquatic crops where the tile itself dries.

<h2 id="recommended-irrigation-checklist">
  Recommended irrigation checklist
</h2>

Use this checklist before committing to a large irrigation project:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Identify the exact land that dries first.</li>
  <li>Decide whether the problem is drinking water, soil coverage, or aquatic crop coverage.</li>
  <li>Choose a source that still has water during drought.</li>
  <li>Use compact depth for storage, and close water routes for coverage.</li>
  <li>Use one-tile canals for efficient irrigation unless you also need storage volume.</li>
  <li>Build controlled fill and release before the drought warning becomes urgent.</li>
  <li>Check green coverage late in drought, not only during flow season.</li>
  <li>Keep aquatic crops directly covered by water.</li>
  <li>Use gauges or visual checks to tune flow and depth.</li>
  <li>Keep clean irrigation separated from badwater routes.</li>
</ol>

<h2 id="faq">
  Timberborn irrigation and reservoir FAQ
</h2>

### Why are my crops drying out even though I have water tanks?

Tanks store drinking water. They do not irrigate soil unless water is routed close enough and deep enough to keep the farm green.

### Why do the outer crops dry first?

As water level drops, irrigation coverage can shrink. The farthest crop tiles from the water source often lose coverage first.

### How deep should a Timberborn reservoir be?

Use 2.0 m+ as a practical target before relying on it for longer droughts. Deeper compact storage is usually better than wide shallow storage.

### How much water evaporates in Timberborn?

A useful planning number is about 0.045 m per exposed surface tile per day. This is why wide shallow reservoirs lose more water than deep compact reservoirs with the same volume.

### Should my irrigation canal be wider?

Usually no if the goal is soil coverage. One-tile-wide canals are often the most space-efficient. Wider canals are useful when you also need storage volume or flow capacity.

### Why did my Cattails or Spadderdock die?

Aquatic crops need direct water coverage on their growing tiles. If drought drains the basin or riverbed, they can die even if nearby land is still green.

### Should I refill the whole river to irrigate crops?

Only as an emergency or test. It can waste a lot of water if you only need to keep a small farm zone green.

### When is underground irrigation worth it?

It is worth it when a specific farm or forest area keeps drying out and refilling the surface river wastes too much water.

### Why is my underground irrigation not making land green?

The route may be too far from the target soil, too shallow, too short, or not holding enough water. Check green coverage, not just water level.

### Should I automate irrigation?

Only after the manual system works. First confirm the source, outlet, flow, depth, and green coverage. Then add sensors or valves for reliability.
