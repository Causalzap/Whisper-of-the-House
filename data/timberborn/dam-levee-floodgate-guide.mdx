<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
In **Timberborn**, use a **dam** for a cheap first drought buffer, **levees** for solid walls, and **floodgates** when you need adjustable water height.

The key numbers are simple: a dam holds about **65%** water height, single/double/triple floodgates control about **1.0 m / 2.0 m / 3.0 m**, exposed water evaporates at about **0.045 per tile per day**, and Water Pumps need roughly **0.3 m** water depth to keep working.

For reservoirs, build deep and compact before wide and shallow. During temperate seasons, keep gates closed to store water. During drought, open outlets only slightly so the stored water lasts instead of dumping the whole reservoir at once.

<h2 id="timberborn-dam-levee-floodgate-guide">
  Timberborn dam, levee and floodgate guide
</h2>

This **Timberborn dam, levee and floodgate guide** explains which water-control structure to build, when to upgrade, and how to avoid wasting a whole season on the wrong project.

The short version is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Use **dams** when you need a fast early buffer.</li>
  <li>Use **levees** when you need solid walls or separated channels.</li>
  <li>Use **floodgates** when you need adjustable water height.</li>
  <li>Use **reservoirs** when short river buffers no longer survive drought.</li>
  <li>Use **throttling valves** when controlled flow rate matters more than gate height.</li>
</ul>

The real skill is reading the map: river width, depth, access, log supply, badwater direction, evaporation surface, and whether the build can finish before the next disaster.

The important lesson is that each structure solves a different numeric problem. A dam gives an early buffer. Floodgates control height. Reservoir depth increases stored volume. Evaporation punishes exposed surface. Pumps fail when the remaining water is too shallow.

<h2 id="dam-vs-levee-vs-floodgate">
  Dam vs levee vs floodgate: what each structure is for
</h2>

Before building a large water project, choose the right tool for the job.

caption="Timberborn dam, levee, floodgate, reservoir and valve differences."
columns={structureColumns}
rows={structureRows}
/>

A dam is usually your first answer because it is cheap and fast. A levee is your wall. A floodgate is your controller. A reservoir is the storage system those pieces create.

<h2 id="map-start-adjustments">
  Map start adjustments: do not force one water build order
</h2>

Some starts have a perfect narrow dam site near the district. Other starts have wide rivers, awkward bends, sparse forests, or long walking routes.

<GuideTable
caption="How different map starts change your first water-control move."
columns={mapColumns}
rows={mapRows}
/>

In our 1.0 test colony, the first dam worked best only when the river was narrow and the build site was close. On a wider start, chasing the same early dam wasted logs and builder time; filled tanks were safer until the colony had better access.

<h2 id="evaporation-and-depth">
  Evaporation: why deep narrow reservoirs beat wide shallow ones
</h2>

Evaporation is the main reason “depth first, width second” is more than a slogan.

A good planning rule is:

**Every exposed water tile evaporates about 0.045 water per day.**

That means two reservoirs with the same stored volume can behave very differently if one exposes much more surface area.

<GuideTable
caption="Reservoir evaporation example: same storage, different exposed surface."
columns={evaporationColumns}
rows={evaporationRows}
/>

This is why a deep 10 × 3 × 6 basin can be much more efficient than a wide 2 m-deep basin holding the same volume. The volume is the same, but the shallow version exposes more surface, so it loses more water every day.

When you ask “how deep should my reservoir be?”, the practical answer is:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>use 2.0 m+ as the early serious-storage target,</li>
  <li>go deeper when the map gives you a natural hole or canyon,</li>
  <li>avoid spreading water over huge shallow surfaces unless you need irrigation coverage,</li>
  <li>keep exposed surface small when the goal is storage, not soil moisture.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="why-first-dam-matters">
  Why the first dam matters
</h2>

The first dam is not your final water system. It is a survival bridge.

A dam keeps part of a river section usable during drought instead of letting everything drain away immediately. It also often doubles as a crossing, giving beavers access to wood or land on the other side.

Do not overbuild this first step. If drought is close, a small finished dam is better than a large advanced structure that never stores water.

<h2 id="when-to-upgrade">
  When to upgrade from dam to floodgate
</h2>

You upgrade when you need control, not just storage.

Floodgates become useful when you want to:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>hold water higher than a dam can,</li>
  <li>choose when a reservoir fills,</li>
  <li>split water between two routes,</li>
  <li>protect farms from badwater,</li>
  <li>release water only during drought,</li>
  <li>prepare for automation later.</li>
</ul>

<GuideTable
caption="Safe upgrade path from first dam to controlled waterworks."
columns={upgradeColumns}
rows={upgradeRows}
/>

If you are still surviving on one small water buffer, do not rush a complex floodgate plan. Unlocking a floodgate is not the same as having planks, builders, paths, and a safe outlet ready.

<h2 id="gate-operation">
  Gate operation: close to store, barely open to release
</h2>

Many reservoir failures happen after the build is finished. The gate is opened too far, the outlet floods, or the stored water disappears in a day.

Use this habit:

<GuideTable
caption="How to operate reservoir gates through temperate seasons and drought."
columns={gateOperationColumns}
rows={gateOperationRows}
/>

The beginner rule is: **closed to store, barely open to sustain.**

During temperate seasons, store water. When drought starts, do not immediately drop the whole gate. Open a small amount, watch the pump or irrigation zone, and only increase release if the target area still needs more water.

<h2 id="levees-and-spillways">
  Levees and spillways: walls need overflow plans
</h2>

Levees block water completely. That makes them useful for reservoir walls, channel sides, badwater barriers, and support blocks.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/timberborn/timberborn-levee-river-control.webp"
    alt="Levees narrowing a Timberborn river for water control"
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Levees are powerful because they fully block water, but every block is a real log investment. Build the smallest wall that changes the route.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

A complete dam or reservoir design should include more than a tall wall. It needs a controlled release route and a spillway.

<GuideTable
caption="Main wall, gate, spillway and outlet roles."
columns={spillwayColumns}
rows={spillwayRows}
/>

A spillway is simply a planned safe overflow path. It should be lower than the parts of the wall where overflow would be dangerous. Without one, a full reservoir can escape through the weakest edge and flood farms, paths, or badwater routes.

<h2 id="reservoir-planning">
  Reservoir planning: depth first, outlet second
</h2>

A reservoir is not just a hole with water. A good reservoir has depth, low exposed surface, a controlled inlet, a safe outlet, build access, and overflow handling.

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/timberborn/timberborn-reservoir-hole-plan.webp"
    alt="Planning a deep reservoir hole in Timberborn"
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    A deep natural hole is valuable only if you can fill it before drought and release it without flooding the wrong area.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

The planning rule is:

**Depth first, width second.**

A wide shallow river exposes a lot of surface and may still hold too little useful volume. A deeper controlled basin gives more drought value in the same footprint.

  Diverting water and T-valves
</h2>

Once you have floodgates and levees, you can send water into a side reservoir, farm district, bypass, or badwater outlet.

  </figcaption>
</figure>

A useful advanced pattern is the **T-valve split**. At a T-shaped junction, build two branches and place a floodgate or controlled outlet on each branch. Open one branch and close the other to send the river in one direction. Swap the gate states to redirect the flow.

rows={diversionRows}
/>

Use a T-valve when you want to:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>alternate water between two farm districts,</li>
  <li>fill a secondary reservoir after the main reservoir is full,</li>
  <li>route around an obstacle,</li>
  <li>separate normal clean flow from badwater or emergency flow.</li>
</ul>

Check three things before trusting any diversion:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Can builders reach every gate, wall, stair, and platform?</li>
  <li>Will the river still feed farms, pumps, or irrigation that need water?</li>
  <li>Where does overflow go if the reservoir fills too quickly?</li>
</ol>

If the answer is unclear, keep the diversion manual until you see how the water behaves.

</h2>

Use throttling valves when:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>you want slow reservoir release,</li>
  <li>a full gate opening floods the channel,</li>
  <li>you are feeding a small irrigation route,</li>
  <li>badwater should leave gradually,</li>
  <li>future automation needs predictable flow.</li>
</ul>

Do not use valves to hide a broken route. First make sure water has a safe path. Then use valves to make that path more stable.

<h2 id="faq">
  Timberborn dam, levee and floodgate FAQ
</h2>

### What does a dam do in Timberborn?

A dam is an early water-buffer structure. It holds part of the water height and lets excess water pass, so it is not the same as a full wall.

### Should I build a dam or tanks first?

If the river is narrow and reachable, build the dam early. If the river is wide, far, or awkward, fill tanks first and build the dam later.

### What is the difference between a dam and a floodgate?

A dam has a fixed pass-through behavior. A floodgate can be adjusted, so you can control whether water is held, released, or diverted.

### How tall are floodgates?

Plan single, double, and triple floodgates as about 1.0 m, 2.0 m, and 3.0 m of controllable water height.

### How much water evaporates in Timberborn?

A useful planning number is about 0.045 water per exposed tile per day. This is why wide shallow reservoirs lose more water than deep compact reservoirs with the same volume.

### Why does my Water Pump stop when I can still see water?

The water may be too shallow. Water Pumps need roughly 0.3 m of water depth to keep working.

### What is a spillway?

A spillway is a planned overflow route. It should send extra water somewhere safe instead of letting a full reservoir overtop a random weak edge.

### What is a T-valve in Timberborn?

A T-valve is a water split where two gated branches let you switch the flow direction. Open one branch and close the other to redirect water.

### How deep should a reservoir be?

Use 2.0 m+ as a practical early target before treating a reservoir as serious drought storage. Deeper compact storage is usually better than wide shallow storage.

### When should I automate floodgates?

Automate only after the manual route works. Automation improves a safe system; it does not fix bad routing.
