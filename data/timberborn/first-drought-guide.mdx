<GuideQuickAnswer title="Quick Answer">
To survive the **first drought in Timberborn**, start with water math, not a fixed build order.

A good baseline is about **2.1 water per adult beaver per day**. For beginner planning, use **2.5 water per beaver per day** as a safer target, and use **3/day** only as a conservative buffer for Hard Mode, messy layouts, or extra safety. A **Small Tank stores 30 water**, so one full Small Tank is not enough for a 10-beaver colony through a Normal first drought.

During the drought, estimate survival time with this quick formula: **days left ≈ current stored water ÷ (beavers × daily water use)**. If that number is lower than the remaining drought days, pause growth, focus pump work, and prepare a stronger recovery plan after the river returns.

<h2 id="timberborn-first-drought-guide">
  Timberborn first drought guide
</h2>

This **Timberborn first drought guide** is a decision tool for the first dry season. It is not a full beginner build order. The goal is to answer one question:

**When the river stops helping, can this colony still drink, eat, pump, and recover?**

The first drought tests five systems:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>stored drinking water,</li>
  <li>pump reach as the river drops,</li>
  <li>whether your dam plan can finish in time,</li>
  <li>food already in storage,</li>
  <li>population control before demand grows.</li>
</ul>

If one of those systems is weak, fix that first. Do not start a long-term beauty project while the survival timer is already running.

<h2 id="what-drought-does">
  What drought actually does
</h2>

When drought begins, normal water sources stop feeding the map. The water already on the map can still move, drain, be pumped down, and evaporate. That is why a river can look safe at the start of drought and still fail before the timer ends.

<GuideTable
caption="What drought changes in Timberborn."

rows={mechanicsRows}
/>

This is the core mechanic behind every first-drought decision. Tanks are not optional. A dam is not useful until it is finished. A pump is not safe just because it touches water on day 1.

<h2 id="first-drought-numbers">
  First drought numbers: use baseline, safe, and conservative math
</h2>

The water number matters because it controls every storage decision.

Here is the practical way to use the table:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Use **2.1/day** when you want the baseline estimate.</li>
  <li>Use **2.5/day** when planning a beginner-safe buffer.</li>
  <li>Use **3/day** when playing Hard, using long paths, or deliberately overpreparing.</li>
</ul>

Example: 10 beavers on a Normal first drought of about 2–3 days need roughly 50–75 water using the 2.5/day planning value. That means one full Small Tank is too thin, two is workable, and three is much more comfortable if your pump or dam plan is risky.

<h2 id="map-based-plan">
  Map-based plan: dam early or tanks first?
</h2>

Do not force the same first drought plan on every map. River shape and pump depth matter.

Use the table as the decision point, then use the dam section below only if the dam is still realistic for your map.

In our test colony, the early dam worked only when the site could actually finish before the drought warning ended. On a wider start, the safer play was more storage, shorter paths, and a later dam.

<h2 id="difficulty-and-scaling">
  Difficulty matters: the first drought is not always the same
</h2>

A first drought on Easy, Normal, and Hard should not be planned with the same buffer.

On Easy, the first drought is often short enough that a simple storage plan can carry you. On Normal, expect a real 2–3 day test. On Hard, the first drought can be long enough that weak tank math, shallow pumps, or an unfinished dam become colony-ending mistakes.

The second lesson is more important: **future droughts get worse.**

If you barely survive the first one, do not celebrate by adding many new beds. The first drought is the colony’s diagnostic report. Fix the system that almost failed before the next cycle becomes longer.

<h2 id="water-tanks-and-pump-reach">
  Water tanks and pump reach
</h2>

Stored water and pump reach are different problems.

A tank tells you how long beavers can drink. A pump tells you whether you can keep adding water before and during the drought. If the pump loses reach, the colony switches from “still producing water” to “living only on storage.”

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

Before the warning, confirm four things:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>Your stored water covers the current population for the expected drought length.</li>
  <li>Your pump has short paths to storage and enough free tank capacity.</li>
  <li>Your food is partly in storage, not only still growing in fields.</li>
  <li>Your first dam is either realistically finishable or intentionally delayed.</li>
</ul>

If the pump is on shallow edge water, be more conservative. Visible water is not always useful water. Once the level gets too low, the pump can stop while the channel still looks wet.

  Finish the dam only if the site is realistic
</h2>

A completed dam is useful. An unfinished dam is just stored logs in the wrong shape.

Build the first dam early if:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>the river is narrow,</li>
  <li>the build site is close,</li>
  <li>logs are available,</li>
  <li>builders can reach it by short paths,</li>
  <li>it can finish before the drought starts.</li>
</ul>

Delay the dam if:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>the river is wide,</li>
  <li>the site needs too many blocks,</li>
  <li>builders walk too far,</li>
  <li>you still lack filled tanks,</li>
  <li>food and storage are already behind.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="faction-and-population">
  Faction and population control
</h2>

The first drought is not only a water problem. It is also a demand problem. More beavers can build faster, but they also drink and eat.

<GuideTable
caption="Folktails, Iron Teeth, pumps and first drought population control."
columns={factionColumns}
rows={factionRows}
/>

For Folktails, the easiest population brake is simple: do not keep adding housing before the first drought. For Iron Teeth, keep breeding infrastructure under control when water math is tight.

Extreme population actions are not a normal beginner plan. The safer habit is to stop growth early, move workers into survival jobs, and recover the colony before expanding again.


<h2 id="during-the-drought">
  During the drought: calculate how many days you can last
</h2>

Once the drought starts, do not only look at the current water number. Compare stored water against the remaining drought length.

Use this quick estimate:

**Days left ≈ current stored water ÷ (beavers × daily water use)**

For daily water use, use **2.1** as the baseline, **2.5** as a safer beginner estimate, or **3** if you want a conservative buffer.

Example: if you have 60 stored water and 10 beavers, then:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>at 2.1/day, you have about 2.8 days,</li>
  <li>at 2.5/day, you have about 2.4 days,</li>
  <li>at 3/day, you have about 2.0 days.</li>
</ul>

If the estimate is lower than the remaining drought timer, stop growth, pause optional jobs, and focus only on water, food, pumping, hauling, and emergency construction.

<h2 id="faq">
  Timberborn first drought FAQ
</h2>

### How much water does one beaver need per day?

A useful baseline is about 2.1 water per adult beaver per day. For beginner planning, use 2.5/day. Use 3/day only as a conservative safety buffer.

### Is one Small Tank enough for the first drought?

Usually no. A Small Tank stores 30 water. For 10 beavers, that is only about one day if you use the conservative 3/day planning value.

### Should I always build a dam before the first drought?

No. Build it early only if the river is narrow, close, and reachable. If the site is wide or awkward, filled tanks are safer than an unfinished dam.

### Why did my pump stop during drought?

The river level likely dropped too low for the pump to keep working. Pump reach matters as much as the amount of visible water.

### Is the first drought longer on Hard Mode?

Yes, Hard can start with a much longer first drought than Easy or Normal. Use the forecast and plan with a larger water buffer.

### Why do later droughts feel harder?

On Normal and Hard, drought pressure increases over time. The first drought is only the first test, not the final water target.

### Should I add more housing before the first drought?

Only if water and food are clearly ahead. More beavers mean more labor, but they also increase water and food demand.

### Why did my crops die even though beavers survived?

Tanks solved drinking water, not soil irrigation. Crops need nearby water or green land to stay alive.

### What should I fix first after the drought ends?

Fix the system that almost failed: tank storage, pump reach, dam timing, food, logs, or crop irrigation.

### Does evaporation matter for the first drought?

Usually not as much as storage and pump reach, but it matters when you start building reservoirs. Deep compact storage loses less useful water than wide shallow storage.
