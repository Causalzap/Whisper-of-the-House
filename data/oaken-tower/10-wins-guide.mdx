<h2 id="ten-win-mindset">
  The 10-win mindset
</h2>

A 10-win run is not the same thing as a perfect run.

Perfect run thinking sounds like this:

- I need the exact build I saw in a video.
- I need a 3-star carry.
- I need the funniest combo.
- I need to farm more value.
- I should pivot because this other build looks stronger.

10-win thinking sounds like this:

- Can I win the next fight?
- What is my real carry?
- What item actually improves my board?
- Can I level without dying?
- Am I rolling with a target?
- Do I already have enough to finish the run?

That second mindset wins more often.

<h2 id="simple-plan">
  Simple 10-win plan
</h2>

The cleanest path is:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>Buy early tempo.</strong> Your first board needs damage, shield, healing, or a scaling item that starts working immediately.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Follow the shop's direction.</strong> Poison, Burn, Bleed, bow carry, weapon carry, shield / heal plus damage, or a real combo line can all win.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Feed the carry.</strong> Once one item is winning fights, support it instead of spreading gold everywhere.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Protect your hearts.</strong> Late losses are expensive, so stop greeding when one bad fight can end the run.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Stop when you are strong enough.</strong> If the next fight is already winning, do not ruin the run chasing style.
  </li>
</ol>

<h2 id="why-stuck">
  Why you get stuck at 6-7 wins
</h2>

If you can already reach 5, 6, or 7 wins, you do not need a basic tutorial. You need to fix the transition from “my board works” to “my board can finish the run.”

Most players get stuck there because the enemies start showing real scaling while their own board is still half tempo, half dream build.

The key difference is this: early wins prove your board has tempo. They do not prove your board has a final carry.

<h2 id="heart-checkpoints">
  Heart checkpoints: stop greeding when losses get expensive

Oaken Tower punishes late losses harder than early losses. That means your risk tolerance should change as the run goes on.

<h2 id="day-four-encounter">
  Day 4 encounter: use it to fix the run
</h2>

The Day 4 encounter is often the first big correction point. Do not choose the funniest option. Choose the option that solves your board's current weakness.

<GuideTable
  caption="Best Day 4-style encounter choices for a 10-win run."
  columns={encounterColumns}

A simple rule:

- If you are poor, take gold or economy.
- If you are dying too fast, take defense.
- If you know the exact item you need, take rerolls.
- If you already have a 2-star carry, consider upgrade value.
- If your board has no direction, take the option that gives one.

<h2 id="early-game">
  Early game: buy tempo before dreams
</h2>

In the first shops, your job is to avoid building a tower that does nothing.

Good early purchases usually do one of these:

- deal immediate damage,
- apply Poison, Burn, Bleed, Shock, or another useful pressure,
- shield or heal enough to buy time,
- start permanent scaling early,
- fit the perk or slot you already rolled,
- give you a clear direction for future shops.

Bad early purchases are items that only become good after four other things happen.

If you have to choose between a boring item that wins the next fight and a fancy item that might become good later, take the boring power unless the scaling payoff is obvious.

<h2 id="pick-carry">
  Pick one carry and stop spreading gold
</h2>

Most 10-win boards are carried by one main engine. That engine might be Poison, Burn, a bow, a shield-supported damage plan, or a scaling item.

Once you find it, stop treating every item as equally important.

<GuideTable
  caption="Specific item directions that can carry 10-win runs."
  columns={itemColumns}
  rows={itemRows}
/>

The item names matter less than the job. A carry must either kill the enemy tower, scale into the final fights, or buy enough time for another item to kill.

<h2 id="molten-bow-example">
  Example: when one carry is enough
</h2>

This is the kind of decision that separates a 10-win run from a thrown run.

In the Molten Bow run, the player started looking at other options. There was a possible sword / Katana direction, and later even the thought of pivoting into another carry. But the important thing was already obvious: **the bow was winning fights.**

The correct lesson is not “force Molten Bow every run.” It is this:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>If one item is already carrying, do not rebuild the tower just because a new shiny item appears.</strong>
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>If a pivot makes the board weaker right now, it is probably wrong for a 10-win push.</strong>
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>If your carry only needs support, buy support instead of starting a second build.</strong>
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>If you are close to 10 wins, the safest improvement is better than the coolest pivot.</strong>
  </li>
</ul>

That is why the Molten Bow example matters. The bow had already been farmed and was doing the real work. Swapping into a new sword plan looked tempting, but it risked weakening a board that was already close to finishing the run.

<h2 id="gold-decisions">
  Gold decisions: buy, level, reroll, freeze, or save
</h2>

The fastest way to lose a promising run is to spend gold without a reason.

Before spending, name the job:

- this item wins the next fight,
- this level gives me better shops and more health,
- this reroll is looking for one specific item,
- this freeze keeps a key item for next turn,
- this save gives me a better next shop.

If you cannot name the job, do not spend yet.

<h2 id="freeze">
  Freeze is stronger than beginners think
</h2>

Freeze is not just for saving an item you cannot afford. It is a timing tool.

Good freeze examples:

<ul className="my-6 list-disc space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>Freeze then level:</strong> if the shop has a key item but leveling first gives better health or shop odds, freeze the item, level, then buy it next day.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Freeze a duplicate:</strong> if a duplicate creates a 2-star or pushes your carry toward 3-star, keep it instead of rolling it away.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Freeze a support pair:</strong> if the shop has both a carry and the support that makes it work, freeze when you cannot afford both now.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Freeze before a pivot:</strong> if a new build direction appears but you need one more shop to decide, freezing buys time without losing the option.
  </li>
</ul>

Bad freeze examples:

- freezing random value,
- freezing an item that does not improve the next fight,
- freezing because the shop looks rare,
- freezing a pivot piece when your current board is already winning.

A frozen shop should answer a question. If it does not, let it go.

<h2 id="level-reroll">
  When to level or reroll
</h2>

Leveling and rerolling are not opposites. They answer different questions.

**Level** when the board is stable and you need better shop quality, more health, or access to higher-rarity pieces.

**Reroll** when the board has a target and one specific item changes the run.

The mistake is using either button emotionally. Do not level because you are bored with the shop. Do not reroll because you are tilted from a close loss.

For the full breakdown, use the <Link href="/oaken-tower/level-vs-reroll">Level vs Reroll Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="final-fights">
  Final fights: stop getting greedy
</h2>

The last stretch is where players throw.

When you are close to 10 wins, ask one question:

**What wins the next fight?**

Not:

- What makes the build cooler?
- What gives the biggest screenshot?
- What lets me farm more?

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### How many wins do I need in Oaken Tower?

You need 10 wins to complete a normal run. After that, you can think about extra scaling, big damage, or fighting the Oak, but the clean target is 10 wins first.

### Why do I keep getting stuck at 6 or 7 wins?

Because early tempo is no longer enough. By that point, you need a real carry, a scaling path, enough hearts, and disciplined gold spending. Many players throw this stage by over-rolling or chasing a weak 3-star.

### What is the easiest way to get 10 wins?

Build early tempo, choose one carry direction, and stop wasting gold. Poison, Burn, bow carry, shield plus damage, and strong weapon carry boards are all fine if the shop supports them.

### Should I force the same build every run?

No. Let the shop and perk decide. Forcing a build with no support is one of the easiest ways to lose before 10 wins.

### Is leveling early good?

Leveling early is good when your board is already stable. It is bad when you skip power and die because the tower has no damage.

### Should I reroll early?

Only if you have a target. Rerolling for a duplicate, carry, or support item is fine. Rerolling because the shop is boring usually loses gold.

### Do I need 3-star items to get 10 wins?

No. A 3-star carry can help, but many 10-win runs are carried by a strong 1-star or 2-star item with good support. Chase 3-star only when it is actually your win condition.

### Does positioning matter in Oaken Tower?

Yes. Some items care about adjacency, slots, ranged behavior, or what item they trigger. Put the carry first, then place support around it.

### Why do I lose with a strong build idea?

Because a strong idea is not the same as a strong board. You still need early tempo, enough hearts, correct leveling, useful positioning, and a carry that actually triggers before the enemy kills you.
