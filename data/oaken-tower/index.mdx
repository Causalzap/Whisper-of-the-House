<h2 id="simple-game-plan">
  The simple plan for winning more runs
</h2>

A clean Oaken Tower run usually follows this pattern:

<ol className="my-6 list-decimal space-y-2 pl-6">
  <li>
    <strong>Buy early tempo.</strong> Your first shops need damage, shield, healing, or scaling that starts working soon.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Read the shop direction.</strong> Poison, Burn, bow carry, weapon carry, shield / heal, or combo lines can all work if the shop supports them.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Feed one real carry.</strong> Once one item is winning fights, support it instead of rebuilding the whole tower.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Spend gold with a job.</strong> Level for future value, reroll for a named target, freeze timing, or buy power for the next fight.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Stop chasing when the board is already good.</strong> Many 10-win attempts fail because the player keeps shopping for a prettier board.
  </li>
</ol>

The mistake is trying to make every shop perfect. You do not need perfect. You need enough strength to live, enough scaling to finish, and enough discipline to stop spending gold on ideas that do not help the next fight.

<h2 id="poison-first">
  Why Poison is a good first build to learn
</h2>

Poison is a good first direction because it teaches the core Oaken Tower loop: apply pressure, make the right item trigger more often, survive long enough for scaling to matter, and adjust when enemies bring shield, heal, burst, or bow carry boards.

You should consider Poison when:

- your opening shop gives you Poison Dagger, Petal Staff, or another Poison signal,
- Toxic or a Poison Slot points you there,
- you find Venomstrike, Toxins, Venom Ring, or Poison support,
- your board has fast triggers, cooldown help, or defensive time,
- you need a damage plan that keeps working as the fight drags on.

The mistake is forcing Poison from nothing. If the game gives you a clean Poison start, take it. If it does not, play the strongest board first and pivot only when the shop gives you a real reason.

<h2 id="ten-wins">
  How to think about 10 wins
</h2>

A 10-win run is not about making the funniest late-game board. It is about surviving long enough for your real damage plan to matter.

The most common wall is around 6-7 wins. At that point, early tempo is no longer enough. You need a real carry, enough hearts, and the discipline to stop over-rolling for a dream board.

Your shops should answer three questions:

1. **Can I win or safely survive the next fight?**
2. **Do I know which item is my carry?**
3. **Does this purchase help the carry, protect my hearts, or finish the run?**

If the answer is no, do not overthink it. Buy tempo, save gold, freeze, or level only if your board can survive.

<h2 id="level-vs-reroll">
  Level vs reroll: the decision that decides most runs
</h2>

The hardest shop question is not “what is the best item?” It is:

**Should this gold become XP, a reroll, a freeze, immediate power, or saved value?**

Use this quick rule:

- **Level** when your board is stable and future shop value matters.
- **Reroll** when you can name the exact target.
- **Freeze** when the shop has a key item but the timing is wrong.
- **Buy power** when the next fight is dangerous.
- **Save** when your board already wins and the shop adds nothing.

<h2 id="three-star-items">
  Do not chase every 3-star item
</h2>

Chasing a 3-star item is not a single shop button like leveling, rerolling, freezing, buying, or saving. It is a multi-turn plan, so judge it separately from your normal gold decision.

3-star items look exciting, but they are not automatically correct. A 3-star support item that does not win fights can be worse than a lower-star carry that actually kills the enemy tower.

Chase a 3-star item when:

- it is your main damage source,
- it is your Poison, Burn, Bleed, Shock, shield, heal, or scaling engine,
- you already have duplicates,
- Crown Forge, Mirror Elixir, or another shortcut supports the path,
- the upgrade changes the next fight.

Do not chase a 3-star item when:

- you are rolling from zero copies,
- the item is not central to your build,
- you need health or shop rarity more,
- you are upgrading support while your damage is weak,
- your current board already reaches 10 wins safely.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### What should I do first in Oaken Tower?

Build a board that can win or safely survive early fights. Do not force a perfect late-game idea. Buy tempo, pick one real direction, and only level when your board can survive.

### What is the easiest build for beginners?

Poison is one of the easiest directions to learn because it teaches repeated triggers, scaling pressure, and damage-over-time decisions. Do not force it from nothing; take it when the shop or perk setup gives you a real Poison signal.

### Why do I get stuck around 6 or 7 wins?

Because early tempo stops being enough. You need a real carry, a scaling path, enough hearts, and better gold discipline. Many players throw this stage by over-rolling or chasing weak 3-star upgrades.

### Should I level or reroll?

Level when your board is stable and future shops are worth the XP. Reroll when you can name the exact item, duplicate, support piece, or matchup fix you need.

### Should I always chase 3-star items?

No. Chase 3-star only when the item is your carry or creates a major breakpoint. If the item is not central to your build, the gold may be better spent leveling, buying power, freezing, or saving.

### Why did my perk not unlock?

Usually because the condition was stricter than expected, a potion or encounter reward may have interfered, or the perk was unlocked but not added to your selected pool. Check the exact in-game condition before repeating the attempt.

### Is Oaken Tower more like a wiki game or a decision game?

The wiki is useful for checking raw item and perk data. This guide hub is for decisions: what to build, when to level, when to reroll, when to chase upgrades, and how to stop losing winnable runs.
