<h2 id="opening-route">
  The opening route I use from an empty warehouse
</h2>

The early tutorial became much easier once I stopped treating every new menu as
something I needed to use immediately.

I begin in this order:

1. enter the warehouse;
2. turn on the power;
3. open the garage doors;
4. create the company on the computer;
5. open **Properties**;
6. buy Greenpark Lot;
7. collect the truck keys;
8. drive to the marked property.

Greenpark Lot started with the seller asking around **$800**.

I offered **$700**, then **$730**, and finally bought it for **$750**.

I do not spend time comparing every other property during the tutorial.

My Greenpark Lot contained:

- Stone;

- Sandstone;
- Limestone;
- Coal.

That was enough to learn the basic mining, processing, storage, sales, and
contract systems.

A different property becomes useful once I know which resource or production
stage is blocking progress.

<h2 id="first-dig">
  First dig: collect 12 ore and return
</h2>

The first major beginner trap is staying at the property too long.

The tutorial asks for **12 ore**.

Once I have those 12 items, I:

1. pack them into a mining crate;
2. place the crate on the truck;
3. return to the warehouse.

I do not fill the entire truck first.

Returning advances the tutorial and gives me access to the Sorting Station and
the free Stone Crusher.

Another long Surface-mining session before that point only produces resources
I cannot process efficiently yet.

### When I keep mining

After the basic factory loop is working, I continue mining when:

- the Detector shows a stable target within roughly **1–3 meters**;
- the target matches an active contract or immediate production need;
- the backpack and truck still have useful capacity;
- and I do not need to return for a daytime transaction.

### When I turn back

I return when:

- I have enough material for the current contract;
- the **50-item** backpack or truck is effectively full;
- fatigue is causing long blinks and noticeably slower work;
- or I still need to complete Stock Sell, submit a contract offer, or purchase a
  property before night.

Mining and active machines can continue after dark.

I treat night as a restriction on certain business transactions rather than
the end of all productive work.

Machines can then use those stored resources without me carrying each raw item

### Why a machine says it has no material

When the warehouse contains the resource but a machine does not use it, I
check:

1. whether the mining crate went through the Sorting Station;
2. whether the resource appears in Factory Storage;
3. whether the correct input is selected;
4. whether the correct recipe is active;
5. whether a valid output pallet or area exists;
6. whether I pressed Start.

A machine can contain the right material and still remain idle when no recipe,
output, or active queue has been selected.

<h2 id="first-sale">
  Make one Stock Sale, but do not build the whole route around it
</h2>

My first Stock Sell transaction used:

- **6 Sand**;
- **$11 per unit**;
- **$66 total revenue**.

That transaction is useful because it confirms that:

- a partial pallet can be sold;
- the marked storage area is tracking the pallet;
- and the warehouse computer recognizes the product.

After that first test, I use Stock Sell mainly for:

- immediate operating cash;
- severe overstock;
- blocked storage space;
- or the final amount needed for one specific purchase.

I do not sell every useful product merely because an offer exists.

I keep a small reserve of commonly requested processed materials and sell only
the surplus after accounting for the next likely contract or production step.

<h2 id="contracts">
  Use Contracts as the main early progression route
</h2>

Contracts require more preparation than Stock Sell, but they provide a stronger
early progression route.

Before accepting one, I check:

- the exact product names;
- the required quantities;
- tracked finished stock;
- the machines needed for missing products;
- available raw resources;
- and the remaining delivery time.

The exact product name matters.

These are separate contract items:

- Clay Block and Clay Brick;
- Stone Block and Stone Brick;
- Limestone Block and Cement;
- Coal and Crushed Coal.

My first repeatable contract route is:

1. accept an offer on the warehouse computer;
2. select that contract inside the Pallet Packer;
3. produce the exact requested products;
4. keep Contract Output and Excess Output clear;
5. move the completed delivery load with the forklift;
6. place every required load on the truck;
7. finish the handoff before the deadline.

<h2 id="upgrade-order">
  My early upgrade order
</h2>

I buy upgrades that shorten the next complete mining, production, or contract
cycle.

I do not rank an upgrade only by how exciting it looks in the unlock menu.

<GuideTable
  caption="My tested early upgrade order and the bottleneck each purchase needs to solve."
  columns={earlyUpgradeColumns}
  rows={earlyUpgradeRows}
/>

The first three purchases solve different stages:

```text
Shovel
→ Opens the route

Pickaxe
→ Recovers the exposed resource

Detector
→ Locates the next specific target
```

The order can change when one stage is already working well.

For example, I delay the Detector while Limestone remains visible, but buy it
earlier when a contract requires one resource that I can no longer locate from
the Surface.

The Backpack upgrade is less urgent when:

- the truck is already full;
- the factory cannot process the current stock;
- the missing contract item is not in the active property;
- or the production line is blocked.

More carrying capacity does not repair those bottlenecks.

<h2 id="mining-lift">
  Build a narrow Mining Lift route instead of clearing everything
</h2>

My first instinct was to flatten the entire Surface.

That made the site look tidy but spent too much time removing ordinary
material.

Once useful resources moved into the Mid and Deep Layers, I placed a Mining
Lift along the property edge.

The initial tested costs were:

- **$25** for the Lift Head;
- **$10** for each Lift Extension.

My construction sequence is:

1. place the Lift Head on a clear property edge;
2. face the attached ladder toward terrain I can excavate;
3. clear one narrow section below it;
4. add one Extension;
5. test the ladder downward and back upward;
6. clear the ladder face and landing when access fails;
7. add another Extension only after the current connection works.

<h2 id="faq">
  Ore Factory Squad beginner FAQ
</h2>

### Should I buy Metal Processing or Warehouse Automation first?

I choose Metal Processing when Copper or Iron is already in Factory Storage and
Ingot contracts are appearing.

I choose Warehouse Automation only when manual pallet movement has become the
real bottleneck and I can afford the license plus the required logistics
equipment.

When neither condition is true, I continue completing basic contracts and
improving the mining route instead of buying either system early.

### Does Level 5 give me a free Warehouse Robot?

No.

Level 5 unlocks access to later logistics systems, but it does not place a
robot, Drop Zone, or Pallet Dispenser in the warehouse automatically.

I still need to purchase and configure the required equipment. Until that setup
is complete, the forklift remains part of the normal pallet route.

### Can machines keep working overnight?

Yes. My active machines continued processing after dark.

Before using End Day, I check the contract timer because an order with one day
remaining expired when I advanced the day.

I let nearly completed production finish before moving to the next morning.

### Is Westmont Property a good replacement for Greenpark Lot?

Westmont was listed at **$700** and covered **2,000 m²**, but its preview still
focused on Limestone, Clay, Stone, Sandstone, and Coal.

It can replace Greenpark as another basic-material property, but it does not
solve a Copper or Iron bottleneck.

I continue comparing listings when metal processing is the reason for moving.

### Can I use a controller with the forklift?

Yes. I tested an Xbox-style controller with the forklift.

Lifting, fine movement, and fine steering worked.

I still preferred keyboard controls when placing pallets tightly inside the
marked storage area or aligning a completed contract load with the truck.
