<h2 id="remaining-resources">
  Check Remaining Resources before digging
</h2>

I do not begin by choosing a corner and digging straight down.

I open the warehouse computer and use:

<div className="my-5 rounded-xl border border-slate-200 bg-slate-50 px-5 py-4 shadow-sm">
  <div className="font-mono text-sm leading-7 text-slate-700">
    Computer → Properties → Active Property → Remaining Resources
  </div>
</div>

The property panel separates its estimate into:

- **Surface Layer**;
- **Mid Layer**;
- **Deep Layer**.

A Surface value of zero does not mean the entire property has no Limestone.

In one later Greenpark Lot snapshot, the surface had already been depleted
while the panel still showed approximately:

- **Mid Layer: 0–2 Limestone**;
- **Deep Layer: 2–6 Limestone**.

These ranges are estimates, not exact coordinates or guaranteed item counts.

I use them as a decision tool:

- Limestone in Surface means shallow excavation is still reasonable;
- Limestone only in Mid means I reuse an established lower route;
- Limestone only in Deep means I prepare the Mining Lift before widening more
  tunnels;
- no Limestone in any layer means I stop searching that property.

My Greenpark Lot contained enough Limestone for early Cement production, but I
do not treat its quantity or exact layer distribution as a fixed spawn for
every save.

<h2 id="detector">
  How I follow the Limestone Detector
</h2>

I set the Detector target specifically to **Limestone** rather than leaving it
on a broad search mode.

Then I compare consecutive distance readings.

In one search, the distance moved through:

- **2.1 m**;
- **1.3 m**;
- **1.4 m**.

The drop from 2.1 m to 1.3 m told me I was moving toward the target. The slight
increase to 1.4 m suggested I had moved past the closest point.

When another movement caused the reading to jump toward **6.5 m**, I returned
to the previous position instead of extending the same tunnel.

### The Detector measures three-dimensional distance

A short reading does not prove that the Limestone is directly in front of me.

The target may be:

- behind the current wall;
- below the floor;
- above the tunnel ceiling;
- or slightly behind my current position.

When the Detector is close but no Limestone is visible, I:

1. rotate and compare the reading in several directions;
2. move one small step and scan again;
3. check the floor and ceiling;
4. remove a small amount of ordinary terrain;
5. stop when the distance suddenly becomes much longer.

I do not keep digging forward merely because the Detector continues making a
sound.

The Sorting Station separates the crate contents and transfers the raw
Limestone into the factory's internal resource storage.

This is different from finished-product storage:

- raw Limestone enters internal **Factory Storage** through the Sorting
  Station;
- finished Limestone Blocks and Cement remain on physical product pallets;
- contracts and Stock Sell count those finished products when their pallets are
  inside the marked storage area.

I return once I have enough Limestone for the current production target or the
nearest Detector reading becomes too distant to justify another tunnel.

<h2 id="forming-press">
  Feed Limestone Blocks into the Forming Press
</h2>

In my manual setup, I complete the physical machine route rather than expecting
the Forming Press to pull finished Blocks automatically from the warehouse.

My sequence is:

1. select **Limestone Block** on the Stone Crusher;
2. make sure a product pallet is available at the Crusher output;
3. let the Crusher produce the required Blocks;
4. move the Limestone Block pallet to the Forming Press input;
5. select the **Cement** recipe;
6. make sure a valid output pallet is ready;
7. press **Start**.

The critical distinction is that the Forming Press needs **Limestone Blocks**,
not raw Limestone.

I also check the machine interface after positioning the input. A pallet placed
beside the machine but outside its input detection area may not supply the
recipe.

### My tested 20-Cement output

I confirmed one Cement output reaching **20 units**.

That amount required:

- **40 Limestone Blocks**;
- **100 seconds** of uninterrupted Forming Press time.

I did not separately test whether another pallet or later setup can hold more,
so I treat 20 as my confirmed output amount rather than claiming it is the
absolute capacity in every configuration.

<h2 id="keep-or-convert">
  How many Limestone Blocks should I keep?
</h2>

I do not automatically convert every Limestone Block into Cement.

Contracts treat **Limestone Block** and **Cement** as separate finished
products.

One documented early contract required **20 Limestone Blocks**, while another
later order required Cement.

My inventory rule is:

1. check accepted contracts;
2. reserve the exact number of Limestone Blocks already required;
3. convert only the excess into Cement;
4. keep both product pallets inside the marked storage area.

For example, with **61 Limestone Blocks** and an active requirement for 20
Blocks:

- reserve **20 Limestone Blocks**;
- leave **41 Blocks** available;
- produce **20 Cement** from 40 Blocks;
- keep **1 extra Limestone Block**.

That is safer than converting all 61 and returning underground because the next
contract needs the intermediate product.

<h2 id="faq">
  Ore Factory Squad Limestone and Cement FAQ
</h2>

### Why do 61 Limestone Blocks only make 30 Cement?

The recipe consumes two complete Limestone Blocks for each Cement. Sixty Blocks
produce 30 Cement, while the final odd Block remains unused until another Block
is added.

### Does Cement production continue after dark?

Yes. An active Forming Press continued processing after nightfall in my test.

This confirms that production can continue during the nighttime period. It
does not prove that an unfinished queue always survives End Day, a save reload,
or every later update.

### Why does my Cement not appear in contracts or Stock Sell?

Raw resources and finished products use different storage handling.

Raw Limestone enters internal Factory Storage through the Sorting Station.
Finished Cement must remain on a product pallet inside the marked storage area
before the warehouse stock system can count it for contracts and Stock Sell.

### What does a Remaining Resources range such as 0–2 mean?

It is an estimate for that property layer, not a guaranteed exact quantity or
coordinate.

I use it to decide whether another Detector search is reasonable, not as proof
that two easy deposits remain.

### Should I buy the Detector or Forming Press first?

I buy the Detector first when the bottleneck is locating a specific Limestone
deposit below the surface.

I prioritize the Forming Press when Limestone Blocks are already stored and
Cement is the product preventing a contract from being completed.
