<h2 id="why-battery-drains">
  Why your van battery keeps draining in Outbound
</h2>

Your van is electric, and it uses battery power as you drive. Early on, that feels manageable because the first routes are short. The problem starts when you drive farther, add more workstations, build upward, or try to force hills before your vehicle upgrades are ready.

Most early battery problems come from one of these causes:

- you drove too far without stopping,
- you ignored wood and fiber as emergency fuel,
- you passed a charging station without topping off,
- you expected passive power to work while driving,
- your battery capacity is still low,
- your motor / slope upgrade is not ready,
- your van build is getting too heavy.

The fix is not one single item. It is a habit:

**Charge before you are empty, keep common fuel, avoid overbuilding before weight upgrades, and upgrade the van before forcing longer routes.**

The Power Unit is at the back of your van, near the battery and motor information.

Early on, this is the system you should understand before anything else. You can put **wood** or **fiber** into the Power Unit to charge the battery.

For new players, the best rule is:

**Wood is your main early emergency fuel. Fiber is your backup.**

Wood is usually easier to justify as fuel because you find loose logs often, and once you have the Axe, bigger wood sources become better. But wood is also used for Planks, bridges, workstations, and building pieces, so do not burn your entire supply.

Fiber works when you are stuck, but it becomes important for Sewing Table, gear, backpack, and clothing upgrades. Use it when you need enough charge to recover the route, not as your permanent main fuel.

Charging stations are useful, but they are not infinite.

A charging station can only be used again after a cooldown, so do not build your route around repeatedly draining and refilling at the same station. Treat chargers like **checkpoints**.

Use a station when:

- you pass one during a long drive,
- you are about to enter a new biome,
- you are heading toward a far landmark,
- you are trying a steep route,
- you are low on fuel materials,
- you are in co-op and multiple players are using the same van battery.

A charging station is also a good moment to reset the whole trip: deposit resources, eat food, check your vouchers, and decide if the next detour is still worth pushing.

<h2 id="passive-power-parked">
  Passive power only helps when your van is parked
</h2>

This is the biggest passive-power mistake.

Solar, wind, and rainwater / hydro systems are not a magic battery that refills while you freely drive across the map. They are best treated as parked support.

That means passive power works best when you:

- stop to build,
- stop to process materials,
- stop to organize storage,
- park near a useful resource area,
- wait out a route reset,
- place the van correctly for water or weather systems,
- stack multiple passive generators over time.

Do not expect passive systems to replace manual fuel and battery upgrades. They reduce pressure when parked, but your driving route still depends on stored charge.

**manual fuel + charging stations + battery upgrades + wind turbines = safer travel**

Once you unlock stronger wind options, upgrade them when the materials make sense. Passive energy gets better the more sources you stack.

If you want the most general passive power first, wind is usually easier to recommend. If you are planning a water-side base stop, rainwater / hydro becomes much more attractive.

Energy problems are not always about “how do I get more charge?”

Sometimes the real answer is:

**Your van needs an upgrade.**

The main vehicle progression stats to watch are:

- **Battery capacity**
- **Motor level**
- **Slope level**
- **Weight capacity**
- **Building space**

Battery capacity lets you travel longer before every drive becomes stressful. Motor and slope upgrades matter because certain roads are vehicle-gated. Weight capacity matters because your van becomes heavier as you add workstations, storage, furniture, building pieces, roof floors, and decorations.

A heavier van can feel slower, climb worse, and burn through energy faster on rough routes. That does not mean you should avoid building. It means you should upgrade weight capacity and vehicle systems alongside your mobile base.

<h2 id="pre-trip-checklist">
  Pre-trip power checklist
</h2>

Before a long drive, check only the things that change the next route:

1. **Battery:** Is there enough charge to reach the next stop?
2. **Fuel:** Do you have spare wood or emergency Fiber?
3. **Weight:** Did you add heavy workstations or roof pieces before upgrading capacity?
4. **Route:** Is there a charger, water stop, or safe parking point nearby?
5. **Backpack:** Are you leaving with enough empty space to make the trip useful?

If two or more of those answers look bad, fix the van before you leave.

<h2 id="fuel-vs-crafting">
  Should you burn wood, fiber, or rare materials?
</h2>

This is the main energy tradeoff.

Wood, Fiber, and later biome materials can help power the van, but they are also recipe ingredients. Use this table before feeding everything into the Power Unit.

  caption="Outbound fuel vs crafting priority."

The simple rule is:

**Burn common materials when you are stranded. Save rare materials until you know your next upgrade cost.**

For example:

- burn some common Everwood if the van is low and you are still early,
- avoid burning all your wood if you need Planks for a bridge,
- avoid burning new biome wood before checking tool and workstation recipes,
- use Fiber as backup instead of your main plan,
- keep rare resources in van storage until you understand their upgrade chain.

If you are missing materials, use the <Link href="/outbound/early-resources">Outbound Early Resource Guide</Link>.

<h2 id="what-to-do-if-out-of-power">
  What to do if your van runs out of power
</h2>

If your van runs out of power, do not panic. Fix the situation in order:

1. **Check nearby resources.**  
   Look for loose logs, Fiber bushes, supply boxes, or roadside junk.

2. **Deposit heavy items if possible.**  
   If your backpack is full, every trip feels worse.

3. **Feed the Power Unit.**  
   Use wood first if you have it. Use Fiber if you need emergency charge.

4. **Wait for enough battery to move.**  
   You do not need a perfect charge. You need enough to reach a safer stop.

5. **Drive toward a charger, camp, or better parking spot.**  
   Do not immediately start another long detour.

6. **Change the route.**  
   If the trip drained you once, it may drain you again unless you upgrade, restock, reduce weight, or choose a better stop.

If this happens often, your problem is not bad luck. Your route is too long, your van is too heavy, or your upgrade path is behind your exploration route.

<h2 id="energy-and-first-biome">
  Energy and first biome progression
</h2>

Energy becomes a progression issue in the first map because several blockers ask more from your van than basic driving.

The clearest example is the steep slope.

If you are still trying to leave the first map, energy connects to:

- getting enough battery to explore,
- bringing materials back to the van,
- making Bolts,
- unlocking Vehicle Research,
- upgrading the motor,
- upgrading slope handling,
- managing weight as the van build grows,
- reaching the Mossrock exit chain.

Read <Link href="/outbound/leave-first-biome">How to Leave the First Biome in Outbound</Link> if your battery problem is tied to bridges, slopes, Mossrocks, or the first map exit.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Why does my battery keep running out in Outbound?

Your van battery drains as you drive, and early battery capacity is limited. Long routes, heavy van builds, steep terrain, and poor fuel habits make this worse. Use the Power Unit, charging stations, and vehicle upgrades.

### How often can I use a charging station?

Charging stations have a cooldown, so you cannot spam the same station endlessly. Treat them as route checkpoints and top off before you are desperate.

### Does solar or wind charge while driving?

Passive power is mainly useful while the van is parked. Solar, wind, and rainwater / hydro systems reduce pressure during stops, but they do not replace battery capacity and fuel planning while traveling.

### What is the best early fuel?

Common wood is the best early manual fuel. Fiber works as an emergency backup, but you should save most Fiber for Sewing Table, gear, backpack, and clothing upgrades.

### Is solar power good in Outbound?

Solar is useful as passive daytime support, but early solar can feel slow and depends on daylight and weather. Do not rely on solar alone in the first map.

### Is wind power better than solar?

Wind becomes the strongest long-term passive layer once you unlock better turbines. Solar is still useful, but wind is easier to recommend as your later passive backbone.

### How does rainwater or hydro power work?

Rainwater / hydro power is position and condition dependent. Park near a valid water source or in the right weather setup, then treat it as parked passive generation rather than driving power.

### Why can’t my van climb a steep slope?

You need a motor or slope upgrade, and a heavy van can make the problem worse. Unlock Vehicle Research, craft the required parts, and upgrade the vehicle before trying again.

### Does van weight affect energy?

Yes. More workstations, storage, furniture, roof pieces, and decorations make the van heavier. A heavier van can slow down, climb worse, and consume power less efficiently, so weight capacity upgrades matter.

### How does energy work in co-op?

The van battery is shared. Multiple players crafting, building, processing, and driving can drain power faster, so assign one player to monitor battery and fuel.
