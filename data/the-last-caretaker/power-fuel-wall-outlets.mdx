<h2 id="what-wall-outlets-are">
  What wall outlets are in Update 5
</h2>

Wall outlets are **buildable connection points** added with Update 5. They are not the same as the original fixed sockets already built into parts of the ship.

That distinction matters because old fixed sockets can feel random or awkward, while buildable wall outlets let me choose cleaner connection points for the systems I actually use.

The short version is:

* **power wall outlets** help organize electricity;
* **fuel wall outlets** help organize oil, diesel, and petrol routes;
* **gas wall outlets** help organize oxygen or gas-style routes;
* **electric cable extenders** still help when the outlet is close but not close enough;
* **Grid Scan** helps when the power network is too messy to read by sight.

Wall outlets are not wireless routing. They only make a better connection point. The outlet still needs the right network, the right direction, enough range, and a clear output.

<h2 id="unlock-and-build"> How to unlock and build wall outlets </h2>

Wall outlets are unlocked from the Update 5 skill screen. After unlocking the outlet type you need, open the build prompt and check the current material cost before rebuilding your ship layout.

I do not start by replacing every old socket. I use this order:

Unlock the outlet type I need.
Check the build prompt for that outlet.
Place the outlet near the machine, tank, or system it should support.
Connect the new route.
Test the machine, tank, or socket.
Remove the old cable only after the new route works.

Do not delete old cables first. If the new wall outlet route fails, the old route gives you a fallback while you fix placement, direction, range, or blocked output.

<h2 id="when-to-use-wall-outlets">
  When to use wall outlets
</h2>

I use wall outlets when direct cable routing becomes hard to read.

That usually happens after I add:

* extra batteries or generators;
* Oil Whale transfer equipment;
* oil, diesel, or petrol tanks;
* refineries or pumps;
* Project Eden support inputs;
* oxygen or gas routing;
* more machines inside the cabin or on the deck.

If the starter boat still has a simple layout, I do not rebuild everything immediately. Wall outlets become valuable when the ship has enough systems that I can no longer tell what each cable or hose is doing at a glance.

The best use case is not making the boat look cleaner. It is making the boat easier to fix when something stops working.

<h2 id="power-wall-outlets"> Power wall outlets and electric extenders </h2>

Power is the first network I clean up because every other system depends on it.

I place a power wall outlet near the machines it should support, then connect that outlet back to a stable power source. Electric extenders are still useful for short gaps, but I keep the main outlet close enough that the route stays readable.

<figure className="my-8"> <Image src="/images/the-last-caretaker/the-last-caretaker-electric-cable-extender-input.webp" alt="The Last Caretaker electric cable extender connected into a power route." width={1600} height={900} className="rounded-xl" /> <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400"> Extenders help bridge small gaps, but the wall outlet should still be placed near the section it is meant to support. </figcaption> </figure>

My power setup order is:

Pick the machine or ship section I want to power.
Place the outlet close to that area.
Connect the outlet to a stable power source.
Test the machine.
Remove the old temporary cable after the new route works.

This keeps the route easy to debug. If a machine stops working later, I know which outlet controls that section.

<h2 id="range-and-distance">
  Range, cable distance, and walls
</h2>

Range is where many wall outlet setups fail.

The visible distance is not always the only distance that matters. If a route cuts through walls, floors, or awkward ship geometry, the cable budget can disappear much faster than expected. A route that looks short in a straight line can behave like a much longer connection once it passes through structure.

When range feels wrong, I check:

* Is the cable trying to pass through walls or floors?
* Is the target technically close but blocked by ship structure?
* Would moving the outlet one wall closer reduce the whole route?
* Am I stacking extenders because the first placement was bad?
* Is there a cleaner wall or deck point for the same network?

A good outlet route should be short, visible, and easy to follow. If I have to trace it through half the ship, it is not a cleanup anymore.

<h2 id="fuel-and-gas-wall-outlets">
  Fuel and gas wall outlets
</h2>

Fuel and gas outlets are the same planning problem from a different angle: they need readable routes and clear outputs.

I use fuel wall outlets for:

* oil storage;
* diesel tanks;
* petrol tanks;
* refinery output;
* engine support;
* Oil Whale transfer routes;
* cleaner tank rooms.

I use gas wall outlets for:

* oxygen-style routing;
* Project Eden support planning;
* underwater preparation systems;
* routes where output direction matters more than appearance.

The main rule is to keep each network readable. Oil, diesel, petrol, and gas should not all become one wall of confusing sockets.

A good fuel or gas route should answer three questions quickly:

* What is feeding this outlet?
* What is this outlet sending to?
* What tank, machine, or support system depends on it?

If I cannot answer those questions by looking at the setup, I simplify before adding more tanks.

<h2 id="blocked-output">
  Blocked output and bad placement
</h2>

A wall socket can look correct and still fail because the output side is blocked.

    If the build prompt says blocked output, move the socket. A clean-looking placement is useless if the output cannot connect.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

When I see a blocked output or a socket that refuses to work, I check this order:

1. Is the output side blocked by a wall, tank, machine, or floor edge?
2. Is the socket facing the wrong direction?
3. Is the target close enough?
4. Is the outlet connected to the correct network type?
5. Is another socket or cable taking the route?
6. Did I place it for looks instead of function?

Most outlet problems are placement problems. I move the socket before rebuilding the whole network.

<h2 id="grid-scan">
  When to use Grid Scan
</h2>

Grid Scan is useful when the ship looks connected but a system still does not work.

I use it when:

* power seems to disappear;
* a machine looks connected but stays off;
* I move batteries or generators;
* I add an Oil Whale setup;
* I clean up old extenders;
* I want to confirm a section is powered before removing backup cables.

Grid Scan is not needed for every small starter layout. It becomes valuable once the boat has multiple networks and I cannot trust my eyes alone.

<h2 id="cleanup-order">
  My ship cleanup order
</h2>

When the ship is messy, I rebuild one network at a time.

My order is:

1. Turn off nonessential machines.
2. Confirm battery reserve and power generation.
3. Pick one network: power, fuel, or gas.
4. Build the new outlet route.
5. Test the route.
6. Remove the old cable or hose.
7. Move to the next network.

I do not rebuild power, oil, diesel, petrol, gas, oxygen, and Project Eden routing all at once. That makes it harder to know what broke.

<h2 id="common-mistakes">
  Common wall outlet mistakes
</h2>

* **Confusing buildable wall outlets with old fixed sockets:** Update 5 outlets are placed by you, so placement is part of the solution.
* **Treating outlets like wireless routing:** they still need input, output, range, and the correct system type.
* **Deleting old cables too early:** test the new route first.
* **Ignoring wall and floor distance:** routes through structure can consume more range than expected.
* **Placing sockets for looks:** a clean-looking socket can still have blocked output.
* **Mixing oil, diesel, petrol, and gas routes:** keep each route readable.
* **Using too many extenders:** one extender can help; several extenders usually mean the outlet is misplaced.
* **Rebuilding everything at once:** fix one network, test it, then move on.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Are wall outlets new in Update 5?

Yes. These are buildable wall outlet tools added with Update 5, separate from the ship’s older fixed sockets.

### Why is my wall outlet not working?

Check output direction, range, input source, target system, network type, and blocked output. Most wall outlet problems come from placement or connection issues.

### How do I unlock wall outlets?

Unlock them through the Update 5 progression / skill route, then check the current build prompt for the material cost before placing them.

### Are wall outlets wireless?

No. Wall outlets are cleaner connection points, not wireless routing. They still need valid connections and enough range.

### Do cables lose range through walls or floors?

They can feel much shorter when the route passes through ship structure. If a cable burns too much range, move the outlet closer or route around the obstruction.

### What does blocked output mean?

The socket is too close to a wall, machine, tank, floor edge, or another object. Move it until the output side has clear space.

### Do I still need electric cable extenders?

Yes. Extenders are useful for small gaps. If you need several extenders, move the wall outlet closer instead.

### Should I clean up power or fuel first?

Clean up the system that is blocking progress. If machines lose power, fix power first. If oil, diesel, or petrol storage is confusing, fix fuel routing first.

### Do wall outlets help Project Eden?

Yes. Project Eden can need support inputs after the frame is placed, so cleaner power, fuel, gas, and oxygen-style routing can save time later.
