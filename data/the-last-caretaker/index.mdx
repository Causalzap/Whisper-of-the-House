<h2 id="start-here">
  Start here: new player or returning player?
</h2>

The Last Caretaker now has two very different starting routes.

If I am **new**, I focus on the survival foundation first: power, batteries, cables, recycler, fabricator, diesel, the first boat setup, and how not to drain the wrong power source.

If I am **returning for New Horizons**, I care about a different set of problems: what changed, where Pishon is, whether Project Eden is ready, whether the Oil Whale is worth the cost, how to enter Research Outpost Theta 9, and whether my boat routing is clean enough for more systems.

That is why I use this page as a route picker. Start here, then jump to the guide that matches the system blocking you right now.

<h2 id="first-hour-route">
  First-hour route for new players
</h2>

If this is your first run, do not rush straight into every new system. The early game is still about learning how the ship survives.

My first-hour priority is:

1. **Reconnect power** so doors, lights, stations, and early rooms stop blocking progress.
2. **Learn cables and outlets** because most later problems are bigger versions of the first cable puzzle.
3. **Use the recycler and fabricator** so trash turns into useful parts instead of staying as dead weight.
4. **Fix the boat systems** before leaving the dock.
5. **Understand battery and diesel storage** before sailing into darker water.
6. **Unlock early solar power** when the game points you toward it.
7. **Keep backup saves** before large locations, deep dives, or new Update 5 systems.

<GuideCallout title="Beginner rule">
The beginner mistake is not missing one secret item. It is leaving the safe area before you understand power, cable length, battery flow, recycler output, fabrication, and how to recharge without draining the wrong source.
</GuideCallout>

<h2 id="new-horizons-route">
  New Horizons route for returning players
</h2>

New Horizons is more than a few quality-of-life changes. It adds long-term project goals, oil sources, underwater points of interest, ship organization tools, and systems that can punish an old save if the boat is already messy.

The returning-player route I would use is:

1. Check the **New Horizons update notes** and new skill options.
2. Visit or mark **Pishon** for Project Eden.
3. Prepare **Stainless Steel** and construction parts before committing to the Eden build.
4. Decide whether **Oil Whale / Project Jonah** is worth the power and storage cost right now.
5. Clean up power, fuel, and gas routing with **wall outlets** before adding more ship systems.
6. Prepare for **Research Outpost Theta 9** with underwater mobility, weapons, empty inventory, and a save.

The important thing is not to do everything in one trip. Project Eden, Oil Whale, Theta 9, and ship cleanup can all compete for power, inventory space, and attention.

<h2 id="oil-whale">
  Oil Whale: useful, expensive, and not always faster
</h2>

Oil Whale is one of the biggest New Horizons systems, but it is easy to misunderstand.

The Whale can give a large oil pull, but the real question is not “can it give oil?” It is **whether your boat has enough power, storage, and routing to make the cycle worth it**.

  Research Outpost Theta 9

It starts from an old emergency survivor signal and leads into a sealed deep-sea facility tied to **Roth's Anomaly**. Inside, the route becomes a mix of underwater movement, enemy nests, door lock colors, terminal authorization, and the **Ventilation 3.3** blocker.

<h2 id="power-fuel-wall-outlets">
  Power, fuel, gas, and wall outlets
</h2>

Wall outlets are one of the most useful New Horizons changes because they reduce cable spaghetti. They are also easy to misuse.

The key idea is simple: wall outlets give you cleaner connection points, but they do not turn the ship into a wireless network. Range, direction, blocked output, input type, and what each connector is attached to still matter.

Use the <Link href="/the-last-caretaker/power-fuel-wall-outlets">Power & Fuel Wall Outlets Guide</Link> if you are trying to solve:

* power wall outlet placement;
* fuel wall outlet placement;
* gas wall outlet use;
* blocked output;
* cable range through walls and floors;
* Grid Scan checks;
* oil, diesel, and petrol routing;
* refinery or oil storage cleanup;
* ship cleanup before Project Eden or Oil Whale.

The key decision is whether the ship is still readable. If every cable, tank, and powered machine is obvious, do not rebuild just because outlets exist. If you have to follow three routes across the boat to know what is connected, clean the ship before adding more systems.

<h2 id="what-can-wait">
  What can wait until later?
</h2>

Not every name or signal needs to become your next major route immediately.

For now, I treat these as “check when nearby” goals unless the game gives me a clear reason to push them:

* Project Jonah details beyond the Oil Whale trigger route;
* Committee-related discoveries;
* Cultural Memories and Holo Memories away from my current route;
* Tower 4 / Pen 4 theories unless the game gives me a working objective;
* generic performance or settings checks if the boat route itself is stable;
* extra side exploration while Project Eden, Theta 9, or ship power is still unfinished.

This keeps the run focused. I would rather finish one system cleanly than half-start every New Horizons thread and return to the boat with no power, no space, and no clear next step.

<h2 id="common-mistakes">
  Hub-level mistakes that waste the most time
</h2>

These are the mistakes that affect more than one system:

* **Treating New Horizons as one giant trip:** Project Eden, Oil Whale, Theta 9, and wall outlets all compete for inventory, power, and attention.
* **Starting a route before its prerequisite is ready:** Pishon needs construction support, Oil Whale needs Project Jonah setup, and Theta 9 needs underwater readiness.
* **Adding systems onto a boat you cannot debug:** if power, fuel, gas, and storage are already confusing, every new objective becomes harder.
* **Overlooting before solving the blocker:** finish the route objective first, then do a cleanup pass for heavy loot.
* **Ignoring the return trip:** every deep dive, whale cycle, and construction run still has to end with you getting back safely.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

### Is The Last Caretaker worth starting after New Horizons?

Yes, if you like survival crafting, physical ship systems, cable routing, deep-water exploration, and long-term project goals. New Horizons gives returning players more to chase, but new players should still learn power, recycler, fabricator, battery, diesel, and boat basics first.

### What should I do first as a new player?

Reconnect power, learn cables, use the recycler and fabricator, stabilize the boat, and understand battery and diesel storage before sailing too far.

### What should I do first in Update 5?

Pick the route that matches your current blocker. Project Eden is for Pishon and first roots, Oil Whale is for Project Jonah and oil pulls, Theta 9 is for the underwater station, and wall outlets are for ship routing cleanup.

### When should I skip Project Eden for now?

Skip a real Eden build if you cannot craft Stainless Steel, cannot support water and power, or cannot carry construction parts. Mark Pishon first, then return prepared.

### When should I skip Oil Whale for now?

Skip the Whale if Lazarus Complex is not producing a mature human yet, if you only have seeds, or if your boat power and oil storage are not ready for the cycle.

### When should I skip Theta 9 for now?

Skip Theta 9 if you are overweight, missing underwater mobility, short on weapons, low on energy, or have no recent save. The station is safer when you enter prepared.

### Are wall outlets only quality of life?

No. They clean up the ship, but they also change how you plan power, fuel, gas, oil, diesel, petrol, and Project Eden support. Bad placement can still break the route.
