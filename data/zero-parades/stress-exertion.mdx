<h2 id="stress-guide-overview">
  ZERO PARADES Stress Guide Overview
</h2>

ZERO PARADES does not punish pressure the same way a normal RPG does.

You are not only watching health. You are watching **Fatigue**, **Anxiety**, and **Delirium**. These meters rise when Cascade pushes too hard, makes mistakes, goes too deep into certain dialogue paths, or uses Exertion to force a better chance on a check.

The important thing to understand early is this:

**Stress is a resource, not just a warning light.**

You can spend it when a roll matters. You can also waste it on a check that only gives a little extra text. The difference between those two habits is what makes the opening feel controlled or chaotic.

<h2 id="fatigue-anxiety-delirium-explained">
  Fatigue, Anxiety, and Delirium Explained
</h2>

Each stress meter tracks a different kind of pressure.

<GuideTable
  caption="ZERO PARADES stress meters and how beginners should read them."
  columns={stressMeterColumns}
  rows={stressMeterRows}
/>

You do not need to memorize every exact trigger in your first hour. Just watch the pattern:

- physical strain tends to push **Fatigue**,
- tense social or emotional pressure tends to push **Anxiety**,
- strange mental overload tends to push **Delirium**.

If one meter keeps rising, that is the game telling you what kind of pressure your current route is creating.

Stress can rise in several ways:

- going too far down a tense dialogue path,
- failing or forcing certain skill checks,
- using Exertion,
- interacting with strange objects or information,
- or choosing options that strain Cascade physically, socially, or mentally.

The early safe house already teaches this. You can gain pressure from memories, the red disc, and risky reads before you even understand the bigger mission.

That means you should not think of stress as something that only matters in action scenes. ZERO PARADES can pressure you through conversation, investigation, and memory.

Stress does not work like a simple health bar.

When a stress meter reaches its limit, ZERO PARADES can force you to lower a related skill to mitigate the damage. In the Anxiety example, the game offers Relation-side skills such as **Statehood**, **Cold Read**, and **Nerve**.

That is the important part: the penalty attacks the kind of ability tied to the pressure.

If Anxiety boils over, it can damage social or Relation-side tools. If Delirium gets too high, it threatens your ability to keep pushing strange mental checks. Fatigue should be treated the same way for physical pressure until you have confirmed the exact category behavior in your own run.

Most checks are built around dice and your relevant skill.

Exertion lets Cascade push harder. In practical terms, it gives you a better chance on the roll, but it raises the related stress meter. Current examples show stress increases around **+4 to +5**, depending on the check or scene.

That makes Exertion a trade:

**better chance now, more pressure later.**

This is why Exertion feels tempting. It can turn a borderline check into a real chance. But if you use it on everything, your meters climb quickly and the game starts taking options away from you.

Use Exertion when the result matters more than the stress.

**Would I reload if this failed?**

Dramatic Encounters are one of the best places to consider Exertion.

These scenes play out one action at a time. You may be trying to flee, blend in, improvise, or take control before the situation collapses. Because the outcome of one action can shape the next step, an important roll can be worth spending stress on.

That does not mean you should Exert every option.

Read the scene first. If the check lines up with your build and the result gives you a real route forward, push it. If the roll is a desperate action using one of your weak skills, look for another option before spending stress.

Stress recovery is where consumables start to matter.

The clearest confirmed pattern so far is tied to **Fatigue**. The Thought **Latest Synthetic Desires** gives a **1.33x bonus** to reducing Fatigue when consuming specific drinks, which confirms that consumables are not just flavor items.

The practical rule is:

**Use consumables for recovery, and use gear or route changes to avoid creating more stress in the first place.**

If Fatigue is threatening Action checks, drinks that reduce Fatigue matter more than a generic skill bonus. If Anxiety or Delirium is climbing, stop forcing the same kind of dialogue or mental reads until you have a safer path or a confirmed recovery option.

<h2 id="should-you-reload-stress-mistakes">
  Should You Reload After Stress Mistakes?
</h2>

Usually, no.

If you used Exertion on a bad check and raised a meter, that is annoying, but it is not always worth reloading. ZERO PARADES is built around pressure and consequences. A messy path is not automatically a failed path.

Reload only if:

- you lost access to a route you specifically wanted,
- you triggered skill loss right before a major check,
- you wasted stress because you misunderstood a key system,
- or you are testing a specific outcome.

Otherwise, keep going. Learn what kind of pressure your choices create, then play cleaner from there.

<h2 id="best-beginner-rules">
  Three Rules for Stress Management
</h2>

Use these three rules if you are still learning:

1. **Spend stress only for route-changing checks.** If a roll does not change your goal, do not pay pressure for it.
2. **Fix checks before forcing checks.** Try gear, context, another route, or recovery before Exertion.
3. **Recover the meter you keep abusing.** If the same meter keeps rising, change your approach instead of pushing harder.

That last rule is the real difference between a messy run and a controlled one.

A clean ZERO PARADES run is not a run where every roll succeeds. It is a run where you spend pressure only when the result is worth the cost.

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

<h3 id="what-are-fatigue-anxiety-and-delirium">
  What are Fatigue, Anxiety, and Delirium in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

Fatigue, Anxiety, and Delirium are the three main stress meters. They track different kinds of pressure and can make your run harder if you let them climb too high.

<h3 id="how-much-stress-does-exertion-add">
  How much stress does Exertion add?
</h3>

Exertion raises the related stress meter. Current examples show increases around **+4 to +5**, depending on the check or scene, so do not treat Exertion as a free retry.

<h3 id="what-does-exertion-do">
  What does Exertion do?
</h3>

Exertion lets you push a skill check for a better chance, but it raises the related stress meter. It is useful for important rolls, but dangerous if you use it casually.

<h3 id="should-i-use-exertion-on-every-check">
  Should I use Exertion on every check?
</h3>

No. Use Exertion only when the check matters. If a roll is optional, low-value, or fixable with gear, save your stress.

<h3 id="what-does-pressured-mean">
  What does Pressured mean in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

Pressured means one of your stress meters is high enough that Exertion becomes restricted. A Delirium example shows the meter at 15 with Limit Reached and Cannot exert, but other meters should be tested separately before assuming every category uses the exact same visible threshold.

<h3 id="what-number-triggers-pressured">
  What number triggers Pressured or skill loss?
</h3>

A Delirium example shows Limit Reached and Cannot exert at 15. An Anxiety example shows Anxiety Limit Reached, a forced skill reduction, and Anxiety reset afterward. More testing is still useful before treating every meter and every difficulty setting as identical.

<h3 id="can-stress-kill-you">
  Can stress kill you in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

Stress itself is more about skill loss than a normal death. Some specific choices or checks can still lead to death scenes, but maxing a stress meter is mainly dangerous because it can damage your skills.

<h3 id="what-happens-when-anxiety-limit-is-reached">
  What happens when Anxiety limit is reached?
</h3>

A visible Anxiety example shows Anxiety Raised +5, Anxiety Limit Reached, then a prompt to lower a Relation-side skill such as Statehood, Cold Read, or Nerve. After Cold Read is lowered, Anxiety resets.

<h3 id="how-do-i-lower-stress">
  How do I lower stress?
</h3>

Use recovery options, consumables, safer routing, and fewer unnecessary Exertions. Fatigue recovery has confirmed consumable synergy through Latest Synthetic Desires, which boosts Fatigue reduction from specific drinks.

<h3 id="which-consumables-reduce-fatigue">
  Which consumables reduce Fatigue?
</h3>

Rumba Canned Coffee, Jefa Red & Black Blend, and Camisado Ultra Sweet are shown as drinks affected by Latest Synthetic Desires, which gives a 1.33x bonus to reducing Fatigue when consuming them.

<h3 id="is-skill-loss-a-run-ender">
  Is skill loss a run ender?
</h3>

Usually no. Skill loss hurts, but you can keep playing, use gear, adjust your route, and rebuild. Reload only if the loss ruins a specific outcome you care about.

<h3 id="what-is-the-best-stress-tip-for-beginners">
  What is the best stress tip for beginners?
</h3>

Save Exertion for checks that change your route or protect a goal. Do not raise stress just to win every small roll.
