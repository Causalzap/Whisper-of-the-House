The Umbral Host is the best example of why you need to read the violation text before conditioning a Thought.

On paper, it is excellent:

- **Shadowplay skill potential +2**
- **+1 bonus to all active unlocked checks at night**
- **Embrace the Shadow Realm**

That sounds like an obvious pick for a covert spy build. But the violation says you gain **Action disadvantage when sleeping at night between 9 PM and 4 AM**, and you can temporarily lose reinforced effects.

That is not decorative flavor text.

It changes how you route your day. If you naturally sleep at night to stabilize your run, The Umbral Host fights your habits. If you are willing to avoid normal night sleep and lean into night activity, it becomes much stronger.

That is the whole Conditioning system in one example:

**the best Thought is not the one with the biggest upside. It is the one whose rule you can actually obey.**

<h2 id="what-conditioning-does">
  What Conditioning Does in ZERO PARADES
</h2>

Conditioning is the system that turns a Thought into a real part of your build.

In practice, that usually means three things:

- it raises the **skill potential** of one or more skills,
- it adds a **gameplay bonus, option, or behavior rule**,
- and it gives you a **violation condition** that can temporarily strip the reinforced effects or apply a disadvantage.

A clothing item helps with one moment.  
A Thought can reshape how Cascade plays for hours.

That is why Conditioning is more dangerous than gear. If you pick a bad jacket, you can swap it. If you pick a Thought that fights your habits, you may have to play around that rule for a long time.

- **The Umbral Host** punishes normal night sleep with Action disadvantage.

So before you commit, ask:

**Will this Thought make me play better, or just make me babysit a rule I hate?**

Do not take a Thought just to pass one check.

If you only need a temporary skill boost, check your clothing and held items first. Gear is reversible. Conditioning is a bigger commitment.

Use this rule:

**Gear for one check. Conditioning for a build. Exertion for a moment.**

That keeps you from turning every small problem into a long-term drawback.

<h2 id="conditioning-vs-thought-cabinet">
  Conditioning vs Disco Elysium Thought Cabinet
</h2>

Conditioning is the system Disco Elysium players will compare to the Thought Cabinet first, but the comparison is only partly useful.

The Thought Cabinet usually felt like a mix of roleplay identity, passive modifier, joke, and long-form worldview. Conditioning is closer to a **build rule**. It still shapes who Cascade is, but it is much more directly tied to skill potential, practical bonuses, and behavior-driven drawbacks.

<h2 id="can-you-undo-conditioning">
  Can You Undo or Remove Conditioning?
</h2>

I have not confirmed a full remove or respec option for Conditioning yet.

Right now, the safest advice is:

- treat every Thought as a real commitment,
- save before conditioning anything with a serious downside,
- and do not assume you can freely undo a Thought later.

The current screenshots show a **Hold to Condition** prompt, but no obvious general remove or undo button. Until you confirm one in your own run, play as if the choice matters.

<h3 id="what-is-the-best-conditioning-in-zero-parades">
  What is the best Conditioning in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

Based on the currently confirmed Thoughts, **Primitive Accumulation** looks like the best general first-run Conditioning choice because it supports Blueprints and Personalism. **Latest Synthetic Desires** is better if you want Instincts and Fatigue management.

<h3 id="which-thought-is-best-for-a-first-run">
  Which Thought is best for a first run?
</h3>

Primitive Accumulation is the safest confirmed first-run pick so far. It reinforces two broadly useful skills and has a clear drawback you can plan around.

<h3 id="can-you-undo-or-remove-a-thought">
  Can you undo or remove a Thought in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

I have not confirmed a general undo or respec option yet. Until that changes, assume a Thought is a meaningful commitment and save before taking one with a harsh drawback.

<h3 id="can-a-bad-thought-ruin-your-run">
  Can a bad Thought ruin your run?
</h3>

Usually not, but it can make your run messier. A bad Thought can push you toward a style you do not enjoy, or keep applying violations you regularly trigger.

<h3 id="what-happens-if-you-violate-a-thought">
  What happens if you violate a Thought?
</h3>

Confirmed Thoughts show a common pattern: you temporarily lose reinforced effects, and some Thoughts also apply a category disadvantage such as Action disadvantage or Relation disadvantage.

<h3 id="which-thought-is-best-for-a-relation-build">
  Which Thought is best for a Relation build?
</h3>

Primitive Accumulation and Apology World Tour 96 are the best confirmed Relation-adjacent options so far. Primitive Accumulation gives Personalism and Blueprints, while Apology World Tour 96 gives Personalism and Records.

<h3 id="which-thought-is-best-for-an-action-build">
  Which Thought is best for an Action build?
</h3>

The Wang Way fits Coordination and Technoflex, while The Umbral Host is better for Shadowplay-heavy or night-focused covert play. Latest Synthetic Desires also helps if your Action build needs Fatigue support.

<h3 id="is-conditioning-the-same-as-thought-cabinet">
  Is Conditioning the same as the Thought Cabinet in Disco Elysium?
</h3>

No. They are similar in spirit, but Conditioning is more explicit about skill potential, visible bonuses, and violation rules. You should judge a Thought as a gameplay commitment, not just a roleplay idea.

<h3 id="should-i-take-thoughts-early-or-wait">
  Should I take Thoughts early or wait?
</h3>

Wait if you do not understand your build or the drawback yet. Commit early only when the Thought clearly supports your route and you know the violation rule will not fight your normal habits.
