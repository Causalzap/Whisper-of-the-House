<h2 id="zero-parades-vs-disco-elysium-overview">
  ZERO PARADES vs Disco Elysium Overview
</h2>

ZERO PARADES feels familiar if you loved Disco Elysium, but it is not a game you should play on autopilot.

You still spend a lot of time reading, choosing dialogue, following strange leads, and letting your character's inner systems comment on the world. The difference is that Cascade is not Harry, Portiro is not Martinaise, and ZERO PARADES is built around spy pressure instead of detective collapse.

The safest way to start is:

**bring your Disco Elysium reading habits, but leave your exact Disco Elysium build habits behind.**

If you want the general starter guide, read the <Link href="/zero-parades">ZERO PARADES guide hub</Link>.

<h2 id="do-you-need-to-play-disco-elysium-first">
  Do You Need to Play Disco Elysium First?
</h2>

No, you do not need to play Disco Elysium first.

ZERO PARADES is its own game with its own protagonist, setting, factions, systems, and opening mystery. Disco Elysium experience helps because you already understand this style of RPG: lots of reading, internal commentary, skill checks, strange political humor, and failure that does not always mean “game over.”

But you can start here.

<h2 id="is-zero-parades-connected-to-disco-elysium-story">
  Is ZERO PARADES Connected to Disco Elysium's Story?
</h2>

No. Treat ZERO PARADES as a separate story.

It does not ask you to remember Revachol, Harry, Kim, the murder case, the Pale, or Disco Elysium's ending. The useful connection is not plot continuity. It is **design language**: dialogue-heavy RPG structure, skill checks, internal systems, political worldbuilding, failure, clothing modifiers, and strange character-shaping choices.

That means the right buying question is not “Will I understand the story without Disco Elysium?”

You will.

The better question is:

**Do I want another dense narrative RPG that uses some familiar design instincts in a different spy-fiction structure?**

The opening feels lonelier than Disco Elysium.

Harry has Kim as a stabilizing presence early. Kim gives pushback, context, and a second mind in the room. Cascade begins in a different position. Pseudopod is there, but he is incapacitated, and that changes the rhythm immediately.

That means you need to do more of the early framing yourself.

For the first 30 minutes, treat **object inspection and journal reading as your Kim substitute**. The safe house, the red disc, the purchase order, and the journal tasks are doing part of the work Kim used to do: they give you context, pressure, next steps, and reality checks when the scene feels too strange.

So when the opening feels like it is not explaining enough, do not rush out. Read the room, inspect the weird object, then check the journal entry that updates afterward.

ZERO PARADES has fewer skills than Disco Elysium.

That matters because each investment feels more concentrated. Instead of spreading your identity across a larger skill sheet, you are choosing a sharper direction for Cascade.

Disco Elysium players need to unlearn one thing quickly:

**Fatigue, Anxiety, and Delirium are not just Health and Morale with different names.**

  Exertion Changes How You Think About Rolls

<figure className="my-8">

  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

In Disco Elysium, you usually look at a check and decide whether to roll now, improve the odds, or come back later.

ZERO PARADES adds another temptation: **Exertion**.

Exertion can make a check more favorable, but it raises stress. That makes every important roll a small resource question:

- Is this check important?
- Is the reward worth the pressure?
- Can I improve it with gear first?
- Is the related meter already dangerous?
- Would I reload if this failed?

If the answer is no, save the stress.

Disco Elysium players will naturally compare Conditioning to the Thought Cabinet.

That comparison is useful, but incomplete.

Conditioning is about internalizing Thoughts, raising potential, and changing how Cascade plays. Some Thoughts give strong benefits. Some come with drawbacks or violation rules. That makes them feel less like collectibles and more like commitments.

The first-run rule is:

**do not condition a Thought until you understand the drawback.**

  Gear Matters More Than You Might Expect
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image

    alt="Inventory screen in ZERO PARADES showing gear and skill modifiers."

    height={900}

  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">

  </figcaption>

If you already checked clothes before important rolls in Disco Elysium, keep that habit.

ZERO PARADES rewards the same instinct, but the stakes are different because Exertion has a stress cost. A gear swap is safer than pushing a roll when your Fatigue, Anxiety, or Delirium is already high.

Before using Exertion:

1. Check the skill.
2. Open your inventory.
3. See whether gear changes that skill.
4. Look for better context or modifiers.
5. Exert only if the result is worth the pressure.

This is one of the best habits to bring over from Disco Elysium.

<h2 id="dramatic-encounters">
  Dramatic Encounters Are Not Normal Combat
</h2>

<figure className="my-8">
  <Image
    src="/images/zero-parades/zero-parades-dramatic-encounter-bazaar.webp"
    alt="A dramatic encounter in ZERO PARADES during a tense bazaar escape."
    width={1600}
    height={900}
    className="rounded-xl"
  />
  <figcaption className="mt-3 text-sm text-slate-400">
    Dramatic Encounters are tense step-by-step scenes, not a traditional combat system.
  </figcaption>
</figure>

ZERO PARADES has scenes that look more structured than most Disco Elysium confrontations.

Do not read them as normal battles.

Dramatic Encounters are high-pressure sequences where you choose actions step by step. You may flee, blend in, push forward, read the situation, or improvise. Your build matters because different options may lean on different skills.

For example, a bazaar escape-style scene is not just “pass one roll and win.” You may need to read the crowd, choose a path, decide whether to move physically or socially, and then live with how that route changes the next step. That is why a Disco-style pure dialogue build can still feel exposed if it has no Action backup.

The best way to handle Dramatic Encounters is:

1. Read the scene before choosing.
2. Look for options that match your build.
3. Use Exertion only when the result matters.
4. Accept that one failure may create a new route rather than a full stop.

<h2 id="failure-works-differently">
  Failure Works Differently
</h2>

Disco Elysium is famous for making failure interesting.

ZERO PARADES still has failure, but the rhythm is different. Stress, skill loss, and route pressure can matter more than a funny immediate disaster.

<GuideTable
  caption="How to think about failure in ZERO PARADES after Disco Elysium."
  columns={failureColumns}
  rows={failureRows}
/>

The beginner rule is:

**do not reload every failed check, but do save before major commitments.**

<h2 id="faq">
  FAQ
</h2>

<h3 id="is-zero-parades-connected-to-disco-elysium">
  Is ZERO PARADES connected to Disco Elysium?
</h3>

No. ZERO PARADES is not a direct sequel and does not continue Disco Elysium's story. It has its own protagonist, world, factions, and systems, even though Disco Elysium players will notice familiar design DNA.

<h3 id="do-i-need-to-play-disco-elysium-first">
  Do I need to play Disco Elysium first?
</h3>

No. You can start with ZERO PARADES. Disco Elysium helps you understand the style, but the story and systems are separate.

<h3 id="is-zero-parades-like-disco-elysium">
  Is ZERO PARADES like Disco Elysium?
</h3>

Yes, in broad RPG structure: lots of reading, dialogue, skill checks, internal commentary, politics, and strange humor. But it plays differently because of stress meters, Exertion, Conditioning, broader exploration, and Dramatic Encounters.

<h3 id="zero-parades-vs-disco-elysium-which-is-better">
  ZERO PARADES vs Disco Elysium: which is better?
</h3>

Disco Elysium is probably the better fit if you want a tighter detective story, Kim-style companion structure, and a stronger internal skill chorus. ZERO PARADES is better if you want a separate spy RPG with pressure meters, route movement, gear checks, Conditioning, and more structured high-tension sequences.

<h3 id="how-many-skills-does-zero-parades-have">
  How many skills does ZERO PARADES have?
</h3>

ZERO PARADES has 15 skills grouped into three broad Faculties. Disco Elysium had 24 skills across four categories.

<h3 id="what-replaces-health-and-morale">
  What replaces Health and Morale in ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

ZERO PARADES uses Fatigue, Anxiety, and Delirium. These meters track different kinds of pressure and can punish your skills if they get too high.

<h3 id="is-conditioning-the-same-as-thought-cabinet">
  Is Conditioning the same as the Thought Cabinet?
</h3>

No. Conditioning feels related, but it works inside ZERO PARADES' own build and stress systems. Thoughts can raise skill potential, add effects, and include drawbacks or violation rules.

<h3 id="should-i-build-cascade-like-harry">
  Should I build Cascade like Harry?
</h3>

No. Build Cascade for spy work: reading people, understanding clues, managing pressure, and staying flexible during route changes.

<h3 id="should-disco-elysium-fans-play-zero-parades">
  Should Disco Elysium fans play ZERO PARADES?
</h3>

Yes, if you want another dense narrative RPG and are open to a different rhythm. Do not expect the exact same companion dynamic, writing feel, or failure system.
